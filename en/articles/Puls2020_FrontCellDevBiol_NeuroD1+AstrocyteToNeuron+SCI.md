# Regeneration of Functional Neurons After Spinal Cord Injury via in situ NeuroD1-Mediated Astrocyte-to-Neuron Conversion

## Citation (NLM)
Puls B, Ding Y, Zhang F, Pan M, Lei Z, Pei Z, Jiang M, Bai Y, Forsyth C, Metzger M, Rana T, Zhang L, Ding X, Keefe M, Cai A, Redilla A, Lai M, He K, Li H, Chen G. Regeneration of Functional Neurons After Spinal Cord Injury via in situ NeuroD1-Mediated Astrocyte-to-Neuron Conversion. Front Cell Dev Biol. 2020;8:591883. doi:10.3389/fcell.2020.591883

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.3389/fcell.2020.591883](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcell.2020.591883)

---

## Background

Spinal cord injury (SCI) causes loss of motor and sensory function, in part because injury-induced neuronal loss cannot be replenished endogenously. After SCI, resident astrocytes become reactive, proliferative, and hypertrophic, forming a glial scar that inhibits axonal regeneration. Decades of effort have focused on overcoming the scar and regrowing axons, but lost spinal neurons also need replacement to rebuild local circuits; cell transplantation faces challenges of cell identity and circuit integration.

In vivo neuronal reprogramming — forced expression of neurogenic transcription factors to convert endogenous glia into neurons — has emerged as an alternative. The authors previously showed the single factor NeuroD1 converts reactive astrocytes into neurons in injured brain and Alzheimer's models. This study tests whether AAV-delivered NeuroD1 can regenerate functional neurons from reactive astrocytes in the injured spinal cord.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. SCI models in adult mice (2–4 months): T11–T12 laminectomy with stab injury (31G needle) or contusive injury (Infinite Horizon Impactor, 45 kdyn).
2. Viral delivery of NeuroD1: CAG-driven retrovirus (NeuroD1-GFP, targets dividing cells, injected 4 dpi) and AAV9 Cre-Flex system with human GFAP promoter (AAV GFAP::Cre + AAV FLEX-CAG::NeuroD1-P2A-mCherry/GFP) to target dividing and non-dividing reactive astrocytes.
3. Immunohistochemistry with GFAP, NeuN, Olig2, Iba1, and subtype markers (Tlx3, Pax2; cortical FoxG1/Tbr1; CaMKII-GFP; GAD-GFP transgenic mice; S100b) to quantify conversion efficiency and neuronal subtype.
4. Combinatorial NeuroD1 + Dlx2 AAV5 delivery to bias toward GABAergic (Pax2+) fate.
5. Side-by-side conversion in M1 motor cortex vs. spinal cord to test region-specific identity.
6. Whole-cell patch-clamp electrophysiology on spinal cord slices (8–10 wpi); synaptic marker staining (SV2, VGluT1/2); c-Fos after running-wheel activity.
7. Contusive SCI conversion with short-delay (viral injection 10 days post-injury) and long-delay (16 weeks post-injury) paradigms.

---

## Results

- NeuroD1 retrovirus converted dividing glia in stab-injured dorsal horn into NeuN+ neurons, reaching 93.5% by 6 wpi; control GFP infected astrocytes, OPCs, and microglia but not neurons.
- AAV GFAP::Cre + FLEX-NeuroD1 converted reactive astrocytes into NeuN+/GFAP- neurons with ~95% efficiency by 8 wpi, passing through GFAP+/NeuN+ "AtN transitional cells"; minimal apoptosis during conversion.
- Converted dorsal horn neurons were mainly Tlx3+ glutamatergic (62.6% AAV; 50.3% retrovirus) with a minority Pax2+ GABAergic (8.8%/16.4%); CaMKII-GFP and GAD-GFP confirmed subtypes.
- Combining NeuroD1 + Dlx2 increased Pax2+ GABAergic neurons ~5-fold (to 32.5%), showing subtype ratio can be tuned.
- Region-specific identity: cortical conversion yielded FoxG1+/Tbr1+ neurons (0% Tlx3/Pax2), whereas spinal conversion yielded Tlx3+/Pax2+ neurons (0% FoxG1/Tbr1), indicating glial lineage and local environment dictate fate.
- Converted neurons were functional: repetitive action potentials, large Na+/K+ currents, spontaneous EPSCs (no significant difference from native neurons), synaptic puncta (SV2, VGluT1/2), and c-Fos activation.
- In contusive SCI, conversion succeeded around the lesion core with both short-delay (~55% efficiency, ~2,000 converted neurons) and long-delay/chronic (>95% efficiency after glial scar formation) injection, with Tlx3+ glutamatergic subtype maintained.

---

## Perspective

The study demonstrates that a single transcription factor, NeuroD1, delivered by AAV under an astrocyte-specific promoter, can regenerate functional, region-appropriate neurons from reactive astrocytes in the injured spinal cord across both stab and clinically relevant contusive models, including chronic (long-delay) treatment. This suggests a paradigm shift from classical axonal regeneration toward neuronal regeneration for spinal cord repair, leveraging internal glia to regenerate gray-matter neurons with intrinsic region-specific identity — an advantage over external cell transplantation. Notable points/limitations: the dorsal-horn focus (motor neuron regeneration in the ventral horn is a separate ongoing study), efficiency estimates that may overcount converted neurons in contusion samples, and the absence here of behavioral/functional recovery data. The clinically relevant contusive model sets up future work to evaluate whether this reprogramming translates into functional recovery after SCI.
