# Foxj1 expressing ependymal cells do not contribute new cells to sites of injury or stroke in the mouse forebrain

## Citation (NLM)
Muthusamy N, Brumm A, Zhang X, Carmichael ST, Ghashghaei HT. Foxj1 expressing ependymal cells do not contribute new cells to sites of injury or stroke in the mouse forebrain. Sci Rep. 2018;8:1766. doi:10.1038/s41598-018-19913-x

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-018-19913-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-018-19913-x)

---

## Background

The stem cell source of neural and glial progenitors in the periventricular regions of the adult forebrain has been controversial. Several prior studies used a human FOXJ1 promoter element (viral, electroporated, or transgenic) to lineage-trace ependymal cells (ECs) and reported that ECs generate new neurons for the olfactory bulbs and expand after stroke, and in the spinal cord that ECs contribute substantially to the glial scar. The authors were concerned that the ~1 kb human FOXJ1 promoter used in these studies produces ectopic expression outside the EC layer, potentially mislabeling non-EC progeny.

To trace EC progeny from the endogenous locus, they used a knock-in Foxj1creERT2::GFP mouse (crossed to the Ai9 tdTomato reporter). A recent study using this line already showed that spinal cord injury fails to make Foxj1+ ECs proliferate or contribute substantially to the glial scar. Here they test whether stab injury and three stroke models in the forebrain induce ependyma to become neurogenic or gliogenic progenitors.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Knock-in Foxj1creERT2::GFP mice on the Ai9 (tdTomato) reporter background; tamoxifen (TAM) induction over 5 days to lineage-label ECs.
2. Bilateral cortical stab injuries in the motor cortex, with recombination induced either in young adults (P39, injured P42) or during early postnatal days (P1-P5, injured P42); analysis at P56 (2 weeks) and at 1 month post-injury for prolonged survival.
3. Three stroke models in P95 mice: photothrombotic (PT) motor-cortex stroke (Rose Bengal), middle cerebral artery occlusion (MCAO) for barrel-field cortex, and L-NIO-induced striatal stroke; 14-day survival.
4. Immunohistochemistry of 50 um sagittal sections for tdTom (RFP) plus markers: GFAP and S100 (astrocytes), Olig2 (glial progenitors), DCX/Tuj1 (neuroblasts/immature neurons), NeuN (mature neurons); DAPI counterstain.
5. Confocal tile-scan imaging and cell counting (ImageJ Cell Counter) across forebrain regions (SEZ, white matter, cortex, olfactory bulb) using the Allen Brain Atlas for boundaries; statistics by 2-tailed t test.

---

## Results

- After cortical stab injury, little to no tdTom+ cells were found along the injured site or surrounding forebrain; the rare tdTom+ cells near the injury sat within the scar and were nearly all glia-like.
- The scarce delaminated tdTom+ cells overlapped robustly with S100 but far less with GFAP or Olig2, and only a slight elevation of (mostly immature-neuron-like) cells appeared in the olfactory bulb.
- Prolonged survival (1 month) captured no additional tdTom+ cells in the forebrain.
- Early postnatal (P1-P5) induction, which labels a quiescent population producing some OB neurons, still yielded little to no tdTom+ cells at the injury site and no significant difference in OB cell number/type between injured and naive mice.
- In all three stroke models, few if any tdTom+ cells were found outside the EC layer. In the PT model a few cells delaminated into the overlying white matter, and presumptive ECs near the stroke showed a striking transformation with elongated processes directed toward the stroke; these transformations were not seen with blunt injury.

---

## Perspective

Using endogenous-locus lineage tracing, the study rules out Foxj1+ ependymal cells as a stem cell source generating new neurons or glia in response to forebrain injury or stroke, consistent with prior findings in the spinal canal. The authors attribute the earlier positive reports to ectopic labeling by the human FOXJ1 promoter element. The morphological transformation of ECs specific to ischemic stroke (but not blunt injury) suggests trauma versus ischemia engage distinct, non-stem/progenitor ependymal responses that remain to be defined. Limitations noted include the possibility that a Foxj1-negative EC subpopulation with progenitor capacity could escape tracing (deemed unlikely since the EC phenotype depends on Foxj1). The work reframes the role of ependyma toward homeostasis and injury responses rather than neurogenesis/gliogenesis.
