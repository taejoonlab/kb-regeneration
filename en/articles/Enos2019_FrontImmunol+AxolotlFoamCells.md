# Meningeal Foam Cells and Ependymal Cells in Axolotl Spinal Cord Regeneration

## Citation (NLM)
Enos N, Takenaka H, Scott S, Salfity HVN, Kirk M, Egar MW, Sarria DA, Slayback-Barry D, Belecky-Adams T, Chernoff EAG. Meningeal Foam Cells and Ependymal Cells in Axolotl Spinal Cord Regeneration. Front Immunol. 2019;10:2558. doi:10.3389/fimmu.2019.02558

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.3389/fimmu.2019.02558](https://doi.org/10.3389/fimmu.2019.02558)

---

## Background

Unlike mammals, the adult axolotl (*Ambystoma mexicanum*, an aquatic salamander) can regenerate a transected spinal cord across a lesion gap. In mammalian SCI, penetrating injury produces a meningeal (fibrotic) scar that reinforces the astrocytic scar and forms a permanent barrier to axonal regrowth. In urodele amphibians the fibrotic meninges is instead remodeled and excluded to the periphery of the regenerating cord, a process involving ependymal (ependymoglial) outgrowth and digestion of extracellular matrix (ECM). While ependymal cells have been studied extensively in urodele regeneration, the meningeal reaction has received far less attention.

This study reports a previously unidentified population of foam cells (foamy macrophages) that accumulates in the invasive fibrotic meninges during gap regeneration of transected adult axolotl spinal cord, together with multinucleated giant cells (MNGCs). Foamy macrophages are monocyte-derived, lipid-laden cells known from mammalian pathologies (multiple sclerosis, atherosclerosis, ALS, Charcot-Marie-Tooth) where they can act as sinks for oxidized lipoproteins and myelin fragments and can degrade ECM. The authors ask whether such cells participate in salamander spinal cord regeneration and whether they interact with reactive ependymoglia.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Complete spinal cord transection (gap lesion) surgery in adult and juvenile axolotls; regeneration followed to ~5 weeks.
2. Primary explant tissue culture of ~2-week regenerating cord outgrowth on poly-D-lysine/fibronectin-coated dishes.
3. Histological staining: trichrome (collagen), metanil yellow + alcian blue (collagen/proteoglycan) on wholemounts and paraffin sections.
4. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) of lesion-site MNGCs, ependymal cells, foam cells, and fibrillar collagen.
5. Actin cytoskeleton labeling with rhodamine-phalloidin (podosomes, sealing ring, ruffled border).
6. Lipid detection: Oil Red O (neutral lipid), DiI (polar lipid probe), and DyRect polar-lipid live-cell stain.
7. Oxidized LDL uptake assay using DiI-labeled Ox-LDL, with CD36 functional inhibition by sulfo-N-succinimidyl oleate.
8. Myelin studies: axolotl brain myelin extraction and DiI labeling, plus Fluoromyelin Red staining of endogenous myelin uptake.
9. Immunohistochemistry for CD36 (scavenger receptor/fatty acid translocase), TLR4, and cathepsin K in vivo and in vitro.
10. 3H-glucosamine uptake autoradiography to assay glycosaminoglycan/proteoglycan (ECM) production.
11. Quantification of ependymal outgrowth morphologies (condensed vs. dispersed, with/without foam cells) by one-way ANOVA with Tukey's post-hoc test.

## Results

- Fibrotic meningeal ECM (fibrillar collagen plus sulfated proteoglycan) fills the lesion gap and wraps the regenerating stumps; lipid-laden mononucleated cells and MNGCs are embedded throughout this ECM, concentrated near the regenerating end and absent ~2 cm distal to the transection.
- Rhodamine-phalloidin revealed osteoclast-like podosome/sealing-ring and ruffled-border structures on MNGCs. TEM showed close juxtaposition of MNGCs, foam cells, and ependymal cells, with perinuclear (vimentin) intermediate filaments, engulfed myelin fragments, and foamy macrophages engulfing fibrillar collagen.
- Both foamy macrophages and reactive ependymoglia contained neutral and polar lipids (Oil Red O, DiI, DyRect positive); foam cells carried larger and more abundant lipid droplets.
- Both cell types expressed the lipid scavenger receptor CD36 (co-expressed with TLR4) in vivo and in vitro; TLR4 was absent from large lipid-free MNGCs.
- A subpopulation of foamy macrophages took up DiI-Ox-LDL in vitro, whereas ependymal cells and MNGCs did not. CD36 inhibition (sulfo-N-succinimidyl oleate) markedly reduced Ox-LDL uptake, confirming a functional role for CD36.
- Both foam cells and ependymoglia took up myelin fragments in situ (Fluoromyelin) and in vitro (DiI-myelin); foam cells retained labeled myelin longer while ependymal cells turned it over during a 12-day chase.
- Both cell types expressed the ECM-degrading cysteine protease cathepsin K; in foam cells cathepsin K was concentrated within the sealing ring against the substratum, consistent with localized ECM digestion.
- 3H-glucosamine uptake indicated only ependymal cells produce glycosaminoglycan/proteoglycan ECM, while both cell types remove ECM — suggesting complementary roles.
- Later in regeneration (4-5 weeks) the reactive meninges was excluded to the periphery, the central canal re-formed, and denticulate ligaments (pial extensions) reappeared, indicating pial restoration around the regenerating cord.

---

## Perspective

This is the first report that foamy macrophages accumulate in the reactive axolotl spinal meninges and participate in regeneration — a striking contrast to mammalian SCI, where foam cells form within cord tissue and are not reported in the meninges. The data suggest foam cells and ependymoglia act as complementary sinks for toxic lipids and myelin debris while cooperatively remodeling fibrotic ECM: foamy macrophages digest meningeal ECM from outside, ependymal cells act from within the cord while also secreting new ECM. This complementary lipid-clearing and matrix-remodeling activity may help convert an inhibitory fibrotic barrier into a regeneration-permissive environment, offering a mechanistic contrast that could inform strategies to make the mammalian fibrotic scar more permissive. Limitations include the descriptive/correlative nature of much of the in situ work, reliance on in vitro explant behavior, uncertainty over whether non-uptaking cells were saturated or culture-limited, and lack of definitive proof that foam cells are net beneficial in vivo. Future work should establish the origin and functional necessity of these cells and their signaling interactions with ependymoglia.
