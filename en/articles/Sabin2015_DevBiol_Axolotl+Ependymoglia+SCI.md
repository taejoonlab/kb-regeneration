# Dynamic membrane depolarization is an early regulator of ependymoglial cell response to spinal cord injury in axolotl

## Citation (NLM)
Sabin K, Santos-Ferreira T, Essig J, Rudasill S, Echeverri K. Dynamic membrane depolarization is an early regulator of ependymoglial cell response to spinal cord injury in axolotl. Dev Biol. 2015;408(1):14-25. doi:10.1016/j.ydbio.2015.10.012

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ydbio.2015.10.012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ydbio.2015.10.012)

---

## Background

The Mexican axolotl (a salamander) can functionally regenerate its spinal cord after injury, regaining sensory and motor function comparable to pre-lesion levels — an ability mammals lack. After spinal cord injury (SCI), ependymoglial cells, which act as resident neural stem cells lining the central canal, proliferate and migrate to bridge the lesion and guide subsequent axon regeneration. While the cellular response has been characterized, the molecular signals that initiate the pro-regenerative response remain poorly understood.

Membrane potential (Vmem), the charge separation across the plasma membrane, is increasingly recognized as a regulator of regeneration. In Xenopus tail regeneration, the H+ V-ATPase repolarizes the injury site within 24 h and is required for blastema proliferation and downstream morphogen expression. Prior imaging in axolotl showed rapid ion and Vmem changes near injury, but their functional significance for SCI was untested. This study develops an axolotl SCI model and asks whether dynamic Vmem changes in ependymoglial cells are required to launch spinal cord regeneration.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Reproducible spinal cord ablation model in 3–5 cm axolotls (~500 µm segment removed); repair tracked by Acid fuchsin orange G (AfoG) histology and rhodamine-dextran dye continuity tests.
2. In vivo labeling of ependymoglial cells with GFAP- or nestin-promoter-driven GFP by injection/electroporation, followed by daily fluorescence imaging to trace cell origin, migration, and contribution.
3. BrdU pulse (24 h) to measure proliferation kinetics at 3, 5, 7 dpi.
4. In vivo Vmem imaging with the voltage-sensitive dye DiBAC4(3) before injury and at 7 h and 24 h post-injury.
5. Pharmacological/genetic Vmem manipulation: ivermectin and glycine (open glycine-gated Cl- channels → depolarization), and electroporation of Kir2.1 (hyperpolarizing) or Exp-1 (depolarizing) channels; intracellular chloride assay.
6. Microarray transcriptional profiling (Affymetrix Amby002) of uninjured, control-injured, and ivermectin-injured cords at 1, 3, 7 dpi; Ingenuity Pathway Analysis (GEO GSE71934).
7. Immunohistochemistry for c-Fos, GFAP, glycine receptor, β-III tubulin (whole-mount axon regeneration); ERK/JNK inhibition (FR180202, SP600125) and Western blot for phospho-ERK.

---

## Results

- The ~500 µm lesion sealed into terminal vesicles and reconnected by 7 dpi; dye tests confirmed the severed ends tightly seal during early regeneration.
- Live imaging showed cells from both rostral and caudal sides contribute bi-directionally to the regenerate; only cells within ~500 µm rostral and ~350 µm caudal of the injury migrate and participate. Proliferation peaked at 3 dpi and returned to homeostatic levels by 5 dpi.
- DiBAC imaging revealed a rapid, significant depolarization of ependymoglial cells peaking ~7 h post-injury (within ~500 µm of the lesion), repolarizing to near-uninjured levels by 24 h.
- Prolonged depolarization (ivermectin or glycine) reduced BrdU+ ependymoglial cells at 3 dpi, prevented lesion closure, and blocked axon regeneration across the lesion; the glycine receptor was expressed exclusively by GFAP+ ependymoglial cells. Kir2.1 (hyperpolarizing) and Exp-1 (depolarizing) overexpression also blocked axon regrowth, and Kir2.1-GFP+ cells failed to migrate — indicating a cell-autonomous requirement for the dynamic Vmem change.
- Microarray identified a set of genes upregulated at 1 dpi that rapidly return to baseline; c-Fos was a key voltage-sensitive early-response gene, expressed specifically in ependymoglial nuclei at 1 dpi and blocked by ivermectin.
- ERK/JNK inhibition phenocopied Vmem perturbation (blocked proliferation and axon regeneration); injury-induced phospho-ERK was blocked by ivermectin, placing membrane depolarization upstream of ERK → c-Fos activation.

---

## Perspective

This work provides functional evidence that a transient, tightly timed depolarization–repolarization of ependymoglial cell membrane potential in the first 24 h after SCI is an essential, cell-autonomous trigger for the pro-regenerative program in axolotl, acting upstream of an ERK/c-Fos early-response cascade that drives proliferation and axon regeneration. It extends bioelectric signaling — previously studied in tail/appendage regeneration — to CNS regeneration, and notably shows the ERK/c-Fos voltage-sensitive network operating in non-excitable glial cells.

Limitations include reliance on pharmacological/overexpression tools (e.g., Exp-1 not directly tagged, inferred via co-electroporation), the correlative nature of some array data, and open questions about how Vmem is sensed and transduced. The authors note that Vmem effects on proliferation are cell-type- and context-specific. Future directions include identifying the voltage-sensing machinery, additional c-Fos partners in ependymoglia, and parallel ionic/mechanical signals shaping the regenerative response.
