# Ectoderm to Mesoderm Lineage Switching During Axolotl Tail Regeneration

## Citation (NLM)
Echeverri K, Tanaka EM. Ectoderm to Mesoderm Lineage Switching During Axolotl Tail Regeneration. Science. 2002;298(5600):1993-1995. doi:10.1126/science.1077804

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1077804](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1077804)

---

## Background

Lineage restriction into ectodermal, mesodermal, and endodermal germ layers established during development has traditionally been considered irreversible. However, evidence from brain, skin, and bone marrow cells suggested that adult cells may form cell types of other lineages when exposed to foreign environments. Whether such observations represent true lineage switching, and whether the phenomenon is relevant to natural tissue repair, remained controversial—in part because most prior assays placed cultured cell populations into unnatural environments and scored outcomes only after many cell divisions.

Urodele amphibians such as the axolotl can perfectly regenerate multitissue structures including the limb and tail. This makes them a natural system to test whether neural cell plasticity contributes to functional tissue regeneration. The authors developed a method to follow individual spinal cord neural precursor cells live during axolotl tail regeneration to determine whether lineage switching is an integral feature of regeneration in a genuine physiological context.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Single-cell electroporation to express GFP in radial glial (neural precursor) cells lining the spinal cord lumen, targeted by inserting a DNA-filled microelectrode into the central canal through the cut end of the amputated tail.
2. GFP expression driven by the GFAP (glial acidic fibrillary protein) promoter via a GAL4-VP16 enhancer system, ensuring persistent labeling even if cells downregulate GFAP after transdifferentiation.
3. Live light-microscopy tracking of individual labeled cells with single-cell resolution in ~2-cm animals during tail regeneration.
4. Cryosectioning of transfected tails to confirm labeling specificity (labeled cells always in the layer lining the neural canal).
5. Whole-mount immunohistochemistry with muscle-specific myosin heavy chain to confirm muscle transdifferentiation.
6. Quantification of cell-fate frequencies across 25 followed animals.

---

## Results

- Labeled GFAP-positive radial glial cells behaved as bona fide spinal cord precursors, extending fine radial processes toward the pial edge and lining the lumen.
- While many descendants formed expected neural cell types, a high frequency of labeled spinal cord cells migrated out of the regenerating spinal cord and contributed to mesodermal structures.
- Muscle was the most frequent switched fate: cells exited the spinal cord, adopted an elongated shape resembling nascent muscle fibers, and stained positive for myosin heavy chain (observed in 24% of animals; ~20% of starting labeled cells).
- Cartilage formation was also observed (12% of animals; ~8% of starting cells). Because cartilage cells are mononucleate, this fate could not be explained by cell fusion with predetermined cells.
- Cell-fate frequencies (per animal): muscle 24%, cartilage 12%, neurons 16%, neural crest (side exit) 28%, neural crest (end exit) 24%, melanocytes 8%, glial cells 28%.
- Neural crest cells exited both through the side walls (as expected from prior histology) and out of the end of the growing spinal cord tube.
- In some cases all visible progeny of a labeled cell exited the spinal cord, leaving no self-renewing cell behind, suggesting single neural progenitors may generate a limited number of cell types and may not always self-renew.

---

## Perspective

This work provides direct, live evidence that ectoderm-derived GFAP-positive radial glial cells switch lineage during a genuine regeneration event, contributing to mesodermal muscle and cartilage in addition to re-forming the spinal cord. It moves the plasticity debate beyond artificial culture assays by demonstrating lineage switching in a natural regenerative context with single-cell resolution.

A key limitation is that it remains unknown whether cells are predetermined before exiting the spinal cord or whether the extracellular environment (the blastema) instructs the fate switch. The authors propose that mammalian and axolotl neural stem cells may be intrinsically similar, but that mammals lack the ability to form an inductive blastema environment that induces lineage switching. This raises the central future question of whether regenerative failure in mammals reflects intrinsic differences in neural cell plasticity or differences in the injury environment—directly relevant to CNS regenerative medicine.
