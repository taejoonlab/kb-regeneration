# Skeletal stem cells

## Citation (NLM)
Bianco P, Robey PG. Skeletal stem cells. Development. 2015;142(6):1023-1027. doi:10.1242/dev.102210

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.102210](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.102210)

---

## Background
Bone marrow stroma contains self-renewing, multipotent progenitors for skeletal lineages (cartilage, bone, marrow adipocytes, fibroblasts), termed skeletal stem cells (SSCs). These cells have been conflated with the broader and misapplied concept of "mesenchymal stem cells" (MSCs). This review clarifies the biology of SSCs in the context of skeletal development and postnatal physiology, arguing that the term MSC is a misnomer that should be abandoned in favor of the more precise SSC concept.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Heterotopic transplantation of bone marrow specimens and cell suspensions to form ectopic ossicles as the gold-standard assay for SSC function
2. Single-cell clonal analysis (CFU-F) to assess multipotency at the individual cell level
3. Lineage tracing studies to track periosteal osteoprogenitor migration into developing bone marrow
4. Surface marker-based prospective isolation and anatomical characterization of perivascular adventitial reticular cells (ARCs)
5. Serial passaging and transplantation to demonstrate self-renewal capacity

## Results
1. SSCs reside as perivascular cells (adventitial reticular cells, ARCs) at the outer side of bone marrow sinusoid endothelial lining, with distinct phenotype and reticular morphology
2. SSCs give rise to skeletal tissues only (cartilage, bone, stroma, marrow adipocytes) and can be assayed in bone marrow only; evidence does not support multipotency beyond skeletal lineages in postnatal organisms
3. Skeletal lineages emerge in a specific developmental sequence: cartilage forms first, followed by bone and stroma, with adipocytes appearing last during postnatal growth and aging
4. The term "MSC" is a misnomer: cells from extra-skeletal tissues (muscle, placenta, adipose) do not display skeletogenic properties assayable in vivo and are not generated by skeletal progenitors
5. SSCs function simultaneously as stem cells and niche cells, organizing neighboring tissues including nascent blood vessels and the hematopoietic microenvironment (HME)
6. Developmental commitment to chondrogenesis, osteogenesis, and adipogenesis is regulated by master transcription factors Sox9, Runx2, and PPARγ2, respectively, with extensive crosstalk via TGFβ/BMP, Wnt, and hedgehog signaling
7. Current protocols for generating SSCs from pluripotent stem cells (ESCs/iPSCs) fail to recreate bone marrow cavity and stroma, falling short of recapitulating late events of skeletogenesis
8. Self-renewal can only be proven by demonstrating generation of an anatomically, phenotypically, and functionally equivalent cell compartment in vivo, along with serial transplantation

## Perspective
This review provides a rigorous conceptual framework for understanding SSCs, distinguishing them from the broadly misapplied MSC concept. The dual function of SSCs as both progenitors and niche organizers positions them at the intersection of skeletal and hematopoietic physiology. The authors caution against premature clinical applications (e.g., systemic infusion of uncharacterized cells) and advocate for anchoring SSC-based therapies to their specific developmental and physiological properties. Future work should focus on characterizing SSC subpopulations, understanding their regulatory circuitries, and developing stem cell-based in vivo disease models. Advances in mouse SSC identification (Chan et al., 2015; Worthley et al., 2015) reveal novel aspects of stromal system ontogeny that, combined with developmental insights, may contribute to broader understanding of mammalian stromal biology.
