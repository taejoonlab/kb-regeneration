# RNA Profiling of the Human and Mouse Spinal Cord Stem Cell Niches Reveals an Embryonic-like Regionalization with MSX1+ Roof-Plate-Derived Cells

## Citation (NLM)
Ghazale H, Ripoll C, Leventoux N, Jacob L, Azar S, Mamaeva D, Glasson Y, Calvo CF, Thomas JL, Meneceur S, Lallemand Y, Rigau V, Perrin FE, Noristani HN, Rocamonde B, Huillard E, Bauchet L, Hugnot JP. RNA Profiling of the Human and Mouse Spinal Cord Stem Cell Niches Reveals an Embryonic-like Regionalization with MSX1+ Roof-Plate-Derived Cells. Stem Cell Reports. 2019;12(5):1159-1177. doi:10.1016/j.stemcr.2019.04.001

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.stemcr.2019.04.001](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.stemcr.2019.04.001)

---

## Background

The adult central nervous system maintains neural stem cells in specific niches, principally the subventricular zone (SVZ) and hippocampus. A third stem cell niche is found in the adult spinal cord around the central canal in anamniotes, rodents, and humans — the ependymal zone (EZ), a pseudo-epithelium originating from the embryonic neuroepithelium. The EZ represents a possible endogenous source for repair in mammalian spinal cord lesions, as it can activate and produce new cells upon injury and contribute to glial scar formation.

Compared with brain niches, cell diversity and region-specific genes of the spinal cord EZ are poorly defined, and a detailed transcriptomic profiling of the human and mouse EZ was lacking. Such a resource would clarify the specificity/diversity of these cells, identify conserved gene expressions and pathways between primates and rodents, and offer insight into why (unlike anamniotes) mammalian ependymal cells cannot regenerate neurons after spinal cord injury. This study provides a cellular and molecular resource for the mouse and human EZ.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Microdissection of the EZ and adjacent peri-EZ tissue from two human samples (aged 17 and 46 years) and four mice, followed by microarray-based RNA profiling.
2. Regional microdissection of dorsal, lateral, and ventral parts of the mouse EZ (n=4) to identify regionally expressed genes.
3. Comparison of enriched genes between species (volcano plots, Venn diagrams) and cross-referencing with published single-cell RNA-seq databases (Rosenberg 2018, Zeisel 2018) and the Allen brain atlas.
4. Immunofluorescence validation of transcription factor protein expression (ARX, FOXA2, FOXJ1, ID4, MEIS2, MSX1, NFIA, PAX6, PBX1, SOX4, SOX9, SOX11) in human and mouse EZ.
5. Vegfr3-YFP transgenic mice to detect roof/floor EZ subpopulations; co-staining with FOXJ1, SOX2, PAX6, MSX1, c-RET, ARX.
6. Genetic lineage tracing with Msx1-CreERT2/Rosa-Tomato mice (tamoxifen at E11.5 and in adults) to trace developmental origin of MSX1+ cells.
7. Immunofluorescence at multiple developmental stages (E13, E18, P1, P6, P22, P38) to track MSX1+ and ARX+ cells.

---

## Results

- 1,223 genes were commonly enriched in both mouse and human EZ (out of 8,733 mouse and 2,122 human enriched genes), including ~120 transcription factors. GO/KEGG analyses showed enrichment for cilia formation, smoothened and hippo pathways, cell division, and transcription.
- Ependymal cells expressed many solute carrier family members; unexpectedly, CFTR (chloride transporter) was specifically expressed in the EZ of both species. Tmem212 was a conserved ependymal marker; Espn and Pkd2l1 marked CSF-contacting neurons (CSF-N) across studies.
- The EZ maintains an embryonic-like dorsal-ventral pattern of spinal cord developmental transcription factors. Ventral EZ expressed SHH-pathway genes (Arx, Foxa1, Sulf1) and Decorin; dorsal EZ expressed BMP morphogens (Bmp6, Gdf10/Bmp3b) and Prokr2, Thbs2.
- Conserved dorsal-ventral regionalization of TF protein expression: PAX6 in dorsal/lateral cells, MSX1 strongly in dorsal (roof) cells, ARX confined to ventral (floor) cells in both human and mouse. FOXA2 detected in ventral cells.
- Vegfr3-YFP+ cells were enriched at the roof and floor of the EZ. ~67% of roof YFP+ cells expressed MSX1 (and ~77% were Msx1-Tomato+), ~71% expressed c-RET; ~90% of floor YFP+ cells expressed ARX.
- Lineage tracing (Msx1-Tomato) and staged immunofluorescence demonstrated an early embryonic origin: MSX1+ roof-plate-derived and ARX+ floor-plate-derived cells arise at ~E13 at the dorsalmost/ventralmost spinal cord, migrate centrally by E18, and persist as a few cells in the adult EZ roof/floor.
- Roof MSX1+ cells are radial quiescent cells, consistent with the previously reported enrichment of neurosphere-forming, GFAP+ radial glial-like cells in the dorsal EZ.
- No convincing evidence for CSF-N (Espn/Pkd2l1/DCN markers) was found in the human EZ, unlike in mouse.

---

## Perspective

This work delivers a detailed transcriptomic and protein-level resource of the human and mouse spinal cord ependymal stem cell niche, revealing that the adult EZ unexpectedly retains an embryonic-like dorsal-ventral patterning of developmental transcription factors and harbors distinct roof (MSX1+, roof-plate-derived, radial quiescent) and floor (ARX+, floor-plate-derived) cell populations conserved between rodents and humans.

Significance: it defines conserved markers and subpopulations of a candidate endogenous repair niche, directly relevant to spinal cord injury regeneration strategies. The persistence of embryonic patterning suggests the EZ retains developmental positional identity into adulthood. Limitations include the small number of human samples (two, with reduced histological quality in the older donor) and species differences (e.g., apparent absence of CSF-N in human EZ). Understanding why mammalian ependymal cells fail to regenerate neurons after injury — in contrast to anamniotes — remains a key motivating question and future direction.
