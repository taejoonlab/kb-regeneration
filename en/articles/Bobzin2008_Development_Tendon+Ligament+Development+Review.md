# Development and maintenance of tendons and ligaments

## Citation (NLM)
Bobzin L, Roberts RR, Chen HJ, Crump JG, Merrill AE. Development and maintenance of tendons and ligaments. Development. 2021;148(15):dev186916. doi:10.1242/dev.186916

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.186916](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.186916)

---

## Background
Tendons and ligaments are dense fibrous connective tissues vital for musculoskeletal mobility and stability. Despite their similar composition of collagen I fibrils, proteoglycans, and elastin, they exhibit distinct structural organization and function. Recent studies have revealed heterogeneity and plasticity within tendon and ligament cells, raising questions about the developmental mechanisms that establish and maintain their identity. This review examines region-specific developmental programs, cell fate maintenance mechanisms, and the cellular lineages contributing to repair.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Genetic lineage tracing using Scx-GFP reporter and Scx-Cre alleles in mice to track tendon/ligament progenitor patterning and differentiation
2. Analysis of Scx knockout mice and zebrafish scxa/scxb mutants to assess tendon morphogenesis requirements
3. Conditional deletion of Tgfbr2 using Scx-Cre to investigate growth factor roles in tendon cell fate maintenance
4. Mouse genetic models of attachment progenitor (AP) regulation (Gli3, Pbx1/2, Hoxa11/11, Klf2/4 mutants)
5. Lineage tracing of tendon stem/progenitor cells (TSPCs) following injury using αSMA, S100a4, Tppp3, and Bglap markers
6. Comparative transcriptomic and chromatin accessibility analysis of limb attachment cells

## Results
1. Scleraxis (Scx) is the earliest known marker of tendon/ligament progenitors; it is required for long-range tendon extension but not for initial tendon anlage formation
2. TGFβ signaling from neighboring cartilage/muscle recruits mesenchymal cells to form tendon progenitors by activating Scx expression; loss of Tgfβ2/3 causes widespread tendon loss
3. Region-specific regulation: axial tendon progenitors depend on Fgf signaling from myotome, while cranial and distal limb (autopod) tendon induction is muscle-independent and cartilage-dependent
4. Tendon cell fate is reversible and requires continuous extrinsic signals for maintenance; postnatal Tgfbr2 deletion causes dedifferentiation and reactivation of progenitor genes
5. ECM components (Adamts7/12, Bgn, Fmod) maintain tendon cell fate by suppressing BMP signaling; their combined loss leads to heterotopic ossification (HO)
6. Mechanical signals activate Egr1/2 and Mkx to maintain tenogenic gene expression; Mkx knockout causes HO via aberrant chondrogenic differentiation of Scx-lineage cells
7. Attachment progenitors (APs) at the tendon-bone interface co-express Scx and Sox9, possessing hybrid tenochondral potential; Bmp4 promotes chondrogenic differentiation while Fgf signaling promotes tenogenic fate
8. Hedgehog signaling (Shh/Ihh) induces Gli1 expression in attachment cells, which give rise to mineralized fibrocartilage in the mature enthesis
9. Tendon repair is age-dependent: neonatal mice heal regeneratively via Scx-lineage tenocytes, while adults heal fibrotically via αSMA+ cells from the tendon proper and sheath
10. Enthesis healing in juveniles involves Gli1-lineage cells in a regenerative process, but adult enthesis healing shows limited intrinsic cell involvement

## Perspective
This review synthesizes current understanding of tendon and ligament development, highlighting the critical roles of TGFβ signaling, mechanical feedback, and ECM components in maintaining cell fate. The discovery that tendon cell identity is reversible and requires continuous maintenance signals has important implications for understanding tendon pathologies and heterotopic ossification. The age-dependent shift from regenerative to fibrotic healing presents a key therapeutic target. Future research should address how regional and universal developmental programs are integrated, identify transcription factors that actively maintain tendon fate while inhibiting osteochondral differentiation, and determine the extent to which findings from one anatomical region apply to others. Single-cell sequencing technologies will be essential for resolving tendon cell heterogeneity and identifying novel regulators for tissue engineering approaches.
