# Astrocyte scar formation aids central nervous system axon regeneration

## Citation (NLM)
Anderson MA, Burda JE, Ren Y, Ao Y, O'Shea TM, Kawaguchi R, Coppola G, Khakh BS, Deming TJ, Sofroniew MV. Astrocyte scar formation aids central nervous system axon regeneration. Nature. 2016;532(7598):195-200. doi:10.1038/nature17623

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nature17623](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature17623)

---

## Background
Transected axons fail to regrow spontaneously across severe tissue lesions in the mature mammalian central nervous system (CNS). Proposed mechanisms include reduced intrinsic growth capacity of mature CNS neurons, absence of external growth-stimulating and supporting factors, and the presence of external inhibitory factors associated with myelin, fibrotic tissue, or astrocytic scars. Since the mid-twentieth century, astrocytic scars have been widely regarded as barriers to CNS axon regrowth, largely because astrocytes produce chondroitin sulfate proteoglycans (CSPGs) that inhibit axon growth in vitro. This led to the prevailing dogma that astrocytic scars are critical inhibitors of CNS axons and that nullifying this inhibition would enable spontaneous axon regeneration.

Here the authors tested the hypothesis that astrocytic scar formation plays a causal role in the failure of transected mature CNS axons to regenerate across severe tissue lesions. Using multiple transgenic loss-of-function strategies in adult mice, they assessed whether ablating scar-forming astrocytes, genetically attenuating them, or removing chronic astrocytic scars after severe spinal cord injury (SCI) would enable spontaneous axon regeneration.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. **Preventing astrocytic scar formation** using two transgenic loss-of-function mouse models after severe crush SCI: TK+GCV mice (selective killing of proliferating scar-forming astrocytes) and STAT3-CKO mice (astrocyte-selective deletion of STAT3 signalling).
2. **Ablating chronic astrocytic scars** five weeks after SCI using genetically targeted diphtheria toxin receptor plus ultra-low doses of diphtheria toxin; targeting specificity verified with a tdTomato genetic reporter and GFAP immunohistochemistry.
3. **Axon regeneration quantification** in three axonal systems — descending corticospinal tract (CST), ascending sensory tract (AST), and descending serotonergic (5HT) tract — by axonal tract tracing (BDA, CTB) and immunohistochemistry.
4. **Total CSPG assessment** by dot blot with CS56 antibody (relative to Ponceau total protein) and immunohistochemistry of CSPG and GFAP tissue areas.
5. **Genome-wide RNA sequencing** of astrocyte-specific ribosome-associated RNA (ramRNA; haemagglutinin-tagged, RiboTag approach) versus non-astrocyte (flow-through) RNA from wild-type and STAT3-CKO SCI lesions; analysis of 59 axon-growth-modulating molecules.
6. **Stimulated axon regrowth experiments** combining neuron-intrinsic growth activation via peripheral conditioning lesions with sustained local delivery of NT3 and BDNF via synthetic hydrogel depots; laminin dependence tested with anti-CD29 (laminin-integrin) function-blocking antibodies.

---

## Results
- Preventing astrocytic scar formation (TK+GCV or STAT3-CKO) did not produce spontaneous regrowth of transected CST, AST, or 5HT axons through SCI lesions; instead it significantly increased axonal dieback for CST and AST axons. Many 5HT axons remained within astrocyte-devoid lesion centres but still failed to regrow.
- Ablating chronic astrocytic scars five weeks after SCI also failed to enable spontaneous regrowth of CST, AST, or 5HT axons. Ablating scars and adjacent astrocytes over larger areas caused pronounced tissue degeneration, showing chronic scars remain critical for sustaining tissue integrity.
- Total CSPG levels were significantly elevated in wild-type SCI lesions but were not significantly reduced by ablating or disrupting astrocytic scar formation. Regions of ablated scar were filled with CSPG-positive, GFAP-negative (non-astrocyte) cells, showing non-astrocyte cells produce substantial CSPGs.
- RNA sequencing showed STAT3-CKO prevented or attenuated a majority of genome-wide astrocyte changes (63% of genes regulated by wild-type astrocytes were unaffected in STAT3-CKO; STAT3-CKO SCI astrocytes clustered nearer to uninjured astrocytes). Astrocytes and non-astrocyte cells expressed a large, diverse mix of both axon-inhibitory (28 analysed) and axon-permissive (31 analysed) molecules; more permissive molecules were upregulated. STAT3-CKO did not reduce inhibitor expression.
- Aggrecan (the prototypical inhibitory CSPG used in vitro) was not detectably expressed by scar-forming astrocytes. Scar-forming astrocytes upregulated the growth-supportive CSPGs Cspg4 (NG2) and Cspg5 (neuroglycan C), and upregulated axon-growth-permissive matrix molecules including laminins.
- Combining conditioning lesions with sustained hydrogel delivery of NT3 and BDNF stimulated robust, laminin-dependent AST axon regrowth through and past dense astrocytic scars, reaching over 45% of intact sensory tract axon numbers at lesion centres despite abundant CSPG. Anti-CD29 (laminin-blocking) antibody reduced this regrowth by 73%.
- Preventing astrocytic scar formation did not augment stimulated regrowth; it completely prevented (TK+GCV) or significantly attenuated (STAT3-CKO) it, showing scar formation aids rather than inhibits appropriately stimulated axon regeneration.

---

## Perspective
Contrary to the prevailing dogma, astrocytic scar formation is not a principal cause of axon regeneration failure after severe CNS injury; scar-forming astrocytes instead permit and support robust axon regeneration when growth is appropriately stimulated. The findings reframe CSPG biology, showing that specific CSPGs can support rather than only inhibit growth and that CS56 detection of total CSPGs does not indicate a purely inhibitory environment. They also distinguish long-distance regeneration across severe lesions — which requires both intrinsic growth activation and supplementation of missing axon-specific growth factors along supportive matrix (e.g., laminin) — from local sprouting in reactive perilesional grey matter, where blocking inhibitors alone may suffice.

A key limitation is that the stimulated regeneration evidence is based on the AST (sensory) system and needs extension to other axonal systems such as CST and 5HT. Nevertheless, the work suggests that newly generated immature scar-forming astrocytes, whether derived from endogenous or grafted progenitors, may represent exploitable bridges for regrowing axons across severe CNS lesions, with important implications for CNS repair strategies after trauma, stroke, or degenerative disease.
