# Requirement of Sox2-mediated signaling for differentiation of early Xenopus neuroectoderm

## Citation (NLM)
Kishi M, Mizuseki K, Sasai N, Yamazaki H, Shiota K, Nakanishi S, Sasai Y. Requirement of Sox2-mediated signaling for differentiation of early Xenopus neuroectoderm. Development. 2000;127(4):791-800.

---

## Background
Neural induction in *Xenopus* is driven by organizer-secreted BMP antagonists (Noggin, Chordin, Follistatin) that inactivate BMP4, permitting neural rather than epidermal fate. Several transcription factors promote neural differentiation, including Zic-related factors, SoxD, and proneural bHLH genes (Xngnr1). Sox2-class transcription factors (Sox1, Sox2, Sox3; SRY-related HMG-box, group B) are expressed in early neuroectoderm of many vertebrates, but their role in early neural differentiation was largely unknown. Prior work showed Sox2 combined with bFGF promotes posterior neural differentiation in animal caps (Sox2 alone is insufficient), suggesting Sox2 changes ectodermal competence to FGF.

Because at least three closely related Sox2-class members are co-expressed, the authors used a dominant-negative approach (rather than single-gene knockout) to test whether Sox2-mediated signaling is required for neural differentiation of early *Xenopus* ectoderm.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. **Two dominant-negative Sox2 constructs:** Sox2BD(−) (lacking most of the HMG DNA-binding domain, acts by competing for cofactors) and Sox2-EnR (Sox2 HMG domain fused to the Drosophila engrailed repressor domain).
2. **Animal cap assays:** mRNA/DNA microinjection into animal blastomeres of 8-cell embryos; neural differentiation assessed by pan-neural marker N-CAM (in situ hybridization) at stage 19. Neuralization triggered by Sox2+FGF4 or by dominant-negative BMP receptor (dnBMPR).
3. **Glucocorticoid receptor (GR) fusion strategy:** Sox2-GR, Sox3-GR, Sox9-GR, and Sox2BD(−)-GR — inducible/rescue and temporal-requirement experiments using dexamethasone (Dex) to control activity timing.
4. **Whole-mount in situ hybridization in vivo** for pan-neural (N-CAM, N-tubulin, Xngnr1), regional neural (Otx2, Xanf1, En2, Krox20, HoxB9), neural crest (Slug, fkh6), mesodermal (Chordin, MyoD), and early neural (Zic2, Sox2 5′UTR) markers.
5. **Western blot** (FLAG-tagged Sox2BD(−)) to confirm protein accumulation; **RT-PCR** for N-CAM/Keratin/XAG-1 fate analysis; **confocal microscopy** (GFP tracer) for neuroectoderm morphology.

---

## Results
- **dnSox2 constructs specifically inhibit Sox2-class activity:** Both Sox2BD(−) and Sox2-EnR suppressed N-CAM induction by Sox2+FGF4. Suppression was rescued by Sox2-GR (or Sox3-GR) plus Dex but not by Sox9-GR, confirming specificity to Sox2-class members. dnSox2 did not suppress SoxD-driven neuralization, indicating Sox2 does not act downstream of SoxD.
- **Sox2 signaling is required for neural differentiation of animal caps:** Coinjection of dnSox2 blocked N-CAM induction by dnBMPR; rescued by Sox2-GR+Dex.
- **Sox2 signaling is essential in vivo:** Sox2BD(−) suppressed N-CAM, the neuronal markers N-tubulin and Xngnr1, and neural crest markers Slug and fkh6, while dorsal mesodermal markers (Chordin, MyoD) were intact — indicating an ectoderm-autonomous effect on both CNS and neural crest formation.
- **Both anterior and posterior neural markers require Sox2:** Unlike SoxDBD(−) (which suppresses only anterior Otx2), Sox2BD(−) reduced Otx2, Xanf1, En2, Krox20, and HoxB9 (spinal cord/posterior) — but HoxB9 suppression was neural-specific, not in mesoderm.
- **Two temporal phases:** At early gastrula (stage 11), early neural markers Zic2 and Sox2 were unaffected despite abundant dnSox2 protein; at late gastrula/early neurula (stages 12, 14) both markers were reduced — so Sox2 is required to maintain/consolidate, not initiate, neural fate.
- **Temporal requirement:** Sox2BD(−)-GR activation from late blastula (st 9), late gastrula (st 12), and even stage 14 onward inhibited neural differentiation, showing Sox2 is needed beyond late gastrula stages.
- **Cells with reduced Sox2 adopt neither fate:** dnSox2 blocked N-CAM without inducing epidermal Keratin or cement-gland XAG-1; such cells showed abnormal morphology and later peeled off (with only minimal TUNEL/apoptosis), indicating altered adhesion rather than cell death.

---

## Perspective
This study provides the first solid genetic-type (dominant-negative) evidence that Sox2-class signaling is essential for early *Xenopus* neuroectoderm to consolidate neural identity during a secondary step of neural differentiation, after the initial BMP-blockade-dependent neural/epidermal decision. Sox2 plays a permissive (competence-changing) rather than instructive role — Sox2 alone does not neuralize, but its loss prevents both neural and epidermal fate, leaving cells in an undifferentiated, adhesion-altered state. The distinct requirements of Sox2 (general/anterior+posterior) versus SoxD (anterior only) reveal division of labor among Sox factors. Limitations/future directions noted: functional redundancy among Sox2-subfamily members means the specific responsible member(s) remain undefined; the requirement for Sox2 in the initial induction step (~stage 10) could not be cleanly tested due to difficulty removing Dex; and dissecting which downstream neural-induction signaling steps require Sox2 (e.g., via promoter analysis in transgenic frogs) is proposed.
