# Rewired glycosylation activity promotes scarless regeneration and functional recovery in spiny mice after complete spinal cord transection

## Citation (NLM)
Nogueira-Rodrigues J, Leite SC, Pinto-Costa R, Sousa SC, Luz LL, Sintra MA, Oliveira R, Monteiro AC, Pinheiro GG, Vitorino M, Silva JA, Simao S, Fernandes VE, Provaznik J, Benes V, Cruz CD, Safronov BV, Magalhaes A, Reis CA, Vieira J, Vieira CP, Tiscornia G, Araujo IM, Sousa MM. Rewired glycosylation activity promotes scarless regeneration and functional recovery in spiny mice after complete spinal cord transection. Dev Cell. 2022 Feb 28;57(4):440-450. doi:10.1016/j.devcel.2021.12.008

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.devcel.2021.12.008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.devcel.2021.12.008)

## Background

Adult mammalian CNS axons largely fail to regenerate after injury, and spinal cord injury (SCI) remains without effective treatment. A major cause is scarring at the lesion site: fibroblasts and reactive astrocytes assemble a physical and chemical barrier, secreting repulsive guidance molecules and inhibitory extracellular matrix (ECM). Robust spinal cord regeneration occurs in non-mammalian vertebrates (fish, amphibians, reptiles) but is essentially absent in adult mammals.

The spiny mouse (Acomys cahirinus), a murid closely related to Mus, is a striking exception that regenerates skin and ear scarlessly and shows reduced fibrosis and altered inflammation after wounding. Building on a report that Acomys tolerates a minor spinal crush with reduced inflammation/fibrosis, the authors test whether the Acomys CNS can regenerate after complete spinal cord transection and dissect the molecular basis of its pro-regenerative injury environment.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Complete spinal cord transection at T8 in Acomys cahirinus vs. non-regenerative Mus musculus; 8 weeks post-injury (WPI) follow-up, plus re-injury at 8 WPI to confirm regeneration-dependent recovery.
2. Functional assessment: Basso Mouse Scale (BMS) locomotor scoring and bladder/micturition (voided-urine spot) analysis.
3. Histology/immunofluorescence of the lesion: beta-III-tubulin (axons), MBP (myelin), SCG10 (sensory axons), 5-HT (raphespinal axons), plus astrocyte/fibrosis markers (GFAP, Ngal/Lcn2, A1/A2 markers, collagen, PDGFRb).
4. Corticospinal tract (CST) tracing by intracortical AAV1/2-eGFP injection; synapse assessment with vGlut1; compound action potential (CAP) recordings across the lesion (electrophysiology).
5. RNA-seq of the injury site after de novo reconstruction of the Acomys cahirinus transcriptome; PANTHER functional analysis; qPCR and immunofluorescence validation (b3gnt7, Ndst3, Ndst4, b3galnt1, Chst15).
6. Proteoglycan staining for KSPG (5D4) and HSPG (10E4) with keratanase/heparinase controls.
7. Neurite outgrowth assay: Mus DRG and cortical neurons grown on CHO cells overexpressing b3gnt7 (Acomys-like) vs. GFP control.

---

## Results

- Acomys spontaneously recovered motor function after complete transection (BMS ~2 by 2 WPI, ~4 by 8 WPI; 8/14 regained full weight support and coordinated stepping), while Mus stayed at BMS 0; re-injury dropped BMS back to 0, confirming axon-regeneration-dependent recovery.
- Acomys regained normal bladder control by 3 WPI, whereas Mus required manual voiding throughout.
- Unlike the disorganized Mus scar, Acomys formed a bridging tissue preserving spinal cord geometry, densely filled with beta-III-tubulin+ axons, some already myelinated; SCG10+ sensory, 5-HT+ raphespinal, and AAV-traced CST axons regenerated/sprouted across the lesion.
- Synapse formation (vGlut1+/eGFP+ boutons caudal to injury) and CAP conduction across the lesion were detected in Acomys (5/10) but not Mus.
- Both genera showed astrogliosis, but Acomys had reduced pan-reactive astrocyte markers, reduced fibrosis, and lower collagen deposition restricted to the injury site; no significant A1/A2 or pericyte differences.
- RNA-seq revealed two major functional categories differing after injury: inflammation-related pathways and acetylglucosaminyltransferase activity. Acomys upregulated b3gnt7 (KSPG synthesis) and downregulated Ndst3/Ndst4 (HSPG modification) and b3galnt1; Chst15 (C6S sulfotransferase, permissive) was increased.
- Acomys showed >30-fold increased KSPG deposition (reverted by keratanase) and decreased HSPG deposition at the lesion.
- b3gnt7-overexpressing CHO cells robustly increased total neurite length of Mus DRG neurons and axon length of cortical neurons, identifying b3gnt7 as an axon growth enhancer.

---

## Perspective

The study overturns the dogma that adult mammals cannot regenerate the spinal cord, establishing Acomys as a unique mammalian model of spontaneous functional SCI regeneration. Mechanistically, a rewired glycosylation gene-regulatory network produces a specific pro-regenerative proteoglycan signature; notably, KSPG—previously reported as inhibitory—is increased in the permissive Acomys environment, and its biosynthetic enzyme b3gnt7 enhances axon growth in vitro. This positions b3gnt7 and ECM glycosylation as therapeutic targets for human SCI and underscores the value of non-traditional model organisms. The authors list clear limitations: only anterograde (not retrograde) tracing was performed; CSPG deposition (4-O vs 6-O sulfation) was not analyzed; and in vivo gain-of-function of b3gnt7 in Mus, loss-of-function/knockdown in Acomys, and keratanase effects on Acomys regeneration remain to be tested to prove sufficiency and necessity.
