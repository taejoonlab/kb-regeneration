# Development of a straight vertebrate body axis

## Citation (NLM)
Bagnat M, Gray RS. Development of a straight vertebrate body axis. Development. 2020;147(21):dev175794. doi:10.1242/dev.175794

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.175794](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.175794)

---

## Background
The vertebral column is the defining feature of vertebrates, providing axial stability and protecting the spinal cord. Scoliosis, an abnormal lateral curvature of the spine, is a common disorder affecting adolescents. While congenital scoliosis is linked to somite segmentation defects, the molecular genetics and pathogenesis of idiopathic scoliosis (IS) remain poorly understood. This review examines how notochord development, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) physiology, and the Reissner fiber contribute to axial straightening and spine morphogenesis.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Genetic analysis of zebrafish mutants affecting notochord sheath components (col8a1, fibronectin, pkd1/2) and vacuolated cell formation (dstyk, rab32a)
2. Longitudinal live imaging of notochord vacuole dynamics during vertebral bone growth in zebrafish
3. Analysis of motile cilia mutants and their effects on CSF flow and spine curvature in zebrafish and mouse models
4. Genetic studies of the Reissner fiber using scospondin mutant zebrafish and CSF-contacting neuron (CSF-cN) analysis
5. Examination of Gpr126 conditional knockout mouse model of adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS)
6. Comparative analysis of spine development between amniotes and teleosts

## Results
1. The notochord acts as a hydrostatic scaffold for axis elongation: vacuolated cells inflate via lysosomal and caveolar mechanisms, generating pressure that extends the embryonic axis
2. Notochord sheath cell epithelium provides segmentation cues that override somitic patterning in teleosts; Notch-dependent signals within sheath cells dictate vertebral column segmentation
3. Premature vacuole fragmentation or loss causes notochord kinking and asymmetric vertebral bone growth, leading to spine curvature
4. Loss of ECM sheath components (Col8a1, Fbn2) causes notochord undulation and subsequent vertebral malformations and fusions
5. Disruption of motile cilia in the brain and spinal cord reduces CSF flow and causes scoliosis without vertebral malformations, resembling human IS
6. The Reissner fiber, an extracellular glycoprotein thread extending from brain to spinal cord, is essential for axial straightening; its assembly depends on SCO-spondin secretion
7. CSF-contacting neurons (CSF-cNs) directly interact with the Reissner fiber and respond to mechanical bending; loss of the fiber impairs mechanosensory response
8. Increased proinflammatory signaling in the ependyma is sufficient to induce scoliosis in zebrafish, and NSAID treatment can ameliorate the phenotype
9. In amniotes, the notochord transitions into the nucleus pulposus of intervertebral discs, while in teleosts it remains embedded within vertebral bodies

## Perspective
This review establishes the notochord and CSF physiology as central regulators of spine morphogenesis, providing mechanistic insights into scoliosis pathogenesis. The discovery that CSF flow disruption and neuroinflammation can cause scoliosis without vertebral malformations offers a new paradigm for understanding IS. Zebrafish models linking motile cilia, Reissner fiber, and CSF-cN function to spine curvature provide valuable tools for studying human AIS. Future work should develop more sophisticated animal models carrying human AIS-associated alleles and explore therapeutic interventions targeting CSF dynamics and neuroinflammation. The relationship between seemingly distinct spine disorders (congenital and idiopathic scoliosis) may share common genetic origins, warranting further investigation.
