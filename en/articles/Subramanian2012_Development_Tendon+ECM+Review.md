# Tendon development and musculoskeletal assembly: emerging roles for the extracellular matrix

## Citation (NLM)
Subramanian A, Schilling TF. Tendon development and musculoskeletal assembly: emerging roles for the extracellular matrix. Development. 2015;142(24):4191-4204. doi:10.1242/dev.114777

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.114777](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.114777)

---

## Background
Tendons and ligaments are ECM-rich connective tissues that transmit mechanical forces between muscles and bones. Despite their critical roles in musculoskeletal connectivity, the mechanisms controlling tendon development have received less attention than myogenesis or skeletogenesis. The dynamic composition and assembly of tendon ECM determines tissue strength and function. Understanding how tendon progenitor cells (TPCs) are specified, how they produce and organize ECM, and how mechanical forces regulate these processes is essential for treating tendinopathies and developing tissue engineering strategies.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Genetic analysis of transcription factors regulating TPC specification and tendon ECM production (Scx, Mkx, Egr1/2) using knockout mouse models and in vitro differentiation assays
2. Zebrafish and Drosophila models for studying myotendinous junction (MTJ) formation and muscle attachment mechanisms
3. Analysis of ECM component roles (collagens, thrombospondins, laminins, fibronectin, FACIT collagens, SLRPs) during tendon development and MTJ maturation
4. Investigation of TGFβ signaling pathway in tendon maintenance through Tgfb2/Tgfb3 conditional double mutants and Smad3 knockout analysis
5. Studies of mechanical force effects on tenocyte gene expression and ECM remodeling using in vitro stretching systems and in vivo exercise models
6. Analysis of tendon-bone attachment (enthesis) formation using Scx:Cre-driven Bmp4 conditional knockout and Sox9/Scx co-expression studies
7. Examination of integrin signaling and dystrophin complex roles in muscle-tendon connectivity

## Results
- Scleraxis (Scx) is the earliest TPC marker, induced by Shh/FGF (axial) and TGFβ (limb) signaling; Scx directly regulates Col1a1, Col1a2, and other ECM genes
- Scx knockout causes force-transmitting tendon atrophy and disorganized ECM, but TPCs still form, indicating other factors contribute to initial specification
- Egr1/Egr2 (Stripe orthologs) and Mkx act later to regulate ECM production and repress alternative fates (myogenic via MyoD repression, skeletogenic via Sox9 repression)
- Tendon development occurs in two phases: (1) tendon-independent phase where myoblasts produce pre-tendon ECM (Tsp4, Lama2, Fn) for initial attachment; (2) tendon-dependent phase where TPCs produce mature tendon ECM (Col1a, FACIT collagens)
- Thrombospondin 4 (Tsp4) is essential for muscle attachment; forms pentamers that scaffold ECM assembly and facilitate integrin signaling
- TGFβ signaling through Smad2/3 is required for TPC maintenance; TGFβ is stored in ECM as latent complex and released by mechanical force
- Mechanical force triggers TGFβ release from ECM, creating positive feedback loop: force → TGFβ → Scx/Mkx → ECM production → increased tendon strength
- MMPs and TIMPs remodel tendon ECM in response to mechanical loading; low force upregulates Col1a/Tnmd, excessive force upregulates Runx2/Sox9 (osteochondral shift)
- Enthesis formation is regulated by Scx-driven Bmp4 secretion from tenocytes acting on adjacent Sox9+ skeletogenic mesenchyme; Scx/Sox9 co-expressing cells form secondary fields adjacent to skeletal condensations
- FACIT collagens (Col12a1, Col14a1, Col22a1) and SLRPs (decorin, fibromodulin) are critical for collagen fibril crosslinking and force distribution

## Perspective
This review integrates tendon developmental genetics with matrix biology, revealing that ECM is not merely a structural product but an active signaling platform. The two-phase model of MTJ formation (tendon-independent followed by tendon-dependent) provides a framework for understanding how muscle attachments develop. The mechanosensitive TGFβ feedback loop explains how tendons dynamically adjust strength to loading conditions. The close lineage relationship between tenocytes and chondrocytes/osteocytes at entheses helps explain ectopic tendon ossification in aging and disease. Future therapeutic strategies for tendinopathies should target ECM assembly mechanisms (e.g., TSP4 as a scaffold protein), mechanotransduction pathways, and the balance between tenogenic and osteochondral fates. Understanding how mechanical loading parameters optimize ECM production could improve rehabilitation protocols and tissue engineering approaches.
