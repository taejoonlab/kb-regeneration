# Blastema formation and cell division during cockroach limb regeneration

## Citation (NLM)
Truby PR. Blastema formation and cell division during cockroach limb regeneration. J Embryol Exp Morphol. 1983;75:151-164.

---

## Background

Models of insect pattern formation (e.g., positional information and the polar coordinate model) had been derived largely from observations of regenerated cuticular patterns, making untested assumptions about the behaviour of the underlying epidermal cells, their movement, and their patterns of cell division. The polar coordinate model in particular required epimorphic regeneration in which positional values are laid down proximodistally with cell division at the tip of the regenerating limb.

This study tests those assumptions directly by examining wound healing and cell division after amputation at the trochanter-femur joint of the metathoracic leg of the cockroach *Periplaneta americana*, relating the tissue-level (epidermis) approach to the pattern-formation approach.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Amputation of the left metathoracic leg at the preformed trochanter-femur breakage plane in second-instar *Periplaneta americana* larvae.
2. Colchicine injection (12 h before fixation) to arrest and visualise mitotic figures across regeneration stages.
3. Tritiated ([3H]) thymidine injection (4 h before fixation) with autoradiography to map DNA synthesis.
4. Semithin (1.5 µm) resin histological sections stained with toluidine blue or methylene blue/basic fuchsin.
5. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) of the coxa/trochanter cuticle after the post-amputation moult to assess extent of dedifferentiation via bristle/spine patterns.
6. Whole-mount light microscopy of bristle patterns; mitotic frequencies calculated per 100 µm of epidermis.

---

## Results

- Regeneration proceeds in three stages: wound closure, blastema formation, and blastema growth/differentiation.
- After amputation the wound is sealed by a haemolymph clot/scab; epidermal cells to either side become "activated" (enlarged cells, nuclei, nucleoli) and migrate across the underside of flattened haemocytes, restoring epidermal continuity by ~3 days.
- Unlike simple wound healing, activation and cell division continue to spread outward to include the whole trochanter and distal coxa (spread ~600 µm, ~55 cell lengths in a 2nd instar), forming a blastema by dedifferentiation of surrounding epidermis rather than only local wound-adjacent cells.
- Colchicine data: little division during wound closure; a band of high mitotic activity forms behind the spreading activation front (days 4-5); as the blastema grows, mitoses become evenly distributed then decline, falling first in the tarsus (distal) then tibia/femur.
- [3H]thymidine labelling showed DNA synthesis appearing first adjacent to the wound (day 2), spreading with activation but preceding mitosis, with a steeper band; little synthesis at the distal blastema tip after day 4.
- SEM/bristle analysis confirmed regenerated tissue (reduced bristle size/number) extends over the trochanter and distal coxa, marking how far dedifferentiation spreads.
- Joint formation order (femur-tibia and tibia-tarsus first, coxa-trochanter/trochanter-femur later) plus the distal-first pattern of cell division indicate a distoproximal order of redifferentiation, contrary to the earlier proposed proximodistal order.

---

## Perspective

The study demonstrates that cockroach limb regeneration is not purely epimorphic: blastema formation involves respecification (dedifferentiation) of a substantial region of surrounding epidermis (trochanter + distal coxa), disagreeing with the local-intercalation prediction of the polar coordinate model, and it argues for a distoproximal rather than proximodistal redifferentiation sequence. As a classic invertebrate appendage/blastema regeneration study, it is relevant to the knowledgebase's comparative regeneration scope, illustrating dedifferentiation-driven blastema biology and the coupling of wound healing, cell migration, and proliferation. Limitations include its purely descriptive/histological nature (colchicine and thymidine as proxies for division), reliance on morphological bristle markers to infer dedifferentiation limits, and lack of molecular characterisation. The author notes follow-up work on grafting and supernumerary-leg experiments to test intercalation.
