# Adult Neural Stem Cells and Multiciliated Ependymal Cells Share a Common Lineage Regulated by the Geminin Family Members

## Citation (NLM)
Ortiz-Álvarez G, Daclin M, Shihavuddin A, Lansade P, Fortoul A, Faucourt M, Clavreul S, Lalioti ME, Taraviras S, Hippenmeyer S, Livet J, Meunier A, Genovesio A, Spassky N. Adult Neural Stem Cells and Multiciliated Ependymal Cells Share a Common Lineage Regulated by the Geminin Family Members. Neuron. 2019;102(1):159-172.e7. doi:10.1016/j.neuron.2019.01.051

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2019.01.051](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2019.01.051)

---

## Background

In the adult mammalian brain, a subpopulation of astrocytes (type B1) located in the ventricular-subventricular zone (V-SVZ) of the lateral ventricles retains stem cell properties (self-renewal and multilineage differentiation). These type B1 cells are surrounded by multiciliated ependymal cells whose coordinated cilia beating drives cerebrospinal fluid dynamics. Together, B1 astrocytes and ependymal cells constitute the adult neurogenic niche. Both are derived from radial glial cells (RGCs) between embryonic day 13.5 (E13.5) and E15.5 and share phenotypic markers (Sox2, Sox9, Nestin, CD133), yet in the adult they have distinct morphologies and functions: B1 astrocytes are reactivatable quiescent neuronal progenitors whereas ependymal cells are postmitotic for life.

It was previously unknown how these two cell types are allocated to the niche and how they acquire common characteristics but distinct identities. This study addresses their clonal/lineage relationship and asks whether the Geminin superfamily members (GemC1, Mcidas, and their antagonist Geminin), initially characterized as DNA-replication regulators and more recently as regulators of multiciliogenesis and neural cell fate, control the balance between the two lineages.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **Genetic fate mapping** of ependymal cell spatial origin using Ai14 (Cre-dependent tdTomato) crossed with Emx1-Cre, Gsh2-Cre, or Nkx2.1-Cre lines (dorsal-medial, lateral, ventral ventricular walls); combined with EdU birth-dating and nearest-neighbor distance (NND) analysis.
2. **In utero electroporation (IUE)** of RGCs at E14.5 (H2B-GFP) with immunostaining for FoxJ1/Sox9 and FOP/GFAP to distinguish ependymal cells from astrocytes; EdU chase (drinking water) to test adult neural stem cell (olfactory bulb neurogenesis) output.
3. **MAGIC markers (Nucbow) clonal analysis** — permanent multicolor lineage tracing by IUE at E14.5, with automated color/3D-distance segmentation on V-SVZ whole-mounts (7,668 Nucbow+ cells, 418 clones) to define sister cells and clone composition.
4. **MADM (mosaic analysis with double markers)** coupled with IUE of Cre at E13.5/E14.5 to reconstruct division patterns (symmetric vs. asymmetric) and cell-fate decisions of individual RGCs.
5. **Gain-of-function perturbation** of Geminin family members by co-electroporating GemC1 or Geminin with Cre in MADM embryos; plus GemC1 KO and Geminin conditional-KO primary ependymal cultures.

---

## Results

- Ependymal cells differentiate locally from RGCs all along the ventricular zone (pallium, LGE, MGE) and do not migrate long distances; born-together cells stay in the same area.
- IUE at E14.5 labels both multiciliated ependymal cells (FoxJ1+Sox9+, ~2/3 of cells) and type B1 astrocytes that persist in the adult V-SVZ and generate olfactory bulb neurons (EdU+Nucbow+ interneurons).
- Nucbow clonal analysis: most ependymal cells arise from ≤3 divisions; ~half of clones were 2-cell clones, generated by a single terminal RGC division that was either symmetric (E-E) or asymmetric (E-B1) in a ~1:1 ratio. Mixed clones showed FoxJ1– cells positioned deeper in the SVZ.
- MADM confirmed that ependymal cells and type B1 astrocytes are **sister cells**, produced through symmetric (E-E or B1-B1) and asymmetric (E-B1) divisions of RGCs at mid-gestation. B1-B1 symmetric divisions were less frequent and always combined with E-E or E-B1 divisions. More mixed clones and more astrocytes formed from E13.5 than E14.5 electroporation, indicating B1 astrocytes are produced earlier and ependymal cells later.
- **GemC1 overexpression** induced premature terminal E-E divisions, dramatically increasing pure ependymal clones and ependymal cell number at the expense of B1 cells (no astrocyte pairs formed; pinwheels absent in densely electroporated regions).
- **Geminin overexpression** favored generation of B1 cell-containing clones and significantly increased B1-B1 symmetric divisions at E14.5 (clones containing only B1 cells, never seen in controls), without changing overall division rate.

---

## Perspective

This work establishes that the two glial components of the adult neurogenic niche—multiciliated ependymal cells and B1 neural stem cells—share a common RGC lineage as sister cells, produced sequentially (B1 astrocytes first, then a majority of ependymal cells) via defined symmetric/asymmetric divisions. The ~2:1 ependymal-to-B1 ratio in the niche may result from the balance between antagonistic Geminin family members: GemC1 pushes premature ependymal differentiation while Geminin preserves the B1 stem cell pool. This identifies the Geminin superfamily as key tuners of the initial adult neural stem cell pool and links DNA-replication regulators to niche ontogeny.

Limitations/open questions noted by the authors: GemC1 is normally detected only in the choroid plexus at E14.5, so its endogenous levels/timing in progenitors remain uncertain; the fate-decision mechanism driving symmetric vs. asymmetric division (potentially involving organelle—centriole/mitochondria—segregation or DNA-replication events) is unresolved; and the lineage relationship between V-SVZ glia and specific neuronal subtypes needs further study. The findings are relevant to understanding regenerative/neurogenic capacity of the adult CNS.
