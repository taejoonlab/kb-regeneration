# Mechanical regulation of musculoskeletal system development

## Citation (NLM)
Felsenthal N, Zelzer E. Mechanical regulation of musculoskeletal system development. Development. 2017;144(23):4271-4283. doi:10.1242/dev.151266

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.151266](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.151266)

---

## Background
During embryogenesis, the musculoskeletal system develops while containing within itself a force generator in the form of the musculature. This generator becomes functional relatively early in development, exerting an increasing mechanical load on neighboring tissues as development proceeds. A growing body of evidence indicates that such mechanical forces can be translated into signals that combine with the genetic program of organogenesis. This unique situation presents both a major challenge and an opportunity to the other tissues of the musculoskeletal system, namely bones, joints, tendons, ligaments, and the tissues connecting them. This review summarizes the involvement of muscle-induced mechanical forces in the development of various vertebrate musculoskeletal components and their integration into one functional unit.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Chemical and genetic paralysis models in mouse and chick embryos (decamethonium bromide, curare, mdg mice, Myf5/MyoD-deficient mice)
2. Craniofacial development studies using zebrafish nic mutants (chrna1), myf5/myod double morphants, and tricaine-paralyzed embryos
3. Botox-induced paralysis for studying tendon-bone attachments (enthesis)
4. Transcriptomic analysis of gene expression profiles in muscleless limbs
5. Finite element analysis correlating bone morphology with mechanical properties
6. Natural reduction of fracture fragments and proprioceptive mechanosensor-deficient models

## Results
1. Various cartilaginous skeletal elements are shorter than normal in paralyzed chick and mouse embryos, with reduced chondrocyte proliferation in growth plates
2. Bone eminences are significantly smaller or completely absent in the absence of muscle activity
3. Column formation of resting chondrocytes is regulated by mechanical load; paralysis leads to failed column formation and aberrant bone morphology
4. Bone width and circumferential outline are determined by preferential periosteal growth; paralysis results in circular cross-sections that are mechanically inferior
5. In joint development, muscle contraction is dispensable for interzone formation but essential for cavitation and maintenance of joint progenitor cell fate
6. Tendon development stage 1 (cell specification, organization) is mechanical load-independent, but stage 2 (differentiation, maturation, maintenance) requires muscle presence and contraction
7. In enthesis development, the Sox9+/Scx+ cell pool is unaffected by mechanical cues, but subsequent bone eminence growth and mineralized fibrocartilage formation are mechanically sensitive
8. Muscle contraction also influences muscle development itself, affecting proper myotube assembly and alignment, muscle splitting, and maintenance of muscle progenitor cell pools

## Perspective
Mechanical forces serve as critical regulators across multiple aspects of musculoskeletal development. Mechanical signals originating from muscle contraction integrate with genetic programs to regulate morphogenesis and functional integration of bones, joints, tendons, and muscles. However, the molecular mechanisms that transduce these mechanical signals into biological outcomes remain unclear. Candidates such as mechanosensitive ion channels (PIEZO1/2), sensory neurons, and Hippo pathway components have been proposed, but further in vivo animal model studies are needed. Future research should focus on elucidating the precise mechanotransduction pathways of mechanical signals, joint-specific regulatory mechanisms, and the role of mechanical forces in evolutionary adaptation.
