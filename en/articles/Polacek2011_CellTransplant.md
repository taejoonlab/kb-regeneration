# The Secretory Profiles of Cultured Human Articular Chondrocytes and Mesenchymal Stem Cells: Implications for Autologous Cell Transplantation Strategies

## Citation (NLM)
Polacek M, Bruun JA, Elvenes J, Figenschau Y, Martínez I. The Secretory Profiles of Cultured Human Articular Chondrocytes and Mesenchymal Stem Cells: Implications for Autologous Cell Transplantation Strategies. *Cell Transplant*. 2011;20(9):1381-93. doi:10.3727/096368910X550215

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.3727/096368910X550215](https://doi.org/10.3727/096368910X550215)

---

## Background

Osteoarthritis (OA) is a degenerative joint disease characterized by progressive loss of cartilage matrix. Chondrocytes are the sole cell type within articular cartilage and play a central role in regulating matrix synthesis and degradation. IL-1β is a key pro-inflammatory cytokine in OA pathophysiology, inducing matrix-degrading enzyme expression in chondrocytes and suppressing cartilage matrix synthesis. Quantitative analysis of the intracellular proteome and secretome of chondrocytes is important for understanding OA progression mechanisms and discovering novel biomarkers and therapeutic targets. In this study, stable isotope labeling by amino acids in cell culture (SILAC) was applied for the first time to primary human articular chondrocytes (HACs) to quantitatively analyze changes in the intracellular proteome and secretome induced by IL-1β stimulation.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **SILAC standardization and cell culture**: HACs were isolated from cartilage obtained during total knee arthroplasty of OA patients (57–80 years, 3 female/2 male). Cells were cultured in light (L-lysine/L-arginine) and heavy (¹³C₆-lysine, ¹³C₆¹⁵N₄-arginine) SILAC DMEM-Flex media. Arginine titration experiments (84, 42, 28, 21, 0 mg/L) established 28 mg/L as the optimal concentration to prevent arginine-to-proline conversion.

2. **Labeling efficiency confirmation**: After 3 weeks (~6 cell divisions), LC-MS analysis confirmed 100% labeling efficiency. Chondrocyte phenotype maintenance was verified by COL2A1 gene expression via real-time PCR.

3. **IL-1β treatment and sample preparation**: Fully labeled P1 cells were treated with IL-1β (5 ng/mL) for 48 h. Intracellular proteins were separated by 1D SDS-PAGE, cut into 16 gel slices, and subjected to in-gel trypsin digestion. Conditioned medium proteins were concentrated by TCA precipitation and digested in solution.

4. **LC-MALDI-MS/MS analysis**: Peptides were separated by nanoLC (equipped with MALDI spotter) and analyzed on a 4800 MALDI-TOF/TOF mass spectrometer. Protein identification and quantification were performed using ProteinPilot v3.0 (Paragon Algorithm) and the UniProt/Swiss-Prot database (August 2010) with confidence >95% and unused score >1.3.

5. **Western blot validation**: Selected protein changes were validated by Western blot using antibodies against TGFBI, POSTN, TSP-1, and FN1.

6. **Statistical analysis**: Each experiment was repeated at least three times; significance was defined as p ≤ 0.05 with a ratio ≥ 1.2 (or ≤ 0.83).

## Results

**SILAC standardization successful**: HACs maintained normal proliferation and COL2A1 expression under SILAC culture conditions, achieving 100% labeling efficiency after 3 weeks. The optimized arginine concentration (28 mg/L) was sufficient for cell viability while preventing arginine-to-proline metabolic conversion.

**Intracellular proteome analysis**: 368 proteins were identified and 357 were quantified. IL-1β stimulation significantly altered 38 proteins. The major upregulated group comprised stress-induced chaperones (GRP78, HSP71, PDIA3/4/6), while the major downregulated group consisted of actin cytoskeleton-associated proteins (VINC, TPM4, FLNA, MYH9, CALD1, CAPG, ZYX). Protein synthesis and translation factors (EF2, STAT1) were also increased.

**Secretome analysis**: 115 secreted proteins were identified and quantified. IL-1β significantly regulated 35 proteins. Proteins detected exclusively in the IL-1β-treated group (ratio = 9999) included IL-6, IL-8, TSG6, MMP1, and MMP3—all associated with inflammation and matrix degradation. Conversely, thrombospondin-1 (TSP-1) was completely lost in the IL-1β-treated group.

**Major secretory changes induced by IL-1β**: Fibronectin (FN1) showed the greatest increase upon IL-1β stimulation, confirmed by Western blot. Secretion of BGH3 (beta ig-h3), a TGF-β pathway-associated protein, increased, while periostin (POSTN) decreased. Cartilage matrix proteins such as aggrecan, biglycan, SPARC, MGP, and LTBP2 decreased, whereas lumican, CHI3L1, tenascin, type VI collagen, MMP2, and cathepsin D increased. TIMP1 increased while TIMP2 decreased.

**Comparison of intracellular proteome vs. secretome**: Only 28 proteins overlapped between the two analyses (28 of 368 and 28 of 115), demonstrating that intracellular proteome and secretome analyses are complementary. Numerous proteins were regulated exclusively at the secretome level, many of which represent candidate OA biomarkers.

---

## Perspective

This study is the first quantitative proteomics analysis to apply SILAC to primary human articular chondrocytes, providing an integrated analysis of IL-1β-induced changes in both the intracellular proteome and secretome.

Intracellular proteome analysis revealed that IL-1β induces an increase in chaperones and a decrease in actin cytoskeletal proteins, providing a molecular basis for the morphological change of chondrocytes from a cobblestone to a rounded shape upon IL-1β treatment. Secretome analysis expanded our understanding of OA pathology by revealing regulation of diverse protein groups including matrix components, proteases and their inhibitors, and inflammatory mediators.

The reduction in vitamin K-dependent Gla proteins (MGP, POSTN) may be linked to the calcification and hypertrophic phenotype observed in OA cartilage. The increase in type VI collagen and its binding partners (FN1, BGH3) provides a connection between pericellular matrix remodeling and OA progression.

Limitations of this study include the restricted in vitro model and the small number of patient samples. Nevertheless, the identified secreted proteins warrant further evaluation as non-invasive early diagnostic biomarkers and therapeutic targets for OA, and provide a valuable resource for future pharmacoproteomics studies.
