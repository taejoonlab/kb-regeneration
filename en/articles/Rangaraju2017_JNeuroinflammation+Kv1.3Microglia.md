# A systems pharmacology-based approach to identify novel Kv1.3 channel-dependent mechanisms in microglial activation

## Citation (NLM)
Rangaraju S, Raza SA, Pennati A, Deng Q, Dammer EB, Duong D, Pennington MW, Tansey MG, Lah JJ, Betarbet R, Seyfried NT, Levey AI. A systems pharmacology-based approach to identify novel Kv1.3 channel-dependent mechanisms in microglial activation. J Neuroinflammation. 2017;14(1):128. doi:10.1186/s12974-017-0906-6

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1186/s12974-017-0906-6](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12974-017-0906-6)

---

## Background
Microglia, the innate immune cells of the CNS, adopt opposing pro- and anti-inflammatory states, and their dysregulation contributes to neuroinflammatory and neurodegenerative disorders. Kv1.3 is a voltage-gated outward-rectifying potassium channel expressed by microglia, macrophages, and other immune cells, where it regulates membrane potential, calcium signaling, cytokine production, and proliferation. Kv1.3 is selectively upregulated by pro-inflammatory microglia in human disease and animal models, making it an attractive therapeutic target that might inhibit pathogenic microglia while sparing homeostatic/neuroprotective functions. However, the molecular and signaling mechanisms downstream of Kv1.3 in activated microglia were poorly defined.

This study used a systems pharmacology approach — combining highly selective ShK-peptide Kv1.3 blockers with unbiased quantitative proteomics — to identify Kv1.3-dependent molecular pathways and cellular functions in pro-inflammatory (LPS-activated) versus resting microglia, and to validate them in vitro and in vivo.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Confirmation of Kv1.3 upregulation in LPS-activated BV2 microglia and in brain mononuclear phagocytes from LPS-treated mice, using immunoblotting, immunofluorescence, and flow cytometry with a fluoresceinated ShK analog (ShK-F6CA) that binds cell-surface Kv1.3.
2. Label-free quantitative proteomics (MaxQuant) of BV2 microglia under four conditions — control, LPS, ShK-223 (highly selective Kv1.3 blocker), and LPS+ShK-223 — followed by gene ontology (GO-Elite) and canonical pathway (Metacore) analyses.
3. Functional assays of Kv1.3 blockade in LPS-activated microglia: gap-closure and transwell transmigration (taxis), F-actin focal adhesion complex quantification, DCFDA reactive oxygen species assay, and bead phagocytosis.
4. In vivo validation in mice given four daily intraperitoneal doses of PBS, ShK-223, LPS, or LPS+ShK-223, with flow cytometry distinguishing resident microglia (CD11b+CD45low) from infiltrating macrophages (CD11b+CD45high), plus qRT-PCR of purified CNS mononuclear phagocytes.
5. MHCI-restricted antigen presentation assays: ovalbumin-loaded microglia co-cultured with CFSE-labeled Ova-specific OT-1 CD8+ T cells (in vitro and ex vivo).
6. Signaling analyses: phospho-flow cytometry of STAT1 serine (S727) and tyrosine (Y701) phosphorylation, qRT-PCR of IRF1/IRF7/NFkB1, and immunofluorescence co-localization of Kv1.3 with the CD14 LPS-receptor complex.

---

## Results
- LPS upregulated cell-surface Kv1.3 on BV2 microglia and, in vivo, on both microglia and CNS-infiltrating macrophages (baseline and LPS-induced Kv1.3 highest in CD45high macrophages).
- Of 3141 proteins quantified, 144 were LPS-regulated; 21 of these showed significant reversal by ShK-223. EHD1 and TAP1 were the top LPS-upregulated Kv1.3-dependent proteins; SPP1 and GABPA the most downregulated. Enriched processes included MHCI antigen presentation, cell motility/taxis, and focal adhesion formation.
- Functionally, ShK-223 reversed LPS-induced inhibition of migration and reduced LPS-induced F-actin focal adhesion complexes, but did not affect LPS-induced ROS production or phagocytosis.
- In primary microglia, ShK-223 inhibited LPS-induced upregulation of EHD1 protein, TAP1 mRNA, and IL1B mRNA, and increased GABPA mRNA — validating the top proteomic hits.
- Kv1.3 blockade inhibited LPS-induced MHCI (H2Kb) surface upregulation in resident microglia (but not infiltrating macrophages) in vivo, and reduced MHCI-restricted antigen presentation and proliferation of Ova-specific CD8+ T cells both in vitro and ex vivo. LPS selectively increased CD8+ (not CD8−) T cell trafficking to the brain.
- Mechanistically, Kv1.3 co-localized with CD14 and regulated early LPS-induced serine (S727), but not tyrosine (Y701), STAT1 phosphorylation, and partially controlled IRF1/IRF7 (but not NFkB1) induction — consistent with a Kv1.3→STAT1(S727)→IRF1→TAP1/EHD1→MHCI trafficking pathway.
- Additional predicted Kv1.3-dependent roles emerged in RNA processing, nuclear function, and (via GABPA/NRF2) mitochondrial biogenesis.

---

## Perspective
The work establishes previously unrecognized molecular and functional roles for microglial Kv1.3 channels in pro-inflammatory activation, most notably coupling Kv1.3 function to MHCI assembly/trafficking and CD8+ T cell-directed antigen presentation, and links Kv1.3 to the CD14/TLR complex through early STAT1 S727 phosphorylation. It strengthens the therapeutic candidacy of microglial Kv1.3 (targeted by clinically advanced ShK analogs such as dalazatide/ShK-186), suggesting Kv1.3 blockade could selectively dampen the adaptive-immunity-promoting functions of activated microglia in neuroinflammatory and neurodegenerative disease. For CNS injury and repair contexts (including spinal cord injury), where microglial activation and neuroinflammation are central, Kv1.3-selective immunomodulation is a mechanistically defined intervention point.

Limitations acknowledged include reliance on the BV2 line for the primary proteomic screen (partially offset by primary-microglia validation), depth limits of the proteome (~3141 proteins; secreted cytokines likely missed), a single activating stimulus (LPS acting pleiotropically on TLRs), and uncertainty about whether systemically administered ShK peptides act directly in the CNS or via peripheral intermediaries given limited BBB penetration. Future directions include dissecting Kv1.3's proposed roles in mitochondrial biogenesis via GABPA and clarifying the site of action of peripherally delivered Kv1.3 blockers.
