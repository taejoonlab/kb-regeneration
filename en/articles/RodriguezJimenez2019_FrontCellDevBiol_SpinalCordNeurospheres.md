# Organized Neurogenic-Niche-Like Pinwheel Structures Discovered in Spinal Cord Tissue-Derived Neurospheres

## Citation (NLM)
Rodriguez-Jimenez FJ, Clemente E, Moreno-Manzano V, Erceg S. Organized Neurogenic-Niche-Like Pinwheel Structures Discovered in Spinal Cord Tissue-Derived Neurospheres. Front Cell Dev Biol. 2019;7:334. doi:10.3389/fcell.2019.00334

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.3389/fcell.2019.00334](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcell.2019.00334)

---

## Background

The neurogenic niche of the subventricular zone (SVZ) in the adult brain is organized as a pinwheel-like cytoarchitecture: a core of mono-ciliated GFAP+ astrocytes with neural stem cell (NSC) properties surrounded by multi-ciliated ependymal cells. In the spinal cord, ciliated ependymal stem-like cells line the central canal with a less elaborate sub-ependymal layer of astrocytes, oligodendrocyte progenitors, and neurons, but pinwheel structures had not been described there. FoxJ1 is the master transcription factor of motile ciliogenesis and is required for radial glia differentiation into ependymal cells and a subset of astrocytes.

Little is known about the cellular organization and molecular control of cell-type proportions within cultured neurospheres. This study reports, for the first time, pinwheel-like structures in spinal cord- and SVZ-derived neurospheres cultured in vitro and investigates how the balance of ciliated astrocytes and ependymal cells controls their formation.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Neurosphere culture from spinal cord (and SVZ) of GFAP-TK transgenic mice (HSV thymidine kinase driven by GFAP promoter), 3 months old.
2. Immunocytochemistry for pinwheel identification: acetylated tubulin (cilia), GFAP (astrocytes), CD24 (ependymal cells), β-catenin (cell borders), γ-tubulin (basal bodies/MTOCs), FoxJ1.
3. Pharmacological modulation: methyltransferase inhibitor 5-aza-2′-deoxycytidine (5-aza-dc, 48 h) to induce demethylation, and ganciclovir (GCV, 24 h) to selectively ablate dividing GFAP+ astrocytes in GFAP-TK cells.
4. Bisulfite sequencing (BSP) of a FoxJ1 5′ CpG island; MethPrimer primer design; qRT-PCR for FoxJ1 expression (normalized to GAPDH).
5. Western blotting for GFAP and Vimentin protein; densitometry.
6. Quantification of GFAP+/CD24+ positive areas and number of pinwheel structures per 100 cells across conditions.

---

## Results

- Spinal cord- and SVZ-derived neurospheres form SVZ-like pinwheel structures in vitro: a core of acetylated-tubulin/GFAP+ (mono-ciliated astrocyte-like) cells surrounded by CD24+ ependymal cells, with astrocyte extensions connecting adjacent cores and shared ependymal cells between pinwheels.
- A CpG island (−104 to +123 relative to TSS) silences FoxJ1; 5-aza-dc reduced methylation (34.5% → 4.97%) and significantly increased FoxJ1 mRNA (~4.3-fold).
- 5-aza-dc increased GFAP protein and slightly decreased Vimentin, causing aberrant accumulation of ciliated astrocytes (acetylated tubulin/GFAP double-positive small cells).
- GCV ablation of dividing astrocytes decreased GFAP and increased Vimentin/CD24, enriching ciliated ependymal cells and enlarging CD24+ areas.
- Both 5-aza-dc and GCV significantly reduced the number of pinwheel structures (DMSO 8 vs. 5-aza-dc 2.5 vs. GCV 1.75 per 100 cells), disrupting the cytoarchitecture.
- GCV also produced aberrant aggregation of γ-tubulin and acetylated tubulin (not docked at the apical surface), resembling FoxJ1-null cells and indicating disrupted cilia formation.

---

## Perspective

This is the first report that pinwheel neurogenic-niche cytoarchitecture — previously described only in the adult SVZ in vivo — self-assembles in vitro in spinal cord- and SVZ-derived neurospheres, suggesting neuronal-lineage cells possess an intrinsic memory/capacity to self-organize into an organotypic-like arrangement. The correct ratio of ciliated astrocytes to ependymal cells is critical: shifting the balance in either direction (via demethylation or astrocyte ablation) disrupts pinwheel formation. Limitations include the need to assess apical γ-tubulin position, cell polarity, and orientation to confirm faithful mimicry of the in vivo niche. The organotypic-like culture offers a source of potentially transplantable neurogenic cells and a platform for high-throughput genomic/proteomic and drug screening to study CNS neurogenesis under normal and pathological conditions, with relevance to spinal cord regenerative medicine.
