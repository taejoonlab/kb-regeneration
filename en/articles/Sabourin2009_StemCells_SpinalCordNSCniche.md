# A Mesenchymal-Like ZEB1+ Niche Harbors Dorsal Radial Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein-Positive Stem Cells in the Spinal Cord

## Citation (NLM)
Sabourin JC, Ackema KB, Ohayon D, Guichet PO, Perrin FE, Garces A, Ripoll C, Charité J, Simonneau L, Kettenmann H, Zine A, Privat A, Valmier J, Pattyn A, Hugnot JP. A mesenchymal-like ZEB1+ niche harbors dorsal radial glial fibrillary acidic protein-positive stem cells in the spinal cord. Stem Cells. 2009;27(11):2722-2733. doi:10.1002/stem.226

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1002/stem.226](https://doi.org/10.1002/stem.226)

---

## Background

In humans and rodents the adult spinal cord harbors neural stem cells located around the central canal, but their identity, precise location and specific signaling remained ill-defined and controversial. Compared with the two well-characterized brain niches (subventricular zone [SVZ] and hippocampus), the cellular composition, organization and molecular signaling of the spinal cord niche were poorly understood. Bona fide neural stem cells that form long-term passageable neurospheres appear restricted to the central canal region, and after spinal cord trauma or in degenerative disease these cells migrate to the lesion and differentiate into astrocytes and oligodendrocytes.

A prior study (Meletis et al.) had concluded that spinal cord neural stem cells are GFAP-negative ependymal cells, in contrast to the GFAP+ stem cells of the SVZ. This work provides a detailed re-analysis of the spinal cord niche to resolve stem-cell identity, location, and its signaling/structural characteristics.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Microdissection of adult mouse (12-15 week OF1 or hGFAP-GFP transgenic) spinal cord regions (central vs parenchyma; dorsal vs ventral; dorsal medial strip) followed by clonal-density neurosphere assays to localize neurosphere-forming cells.
2. Ki67 immunodetection and BrdU labelling to quantify postnatal proliferation (0-13 weeks) around the central canal; correlation with spinal cord length.
3. Immunofluorescence for a panel of neural/stem markers (GFAP, vimentin, nestin, Sox2/Sox9/Sox4, PSA-NCAM, Cadherin 13, CD133, CD15, EdnrB, BLBP, β-III-tubulin, Dcx, Nkx6.1) plus electron microscopy/immunogold.
4. Flow cytometry sorting of GFP+/GFP− cells from hGFAP-GFP mice and clonal neurosphere formation to test GFAP+ stem-cell identity; multipotency assays (astrocytes, O4+ oligodendrocytes, Dcx+ neurons).
5. Analysis of niche signaling (HES1, Jagged1, DAN/NBL1) and mesenchymal/EMT features (ZEB1/2, SNAI2, SMA, CD44, fibronectin, vimentin) by immunofluorescence and RT-PCR; videomicroscopy motility assay vs fibroblasts and epithelial lines.
6. Conditional dominant-negative ZEB (DB-ZEB) transgenic mouse with CAV-cre adenoviral recombination to test ZEB requirement (neurosphere formation, TUNEL apoptosis).
7. Semiquantitative RT-PCR of all 39 Hox genes in cervical/thoracic/lumbar spinal cord and derived neurospheres.

---

## Results

- Proliferation around the central canal progressively declined and ceased around 9-13 weeks after birth, correlating inversely with the ~2-fold postnatal elongation of the spinal cord (no BrdU+ cells around canal in adult), implicating the niche in postnatal spinal cord growth.
- Neurosphere-forming cells were concentrated around the central canal and, surprisingly, predominantly in the dorsal (dorsal medial) region; dorsal medial-derived neurospheres were larger and multipotent (astrocytes, O4+ oligodendrocytes, GABAergic Dcx+ neurons), maintained over ≥9 passages.
- The central canal region contains several cell types (ependymal and subependymal), including Dcx+ Nkx6.1+ neuronal cells projecting into the lumen, GFAP+ radial cells, and BLBP+ radial glia; clear dorsal-ventral asymmetry (higher dorsal cell density, stronger dorsal CD15).
- Spinal cord neural stem cells are mostly dorsal radial GFAP+ cells: all GFP+ cells around the canal were GFAP+ (52/52), ~90% of neurospheres arose from the GFP+ fraction, and all neurospheres contained strongly GFAP+ cells — challenging the earlier GFAP-negative conclusion (attributed to antibody differences, GFAP isoforms).
- The niche shows elevated Notch signaling (nuclear/cytoplasmic HES1, luminal Jagged1) and the BMP inhibitor DAN, plus rostro-caudal Hox gene combinations retained in adult stem cells and faithfully preserved in region-derived neurospheres.
- Unexpectedly, the niche and cultured stem cells display mesenchymal/EMT traits: expression of ZEB1 (and ZEB2 in neurospheres), SMA, CD44, fibronectin; neurosphere cells were as motile as fibroblasts.
- ZEB proteins are essential: DB-ZEB expression massively reduced neurosphere formation and triggered a drastic increase in apoptosis (TUNEL), indicating ZEB is required for neural stem cell survival/expansion in vitro.

---

## Perspective

The study establishes the adult spinal cord central-canal region as a third CNS neural stem cell niche with its own cellular, structural and molecular identity, identifying the stem cells as dorsally located radial GFAP+ cells (contradicting the prior GFAP-negative model). It uncovers a surprising mesenchymal/EMT signature (ZEB1, SMA) coupled to stemness, with ZEB transcription factors required for stem cell survival, and shows retention of embryonic rostro-caudal Hox patterning in adult stem cells — implying not all neural stem cells are equivalent for cell-based therapy.

The GFAP discrepancy with earlier work is attributed to antibody/isoform differences, which the authors address with polyclonal and monoclonal antibodies, a reporter mouse, and immunogold. Functionally, the niche's role in the adult appears limited (low/absent new-cell production), possibly confined to postnatal growth, though it may be reactivatable in injury or disease. The EMT program may prime niche cells for the delamination/migration seen after spinal cord injury. The work suggests the niche could be manipulated to promote endogenous spinal cord regeneration.
