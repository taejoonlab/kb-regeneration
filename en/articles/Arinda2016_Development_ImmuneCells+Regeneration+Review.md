# Non-traditional roles of immune cells in regeneration: an evolutionary perspective

## Citation (NLM)
Arinda BN, Innabi YA, Grasis JA, Oviedo NJ. Non-traditional roles of immune cells in regeneration: an evolutionary perspective. Development. 2022;149(1):dev199903. doi:10.1242/dev.199903

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.199903](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.199903)

---

## Background
The immune system has traditionally been viewed solely as a defense mechanism against pathogens. However, emerging research over the past two decades has revealed that immune cells participate in diverse non-immune functions, including tissue repair, regeneration, and stem cell regulation. This review examines these non-traditional roles through an evolutionary lens, exploring how immune-stem cell crosstalk has been conserved across species from invertebrates to mammals.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Comparative evolutionary analysis of trained immunity across diploblastic species, protostomes, deuterostomes, and mammals
2. Review of macrophage depletion studies in regeneration models (salamander limb, zebrafish fin, planarian)
3. Analysis of bidirectional crosstalk between immune cells and stem cells across multiple tissue systems (intestine, mammary gland, nervous system, bone marrow)
4. Examination of cell competition mechanisms mediated by innate immune components in Drosophila
5. Synthesis of findings from genetic, pharmacological, and cell depletion approaches across model organisms

## Results
1. Trained immunity (innate immune memory) exists across evolution from sponges and corals to mammals, mediated by epigenetic reprogramming in response to pathogenic signals
2. Macrophages are essential for regeneration in multiple models: salamander limb regeneration fails upon early macrophage depletion; zebrafish fin regeneration requires macrophages for blastema proliferation and patterning
3. In the mammalian intestine, CSF1-dependent macrophages regulate intestinal stem cell (ISC) differentiation; depletion affects Paneth cell lineage and depletes Lgr5+ ISCs
4. Mammary stem cells require macrophages to maintain their microenvironment; absence causes morphological defects in ductal development
5. In the nervous system, M2 macrophages secrete pro-myelination cytokines (IL10, TGFβ, VEGF) that drive remyelination and oligodendrocyte differentiation
6. MSCs and macrophages engage in bidirectional crosstalk: MSCs produce VEGF and PDGF to recruit stromal cells, while promoting monocyte egress from bone marrow
7. In Drosophila, hemocytes secrete Dpp (TGFβ/BMP homolog) to induce ISC proliferation and maintain germline stem cell niche assembly
8. Planarian innate immune system differentiates between infection and tissue repair via TAK1/MKKK/p38 signaling, which can either enhance or repress cell death depending on context
9. In Hydra, FoxO transcription factor links stress response, stem cell renewal, and antimicrobial peptide expression via JNK signaling

## Perspective
This review establishes that immune cells, particularly macrophages and macrophage-like cells, serve essential non-immune functions in development, tissue homeostasis, and regeneration across the animal kingdom. The evolutionary conservation of immune-stem cell crosstalk suggests fundamental mechanisms that could be harnessed for regenerative medicine. Future research should focus on comprehensive organismal-level analyses of tissue interactions, which may yield advances in immunotherapy, neuroimmunomodulation, and regenerative therapies. Understanding how the same signaling pathways (e.g., TAK1/MKKK/p38) drive different cell fates in infection versus repair contexts remains a critical open question.
