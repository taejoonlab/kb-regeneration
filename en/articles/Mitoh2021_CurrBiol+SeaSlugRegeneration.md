# Extreme autotomy and whole-body regeneration in photosynthetic sea slugs

## Citation (NLM)
Mitoh S, Yusa Y. Extreme autotomy and whole-body regeneration in photosynthetic sea slugs. Curr Biol. 2021;31(5):R233-R234. doi:10.1016/j.cub.2021.01.014

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cub.2021.01.014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cub.2021.01.014)

---

## Background
Autotomy — the voluntary shedding of a body part — is common across distantly related animals (arthropods, gastropods, asteroids, amphibians, lizards) and is generally followed by regeneration of shed terminal parts such as appendages or tails. This report (a Current Biology Correspondence) identifies a new, extreme form of autotomy in two sacoglossan sea slug species (Mollusca: Gastropoda), *Elysia* cf. *marginata* and *Elysia atroviridis*, in which the animal sheds the entire main body — including the whole heart, kidney, intestine, and most reproductive organs — and the remaining head regenerates a completely new body.

Sacoglossan sea slugs can incorporate chloroplasts from algal food into their cells and use them for photosynthesis (kleptoplasty). The authors propose that this ability may facilitate survival and regeneration after such drastic body loss.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Observed laboratory-bred and field-collected *Elysia* cf. *marginata* (a species complex) and field-collected *Elysia atroviridis* for spontaneous autotomy at the neck position.
2. Documented time course of head survival, wound closure, heart regeneration, and whole-body regeneration by photography over ~20 days.
3. Induced autotomy experimentally by gently tying a fine nylon string around the pre-existing transverse groove ("breakage plane") at the neck; compared with imitated predator attacks (pinching the head, cutting parapodia).
4. Assessed the parasite hypothesis in *E. atroviridis* by dissecting autotomized/autolyzed bodies for the internal parasitic copepod (*Arthurius* sp.) and comparing parasitized versus parasite-free individuals.

---

## Results
- In *Elysia* cf. *marginata*, 5/15 (33%) laboratory-bred individuals of one morphotype and one field-collected individual autotomized at the neck; one individual autotomized twice. In all cases the pericardium (heart) remained in the shed body.
- The separated head moved autonomously immediately after autotomy; wound closure completed within ~1 day, and relatively young heads (226-336 days post-hatching) began feeding within hours.
- Young heads started heart regeneration within 7 days and completed whole-body regeneration within ~20 days. Older heads (480-520 days) did not feed and died within 10 days.
- Shed bodies moved and responded to stimuli for days to months, and the heart continued beating, but none of the shed bodies regenerated a head; they gradually shrank, paled (losing chloroplasts), and decomposed.
- In *E. atroviridis*, 3/82 (4%) field-collected, copepod-parasitized individuals autotomized at the neck, and two regenerated their bodies within a week; an additional 39/82 (48%) gradually lost body parts (presumed autolysis) and discharged the copepods, of which 13 regenerated. None of 64 parasite-free individuals autotomized.
- Experimental string-tying induced autotomy at the tied neck position in 5/6 *E.* cf. *marginata* within a day; autotomized bodies represented 80-85% of total body weight. Imitated predator attacks did not induce autotomy.
- The authors argue autotomy here is unlikely to be predation escape (it takes hours; mimicked attacks failed to induce it) and instead likely functions to remove internal parasites (all autotomized/autolyzed *E. atroviridis* were parasitized and became parasite-free after regeneration).

---

## Perspective
This work documents a remarkable case of whole-body regeneration in animals with complex body plans: the head can survive and rebuild an entire body (including the heart) after losing ~80-85% of its mass. The authors propose kleptoplasty (photosynthesis via ingested chloroplasts, maintained in the branched digestive gland that extends into the head) as a likely energy source enabling survival and regeneration without a functional digestive/circulatory body. They also report the first successful multi-generation rearing of *Elysia* cf. *marginata*, establishing it as a tractable model system for studying autotomy and whole-body regeneration.

As a short Correspondence, the study is observational and does not resolve mechanism: why the head survives without the heart and other organs is unclear, the cellular/molecular basis of regeneration is not addressed, and the functional significance of autotomy (parasite removal vs. escape from algal entanglement or toxin accumulation) remains to be established. This extreme regeneration model may nonetheless inform comparative regeneration biology, including the general question of how animals rebuild complex organs and tissues.
