# A Mesenchymal-Like ZEB1+ Niche Harbors Dorsal Radial Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein-Positive Stem Cells in the Spinal Cord

## Citation (NLM)
Sabourin JC, Ackema KB, Ohayon D, Guichet PO, Perrin FE, Garces A, Ripoll C, Charite J, Simonneau L, Kettenmann H, Zine A, Privat A, Valmier J, Pattyn A, Hugnot JP. A mesenchymal-like ZEB1+ niche harbors dorsal radial glial fibrillary acidic protein-positive stem cells in the spinal cord. Stem Cells. 2009;27(11):2722-2733. doi:10.1002/stem.226

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1002/stem.226](https://doi.org/10.1002/stem.226)

---

## Background

In humans and rodents, the adult spinal cord harbors neural stem cells located around the central canal, but their identity, precise location, and specific signaling were ill-defined and controversial. Compared with the two well-characterized brain niches (subventricular zone [SVZ] and hippocampus), very little was known about the cellular composition, organization, and molecular signaling of the spinal cord niche. Bona fide neural stem cells able to form long-term passageable neurospheres appear to be located only around the central canal, and after spinal cord trauma or degenerative disease these cells migrate to the lesion and differentiate into astrocytes and oligodendrocytes.

The authors provide a detailed analysis of this third CNS stem cell niche, testing the identity of the stem cells (in particular the contested question of whether they are GFAP-positive or GFAP-negative) and characterizing the niche's signaling and unexpected mesenchymal features.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Microdissection of postnatal/adult (12–15 week) OF1 wild-type and hGFAP-GFP transgenic mouse spinal cord, combined with clonal-density neurosphere assays from defined regions (central vs. peripheral, dorsal vs. ventral, dorsal-medial strip).
2. Ki67 and BrdU labeling to quantify proliferation from 0–13 weeks postnatally, alongside spinal cord length measurement.
3. Extensive immunofluorescence for immature/stem/radial-glial markers (GFAP, BLBP, CD15, CD133, Sox2/Sox4/Sox9, vimentin, nestin, PSA-NCAM, Cadherin 13, EdnrB, Dcx, β-III-tubulin, Nkx6.1), plus electron microscopy and immunogold.
4. Flow cytometry (FACS) sorting of GFP+ vs. GFP− cells from hGFAP-GFP mice followed by clonal neurosphere assays and multilineage differentiation.
5. Detection of niche signaling (Hes1, Jagged1, DAN/NBL1) and EMT/mesenchymal markers (ZEB1/2, SNAI1/2, TWIST1/2, SMA, fibronectin, CD44) by RT-PCR and immunofluorescence; motility assay by videomicroscopy vs. fibroblast and epithelial controls.
6. Conditional dominant-negative ZEB (DB-ZEB) transgenic mouse with CAV-cre infection of neurospheres to test the functional requirement of ZEB, with TUNEL apoptosis assay.
7. Semiquantitative RT-PCR of all 39 Hox genes in neurospheres and spinal cord from cervical, thoracic, and lumbar levels.

## Results

- Proliferation (Ki67+, BrdU+) around the central canal progressively decreased and ceased around 9–13 weeks, correlating inversely with the ~2-fold postnatal increase in spinal cord length; the adult central region is essentially non-proliferative.
- Neurosphere-forming cells were located predominantly dorsally: a dorsal-medial strip including the central canal showed the highest enrichment (~4-fold more than the central region), and dorsal-derived neurospheres were larger and multipotent (astrocytes, O4+ oligodendrocytes, Dcx+ GABAergic young neurons), maintained over 9+ passages.
- The central canal region comprises several cell types, not a single ependymal layer: most cells express vimentin, nestin, Sox2, Sox9, PSA-NCAM, Cadherin 13, and EdnrB; a distinct dorsal population of radial GFAP+ cells (some BLBP+/CD15+) extends long processes toward the pial surface, and Dcx+/Nkx6.1+ CSF-contacting neurons send processes into the lumen.
- Neural stem cells are radial GFAP+ cells: in hGFAP-GFP mice, >80% of bright GFP+ cells were dorsal, all GFP+ cells were GFAP+ (52/52), and ~90% of neurospheres formed from the GFP+ fraction. Clonal neurospheres contained both GFAP+ and GFAP− cells, consistent with asymmetric stem cell division.
- The niche shows elevated Notch signaling (Hes1, Jagged1) and the BMP inhibitor DAN, plus unexpected mesenchymal traits: expression of ZEB1 (and ZEB2, SNAI2 in neurospheres), SMA, vimentin, fibronectin, and CD44. Neurosphere cells were as motile as fibroblasts, whereas epithelial controls were nearly immobile.
- ZEB is functionally required: dominant-negative DB-ZEB expression caused massive apoptosis (TUNEL+) and drastically reduced neurosphere number, without cre-induced toxicity in controls, demonstrating an essential role for ZEB in stem cell survival/expansion in vitro.
- Adult spinal cord neural stem cells maintain region-specific rostro-caudal Hox gene combinations: cervical/thoracic/lumbar-derived neurospheres largely preserved the fetal-like Hox pattern of their level of origin.

---

## Perspective

This study establishes the adult spinal cord central canal as a third CNS neural stem cell niche with its own cellular, structural, and molecular identity, and resolves a controversy by showing (in contrast to Meletis et al.) that the stem cells are dorsal radial GFAP+ cells rather than GFAP-negative ependymal cells — attributing the discrepancy largely to antibody choice (polyclonal vs. monoclonal GFAP). The unexpected coupling of EMT/mesenchymal programs (ZEB1, SMA, motility) with stemness, and the demonstrated survival requirement for ZEB, link this niche to broader themes in stem cell biology and cancer. Persistence of rostro-caudal Hox codes implies that spinal stem cells retain positional identity, which is relevant for the design of stem cell-based therapies (not all neural stem cells may be equivalent).

Limitations noted include the mostly in vitro assessment of ZEB function and mesenchymal motility, and uncertainty about the niche's physiological role — since new-cell production is low/absent in the adult, its main function may be limited to postnatal spinal cord extension, with possible reactivation in injury/disease. Manipulation of this niche is proposed as a route to endogenous spinal cord regeneration.
