# Blood vessel formation and function in bone

## Citation (NLM)
Sivaraj KK, Adams RH. Blood vessel formation and function in bone. Development. 2016;143(15):2706-2715. doi:10.1242/dev.136861

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.136861](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.136861)

---

## Background
Blood vessels in the skeletal system serve not only as conduits for gases, nutrients, and cells, but also play active roles in controlling bone formation, providing niches for hematopoietic stem cells, and facilitating bone healing. The intimate coupling between angiogenesis and osteogenesis has been recognized, yet the architecture, formation mechanisms, and functional specialization of the bone vasculature remain incompletely understood. Understanding how blood vessels form within bone and how they regulate bone development and repair is critical for treating bone diseases such as osteoporosis, osteonecrosis, and impaired fracture healing.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Confocal and two-photon microscopy imaging of endomucin (EMCN) and CD31 immunostained bone sections to visualize vascular architecture
2. Classification of bone capillary subtypes based on marker expression: type H (CD31hi EMCNhi) and type L (CD31lo EMCNlo) vessels
3. Genetic mouse models with endothelial cell-specific gene inactivation (VEGFR2, Notch pathway components DLL4/RBPJκ, FGFR1/2, ADAM10)
4. Analysis of VEGF isoform-specific knock-in mice (VEGFA120, VEGFA164, VEGFA188) to determine roles of soluble vs. matrix-bound VEGF
5. Hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) conditional knockout studies in chondrocytes and osteoblasts
6. MMP9 and MMP13 single and double knockout mouse analyses for matrix remodeling roles
7. Assessment of perivascular cell populations (LEPR+, CXCL12+ CAR cells, PDGFRβ+/NG2+ pericytes, αSMA+ smooth muscle cells) associated with distinct vessel subtypes

## Results
- Bone vasculature has hierarchical organization: arteries feed exclusively into type H capillaries in the metaphysis and endosteum, which then connect to type L sinusoidal network in the diaphysis, draining into a central vein
- Type H vessels (CD31hi EMCNhi) are columnar structures in the metaphysis associated with osterix+ osteoprogenitors; type L vessels (CD31lo EMCNlo) form sinusoidal networks in the bone marrow cavity
- This unique organization creates distinct metabolic environments: metaphysis is well-oxygenated, diaphysis is highly hypoxic
- VEGFA from hypertrophic chondrocytes is the master regulator of endochondral angiogenesis; VEGFR2 signaling controls endothelial cell migration, proliferation, and survival, as well as osteoblast differentiation (autocrine)
- Matrix-bound VEGF isoforms (VEGFA164, VEGFA188) are required for proper angiogenesis; soluble-only VEGFA120 results in decreased vascularization and mineralization
- Hypoxia/HIF signaling in osteogenic cells regulates VEGF expression and couples angiogenesis with osteogenesis; HIF1α deletion in chondrocytes causes massive growth plate cell death
- MMP9 and MMP13 are essential for ECM remodeling during vascular invasion; MMP9 releases ECM-bound VEGF
- Endothelial-specific Notch activation promotes local angiogenesis and osteogenesis through noggin secretion (BMP antagonist), which induces hypertrophic chondrocyte formation
- FGFR1/2 signaling in endothelial cells maintains arterial function and vascular integrity in bone
- During fracture healing, VEGF, FGF, PlGF, TGFβ, and BMP pathways all contribute to revascularization and bone repair; blocking VEGF impairs fracture healing
- Type H vessels and associated osteoprogenitors are reduced in aged mice, correlating with age-related bone loss

## Perspective
The discovery that endothelial cells are not merely passive conduits but active regulators of bone formation through secretion of factors like noggin fundamentally changes our understanding of skeletal biology. The type H/type L vessel classification provides a framework for understanding spatial regulation of osteogenesis. The coupling of angiogenesis and osteogenesis through shared signaling pathways (VEGF, HIF, Notch, FGF) offers multiple therapeutic targets. Reactivating developmental angiogenic processes could potentially treat age-related bone loss and avascular necrosis. However, whether findings from murine models translate to humans requires further investigation. Future work should focus on identifying bone-specific endothelial cell subpopulations, understanding perivascular niche regulation of hematopoietic stem cells, and developing targeted pro-angiogenic therapies for bone regeneration.
