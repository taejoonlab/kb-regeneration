# Microglial trogocytosis and the complement system regulate axonal pruning in vivo

## Citation (NLM)
Lim TKY, Ruthazer ES. Microglial trogocytosis and the complement system regulate axonal pruning in vivo. eLife. 2021;10:e62167. doi:10.7554/eLife.62167

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.62167](https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.62167)

---

## Background

Microglia, the immune cells of the CNS, are thought to shape developing neural circuits by phagocytosing inappropriate or supernumerary synapses. Most evidence is indirect (histology, microglia depletion) and cannot establish whether synaptic material inside microglia comes from living neurons versus apoptotic debris. Trogocytosis — partial phagocytosis of axons and presynaptic boutons — has been documented ex vivo (organotypic slices, fixed EM tissue), but had not been directly observed in vivo, and its mechanisms and circuit function were poorly understood.

This study directly imaged axon trogocytosis by microglia in vivo in the developing Xenopus laevis retinotectal circuit, tested its functional role via microglial depletion, and probed the complement system's contribution, including identifying a neuronally expressed complement inhibitory protein.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. In vivo two-photon time-lapse imaging of Xenopus laevis tadpole optic tectum; microglia labeled with Alexa594-conjugated IB4-isolectin, RGC axons labeled by retinal electroporation with pH-stable GFP (pHtdGFP) and with SYP-pHtdGFP presynaptic reporter.
2. Population-level trogocytosis assay: quantifying microglial green fluorescence (3D masking) versus number of labeled axons on day 4 and day 5 post-labeling, excluding data with axonal blebbing/loss to control for apoptosis.
3. Microglial depletion with the CSF1R inhibitor PLX5622; quantification of microglia number, morphology, and single RGC axon arbor length/branch number.
4. Free-swimming looming-stimulus behavioral assay (dark vs. bright looming) with computer-vision tracking.
5. Bioinformatic screen (STRING protein interaction network, CCP-domain filtering, Allen Brain human cortical RNA-seq) to identify neuronal complement inhibitors; BLAST identification of aRCA3 (Xenopus homolog of human CD46); RNAscope in situ hybridization in retina.
6. Functional manipulation: overexpression of aRCA3-mCherry (colocalization with SYP), and a membrane-bound VAMP2-C3 fusion protein to enhance complement on single axons.

---

## Results

- Real-time imaging showed individual microglia increasing in green fluorescence (2- to 3-fold) after contacting pHtdGFP-labeled axons — direct in vivo evidence of trogocytosis of presynaptic axonal material.
- Xenopus tadpole microglia are highly mobile (~1.7 µm/min), switch between ameboid and primitive ramified morphologies, surveil the tectal neuropil, and respond to laser injury, resembling neonatal mammalian microglia.
- Microglial green fluorescence correlated with the number of pHtdGFP- and SYP-pHtdGFP-labeled axons (strengthening from day 4 to day 5), indicating accumulation of presynaptic material from intact living axons.
- PLX5622 depletion of microglia did not change axon arbor length but increased axon branch number, and reversed behavioral responses to looming stimuli (reduced escape to dark, increased escape to bright), without altering motor escape parameters.
- Bioinformatics identified CD46 as the only CCP-domain complement inhibitor highly expressed by human CNS neurons; its Xenopus homolog aRCA3 is a type I transmembrane protein expressed in RGC neurons (ganglion cell layer).
- aRCA3-mCherry colocalized with the presynaptic marker SYP; aRCA3 overexpression inhibited microglial trogocytosis and increased axon arbor size and branching, whereas VAMP2-C3 (enhanced complement) reduced axon arbor length and branch number.

---

## Perspective

The work provides the first direct in vivo demonstration that microglia trogocytose presynaptic axonal elements during healthy development and supports a model in which microglial trogocytosis prunes and refines axonal arbors to promote proper neural wiring. Critically, neurons exert local control over this process through expression of complement regulatory proteins (aRCA3/CD46), with the complement C3 pathway bidirectionally tuning pruning. Limitations noted by the authors include lack of correlative electron microscopy to confirm engulfed vesicle identity and the possibility that aRCA3 exerts some effects via intracellular signaling rather than purely complement inhibition. The findings connect complement-mediated synaptic pruning with axonal trogocytosis and suggest neuron-intrinsic complement regulation as a mechanism controlling circuit refinement, with implications for understanding aberrant pruning in neurodevelopmental and neurodegenerative disease.
