# Foxj1a is expressed in ependymal precursors, controls central canal position and is activated in new ependymal cells during regeneration in zebrafish

## Citation (NLM)
Ribeiro A, Monteiro JF, Certal AC, Cristovão AM, Saúde L. Foxj1a is expressed in ependymal precursors, controls central canal position and is activated in new ependymal cells during regeneration in zebrafish. Open Biol. 2017;7(11):170139. doi:10.1098/rsob.170139

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1098/rsob.170139](https://doi.org/10.1098/rsob.170139)

---

## Background

Unlike mammals, adult zebrafish efficiently regenerate the spinal cord and recover motor and sensory function after severe injury, largely through the activation of ependymal cells surrounding the central canal. In mammals these ependymal cells retain a stem/progenitor character and neurosphere-forming capacity, but upon injury respond with a strong proliferative burst that is biased toward astrocytes (contributing to the glial scar) rather than neurons. Understanding organisms that spontaneously form new neurons after injury could reveal signals to redirect endogenous stem cells toward neuronal fate.

Study of zebrafish ependymal behaviour has been hampered by the scarcity of ependymal-specific markers. Foxj1, a master regulator of motile ciliogenesis, is expressed by ependymal cells in mouse and human spinal cord. This study tested whether the zebrafish homologue Foxj1a marks ependymal cells and whether Foxj1a-expressing cells participate in spinal cord repair.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Tg(0.6foxj1a:GFP) reporter transgenic zebrafish to follow Foxj1a-expressing cells in adult spinal cord and embryos; fluorescent in situ hybridization (FISH) for endogenous foxj1a.
2. Immunostaining and marker analysis (acetylated α-tubulin for cilia, Nkx6.1, Pax6, GFAP, vimentin, HuC/D, PKD2L1) plus transmission electron microscopy to characterize ciliated ependymal and CSF-contacting neuron populations.
3. Embryonic time-course (24-120 hpf) with ZO-1 labelling to track primitive lumen contraction into the central canal.
4. Antisense morpholino (MO) knockdown of Foxj1a (start-codon targeting), with MO-resistant mRNA rescue and Cas9/gRNA targeting for specificity; lumen size/position quantification.
5. FISH for the Shh target patched2 and phospho-histone H3 (pHH3) staining to assess Shh pathway activity and proliferation in morphants.
6. Adult spinal cord compression injury and larval transection injury; PCNA labelling to quantify proliferation at 3-30 dpi; cyclopamine treatment to inhibit Shh signalling during regeneration.

---

## Results

- Foxj1a (reporter and endogenous transcript) labels most cells surrounding the adult central canal, predominantly ependymo-radial glia (ERG) with pial-projecting processes bearing long, likely motile cilia (central microtubule pair and outer dynein arms by TEM).
- Foxj1a is expressed across the dorsoventral extent of the ependymal region (both Nkx6.1+ ventral and Pax6+ dorsal cells), more broadly than GFAP or vimentin, establishing it as a pan-ependymal marker; it also marks CSF-contacting neurons (HuC/D+, pkd2l1+).
- During embryogenesis Foxj1a is induced in floor plate (24 hpf) and roof plate (48 hpf) and a previously unreported middle domain; these domains contribute to the ependymal region after dorsal-to-ventral "zipping" contraction of the lumen (48-72 hpf).
- Loss of Foxj1a (morphants) delayed lumen contraction and shifted the final central canal position dorsally, without changing dorsoventral identity of ependymal cells; phenotype rescued by MO-resistant mRNA and reproduced by Cas9/gRNA.
- Foxj1a morphants showed increased patched2 (Shh target) expression and more pHH3+ mitotic cells, indicating Foxj1a dampens Shh signalling and promotes progenitor cell-cycle exit; the lumen defect is consistent with prolonged Shh-driven proliferation.
- After adult spinal cord injury, Foxj1a+ ERGs proliferate (dividing ERGs ~47% of proliferating cells at 3 dpi) and expand the ependymal area, contributing to structural restoration.
- Endogenous foxj1a is strongly upregulated in proliferating cells after injury, but the 0.6 kb reporter transgene fails to reproduce this (and the embryonic middle domain), indicating injury-induced expression requires additional regulatory elements.
- Cyclopamine (Shh inhibitor) reduced injury-induced foxj1a expression, showing that regeneration-associated Foxj1a upregulation is Shh-dependent, paralleling its developmental regulation.

---

## Perspective

The work establishes Foxj1a as a pan-ependymal marker in zebrafish across development, homeostasis and regeneration, and shows a dual developmental role: attenuating Shh-driven mitogenic activity to trigger progenitor cell-cycle exit/lumen contraction, and being reactivated (Shh-dependently) in proliferating ependymal cells during spinal cord repair. This reinforces the molecular similarity of zebrafish and mammalian ependymal stem/progenitor cells and provides a tool to follow the regenerative response.

Limitations: the reporter transgene only partially reflects endogenous foxj1a (missing the middle embryonic domain and injury-induced expression), so it cannot fully track new ERGs; markers of mature ependymal differentiation were not tested, leaving open whether Foxj1a is required for ependymal specification (as in mouse brain). The trigger for injury-induced Shh upregulation and the mechanistic link to central canal positioning remain unknown. The authors highlight the interest in regeneration-specific enhancers (as reported for zebrafish heart and fin) potentially regulating Foxj1a during spinal cord regeneration.
