# Fundamental aspects of arm repair phase in two echinoderm models

## Citation (NLM)
Ferrario C, Ben Khadra Y, Czarkwiani A, Zakrzewski A, Martinez P, Colombo G, Bonasoro F, Candia Carnevali MD, Oliveri P, Sugni M. Fundamental aspects of arm repair phase in two echinoderm models. Dev Biol. 2018;433(2):297-309. doi:10.1016/j.ydbio.2017.09.035

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ydbio.2017.09.035](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ydbio.2017.09.035)

---

## Background

Regeneration restores lost body parts, and its success depends critically on the early "repair phase." In vertebrates, this phase comprises re-epithelialisation, an inflammatory/immune response, granulation tissue formation, and extracellular matrix (ECM/collagen) deposition and remodelling; impairment of any of these steps (poor re-epithelialisation, misregulated inflammation, fibrosis) limits regeneration. Most vertebrates heal wounds but cannot fully restore lost body parts, whereas some invertebrate clades — including echinoderms — display striking regenerative abilities.

Echinoderms show the maximum regenerative potential among deuterostomes, regenerating arms, internal organs, and even whole animals, making them valuable comparative models against non-regenerating mammals. Arm regeneration is well studied, but the cellular/tissue and molecular events of the repair phase had never been simultaneously and comparatively examined across echinoderm classes. This study compares repair-phase events after traumatic arm amputation in the brittle star *Amphiura filiformis* (Ophiuroidea) and the starfish *Echinaster sepositus* (Asteroidea), integrating microscopy with the first molecular expression analyses of immune- and collagen-related genes in these tissues.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Traumatic arm amputation and timed regeneration sampling: *E. sepositus* at 24 h, 72 h, 1 week p.a.; *A. filiformis* at 8, 16, 24, 48, 72 h, 5 d, 8 d, and 2–3 weeks p.a.
2. Light microscopy (LM) on semi-thin sections and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) on ultra-thin sections to describe wound closure, re-epithelialisation, cell layers, basal lamina, and collagen ultrastructure.
3. Candidate gene identification and cloning using EchinoBase/EchinoDB, the *A. filiformis* transcriptome, degenerate and specific primers, PCR/3′RACE, and cDART domain verification.
4. Genes analyzed: immune/inflammatory — fibrinogen-like *Ese-fib-like* (starfish) and ficolin *Afi-ficolin* (brittle star), both FReD-domain; collagen biosynthesis enzyme prolyl-4-hydroxylase *Ese-p4h* / *Afi-p4h*; positive controls actin and ets1/2.
5. Whole-mount in situ hybridisation (WMISH) on *A. filiformis* (followed by paraffin sectioning) and optimized paraffin-section ISH on *E. sepositus*, with positive/negative controls.

---

## Results

- **Wound closure:** Both species rapidly limit fluid loss. Starfish form a haemostatic ring/circular constriction; brittle stars instead bend the aboral and oral shields to seal cavities (reflecting their skeletal anatomy). Both use rapid coelomocyte clotting analogous to mammalian platelet clots — faster than the delayed mammalian wound contraction (peaks ~10 days in humans).
- **Re-epithelialisation:** Very rapid in both — earlier in *A. filiformis* (8–16 h) than *E. sepositus* (48–72 h), likely due to smaller arm size, versus ~4 days in mammals. New epithelium forms by elongation/migration of stump epidermal cells that retain their junctional complexes (unlike mammalian keratinocytes, which disrupt junctions to migrate); basal lamina appears only after epidermal differentiation.
- **Cellular "scar":** Beneath the new epidermis a layer of phagocytes/pigment cells forms — a syncytial phagocyte layer + oedematous (granulation-tissue-like) area in starfish (3–7 d), but a non-syncytial, compact phagocyte layer without a true oedematous area in brittle stars. This active "cellular scar" clears debris and isolates wound tissue.
- **Collagen/ECM:** Both deposit non-fibrillar collagen-like material first; organised fibrous ECM appears earlier in brittle stars than starfish, and later than re-epithelialisation — with delayed, less abundant collagen deposition and no fibrosis compared to mammals.
- **Gene expression (first descriptions):** *Ese-fib-like* is expressed in new epidermis, stump/regenerating coelomic epithelium, radial nerve cord, water canal, and coelomocytes. *Afi-ficolin* is expressed in the dermal layer of the regenerative bud and stump radial water canal. *Ese-p4h*/*Afi-p4h* mark the new epidermis and coelomic lining, consistent with roles in collagen biosynthesis.

---

## Perspective

The study shows that emergency reaction and re-epithelialisation are conserved and faster in both echinoderms than in mammals, and that both avoid fibrosis (delayed, reduced collagen), supporting the idea that an effective, non-fibrotic repair phase underlies their high subsequent regenerative capacity. It also delivers the first molecular expression data for immune-related (fibrinogen-like, ficolin) and collagen-biosynthesis (p4h) genes during echinoderm arm regeneration, and establishes an optimized paraffin-section ISH protocol for the previously intractable starfish *E. sepositus*.

Limitations include the descriptive/correlative nature of the expression data (no functional perturbation), use of different marker genes across the two species due to cloning constraints, and unresolved questions about whether regenerative-bud immune cells are newly formed or recruited. As a deuterostome comparative model of scar-free repair, the work is relevant to regeneration biology broadly (including CNS/spinal cord), where fibrosis and inflammation limit mammalian repair. Targeted molecular and functional analyses (e.g., cell tracking, loss-of-function) are proposed to illuminate the evolution of these abilities.
