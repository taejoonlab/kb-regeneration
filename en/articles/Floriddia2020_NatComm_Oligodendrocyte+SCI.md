# Distinct oligodendrocyte populations have spatial preference and different responses to spinal cord injury

## Citation (NLM)
Floriddia EM, Lourenço T, Zhang S, van Bruggen D, Hilscher MM, Kukanja P, et al. Distinct oligodendrocyte populations have spatial preference and different responses to spinal cord injury. Nat Commun. 2020;11(1):5860. doi:10.1038/s41467-020-19453-x

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-020-19453-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-020-19453-x)

---

## Background
Mature oligodendrocytes (MOLs) that produce myelin are transcriptionally heterogeneous, but the functional consequences of this heterogeneity are unclear. Prior single-cell RNA-seq (scRNAseq) had defined multiple transcriptionally distinct oligodendrocyte lineage subpopulations, yet scRNAseq-based predictions of regional enrichment can be biased by dissociation-related viability differences.

This study asked whether the spatial distribution of MOLs correlates with distinct functional states/subtypes, and whether these subtypes respond differently to disease. The authors used in situ methods to map oligodendrocyte lineage populations across the CNS and probed the developmental origin and disease responses of the two most distinct populations, MOL2 and MOL5/6.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. RNAscope in situ hybridization (ISH) and in situ sequencing (ISS) to map OL lineage populations (OPC/COP, MOL1, MOL2, MOL5/6) using population-specific markers (Sox10 pan-marker; Ptprz1 for OPC/COP; Egr2 for MOL1; Klk6/Anxa5/Hopx for MOL2; Ptgds/Car2/Grm3 for MOL5/6) in cortex, corpus callosum and dorsal spinal cord at P20 (juvenile) and P60 (adult).
2. Automated confocal image quantification (CellProfiler) and comparison with a published ALS spatial transcriptomics dataset.
3. scRNAseq of ventrally (eGFP+) vs dorsally (TdTom+) derived OL lineage cells from Emx1::Cre-Sox10::GFP-TdTom mice (P60 corpus callosum); Seurat clustering with label transfer from Marques et al.
4. In vivo lineage tracing with Pdgfrα::CreERTM-RCE(Z/EG) mice, tamoxifen at E12.5 vs P3-5, to fate-map developmental waves.
5. Conditional TFEB knockout (Olig2::Cre-TFEB) to test TFEB-dependent programmed cell death in MOL specification.
6. In vitro microfiber (3D) assay with MACS-sorted brain- vs spinal-cord-derived OPCs differentiated for 3, 7, 14 days.
7. Dorsal funiculi transection (mild traumatic SCI) with analysis at acute (14 dpi) and chronic (3 and 5 months) phases.

---

## Results
- MOL2 (Klk6+) is specifically enriched in the dorsal spinal cord and nearly absent in cortex and corpus callosum; MOL5/6 (Ptgds+) increase along the OL lineage with age in all regions and become the dominant population in adulthood.
- Within the spinal cord, MOL2 preferentially reside in white matter and along ascending sensory tracts (fasciculi gracilis/cuneatus), while MOL5/6 are enriched in gray matter and the dorsal corticospinal (motor) tract. ISS and an independent ALS spatial dataset confirmed this adjacent, distinct distribution.
- Both ventrally and dorsally derived OPCs contributed comparably to all MOL subpopulations; lineage tracing showed developmental origin (E12.5 vs P3-5) does not specify OPCs into distinct MOL populations. A subset of E12.5 pre-OPC progeny persisted into adulthood.
- Conditional TFEB deletion did not change MOL2/MOL5/6 specification in the spinal cord. In vitro microfiber cultures produced mainly NFOLs and MOL5/6 (Ptgds+), with no MOL2 (Klk6+) detected — indicating OPCs are not intrinsically programmed toward distinct MOL subtypes, implicating extrinsic environmental cues.
- After traumatic SCI: during the acute phase MOL2 and MOL5/6 contributed similarly at the injury site, but during the chronic phase MOL2 contribution decreased while MOL5/6 increased above naive levels, revealing differential susceptibility of MOL populations to injury.

---

## Perspective
The work demonstrates that transcriptionally distinct mature oligodendrocyte populations occupy specific CNS regions and functional tracts, that this spatial preference is driven by extrinsic (not intrinsic developmental) mechanisms, and that MOL subtypes differ in their vulnerability during chronic SCI. This refines the understanding of oligodendrocyte heterogeneity and suggests that targeting specific MOL subtypes could improve remyelination and repair. Limitations include reliance on marker-gene proxies for subtype identity, a mild transection injury model, and incomplete labeling in adult lineage-tracing. Future directions include identifying the extrinsic signals (e.g., neuronal activity) that specify MOL identity and testing subtype-specific interventions for CNS repair.
