# Human neuroepithelial stem cell regional specificity enables spinal cord repair through a relay circuit

## Citation (NLM)
Dell'Anno MT, Wang X, Onorati M, Li M, Talpo F, Sekine Y, Ma S, Liu F, Cafferty WBJ, Sestan N, Strittmatter SM. Human neuroepithelial stem cell regional specificity enables spinal cord repair through a relay circuit. Nat Commun. 2018;9:3419. doi:10.1038/s41467-018-05844-8

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-05844-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-05844-8)

---

## Background
Traumatic spinal cord (SC) injury causes persistent disability, largely through disconnection of surviving neural elements rather than cell loss alone. Neural stem cell (NSC) transplantation has long been considered a therapeutic option, but the optimal cell type and the mechanism by which grafts benefit recovery remain poorly defined. Prior human NSC transplants relied on partially characterized cell lines or fetal NSCs that lose self-renewal and regional identity, or on ES/iPSC-derived progenitors whose in vitro identity may diverge from in vivo states.

This study investigates human neuroepithelial stem (NES) cells derived from the developing spinal cord (SC-NES cells) at 5–8 post-conceptional weeks. These cells can be propagated as adherent monolayers for many passages while retaining their in vivo transcriptional profile and caudal (spinal) positional identity. The central question is whether regional cell identity dictates graft integration and functional recovery in a spinal cord injury model.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Derivation of SC-NES cells from six human embryonic post-mortem SC specimens (5–8 PCW), propagated as adherent monolayers; parallel derivation of neocortex-derived NES cells (NCX-NES) from age-matched tissue.
2. In vitro characterization: immunostaining for NSC markers (nestin, SOX2, vimentin, PAX6), caudal identity marker HOXB9, karyotype stability, tripotent differentiation (neurons, astrocytes, oligodendrocytes), and electrophysiology (action potentials, synaptic markers PSD-95/synaptophysin).
3. Transplantation of GFP-labeled SC-NES cells into the lesion site of immunodeficient (NOD/SCID) mice 10 days after thoracic dorsal hemisection.
4. Anatomical analysis of graft survival, differentiation, axonal outgrowth (gray vs white matter), and host-graft/graft-host synaptic connectivity (human synaptophysin, BDA-labeled corticospinal tract, 5-HT raphespinal fibers).
5. Chemogenetic silencing: transduction of grafted cells with hM4Di-DREADD and transient silencing with CNO, assessed by BMS locomotor score, grid-walking, and foot-print (gait) tests.
6. Comparison of SC-NES vs NCX-NES grafts in spinal cord and in motor cortex; global RNA-seq of grafts and in vitro cells.

---

## Results
- SC-NES cells expressed canonical NSC markers, retained HOXB9 caudal identity over >35 passages, maintained a normal karyotype, and differentiated into functional neurons (~79% RBFOX3+), astrocytes, and oligodendrocytes; ~1% were CHAT+ motor neurons in vitro.
- After grafting, SC-NES cells survived in all animals and filled the lesion cavity; ~55.6% became RBFOX3+ neurons and ~27.9% human GFAP+ astrocytes, with 14.7% CHAT+ motor neurons; regional HOXB9 identity was retained in vivo.
- Graft-derived axons elongated extensively, preferentially in white matter (~70%), spanning up to 13 segments rostral and 6 caudal (~4 cm); cells lacked NgR1, consistent with resistance to myelin-associated inhibitors. Human axons were not myelinated by host oligodendrocytes.
- Evidence of a functional relay: human synaptophysin puncta surrounded host neurons; host corticospinal (BDA+) and raphespinal (5-HT+) fibers regenerated into the graft and formed synapses.
- Modest but significant locomotor recovery (BMS) in grafted animals, most pronounced for mild-to-moderate injuries (>25% spared tissue).
- Chemogenetic silencing (hM4Di/CNO) of grafted cells reversibly abrogated the functional benefit across three behavioral paradigms (BMS, grid-walking, stride length), demonstrating that recovery depends on neural relay activity of the graft.
- Regional identity is critical: NCX-NES cells failed to integrate, differentiate, or extend axons in the SC and gave no functional recovery, yet integrated well in the motor cortex (where SC-NES cells did poorly). RNA-seq confirmed clear transcriptional separation by anatomical origin and enrichment of neurogenesis genes in SC-NES grafts.

---

## Perspective
The study provides direct mechanistic evidence that a neural stem cell graft can restore function by forming a neuronal relay reconnecting spared supraspinal inputs with denervated targets below the lesion, rather than acting solely as a scaffold or trophic source. The requirement for anatomical matching of graft regional identity to the injury site is a key translational principle for cell-based SCI therapy. Limitations include the modest magnitude of locomotor recovery, use of immunodeficient mice, lack of human axon myelination in the rodent host, presence of residual proliferating and ectopic cells, and reliance on human embryonic post-mortem tissue as a source. Future directions include improving relay efficiency, defining the molecular determinants of regional identity, and developing scalable, ethically tractable SC-NES cell sources for translation.
