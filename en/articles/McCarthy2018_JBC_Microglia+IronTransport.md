# Inflammation-induced iron transport and metabolism by brain microglia

## Citation (NLM)
McCarthy RC, Sosa JC, Gardeck AM, Baez AS, Lee CH, Wessling-Resnick M. Inflammation-induced iron transport and metabolism by brain microglia. J Biol Chem. 2018;293(20):7853-7863. doi:10.1074/jbc.RA118.001949

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1074/jbc.RA118.001949](https://doi.org/10.1074/jbc.RA118.001949)

---

## Background

Microglia are the resident immune cells of the central nervous system (CNS), and brain inflammatory status is thought to be largely determined by their actions. The brain has a high demand for iron, which serves as a cofactor in myelination, neurotransmission, and mitochondrial energy generation, but excess iron produces toxic reactive oxygen species (ROS). Both transferrin-bound iron (TBI) and non-transferrin-bound iron (NTBI) exist in cerebrospinal fluid, with NTBI at much higher levels in brain interstitial fluid.

How microglia modulate iron transport and metabolism in response to pro- and anti-inflammatory cues was unclear. This study characterized TBI and NTBI uptake by an immortalized adult murine microglial (IMG) cell line and primary mouse microglia under polarizing conditions, testing the hypothesis that microglial polarization state dictates iron transport substrate preference.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Radiolabeled iron uptake assays (55Fe-Tf for TBI; 55Fe-NTBI) in IMG cells under time-, temperature-, and pH-dependent conditions.
2. Polarization of IMG cells and primary adult mouse microglia with proinflammatory LPS (10 ng/ml) or amyloid-β, versus anti-inflammatory IL-4 (10 ng/ml).
3. qPCR and Western blot for iron-related genes/proteins (DMT1, TfR, H-/L-ferritin, ferroportin, HO-1).
4. ICP-MS quantification of steady-state intracellular 57Fe content.
5. Pharmacological (ebselen DMT1 inhibitor) and siRNA (TfR knockdown) manipulation of transport pathways.
6. Seahorse XF extracellular flux assays for glycolysis (ECAR) and mitochondrial respiration (OCR).
7. Cellular heme measurement and calcein-AM assay for the labile iron pool (LIP).

---

## Results

- IMG cells take up both TBI and NTBI in a time- and temperature-dependent manner; TBI uptake is down-regulated (via reduced TfR) when cellular iron increases.
- Proinflammatory LPS and amyloid-β up-regulated DMT1 and H-ferritin and enhanced NTBI uptake; anti-inflammatory IL-4 up-regulated TfR and enhanced TBI uptake. Same pattern confirmed in primary mouse microglia.
- LPS-induced NTBI uptake was blocked by the DMT1 inhibitor ebselen and was strongly pH-dependent; IL-4-induced TBI uptake was reversed by TfR siRNA knockdown, confirming distinct substrate-specific pathways for M1 versus M2 states.
- LPS triggered a metabolic switch: increased glycolysis and extracellular acidification (favoring pH-dependent DMT1 activity) and reduced mitochondrial respiration.
- LPS induced HO-1, decreased cellular heme, and increased the labile iron pool; the LIP increase was blocked by the HO-1 inhibitor zinc protoporphyrin, indicating heme catabolism as an intracellular iron source.
- No hepcidin induction or ferroportin changes were observed, contrasting with systemic macrophages.

---

## Perspective

The study establishes that microglial iron transport substrate preference is dictated by polarization state: proinflammatory microglia sequester both extracellular (NTBI via DMT1) and intracellular (heme-derived) iron into ferritin stores, while anti-inflammatory microglia favor TBI uptake via TfR. This suggests microglia actively modify the iron status of their microenvironment during neuroinflammation, potentially limiting oxidative damage and restricting iron availability, with relevance to Alzheimer's disease where amyloid-β elicited the same proinflammatory iron-handling response.

Limitations include reliance on an immortalized cell line (though key findings were confirmed in primary microglia), the simplified M1/M2 framework, and the in vitro setting. Future directions include defining iron chaperone roles (e.g., poly(rC)-binding proteins), iron trafficking to subcellular compartments, and how microglial iron handling influences neurodegeneration in vivo.
