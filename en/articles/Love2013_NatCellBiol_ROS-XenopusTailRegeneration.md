# Amputation-induced reactive oxygen species are required for successful Xenopus tadpole tail regeneration

## Citation (NLM)
Love NR, Chen Y, Ishibashi S, Kritsiligkou P, Lea R, Koh Y, Gallop JL, Dorey K, Amaya E. Amputation-induced reactive oxygen species are required for successful Xenopus tadpole tail regeneration. Nat Cell Biol. 2013;15(2):222-228. doi:10.1038/ncb2659

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/ncb2659](https://doi.org/10.1038/ncb2659)

---

## Background

Xenopus laevis and X. tropicalis tadpoles can regenerate their tails — including the spinal cord, muscle, and other axial tissues — after amputation, through coordinated growth-factor signaling pathways (Wnt, Fgf, Bmp, Notch, TGF-β). However, little was known about the upstream events that trigger these pathways following injury. A prior microarray screen had revealed coordinated upregulation of genes involved in production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and H2O2 during regeneration.

Because ROS/H2O2, once viewed as merely harmful, are now recognized to have pleiotropic signaling roles, the authors set out to determine whether ROS are produced during tail regeneration and whether they are functionally required, examining the upstream mechanism that links injury to regenerative signaling.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. In vivo ROS imaging using transgenic X. laevis expressing the ratiometric H2O2 sensor HyPerYFP (490 nm/402 nm excitation ratio), plus fluorogenic dyes (CM-H2DCFDA, dihydroethidium) and a pH control probe (pHluorin).
2. Tail amputation of NF stage 41–47 tadpoles and time-course imaging of H2O2 during regeneration.
3. Testing dependence on inflammatory cells: RFP-labeling of inflammatory cells and spib morpholino knockdown to deplete primitive myeloid cells.
4. Pharmacological ROS reduction with NADPH oxidase (NOX) inhibitors diphenyleneiodonium (DPI) and apocynin (APO), and the antioxidant/free-radical scavenger MCI-186 (edaravone); wash-out experiments to test timing of the regenerative window.
5. Genetic knockdown of cyba (p22phox, a NOX subunit) via antisense morpholino, with a morpholino-insensitive myc-cyba rescue construct.
6. Assessment of cell proliferation (phospho-histone H3 mitotic index), Wnt/β-catenin signaling (X. tropicalis TOP:dsGFP reporter line), and fgf20 expression (in situ hybridization, RT-PCR); fgf20 morpholino knockdown.

---

## Results

- Tail amputation induced a marked, sustained increase in intracellular H2O2 that remained elevated throughout the multi-day regeneration process (~50–200 µM), confirmed by multiple probes and not attributable to a pH artifact.
- The ROS increase peaked within 1 hour post-amputation, before inflammatory cell recruitment (~2 hpa), and was unchanged in spib morphants lacking inflammatory cells — indicating ROS are produced largely by non-inflammatory wound-resident cells.
- Lowering ROS with DPI, APO, or MCI-186 impaired tail regeneration (shorter regenerated tails). DPI/APO treatment during the first 3 days permanently precluded regeneration even after wash-out, whereas the weaker scavenger MCI-186 only delayed it — defining a critical early NOX-dependent window.
- Morpholino knockdown of cyba reduced amputation-induced ROS by ~33% and decreased regenerative bud formation; both were rescued by MO-resistant myc-cyba, implicating the NOX–Cyba complex as an enzymatic ROS source required for regeneration.
- ROS inhibition reduced cell proliferation (pH3 mitotic index) at 36 hpa.
- Sustained ROS were required for Wnt/β-catenin signaling: ROS inhibitors markedly reduced TOP:dsGFP reporter activity; nucleoredoxin (nxn), a redox-sensitive Wnt regulator, was expressed during regeneration.
- fgf20, a direct Wnt/β-catenin target and the most upregulated fgf during regeneration, was reduced by ROS inhibitors; fgf20 morphants healed the wound but failed to regenerate axial tissues, confirming fgf20 is required downstream.

---

## Perspective

This study established injury-induced, NOX/Cyba-generated ROS (H2O2) as an essential upstream regulator that couples amputation to the regenerative program in Xenopus tadpole tails, acting through Wnt/β-catenin signaling and its target fgf20 to drive cell proliferation and axial-tissue (including spinal cord) regeneration. Because Wnt signaling is central to regeneration across phyla (Hydra to mammals), sustained ROS may represent a general injury-sensing mechanism for activating Wnt-dependent regeneration, and manipulating ROS could offer a route to inducing regeneration where it is normally limited. Limitations include the inability to perform exogenous H2O2 rescue (systemic H2O2 was toxic), potential off-target effects of pharmacological inhibitors (mitigated by using low doses and genetic knockdown), the transient nature of morpholino knockdown, and the correlative link (via nxn) between ROS and Wnt that was not directly dissected.
