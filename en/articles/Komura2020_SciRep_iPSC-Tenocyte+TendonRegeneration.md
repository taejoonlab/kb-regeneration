# Induced pluripotent stem cell-derived tenocyte-like cells promote the regeneration of injured tendons in mice

## Citation (NLM)

Komura S, Satake T, Goto A, Aoki H, Shibata H, Ito K, Hirakawa A, Yamada Y, Akiyama H. Induced pluripotent stem cell-derived tenocyte-like cells promote the regeneration of injured tendons in mice. _Sci Rep._ 2020 Mar 4;10(1):3992. doi: [10.1038/s41598-020-61063-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-020-61063-6). PMID: 32132573.

---

## Background

Tendons are dense fibrous connective tissues that connect muscle to bone, and tendon injuries and tendinopathy due to overuse or age-related degeneration account for approximately 30% of musculoskeletal disorders. Tendon tissue has low regenerative potential due to hypocellularity and hypovascularity, and tendon injury in adult mice heals with scar tissue by myofibroblasts rather than normal tendon tissue. Scar tissue has lower tensile strength than normal tendon, making physiological healing of tendon disorders in adult patients a significant medical challenge. iPSCs can differentiate into various cell types, but tenocyte differentiation protocols have been limited in efficiency measurement and in vivo therapeutic validation. This study used a tendon-specific Scleraxis (Scx) promoter-based EGFP reporter mouse to induce tenocyte-like cells from iPSCs and demonstrated therapeutic effects on injured tendon regeneration.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

**1. Scx-EGFP knock-in mouse development**

- Inserted IRES-EGFP-pA-rox-PGK-EM7-BsdR-pA-rox cassette downstream of the Scx gene stop codon using the Red/ET BAC recombination system
- Electroporated into V6.5 ESCs, confirmed correct targeting by Southern blot
- Generated chimeric mice and removed the drug selection cassette by crossing with Dre recombinase
- Confirmed tendon- and ligament-specific EGFP expression in homozygous Scx-EGFP mice

**2. iPSC establishment from Scx-EGFP fibroblasts**

- Reprogrammed ear-tip fibroblasts with retroviruses encoding OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, and MYCL
- Established 3 iPSC clones (SGH #3-1, #4-1, #4-2)
- Generated 6 mCherry-expressing iPSC lines by electroporation with CAG promoter-driven mCherry construct (SGH 313, 316, 318, 421, 426, 427)
- Confirmed pluripotency gene (Nanog, Oct3/4, Sox2) expression and teratoma formation

**3. iPSC-to-tenocyte differentiation protocol**

- Day -3: iPSC culture in LIF medium
- Day 0: Embryoid body (EB) formation
- Day 2: Wnt3a (25 ng/mL) + Activin A (9 ng/mL) treatment for mesendoderm induction
- Day 3: bFGF (10 ng/mL) treatment for paraxial mesoderm induction
- Day 5: Replated EBs on collagen-coated plates, induced tenocyte differentiation with bFGF (10 ng/mL) + TGF-β1 (10 ng/mL)
- Day 20: FACS sorting of EGFP-positive cells

**4. Differentiated cell characterization**

- Real-time qPCR for tenocyte-specific genes (Scx, Mkx, Tnmd, Col1a1, Col3a1, Fmod) and pluripotency genes
- Immunocytochemistry for Tenomodulin (Tnmd) expression

**5. In vivo tendon regeneration assessment**

- 2 mm transection of bilateral Achilles tendons in NOD-SCID mice
- Left side: FACS-sorted EGFP+ cells (4×10⁴ cells) + atelocollagen transplantation
- Right side (control): atelocollagen alone
- Histological evaluation at 4 weeks (HE, Safranin O/Fast Green, Masson trichrome)
- Histological scoring: fiber structure, fiber alignment, nuclear roundness, cell density, inflammation, angiogenesis (0–3 points)
- αSMA/Tnmd double staining for myofibroblast quantification
- bFGF immunohistochemistry and RT-PCR for Fgf2 expression analysis

## Results

**Tendon-specific EGFP expression in Scx-EGFP mice** Bright EGFP signals were observed in tendons and ligaments such as the Achilles tendon and plantar fascia in homozygous Scx-EGFP mice. Neonatal (7-day) tendon injury showed physiological healing after 1 month (with EGFP signal), while adult (4-month) tendon injury showed chondrometaplasia at the tendon ends and scar healing by αSMA+ myofibroblasts (no EGFP signal).

**iPSC establishment and characterization** SGH iPSC lines showed Nanog, Oct3/4, and Sox2 expression levels equivalent to ES cells, exogenous 4-factor expression was silenced, and subcutaneous transplantation formed three-germ-layer teratomas, confirming pluripotency.

**Tenocyte differentiation efficiency** At day 20, the FACS-sorted EGFP+ cell ratio was 6.3% (4.1–10.8%) in SGH 313 and 14.3% (10.3–18.0%) in SGH 427, with an overall average of 10.9% (0.7% at day 0). EGFP+ cells showed significant increases in tenocyte-specific transcription factors (Scx, Mkx) and extracellular matrix genes (Tnmd, Col1a1, Col3a1, Fmod), while pluripotency genes decreased. Tenocyte differentiation was also confirmed in mouse ESCs using the same protocol.

**In vivo tendon regeneration promotion** The EGFP+ cell transplantation group showed significantly better histological scores than the control group in fiber structure, fiber alignment, nuclear roundness, and cell density (p<0.01). mCherry+ transplanted cells were present at an average of only 4.7% in the regenerated tissue and co-expressed EGFP and Tnmd.

**Paracrine mechanism-mediated scar reduction** The proportion of Tnmd+/αSMA+ cells (tenocytes) in regenerated tendons showed no difference between groups, but Tnmd-/αSMA+ cells (myofibroblasts) were significantly reduced in the cell transplantation group, indicating suppressed scar formation. iPSC-derived tenocyte-like cells expressed Fgf2, and bFGF expression was higher in the regenerated tendons of the cell transplantation group than in controls. This suggests that **bFGF-mediated paracrine effects** suppress myofibroblast activation and scar formation, promoting tendon regeneration.

---

## Perspective

This study is an important work that developed an iPSC-derived tenocyte differentiation protocol using the Scx-EGFP reporter system and demonstrated in vivo tendon regeneration promotion effects through a paracrine mechanism.

First, the differentiation efficiency measurement using the Scx-EGFP reporter (average 10.9%) enabled quantitative evaluation of the tenocyte differentiation protocol. Compared to previous studies using Col1a1-GFP reporter osteoblast differentiation (45%) and Col2-GFP reporter chondrocyte differentiation (10.3%), tenocyte differentiation efficiency has room for improvement.

Second, it demonstrated that **bFGF-mediated paracrine effects**, rather than direct ECM production by transplanted cells, are the primary mechanism of tendon regeneration. This provides important insights into the mechanism of action of cell therapy and suggests the therapeutic potential of factors that suppress scar formation (bFGF, HGF, etc.).

Third, it confirmed that adult tendon injury progresses to scar healing, reaffirming the need for regenerative therapy in adult tendon disorders. Understanding the difference between the physiological healing capacity of neonatal tendons and the scar healing mechanism of adult tendons is a future research direction.

Future directions include improving differentiation efficiency (current 11% → target >40%), enhancing regeneration effects through combination with tissue engineering scaffolds (decellularized ECM, aligned nanofibers, etc.), extending the protocol to human iPSCs, and evaluating long-term transplantation safety (tumorigenicity risk) and functional recovery (tensile strength).
