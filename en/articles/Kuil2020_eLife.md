# Zebrafish macrophage developmental arrest underlies depletion of microglia and reveals Csf1r-independent metaphocytes

## Citation (NLM)
Kuil LE, Oosterhof N, Ferrero G, Mikulášová T, Hason M, Dekker J, Rovira M, van der Linde HC, van Strien PMH, de Pater E, Schaaf G, Bindels EMJ, Wittamer V, van Ham TJ. Zebrafish macrophage developmental arrest underlies depletion of microglia and reveals Csf1r-independent metaphocytes. eLife. 2020;9:e53403. doi:10.7554/eLife.53403

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.53403](https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.53403)

---

## Background

Tissue-resident macrophages (TRMs), including brain microglia, arise from successive waves of hematopoiesis and shape organ development and homeostasis. Most macrophages depend on colony-stimulating factor 1 receptor (CSF1R) for their development, yet some TRM populations persist in its absence, and it has remained unclear why only specific populations are lost. CSF1R mutations in humans cause severe brain disease (leukodystrophy) associated with lower microglia density, and CSF1R inhibition is used clinically to deplete macrophages in disease. Zebrafish are transparent and genetically tractable, enabling in vivo analysis of immune cell development.

This study used a zebrafish line deficient for both csf1ra and csf1rb paralogs (csf1rDM), which recapitulates the microglia loss and osteopetrosis seen in mice, rats, and humans, to determine how and when loss of Csf1r affects macrophage development throughout life.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. In vivo confocal/time-lapse imaging of mpeg1:GFP-labeled macrophages in control and csf1rDM zebrafish embryos and larvae to quantify numbers, migration, and proliferation.
2. Bulk RNA sequencing of FACS-isolated mpeg1:GFP+ embryonic macrophages at 28 and 50 hpf; PCA, GSEA, and comparison to published macrophage signatures (Tang et al., 2017).
3. Pcna/L-plastin immunostaining and EdU pulse labeling to assess microglial proliferation.
4. Longitudinal in vivo imaging of skin mpeg1+ cells from 8 to 24 dpf, correlating cell number with age and body size; morphological classification (branched vs. amoeboid).
5. Genetic lineage tracing with Tg(kdrl:Cre; ßactin2:loxP-STOP-loxP-DsRed) to distinguish hematopoietic macrophages (GFP+DsRed+) from non-hematopoietic metaphocytes (GFP+DsRed-).
6. Bulk RNA-seq of FAC-sorted skin GFP+DsRed+ and GFP+DsRed- cells and of whole-fish mpeg1+ cells from control, csf1rDM, and il34-/- juveniles; flow cytometry of adult organs (brain, liver, gut).

---

## Results

- Primitive macrophages emerge normally in csf1rDM embryos (Csf1r dispensable for emergence), but after ~48 hpf they fail to proliferate and show reduced migration/tissue colonization.
- RNA-seq showed csf1rDM embryonic macrophages retain a core macrophage gene expression profile (only ~4-5% of macrophage signature genes differentially expressed) but downregulate DNA replication and cell-cycle genes, consistent with a proliferative arrest.
- Microglia in csf1rDM larvae were sparse and non-proliferative (no Pcna+ or EdU+ microglia); macrophage numbers stayed >4-fold lower than controls through 9 dpf.
- From ~15 dpf, mpeg1+ cells increased in the skin of both genotypes, but csf1rDM skin contained only amoeboid cells, lacking the branched putative Langerhans cells (also lost in il34-/- fish).
- Lineage tracing and transcriptomics identified the remaining amoeboid skin cells as metaphocytes — non-hematopoietic (GFP+DsRed-), ectoderm-derived, expressing epithelial/metaphocyte genes (cdh1, epcam, cldnh) with reduced phagocytosis genes.
- Juvenile/adult csf1rDM fish showed systemic depletion of mononuclear phagocytes; metaphocytes were absent from the CNS (brain lacked GFP+DsRed- cells), indicating microglia loss is not compensated by metaphocytes.

---

## Perspective

The study resolves part of the presumed macrophage heterogeneity and their differential sensitivity to Csf1r loss: Csf1r is required for proliferative expansion and tissue colonization of definitive macrophages (including microglia), while ectoderm-derived metaphocytes develop independently of Csf1r. Csf1r-deficient zebrafish are viable and largely macrophage-free (but retain metaphocytes), providing an unprecedented tool to dissect the distinct roles of macrophages versus metaphocytes throughout life and to model consequences of CSF1R-inhibitor therapies. Limitations include the caveat that primitive mesoderm-derived macrophages are also unlabeled in the fate-mapping model, and the work is descriptive of developmental dynamics rather than functional rescue. Future directions include exploiting this model to study macrophage-loss phenotypes and neurodegeneration.
