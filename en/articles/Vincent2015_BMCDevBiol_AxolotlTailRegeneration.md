# Cellular dynamics underlying regeneration of appropriate segment number during axolotl tail regeneration

## Citation (NLM)
Vincent CD, Rost F, Masselink W, Brusch L, Tanaka EM. Cellular dynamics underlying regeneration of appropriate segment number during axolotl tail regeneration. BMC Dev Biol. 2015;15:48. doi:10.1186/s12861-015-0098-1

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1186/s12861-015-0098-1](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12861-015-0098-1)

---

## Background
Salamanders regenerate their tails after amputation at any level along the length, faithfully reconstituting the original number of segments (myotomes) and overall length. How this size and segment-number control is achieved during regeneration—rather than during embryonic development—remains poorly understood. Prior studies of salamander limb and tail regeneration had measured elongation rates or regeneration stages but had not integrated blastema size, cell-cycle kinetics, and myotome differentiation, nor estimated the size and spatial extent of the progenitor pool recruited into the blastema.

This study provides quantitative cellular measurements of tails regenerating from two different amputation planes (proximal vs. distal) to build a framework for modeling growth control during tail regeneration and to test whether growth kinetics depend on the position of the cut.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Amputation of axolotl tails at the 4th (proximal) or 16th (distal) myotome posterior to the cloaca, using 3 cm "white" mutant larvae carrying the CAGGS:GFP transgene to visualize myotome organization.
2. Live scoring of myotome number over time by dissecting microscope to measure onset and rate of myotome formation.
3. Measurement of blastema dimensions (length L1/L2, height H1/H2) over time from fluorescent/brightfield images across two independent experiments.
4. Cumulative BrdU incorporation (Nowakowski method) at day 10 and day 21 to derive cell-cycle length (TC), S-phase duration (TS), and growth fraction (GF) of mesenchymal blastema cells (excluding epidermis, spinal cord, cartilage).
5. Total cell counts on Hoechst-stained cryosections and a mathematical model to estimate the founding progenitor pool size and the spatial extent (distance behind amputation plane) of the recruited zone.

---

## Results
- Onset of myotome organization occurred at the same time (~day 15 post-amputation) regardless of amputation level, but the maximum rate was faster proximally (3.5 ± 0.2 vs. 1.9 ± 0.2 new myotomes/day for proximal vs. distal, days 15–19).
- At day 15, proximal blastemas were significantly larger (length 7.9 ± 1.0 mm vs. 5.0 ± 0.8 mm); by day 21, undifferentiated blastema dimensions were equivalent between levels and matched the distance from last myotome to tip in uncut growing tails.
- Early (day 10) cell-cycle parameters were indistinguishable between proximal and distal blastemas: growth fraction ~95–97%, cell-cycle length ~103–112 h. Nearly all mesenchymal cells were cycling.
- By day 21, growth fraction diverged sharply—69 ± 10% (proximal) vs. 36 ± 3% (distal)—while cell-cycle length remained similar (119 vs. 84 h). The proximal advantage is due to a larger fraction of cells remaining in cycle, not faster division.
- Modeling estimated founding progenitor pools of ~12,552 (proximal) vs. ~7,485 (distal) cells; the proximal amputation plane had a larger cross-sectional area (794 vs. 376 cells). The activation zone extended ~350–440 µm behind the amputation plane in both cases—position-independent and matching the ~500 µm spinal-cord progenitor zone reported previously.
- A two-phase growth model is proposed: (1) a level-independent phase of unrestrained proliferation for ~2 weeks; (2) a level-specific phase where differing fractions of cells remain cycling, dictating final regenerate length.

---

## Perspective
This is the first study to integrate blastema size, myotome differentiation, and cell-cycle kinetics for tail regeneration at two amputation levels, providing quantitative constraints for models of regenerative size control. The finding that the progenitor activation zone (~400 µm) is position-independent and shared between muscle and spinal cord suggests a universal, injury-induced activation signal. The biphasic model separates an early position-independent proliferation phase from a later position-dependent growth-sustaining signal along the anterior–posterior axis.

Limitations include analysis restricted to total mesenchymal cells (myogenic precursors could not be separately marked alongside BrdU), reliance on simplifying assumptions in the cell-cycle model, and unresolved molecular identity of the position-dependent signal. The authors suggest heterotopic/heterochronic transplantation and testing of WNT/FGF (anti-differentiative) and retinoic acid (pro-differentiative) signaling as future directions to explain the level-specific cell-cycle differences.
