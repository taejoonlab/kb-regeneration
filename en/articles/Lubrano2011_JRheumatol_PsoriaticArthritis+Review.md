# Psoriatic arthritis

## Citation (NLM)

FitzGerald O, Ogdie A, Chandran V, Coates LC, Kavanaugh A, Tillett W, Leung YY, deWit M, Scher JU, Mease PJ. Psoriatic arthritis. _Nat Rev Dis Primers._ 2021 Aug 19;7(1):59. doi: [10.1038/s41572-021-00293-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41572-021-00293-y). PMID: 34413300.

---

## Background

Psoriasis is a complex inflammatory disease accompanied by skin and nail psoriasis, and approximately 30% of psoriasis patients develop psoriatic arthritis (PsA). PsA exhibits inflammatory musculoskeletal features in joints, entheses, and the spine, and is typically negative for rheumatoid factor (RF) and anti-citrullinated peptide (anti-CCP) antibodies. The pathogenesis of PsA is considered a result of interactions between environmental factors, genetic predisposition, and adaptive and innate immune systems, with genetic differences including the HLA region explaining disease heterogeneity. The importance of the IL-17/IL-23 pathway has been demonstrated by genetics and immunopathology studies, and biological agents targeting this pathway are effective for both skin and musculoskeletal symptoms. This Primer comprehensively discusses the epidemiology, pathogenesis, challenges in diagnosis, screening, and prevention of PsA, current treatment approaches, impact on quality of life, and unmet needs and future directions.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

**1. Epidemiological and genomic analysis**

- Estimated PsA prevalence and incidence through large cohorts and meta-analyses (applying CASPAR criteria)
- Identified PsA-associated genes through genome-wide association studies (GWAS) and HLA typing
- Analyzed HLA class I alleles (HLA-C*06:02, HLA-B*27, HLA-B*08, etc.) and amino acid positions (Glu45, position 97)

**2. Immunopathology studies**

- Analyzed CD8+ T cell clonal expansion in PsA synovial fluid and tissue
- Profiled TH1/TH17 cells, TC1/TC17 cells, and cytokines (TNF, IL-17, IL-23)
- Analyzed circulation of skin-derived tissue resident memory CCR10+CD8+ T cells

**3. Imaging diagnostics**

- Evaluated synovitis, enthesitis, and spinal/sacroiliac joint inflammation using X-ray, ultrasound, and MRI
- Assessed diagnostic utility of imaging findings in early disease

**4. Screening tool evaluation**

- Compared sensitivity and specificity of PsA screening questionnaires such as PEST and CONTEST
- Evaluated application of screening tools in dermatology and primary care settings

**5. Clinical trials and treatment efficacy analysis**

- Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) of NSAIDs, csDMARDs (methotrexate, etc.), bDMARDs (TNF, IL-17, IL-23 inhibitors), and tsDMARDs (JAK inhibitors)
- Treat-to-target and head-to-head comparison trials

## Results

**Epidemiology** PsA prevalence is 0.10–0.25% in the general adult population and 23.8% in psoriasis patients by CASPAR criteria. Peak prevalence occurs between ages 30–60, with equal occurrence in men and women. Lower prevalence in Asia compared to Europe and North America suggests racial/environmental differences. Pediatric PsA diagnosis is complex using ILAR classification criteria, and ~50% of cases have arthritis preceding psoriasis.

**Genetic factors** The HLA region shows the strongest association. HLA-C*06:02 is found in ~60% of psoriasis patients but only 28% in PsA. HLA-B*27 is positive in 18% of PsA patients. Glutamine at amino acid position 45 of HLA-B (Glu45) is most significantly associated with PsA risk. Non-HLA genes (IL12B, TRAF3IP2, IL23R, PTPN22, RUNX3, etc.) have also been identified as PsA-specific loci.

**Immune mediators** PsA is characterized by TNF, IL-17, and IL-23 cytokine cascades. IL-17-expressing CD8+ T cells predominate in the synovium. There is a strong association between HLA class I alleles and TH17 immune responses, and antigen-presenting cells interact with innate lymphoid cells and naive T cells to drive local clonal expansion of TH1/TH17 and TC1/TC17 cells.

**Environmental factors** Musculoskeletal trauma (particularly entheseal microtrauma), obesity, and infections (streptococcal, HIV) are risk factors for PsA development. Obesity may induce entheseal inflammation through increased mechanical load and secretion of inflammatory mediators from adipose tissue. The role of the gut microbiome requires further study.

**Diagnosis and classification** Diagnostic delay (median 29 weeks in the UK) is associated with functional disability and radiographic damage. CASPAR classification criteria have high sensitivity and specificity but lower sensitivity in early disease. Following the Moll and Wright criteria, CASPAR (2006) is widely used and includes psoriasis, nail dystrophy, negative RF, synovitis, enthesitis, dactylitis, and radiographic new bone formation.

**Treatment** NSAIDs are used for symptom relief but have limited PsA-specific evidence. Methotrexate is first-line for peripheral arthritis but has limited efficacy for axial disease and enthesitis. TNF inhibitors (infliximab, adalimumab, etanercept, etc.) are effective for peripheral and axial arthritis, enthesitis, dactylitis, and skin psoriasis. IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab, ixekizumab) and IL-23 inhibitors (guselkumab, risankizumab) are also effective for musculoskeletal and skin symptoms. JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib) have also been approved. A treat-to-target strategy (remission or minimal disease activity) is recommended.

**Comorbidities** PsA is associated with cardiovascular disease (hypertension, dyslipidemia, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome), obesity, depression and anxiety (10–30%), fibromyalgia (~30%), uveitis (~3%), and inflammatory bowel disease (~3%). Obesity negatively affects treatment response.

## Perspective

This Primer is an authoritative review that comprehensively summarizes current knowledge and clinical challenges in PsA, with the following significance.

First, it emphasizes that PsA is not a single disease but a heterogeneous disease group in which genetic, environmental, and immune factors interact complexly. Amino acid-level association analysis such as HLA-B Glu45 enables more precise risk prediction than HLA alleles alone, providing a foundation for precision medicine approaches.

Second, the importance of the IL-17/IL-23 pathway has been triply demonstrated through genetics, immunopathology, and clinical trials, firmly establishing the rationale for targeted therapy in PsA treatment. However, since not all patients respond to cytokine inhibition, understanding molecular pathways associated with specific disease features to guide treatment selection remains a future challenge.

Third, since diagnostic delay has a major impact on functional and radiographic prognosis, the use of screening tools (PEST questionnaire) in dermatology and primary care and early intervention strategies are important. Defining the progression stage from psoriasis to PsA (Figure 1) and developing predictive models for intervention in the preclinical stage are underway.

Fourth, since PsA comorbidities (cardiovascular, metabolic, mental health) have a major impact on quality of life and lifespan, multidisciplinary integrated management is essential.

Future directions include elucidating the precise molecular mechanisms of PsA pathogenesis, developing early biomarkers, identifying alternative targets in non-responding patients, expanding pediatric PsA drug approvals, and clarifying the causal relationship between the gut microbiome and PsA.
