# Long-Term Safety Issues of iPSC-Based Cell Therapy in a Spinal Cord Injury Model: Oncogenic Transformation with Epithelial-Mesenchymal Transition

## Citation (NLM)
Nori S, Okada Y, Nishimura S, Sasaki T, Itakura G, Kobayashi Y, Renault-Mihara F, Shimizu A, Koya I, Yoshida R, Kudoh J, Koike M, Uchiyama Y, Ikeda E, Toyama Y, Nakamura M, Okano H. Long-Term Safety Issues of iPSC-Based Cell Therapy in a Spinal Cord Injury Model: Oncogenic Transformation with Epithelial-Mesenchymal Transition. Stem Cell Reports. 2015;4(3):360-373. doi:10.1016/j.stemcr.2015.01.006

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.stemcr.2015.01.006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.stemcr.2015.01.006)

---

## Background

Transplantation of neural stem/progenitor cells (NS/PCs) has produced beneficial effects and functional recovery in animal models of spinal cord injury (SCI). Pluripotent stem cells, including embryonic stem cells (ESCs) and induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs), can be differentiated into NS/PCs, oligodendrocyte precursors, and motoneuron progenitors, offering a promising cell source for SCI treatment. iPSCs raise fewer ethical concerns than ESCs, but tumorigenicity remains a major obstacle to their clinical application.

The authors had previously reported the safety and therapeutic potential of neurospheres (NSs) derived from the human iPSC clone 201B7 in a NOD-SCID mouse SCI model. Here they characterized a different iPSC clone, 253G1, established from the same donor's adult human dermal fibroblasts but reprogrammed with only three factors (OCT4, SOX2, KLF4) without c-MYC. Because c-MYC was omitted, 253G1-NSs were initially expected to be less tumorigenic. The study extended long-term post-transplant observation to test this expectation and to investigate the mechanisms of any tumor formation.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **Cell culture and lentiviral labeling:** Neurospheres were derived from human iPSC clone 253G1 and transduced with lentivirus expressing Venus (a yellow fluorescent protein) or ffLuc (Venus fused to firefly luciferase) for in vivo tracking.
2. **Animal SCI model and transplantation:** Adult female NOD-SCID mice received contusive SCI at the Th10 level with an IH impactor (60 kdyn) after laminectomy. Nine days later, 5 x 10^5 253G1-NS-derived cells (or PBS as control) were injected into the lesion epicenter.
3. **Motor function assessment:** Basso Mouse Scale (BMS) score, Rotarod test, and DigiGait treadmill gait analysis, performed by blinded observers up to 103 days post-transplantation.
4. **Bioluminescence imaging:** Xenogen-IVIS system tracking of ffLuc photon counts to monitor grafted cell survival and growth over 103 days.
5. **Histology and immunohistochemistry:** H&E staining and immunostaining for neural lineage markers (NeuN, bIII tubulin, GFAP, APC), Nestin, Ki-67, OCT4, NANOG, synaptic markers (Bassoon, human-specific synaptophysin), and phosphorylated STAT3/ERK1/2/AKT; immuno-electron microscopy for synapse formation.
6. **RT-PCR:** Expression of transgene (Tg) versus endogenous (Endo) OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, and c-MYC in 253G1 cells, 253G1-NSs, and 103-day post-transplant tissue; and EMT genes (SNAI1, SNAI2, TWIST1, TWIST2).
7. **Transcriptome analysis (mRNA-seq):** Human and mouse reads separated with Xenome, mapped with TopHat, followed by hierarchical clustering, PCA, Gene Ontology (GO) analysis, and Ingenuity Pathway Analysis (IPA), comparing 253G1-NS and 201B7-NS grafts at 5 and 103 days.

---

## Results

- **Survival and differentiation (47 days):** Grafted 253G1-NSs survived, migrated into the host cord, and differentiated into three neural lineages: NeuN+ (17.2%) and bIII tubulin+ (42.2%) neurons, GFAP+ astrocytes (15.0%), and APC+ oligodendrocytes (2.7%). About 67% of mature neurons were GAD67+ GABAergic; TH+ and ChAT+ neurons were rare.
- **Synapse formation:** Graft-derived neurons formed synaptic connections with host neurons, confirmed by human-specific synaptophysin apposition to host ChAT+ motor neurons and by immuno-electron microscopy showing bidirectional pre- and post-synaptic structures.
- **Early functional recovery:** The 253G1-NS group showed significantly better recovery than PBS controls by BMS score (from 12 days), Rotarod time (61.1 vs 33.0 s), and stride length (4.2 vs 2.2 cm) at 47 days.
- **Late deterioration and tumor formation:** After 47 days, motor function gradually declined. Photon counts dropped in the first week, then rose from day 14, sharply increasing between days 42-70, reaching more than 10-fold the initial value by 103 days. Histology revealed tumors, classified as small (<200 um), medium (200-700 um), and large (>700 um).
- **Tumor composition:** Tumors consisted of HNu/Nestin double-positive undifferentiated human cells forming microcystic masses (present in all large tumors), but were NANOG-negative (not pluripotent teratomas). By 103 days the Nestin+ fraction rose to 34.2% and Ki-67+ proliferating cells increased, while bIII tubulin+ neurons decreased; ~87.5% of cells still committed to neural lineages.
- **Transgene reactivation:** OCT4-Tg expression was significantly higher in 103-day grafts than in pre-transplant 253G1-NSs, and OCT4+/HNu+ cell proportion increased with post-transplant duration (independent of tumor diameter). KLF4-Tg was also elevated. In contrast, the earlier study found 201B7-NS grafts were non-tumorigenic with no OCT4+ cells.
- **EMT and pathway signatures:** mRNA-seq clustered samples by time rather than clone, but 253G1 and 201B7 profiles diverged at 103 days. GO analysis of 692 genes uniquely upregulated in 253G1 grafts highlighted mesenchymal cell differentiation/development and epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT). EMT-related genes (LEF1, BMP2, HGF, SNAI1, SNAI2, TWIST1, TWIST2) were upregulated; SNAI2, TWIST1, TWIST2 were higher in 253G1-NSs than 201B7-NSs even before transplantation. p-STAT3+ cells (but not p-ERK1/2 or p-AKT) were detected in tumors. IPA identified 56 significantly altered pathways, including upregulated human ESC pluripotency and Wnt/beta-catenin signaling in 253G1 grafts.

---

## Perspective

Contrary to the initial expectation that omitting c-MYC would reduce tumorigenicity, the 253G1-NS clone conferred only temporary functional recovery before slow-growing tumors developed and worsened outcomes. This underscores that short-term efficacy does not guarantee safety and that long-term observation is essential for evaluating iPSC-derived cell therapies, since slow-growing tumors can degrade function over extended periods.

Mechanistically, the study links tumor formation to late-onset reactivation of the OCT4 and KLF4 transgenes, expansion of differentiation-resistant Nestin+ proliferating cells, and a heightened EMT program with STAT3 activation and Wnt/beta-catenin pathway upregulation. Because the iPSCs were generated by retroviral (integrating) reprogramming, transgene reactivation was possible; the authors argue that integration-free iPSCs should be used clinically to avoid transgene-induced tumorigenesis. As a next step, they had already begun integration-free iPSC-NS transplantation in the NOD-SCID SCI model, emphasizing transplantation into immunodeficient animals with long-term follow-up to establish safety and efficacy prior to clinical translation.
