# Secreted inhibitors drive the loss of regeneration competence in Xenopus limbs

## Citation (NLM)
Aztekin C, Hiscock TW, Gurdon JB, Jullien J, Marioni JC, Simons BD. Secreted inhibitors drive the loss of regeneration competence in Xenopus limbs. bioRxiv. 2020 Jun 2. doi:10.1101/2020.06.01.127654

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.06.01.127654](https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.06.01.127654)

---

## Background
Amphibian limb regeneration depends on a specialised wound epidermis (the apical-epithelial-cap, AEC), whose absence is hypothesised to block regeneration in higher vertebrates including mammals. The AEC is considered analogous to the apical-ectodermal-ridge (AER) of limb development, as both express Fgf8 at distal tips and support proximal-distal outgrowth of underlying mesoderm to build a proliferative blastema.

Xenopus laevis tadpoles progressively lose limb regeneration ability during development, coinciding with an inability to form a specialised wound epidermis. Amputations are regeneration-competent at NF stage ~52-54 (full limb), regeneration-restricted at NF ~55-57 (missing digits), and regeneration-incompetent from NF ~58 (wound healing or unpatterned spike). Regeneration also declines with more proximal amputations, where more mature chondrogenic/osteogenic cells reside. This study set out to define the cellular and molecular determinants of wound-epidermis formation and why it fails.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Single-cell RNA-seq (10X Genomics v3) of developing intact hindlimbs (NF ~52, ~54, ~56) and of amputated limbs plus contralateral controls at 5 days post-amputation (dpa) for regeneration-competent, -restricted, and -incompetent stages; 42,348 cells total, cell-cycle corrected, ~60 clusters (interactive atlas provided).
2. Annotation of AER cells (Fgf8+ epidermal cells) and comparison of AER cell abundance across regeneration conditions; comparison with tail regeneration-organising cells (ROCs).
3. Ex vivo regenerating limb explant culture to assay AER (Fgf8) cell formation at distal vs proximal sites and to test signalling requirements with small-molecule inhibitors (FGF/SU5402, BMP/LDN193189, WNT/ICRT3, NOTCH/DAPT) and TGF-β.
4. EdU incorporation assay to test whether AER cells form via cell division; HCR in situ hybridisation for Lgr5.S / Fgf8.L trajectory in basal epidermis.
5. Co-culture and conditioned-media experiments between competent and incompetent explants, with anti-NOGGIN antibody blockade; NOGGIN and BMP4 addition experiments.
6. FGF10 bead / recombinant FGF10 treatment and FGFR inhibition to test effects on chondrogenesis and AER formation, including FGF10 + anti-NOGGIN combination.

---

## Results
- AER cells (Fgf8+ epidermal cells) share a transcriptomic and morphological signature during development and post-amputation regeneration; their abundance on the amputation plane strongly correlates with regeneration outcome (abundant in competent, limited in restricted, largely absent in incompetent tadpoles).
- Injury-induced mesenchymal plasticity (a fibroblast subset up-regulating distal progenitor genes such as Grem1, Shh, Msx1, Fgf10 and chondrogenic Sox9) tracks with AER cell abundance.
- AER cell formation requires activation of FGF, BMP, WNT, TGF-β, and NOTCH signalling; inhibition of any of these blocks Fgf8 formation in explants.
- Only ~40% of new AER cells were EdU-positive, indicating most form without cell division; a step-wise Lgr5.S then Fgf8.L trajectory allows basal epidermal cells to convert directly into AER cells.
- Secreted factors from regeneration-incompetent explants block AER cell formation in competent explants (co-culture and conditioned media); this inhibition is rescued by anti-NOGGIN antibody. Chondrogenic lineage cells (enriched at incompetent stages) express high NOGGIN and other BMP/WNT antagonists.
- FGF10 suppresses chondrogenesis and acts upstream of NOGGIN; FGF10 alone was insufficient to induce AER at the proximal site, but FGF10 combined with anti-NOGGIN induced ectopic Fgf8 near the proximal site. BMP4 boosts chondrogenesis and blocks AER formation.

---

## Perspective
This work provides a single-cell framework unifying disparate findings in limb regeneration, proposing that chondrogenic progression drives loss of regeneration competence by accumulating secreted inhibitory cues (notably NOGGIN) that antagonise wound-epidermis (AER) formation, downstream of FGF10. It suggests that manipulating the extracellular environment or chondrogenesis could help restore regeneration potential in non-regenerative higher vertebrates. Limitations noted by the authors include additional barriers to mammalian regeneration (scarring, more complex immune responses) and open questions about how regeneration-competent salamanders evade such inhibitory cues (e.g., mesenchymal vs epidermal enrichment of AER signals). As a preprint, the findings await peer review.
