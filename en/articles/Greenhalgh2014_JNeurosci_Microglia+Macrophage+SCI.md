# Differences in the Phagocytic Response of Microglia and Peripheral Macrophages after Spinal Cord Injury and Its Effects on Cell Death

## Citation (NLM)
Greenhalgh AD, David S. Differences in the phagocytic response of microglia and peripheral macrophages after spinal cord injury and its effects on cell death. J Neurosci. 2014;34(18):6316-6322. doi:10.1523/JNEUROSCI.4912-13.2014

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.4912-13.2014](https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.4912-13.2014)

---

## Background
After spinal cord injury (SCI), macrophages at the injury site arise from two ontogenetically distinct populations: resident microglia and infiltrating, peripherally derived monocytes. Because the two cell types share immunohistochemical markers and are morphologically indistinguishable, their individual contributions to phagocytosis of degenerating tissue and their fate after injury have been difficult to determine. Phagocytic clearance of debris is important for recovery, but a prolonged/excessive inflammatory response can be detrimental.

This study uses the lysozyme M EGFP-knockin (lys-EGFP-ki) mouse — which expresses EGFP in mature myelomonocytic cells (hematogenous macrophages) but not in microglia — to distinguish the two populations and to compare their phagocytic response, debris clearance, viability, and proliferation after SCI.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. **lys-EGFP-ki mouse model** to tag infiltrating peripheral macrophages (EGFP+) versus resident microglia (Iba1+/EGFP-negative).
2. **Moderate spinal cord contusion** (Infinite Horizon Impactor, 50 kdyn, T11); animals analyzed at 24 h and 3, 7, 14, 28, 42 d.
3. **Axonal tracing:** Fluoro-Ruby injected into the sciatic nerve 7 d before SCI to label ascending dorsal-column axons; quantification of microglia/macrophage contacts with labeled degenerating axons (cells >15 µm to exclude neutrophils).
4. **Immunofluorescence/confocal microscopy:** anti-GFP, Iba1, CD11b, complement C1q, Ki67; autofluorescence (>570 nm) used to identify phagocytosed (lipofuscin-like) material.
5. **In vitro myelin phagocytosis:** bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) vs primary microglia incubated with DiI-labeled or unlabeled CNS myelin (50, 200 µg/ml).
6. **Flow cytometry** (CD45, AnnexinV, viability dye) to quantify apoptotic and end-stage apoptotic/necrotic death.
7. **In vivo TUNEL** to identify apoptotic macrophage/microglia populations; **Ki67 labeling** to quantify proliferation.

---

## Results
- **Early phagocytosis by microglia:** Microglia are the predominant cell contacting and engulfing Fluoro-Ruby-labeled damaged axons at 1 d after SCI and the main cell containing phagocytic material at 3 d. By 3 d peripheral macrophages contact axons at double the frequency of microglia, and by 7 d peripheral macrophages predominate.
- **Complement C1q** localized to damaged axons and associated microglia/macrophages at days 3 and 7, suggesting a role in axon clearance.
- **Differential persistence of debris:** At 3 d, ~50% of microglia contained autofluorescent phagocytic material vs only ~12% of infiltrating macrophages. Thereafter microglial autofluorescence dropped sharply (2-9% at days 7-42), whereas macrophage debris persisted at high levels (up to 47% at day 14; still ~30% at day 42). Weakly EGFP+ debris-laden cells at late times appeared to be dying.
- **Peripheral macrophages more susceptible to death after myelin phagocytosis (in vitro):** BMDMs showed a dose-dependent increase in apoptosis (8.7-fold at 24 h with 200 µg/ml myelin) and end-stage apoptosis/necrosis (5.3-fold), while primary microglia showed much less apoptosis and no necrosis. ~17% of peripheral macrophages died at 24 h with 200 µg/ml myelin.
- **In vivo apoptosis is macrophage-specific:** All TUNEL-positive cells at days 3, 5, 7 were EGFP+ peripheral macrophages; microglia were not apoptotic.
- **Microglia proliferate more:** Ki67+ labeling showed up to ~7-fold greater proliferation of microglia than infiltrating macrophages at days 3, 5, 7 in the region of active phagocytosis.

---

## Perspective
The work shows that resident microglia and peripherally derived macrophages play temporally and functionally distinct roles after SCI. Microglia are the first responders, efficiently processing phagocytosed debris and remaining viable (aided by higher proliferation that may dilute phagocytic load per cell). Infiltrating macrophages dominate later phagocytosis but handle CNS/myelin debris poorly, accumulating lipofuscin-like material and dying in situ — potentially contributing to secondary damage through release of proteases and other harmful agents. This distinction has therapeutic implications for selectively targeting the detrimental peripheral macrophage response while preserving beneficial microglial functions. Limitations include reliance on autofluorescence as a debris proxy, relatively low absolute numbers of TUNEL-positive cells, and the caveat that dying microglia (no longer identifiable) could partly explain their loss of debris signal; the in vitro myelin model may not fully recapitulate the in vivo lesion environment.
