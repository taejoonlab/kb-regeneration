# Development of an in vitro fibrotic scar model of spinal cord injury using macromolecular crowding

## Citation (NLM)
Nemati S, Stanley A, Kilcoyne M, Zeugolis D, McMahon SS. Development of an in vitro fibrotic scar model of spinal cord injury using macromolecular crowding. J Neurosci Methods. 2025;424:110601. doi:10.1016/j.jneumeth.2025.110601

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jneumeth.2025.110601](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jneumeth.2025.110601)

---

## Background

Spinal cord injury (SCI) triggers a cascade of cellular and molecular events leading to permanent tissue damage and functional impairment. Two distinct scars form at the lesion site: an astrocytic (glial) scar composed of proliferating astrocytes, and a fibrotic scar characterized by extracellular matrix (ECM)-secreting fibroblasts. The fibrotic scar begins in the subacute phase, matures during the chronic phase, and acts as a barrier to axonal regeneration; it consists of ECM proteins including collagen I, collagen IV, fibronectin, and laminin. While glial scarring has been studied extensively, fibrotic scar dynamics have received less attention, and a major obstacle to developing anti-fibrotic compounds is the lack of a comprehensive in vitro screening system.

Conventional cell culture in aqueous media differs markedly from the naturally "crowded" tissue environment, so ECM protein deposition (e.g., proteolytic conversion of procollagen to collagen) proceeds slowly. Macromolecular crowding (MMC) — the addition of inert macromolecular crowders to culture medium — accelerates ECM deposition via a volume-exclusion effect and has been used to build fibrotic models for dermal, lung, and vocal fold scarring. This study is the first to apply MMC to primary leptomeningeal (LPG) cells, which have fibroblast-like characteristics, to develop an in vitro model of fibrotic scarring after SCI.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **Primary LPG cell isolation:** Leptomeninges stripped from spinal cords of postnatal day 4 (P4) Sprague Dawley rat pups, trypsin-digested, and cultured in DMEM/F-12 + 10% FBS until confluent (~10 days).
2. **MMC treatment:** A cocktail of Ficoll 70 kDa (37.5 mg/ml) and Ficoll 400 kDa (25 mg/ml) (FC) plus L-ascorbic acid 2-phosphate added to passage-1 cells seeded at 25,000 cells/cm²; medium refreshed every 2 days; assessed on days 5 and 8.
3. **Physical injury:** Scratch assay (SCR) using a P200 pipette tip to disrupt the confluent monolayer.
4. **Chemical injury:** Pro-inflammatory cytokine cocktail (CYT) of TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6 (10 ng/ml each); LPS (100 ng/ml) as a positive inflammatory control.
5. **Metabolic activity:** Alamar blue assay (days 5 and 8).
6. **Nitrite / inflammation:** Griess colourimetric assay (day 5).
7. **ECM protein detection:** Immunocytochemistry for fibronectin, collagen IV, collagen I, and laminin; fluorescence intensity per cell quantified in ImageJ (integrated density / nuclei).
8. **Cytoskeleton / morphology:** Phalloidin (F-actin) staining with fractal dimension and lacunarity analysis (FracLac, ImageJ).
9. **Statistics:** One-way and two-way ANOVA with Dunnett's / Bonferroni's post-hoc tests (n = 3, triplicate).

---

## Results

- **Viability unaffected by FC:** Growth and morphology of LPG cells were unchanged across injury conditions with/without FC on days 5 and 8. On day 5, metabolic activity was lower in injury groups (SCR, LPS, CYT ± FC) versus control, but FC alone showed no difference; by day 8 no group differed from control.
- **Inflammatory response:** Nitrite production rose significantly (to ~5 μM) with LPS and CYT treatment, confirming a robust inflammatory response; FC alone and scratch injury did not increase nitrite.
- **Migration / morphology preserved:** Phalloidin staining showed no changes in actin cytoskeleton organization/density across conditions; fractal dimension and lacunarity were largely unchanged (except a slight complexity change in the SCR group), indicating FC did not alter migration potential.
- **FC promotes ECM deposition:** All FC-treated groups (FC alone, SCR+FC, LPS+FC, CYT+FC) significantly increased fibronectin, collagen IV, collagen I, and laminin deposition compared with their non-FC counterparts on both days 5 and 8; FC alone often showed the highest increase. Notably, inflammatory injury (LPS/CYT) alone raised nitrite but did not substantially increase ECM deposition without FC.

---

## Perspective

This is the first demonstration that macromolecular crowding of primary leptomeningeal cell cultures can accelerate deposition of the key fibrotic-scar ECM proteins (collagen I/IV, fibronectin, laminin), providing a simplified, more physiologically "crowded" in vitro model of the SCI fibrotic scar suitable for higher-throughput anti-fibrotic drug screening. The dissociation between inflammatory signaling (nitrite) and ECM deposition suggests LPG cells may require additional cues (MMC, TGF-β, or mechanical stimuli) to robustly upregulate matrix synthesis.

Limitations acknowledged by the authors: LPG cells do not represent all fibroblast populations implicated in vivo (e.g., pericyte- or perivascular-derived fibroblasts); only two time points (days 5 and 8) were examined, which may not capture delayed matrix remodeling; and the 2D system does not reproduce the full multicellular lesion environment. Future directions include integrating the model into drug-screening platforms and extending toward 3D/co-culture configurations.
