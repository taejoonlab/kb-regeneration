# Forkhead Transcription Factor FoxM1 Regulates Mitotic Entry and Prevents Spindle Defects in Cerebellar Granule Neuron Precursors

## Citation (NLM)
Schüller U, Zhao Q, Godinho SA, Heine VM, Medema RH, Pellman D, Rowitch DH. Forkhead transcription factor FoxM1 regulates mitotic entry and prevents spindle defects in cerebellar granule neuron precursors. Mol Cell Biol. 2007;27(23):8259-8270. doi:10.1128/MCB.00707-07

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1128/MCB.00707-07](https://doi.org/10.1128/MCB.00707-07)

---

## Background

Cerebellar granule neuron precursors (CGNP) form in rhombomere 1 of the embryonic neural tube, migrate dorsally, and undergo a second phase of postnatal proliferation in the external granular layer (EGL), where their expansion depends on Sonic hedgehog (Shh) signaling. Shh acts through Patched/Smoothened to upregulate targets including the Gli proteins, N-myc, and cell-cycle cyclins. The forkhead transcription factor FoxM1 (Trident/HFH-11/WIN) had been implicated as a transcriptional target of Hedgehog signaling and is required for proliferation in myocardium, liver and lung, but its role in the CNS and specifically in the Shh-dependent cerebellum had not been characterized.

This study defines the specific functions of FoxM1 during Shh-induced neuroproliferation in CGNP, asking whether FoxM1 is required for overall Shh-driven proliferation, for mitotic entry, and/or for mitotic spindle formation during the G2/M transition.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. FoxM1 expression analysis during cerebellar development by immunohistochemistry and in situ hybridization (E13.5 through P15), compared with Math1 and cyclin D1.
2. Western blotting of cultured CGNP treated with recombinant Shh to test FoxM1 responsiveness and timing versus direct targets (N-myc).
3. Analysis of FoxM1 induction in Ptc+/− cerebellar tumors and in Wnt1/GAL4 UAS-Shh mice (ectopic Shh in dorsal neural tube/spinal cord).
4. Loss-of-function genetics: conventional FoxM1 null mice, and conditional FoxM1Fl/Fl mice crossed with Math1-cre (CGNP-specific) or Nestin-cre (broad neural precursor) drivers.
5. BrdU incorporation assays (± Shh) and retroviral Cre-IRES-GFP or FoxM1-IRES-GFP transduction to test S-phase requirements and sufficiency.
6. Cell-cycle analysis by flow cytometry and phospho-histone H3 immunolabeling (in vivo and in vitro) to assess G2/M transition; Western blot / in situ for cyclin A2, B1, B2 and Cdc25b.
7. Immunofluorescence for β-tubulin (spindle) and γ-tubulin (centrosome) to score spindle defects and centrosome amplification in mitotic CGNP.

---

## Results

- FoxM1 is expressed in proliferating granule cell precursors of the EGL (peak at P7, co-expressed with Math1 and cyclin D1) and is downregulated as cells differentiate (undetectable by P15).
- Shh upregulated FoxM1 protein in CGNP only at 24 h (unlike direct targets such as N-myc at ~3 h), indicating FoxM1 is an indirect Shh target; FoxM1 was also elevated in Ptc+/− tumors and ectopic Shh (Wnt1-Shh) tissue.
- FoxM1 was dispensable for overall cerebellar morphogenesis: conventional null (dying near birth from cardiac defects), Math1-cre and Nestin-cre conditional knockouts had grossly normal cerebella; Shh targets Gli1, N-myc and Math1 were unaffected, and apoptosis was unchanged.
- FoxM1-deficient CGNP retained a normal S-phase (BrdU) response to Shh, and FoxM1 overexpression did not increase BrdU uptake — FoxM1 is neither necessary nor sufficient for S-phase regulation.
- FoxM1 loss delayed the G2/M transition: significantly elevated phospho-histone H3-positive cells and accumulation in late G2, with decreased cyclin B1 and Cdc25b protein levels.
- FoxM1-deficient CGNP showed mitotic spindle defects (~12.3% abnormal spindles in prometaphase/metaphase) and centrosome amplification (13.2% vs 5.2%), consistent with roles downstream of cyclin B1.

---

## Perspective

FoxM1 functions in Shh-induced neuroproliferation are restricted to the G2/M transition in CGNP, acting most likely through transcriptional control of B-type cyclins (cyclin B1) and Cdc25b, while G1/S progression and overall proliferation rates remain intact through FoxM1-independent (compensatory) pathways. The persistence of normal proliferation despite spindle abnormalities and aneuploidy parallels many cancers, and given that medulloblastoma (thought to arise from granule cell precursors) expresses high FoxM1, the findings suggest potential roles for FoxM1 in cerebellar tumorigenesis. Limitations include the relatively low frequency of mitotic defects (implying redundancy), unresolved mechanistic links between delayed mitotic entry and spindle/centrosome defects, and reliance on mouse conditional genetics. Future work is needed to define compensatory pathways and the contribution of FoxM1 to medulloblastoma. This work is relevant to the CNS knowledgebase as a study of Shh-dependent neural precursor proliferation and cell-cycle control in CNS development.
