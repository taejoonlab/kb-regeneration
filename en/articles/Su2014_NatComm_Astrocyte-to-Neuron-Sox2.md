# In vivo conversion of astrocytes to neurons in the injured adult spinal cord

## Citation (NLM)
Su Z, Niu W, Liu ML, Zou Y, Zhang CL. In vivo conversion of astrocytes to neurons in the injured adult spinal cord. Nat Commun. 2014;5:3338. doi:10.1038/ncomms4338

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/ncomms4338](https://doi.org/10.1038/ncomms4338)

---

## Background

Spinal cord injury (SCI) causes irreversible neuronal loss, disruption of ascending and descending tracts, and glial scar formation, leading to persistent motor and sensory dysfunction. Unlike certain neurogenic regions of the adult brain, the adult spinal cord essentially lacks the ability to produce new neurons. Cell transplantation (including iPSC- or NSC-derived cells) has been the most studied therapeutic strategy, but faces major hurdles: long cell-preparation times that exceed the therapeutic window, risk of tumour formation from residual undifferentiated cells, and secondary injury from the transplantation procedure itself.

As an alternative, the authors explore reprogramming endogenous non-neuronal cells—specifically scar-forming astrocytes, which are abundant throughout the spinal cord and proliferate after injury—directly into neurons in situ. Building on prior work showing that forced expression of transcription factors can respecify cell fate (e.g., pancreatic exocrine cells to β-cells, cardiac fibroblasts to cardiomyocytes, and brain astrocytes to neurons), they test whether resident spinal astrocytes can be converted to neurons in the adult spinal cord.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Lentiviral gene delivery driven by the human GFAP (hGFAP) promoter to target astrocytes in the adult mouse spinal cord (thoracic level T8); GFP or GFP-T2A-SOX2 constructs used to trace transduced cells.
2. Screening of 12 candidate neurogenic/NSC transcription factors (SOX2, PAX6, NKX6.1, NGN2, ASCL1, OLIG2, SOX11, Tlx, OCT4, c-MYC, KLF4, PTF1a) for induction of doublecortin (DCX)+ neuroblasts.
3. Immunohistochemistry for cell-type markers (GFAP, NeuN, OLIG2, NG2, MBP, PLP, IBA1, DCX, TUBB3, MAP2, NeuN, GABA, vGLUT1, GAD65, ChAT, synapsin-1).
4. Testing in both mild (stab wound) and severe (T8 hemisection) injury models.
5. Genetic lineage tracing to establish cellular origin: Ng2-Cre;Rosa-YFP and mGfap-Cre;Rosa-tdTomato mouse lines.
6. Cell transplantation of purified tdT+ astrocytes (infected with control or SOX2 virus) into immunodeficient NSG mouse spinal cords.
7. BrdU and Ki67 labelling to assess proliferation during conversion.
8. In vitro neurosphere assays to test SOX2-induced multipotent NSC generation from spinal astrocytes.
9. Treatment with the histone deacetylase inhibitor valproic acid (VPA) to test enhancement of neuronal maturation.
10. Comparison of hGFAP-driven versus constitutive CAG-driven SOX2 expression to test the role of SOX2 expression dynamics.

---

## Results

- The hGFAP-lentiviral system predominantly targeted astrocytes (~95% GFAP+), with negligible transduction of mature oligodendrocytes, microglia, or neurons.
- Among 12 candidates, only SOX2 induced DCX+ neuroblasts in the adult spinal cord (6–8% of GFP+ cells at 4–5 wpi); DCX+ cells were also induced in aged mice (>12 months).
- Neurogenesis was also induced by SOX2 under a clinically relevant severe hemisection injury (3–6% of GFP+ cells became DCX+ between 4–8 wpi).
- Genetic lineage tracing (mGfap-Cre;Rosa-tdT) confirmed that induced DCX+ and TUBB3+ neurons originate from resident astrocytes, not from NG2+ cells (Ng2-Cre;Rosa-YFP showed no colabelling).
- Transplantation of SOX2-infected spinal astrocytes into NSG mice yielded TUBB3+ and later MAP2+ neurons, confirming astrocyte-to-neuron conversion in vivo.
- ~90% of induced DCX+ cells incorporated BrdU and ~17% were Ki67+, indicating the conversion passes through a proliferative phase.
- In vitro, SOX2 converted spinal astrocytes into multipotent nestin+ neurospheres that could differentiate into neurons, astrocytes, and oligodendrocyte precursors—contrasting with the direct in vivo conversion to neuroblasts, highlighting the influence of the cellular milieu.
- Reprogramming required dynamic SOX2 expression: initial high expression followed by downregulation (hGFAP-driven) was necessary; constitutive CAG-driven SOX2 failed to produce DCX+ cells. SOXB1 factors SOX1 and SOX3 were expressed during conversion.
- Induced neurons matured (MAP2+/NeuN+ by ~8 wpi); VPA nearly doubled/tripled the number of mature and newly generated neurons; neurons survived up to 30 wpi (210 dpi).
- The reprogrammed neurons were synapse-forming GABAergic (and some vGLUT1+) interneurons (not ChAT+ motor neurons); synapsin-1+ terminals from induced neurons were juxtaposed to local ChAT+ motor neurons.

---

## Perspective

This study provides proof of principle that a single transcription factor, SOX2, is sufficient to reprogram endogenous scar-forming astrocytes into proliferative neuroblasts and, ultimately, synapse-forming GABAergic interneurons in the adult spinal cord, with or without severe injury. In situ reprogramming circumvents key hurdles of cell transplantation (preparation time, tumour risk, secondary injury) and turns the detrimental glial scar into a potential source for neuronal replacement.

Limitations noted in the text include the slow kinetics of conversion (neuroblasts not detectable until 4 wpi, mature neurons until 8 wpi), the relatively low conversion efficiency (a few percent of targeted cells), and that induced neurons are interneurons rather than the motor neurons often lost after SCI. The dependence on dynamic SOX2 expression and enhancement by VPA point to the importance of the epigenetic/cellular environment. Future directions include improving efficiency, directing subtype specification, and demonstrating functional recovery, toward endogenous cell-based repair strategies for SCI.
