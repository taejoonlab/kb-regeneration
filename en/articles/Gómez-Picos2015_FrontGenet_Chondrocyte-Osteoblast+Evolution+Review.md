# On the evolutionary relationship between chondrocytes and osteoblasts

## Citation (NLM)
Gómez-Picos P, Eames BF. On the evolutionary relationship between chondrocytes and osteoblasts. Front Genet. 2015;6:297. doi:10.3389/fgene.2015.00297

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.3389/fgene.2015.00297](https://doi.org/10.3389/fgene.2015.00297)

---

## Background
Vertebrates are the only animals that produce bone, but the molecular genetic basis for this evolutionary novelty remains obscure. This review synthesizes information from traditional evolutionary and modern molecular genetic studies to generate a working hypothesis on the evolution of the gene regulatory network (GRN) underlying bone formation. Since transcription factors are often core components of GRNs (kernels), the analysis focuses on Sox9 and Runx2. The argument centers on three skeletal tissues comprising the majority of the vertebrate skeleton: immature cartilage, mature cartilage, and bone. The fossil record, comparative anatomy, and embryology demonstrate clearly that immature cartilage evolved before mature cartilage or bone. Emphasizing molecular genetic evidence on Sox9-Runx2 GRN interactions, we hypothesize that the Runx2 GRN for bone formation was co-opted from mature cartilage.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Fossil record analysis (Haikouella 530 Mya, conodonts 515 Mya, pteraspidomorphi 480 Mya, Palaeospondylus gunni 385 Mya)
2. Comparative anatomical analysis of cartilage taxonomic distribution (from invertebrates to vertebrates)
3. Sox9 and Runx2 loss-of-function and gain-of-function experiments (ectopic expression)
4. Mechanistic analysis of Sox9 direct binding to Runx2, inhibiting transcriptional activity and promoting ubiquitin-mediated degradation
5. Transcriptomic analysis of Sox9 and Runx2 loss-of-function cells
6. Comparative transcriptomics (RNAseq) to compare molecular fingerprints across vertebrate clades

## Results
1. The fossil record, comparative anatomy, and comparative embryology all support that immature cartilage evolved before mature cartilage and bone
2. The Sox9 GRN regulates immature cartilage formation, the Runx2 GRN drives bone formation, and the combination of Sox9 and Runx2 GRNs produces mature cartilage
3. The Sox9 GRN is partially dominant over the Runx2 GRN; Sox9 directly binds Runx2, inhibits its transcriptional activity, and increases ubiquitin-mediated degradation
4. Sox9 overexpression can inhibit Runx2 expression, and Sox9 must be down-regulated for the full Runx2-dependent cartilage maturation program to proceed
5. Mature cartilage and bone share genes not expressed in immature cartilage (Sp7/Osterix, Mmp13, Ihh, Alpl, Spp1, Sparc, Bglap, etc.)
6. Gene expression overlap between mature cartilage and bone is higher in actinopterygians (teleosts) than in sarcopterygians (tetrapods)
7. Cell lineage analyses show that some cells sequentially express immature cartilage genes → mature cartilage genes → bone genes, transitioning from immature chondrocyte → mature chondrocyte → osteoblast

## Perspective
This review proposes the hypothesis that the Runx2 GRN for bone formation evolved gradually from mature cartilage and was subsequently co-opted by a non-chondrogenic mesenchymal cell population. This gradualism model is more parsimonious than the saltationism model assuming de novo emergence of the bone GRN. Comparative transcriptomics (RNAseq) provides a powerful tool to directly test this hypothesis by quantitatively comparing molecular fingerprints of chondrocytes and osteoblasts across vertebrate clades. Whether the divergent model (higher overlap in early-diverged clades) or the convergent model (higher overlap in later-diverged clades) is supported will provide insight into the evolutionary origins of bone. Future research should focus on isolating pure cell populations in vivo to quantitatively evaluate constraint and adaptation of GRN kernels.
