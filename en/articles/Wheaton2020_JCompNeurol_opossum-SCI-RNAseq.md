# Identification of regenerative processes in neonatal spinal cord injury in the opossum (Monodelphis domestica): A transcriptomic study

## Citation (NLM)
Wheaton BJ, Sena J, Sundararajan A, Umale P, Schilkey F, Miller RD. Identification of regenerative processes in neonatal spinal cord injury in the opossum (Monodelphis domestica): A transcriptomic study. J Comp Neurol. 2021;529(5):969-986. doi:10.1002/cne.24994

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1002/cne.24994](https://doi.org/10.1002/cne.24994)

---

## Background
Injury to the adult mammalian spinal cord yields poor recovery because severed axons fail to regenerate. In contrast, the immature nervous system retains substantial regenerative capacity. Marsupials such as the gray short-tailed opossum (Monodelphis domestica) are born very immature (comparable to an E13 rodent), and for a short postnatal window (~1-3 weeks) complete spinal transection is followed by spontaneous axon outgrowth across the injury and supraspinally mediated functional recovery. This regenerative potential diminishes with maturity until it is lost, making the opossum a rare mammalian model of intrinsic spinal cord regeneration.

The mechanisms underlying this age-dependent regeneration remain unknown. Prior studies (cAMP, annexin-1, ubiquitin/proteomics, and short-term ~1 day RNA analyses) hinted at complex, age-dependent responses but did not examine the time course of gene regulation in a regenerating mammalian cord. This study uses RNA sequencing to compare regenerating (injured at P7) and non-regenerating (injured at P28) cords over multiple post-injury time points.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Complete spinal cord transections in opossum pups at postnatal Day 7 (P7SCI, regenerating) or Day 28 (P28SCI, non-regenerating).
2. Cord tissue collected +1, +3, and +7 days after transection, with age-matched controls.
3. RNA sequencing and differential gene expression analysis (significance: log2FC >=1, Padj <=0.05); alignment/quantification with GSNAP, TopHat/Cufflinks, HTSeq; DE with edgeR, DESeq, Cuffdiff.
4. Gene Ontology overrepresentation analysis (PANTHER) of injury gene-sets.
5. Cell-type-association analysis to attribute injury-associated transcripts to specific CNS cell types (microglia, endothelial, astrocyte, oligodendrocyte, neuron).

---

## Results
- Across all time points, 233 genes changed expression after P7SCI (regenerating) versus 472 genes after P28SCI (non-regenerating); 147 genes overlapped (63% of the P7SCI set). Most changes were upregulations.
- The non-regenerating (P28) cord showed a more complex, multifaceted transcriptomic response than the regenerating (P7) cord.
- GO overrepresentation in P7SCI gene-sets was restricted to immune-associated categories, whereas P28SCI gene-sets showed those same immune categories plus additional categories including "cell proliferation," "cell adhesion," and "apoptosis."
- Cell-type-association analysis indicated that, regardless of injury age, injury-associated transcripts were most strongly associated with microglia and endothelial cells, with markedly fewer astrocyte-, oligodendrocyte-, and neuron-related genes.
- A notable exception was a cluster of mostly downregulated oligodendrocyte-associated genes specific to the P7SCI +7d gene-set.
- The greater breadth of responses in non-regenerating cords is interpreted as reflecting the relative maturity of systems including the adaptive immune system, myelination, and apoptosis.

---

## Perspective
This study provides the largest transcriptomic survey to date of the age-dependent regenerative response to complete spinal transection in a mammalian model, and it captures the post-injury time course rather than a single early snapshot. The finding that regenerating cords mount a simpler, predominantly immune-focused response while non-regenerating cords engage broader proliferation/adhesion/apoptosis programs suggests that non-neuronal cells (microglia, endothelium) and maturation of the immune/myelination/apoptosis machinery strongly shape regenerative outcome. The gene lists offer a resource for identifying candidate drivers of successful spinal cord regeneration. Limitations include the descriptive/correlative nature of transcriptomics (no functional perturbation), reliance on GO-level interpretation, and the challenge of annotating the opossum genome. Future functional testing of candidate genes could clarify which processes are causally required for regrowth.
