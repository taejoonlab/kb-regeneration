# Evolutionary Selection and Constraint on Human Knee Chondrocyte Regulation Impacts Osteoarthritis Risk

## Citation (NLM)
Richard D, Liu Z, Cao J, Kiapour AM, Willen J, Yarlagadda S, Jagoda E, Kolachalama VB, Sieker JT, Chang GH, Muthuirulan P, Young M, Masson A, Konrad J, Hosseinzadeh S, Maridas DE, Rosen V, Krawetz R, Roach N, Capellini TD. Evolutionary Selection and Constraint on Human Knee Chondrocyte Regulation Impacts Osteoarthritis Risk. *Cell*. 2020 Apr 16;181(2):362-381.e28. doi:10.1016/j.cell.2020.02.057

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2020.02.057](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2020.02.057)

---

## Background

Human bipedal gait required adaptive changes in the anatomical structures of the hip and knee joints. Natural selection shaped the unique human knee joint morphology through regulatory elements controlling developmental programs in knee chondrocytes. These adaptive processes may also carry negative health consequences. Currently, over 250 million people suffer from osteoarthritis (OA), and OA risk variants are enriched in noncoding sequences near chondrocyte genes. This study aimed to analyze how ancient natural selection, recent genetic drift, and constraint have influenced knee regulatory elements through epigenetic profiling of knee chondrocytes, and to investigate how these connect to OA risk from an evolutionary developmental biology perspective.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **ATAC-seq chromatin accessibility profiling**: Open chromatin regions of E15.5 mouse and stage-matched E59 human long bone chondrocytes were profiled by ATAC-seq. Sets of distal femur (DF), proximal tibia (PT), and knee common (KC) specific elements were established. Knee specificity was validated by comparison with brain and forelimb joint (elbow) elements.

2. **Evolutionary conservation and selection analysis**: Sequence conservation was assessed using phyloP scores from 20 primate species. Overlap analysis with human accelerated regions (HARs) detected ancient natural selection signals. Human–chimpanzee base identity (%ID) and GREAT analyses were performed.

3. **Transcription factor binding motif analysis**: The impact of human–chimpanzee base substitutions on predicted transcription factor motifs (FOXP1/FOXP2, KLF5, CTCF, etc.) was analyzed and validated with ChIP-seq data overlap.

4. **Intra-species genetic diversity analysis**: Human diversity was analyzed using 1000 Genomes Project data and compared with chimpanzee and gorilla diversity. Knee morphological variation was assessed in OA patient cohorts (OAI).

5. **OA risk variant analysis**: Overlap analysis of 95 lead variants from 83 OA GWAS loci with knee regulatory elements was performed. LD score regression evaluated contribution to OA heritability (h²g).

6. **FST and recent selection analysis**: FST analysis across 18 populations assessed genetic drift. Overlap of positive selection signals from the last 30,000 years with OA variants was examined.

7. **GDF5 locus functional validation**: Mouse R4 enhancer lacZ reporter lines were generated. R4 knockout mice and rs6060369 humanized replacement mice were produced for morphological analysis (μCT), OARSI histological OA scoring, and allele-specific expression (ASE) analysis.

8. **In vitro functional analysis**: CRISPR was used to delete the R4 enhancer and its 41 bp subregion (containing rs6060369) in human T/C-28a2 articular chondrocyte cell lines. Luciferase reporter assays measured transcriptional activity differences of the risk "T" allele. PITX1 ChIP validation was performed.

---

## Results

**Mapping knee chondrocyte-specific regulatory elements**: ATAC-seq of mouse and human knee chondrocytes established sets of DF-specific, PT-specific, and KC-specific regulatory elements. Minimal overlap with elbow elements confirmed knee specificity. These elements significantly overlapped with biological signals related to chondrocytes and skeletal development.

**Evidence of ancient positive selection**: Knee-specific elements significantly overlapped with human accelerated regions (HARs), suggesting ancient positive selection acted on knee regulatory elements. GREAT analysis identified genes associated with ossification, skeletal phenotypes, and limb development expression.

**Human-specific sequence constraint patterns**: In humans, DF- and PT-specific elements showed significantly lower genetic diversity than genomic background and promoter/intron elements. PT elements in particular exhibited stronger constraint than DF elements, a human-specific pattern not observed in chimpanzees or gorillas. Correspondingly, morphological variation in the human proximal tibia was significantly lower than in the distal femur.

**Transcription factor motif conservation and change**: Human–chimpanzee substitutions were biased toward preserving FOXP1/FOXP2 motifs while altering KLF5 and CTCF motifs, suggesting that some components of the knee regulatory network maintain ancient constraints while others were modified in the human lineage.

**Link between OA risk variants and knee regulatory elements**: Variants from 83 OA GWAS loci were significantly enriched in knee-specific elements. LDSC analysis showed that long bone chondrocyte elements explained a substantial fraction of OA heritability. Notably, FOXP1/FOXP2 motifs were protected from human–chimpanzee substitutions, yet OA risk variants were biased toward disrupting these motifs.

**Analysis of recent evolutionary forces**: Variation within knee elements did not overlap with signals of recent positive selection (within 30,000 years) but instead showed depletion, suggesting that genetic drift is the predominant recent evolutionary force. The OAI patient cohort showed significantly higher burden of deleterious alternative alleles within knee regulatory elements compared to controls.

**Functional validation of the GDF5 locus**: The R4 enhancer (containing rs6060369) showed activity in developing and adult knee joint chondrocytes in lacZ reporter mice. R4 knockout mice exhibited altered knee joint morphology at P30, including reduced femoral condyle curvature radius and notch size, and developed OA at 1 year with significantly increased OARSI scores. Humanized rs6060369-T substitution mice showed morphological changes in the same direction, with ASE analysis revealing significantly decreased Gdf5 expression (82.3% of control, p = 0.0005). ChIP confirmed PITX1 transcription factor binding to the rs6060369 site, and luciferase assays demonstrated that the risk "T" allele reduces expression.

---

## Perspective

This study is a comprehensive analysis systematically elucidating the relationship between the evolutionary developmental origins of the human knee joint and OA risk at the epigenomic and evolutionary genomic levels.

Ancient positive selection shaped knee-specific regulatory elements to establish bipedal-adapted knee morphology, followed by purifying selection that maintained these traits. Notably, proximal tibial regulatory elements are under stronger genetic constraint than distal femoral elements, a human-specific pattern consistent with reduced morphological variation.

The authors propose a model in which recent genetic drift and antagonistic pleiotropy, following ancient adaptation, create violations to constraint that increase OA risk. That is, regulatory sequence variations that are functionally tolerated during development lead to OA pathology in adulthood. The rs6060369 variant at the GDF5 locus provides a concrete example supporting this model: positive selection for reduced height increased the frequency of this OA risk allele through antagonistic pleiotropy.

A limitation of this study is the inability to analyze human-specific acquired regulatory elements due to ethical constraints on obtaining chimpanzee developmental samples. Nevertheless, the methodology linking the evolutionary history of knee chondrocyte regulatory elements to OA risk provides a framework applicable to the study of other human traits and diseases. Future work should include FOXP1/FOXP2 knockout mouse model studies and functional validation of additional OA risk loci.
