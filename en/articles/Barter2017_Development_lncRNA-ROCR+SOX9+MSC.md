# The long non-coding RNA ROCR contributes to SOX9 expression and chondrogenic differentiation of human mesenchymal stem cells

## Citation (NLM)
Barter MJ, Gomez R, Hyatt S, Cheung K, Skelton AJ, Xu Y, Clark IM, Young DA. The long non-coding RNA ROCR contributes to SOX9 expression and chondrogenic differentiation of human mesenchymal stem cells. Development. 2017;144(24):4510-4521. doi:10.1242/dev.152504

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.152504](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.152504)

---

## Background

SOX9 is the master transcription factor for chondrocyte differentiation, regulating the expression of cartilage-specific genes (Col2a1, Acan, etc.). Loss of SOX9 function completely blocks limb development in mice, and mutations in the human SOX9 coding sequence cause campomelic dysplasia (CD), a skeletal malformation syndrome. SOX9 is located in an approximately 2 Mb gene desert on chromosome 17, and its expression is regulated by various tissue-specific enhancers. Chromosomal rearrangement analysis in CD patients suggested the presence of chondrogenesis-specific enhancers in the 50-350 kb region upstream of SOX9. However, the mechanisms regulating SOX9 expression, particularly the role of long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs), remain largely unknown. LncRNAs exhibit tissue-specific expression patterns and function as key regulators of gene expression, but their roles in cartilage and chondrocyte development have been scarcely studied. This study aimed to establish the lncRNA repertoire of human articular cartilage and discover cartilage-specific lncRNAs that contribute to SOX9 expression.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

- **RNA-seq**: RNA extracted from normal hip articular cartilage (femoral neck fracture patients, n=6, median age 76) followed by RNA-seq. Analysis of lncRNA expression upstream of the SOX9 locus.
- **qRT-PCR**: Confirmation and quantitative analysis of SOX9-AS1 and ROCR expression. Tissue specificity evaluation using a 20-tissue RNA panel.
- **RACE (Rapid Amplification of cDNA Ends)**: Identification of ROCR transcript variants.
- **RNA-FISH**: Attempted analysis of ROCR subcellular localization.
- **MSC chondrogenic differentiation (Transwell and Pellet methods)**: Differentiation of human bone marrow-derived MSCs into chondrocytes. Expression dynamics analysis of ROCR and SOX9-AS1.
- **RNA interference (siRNA)**: Gene expression suppression by ROCR and SOX9-AS1-specific siRNAs followed by chondrogenic capacity assessment. Safranin O staining, GAG quantification (DMB assay), DNA quantification (PicoGreen assay).
- **LNA GapmeR**: Independent validation of ROCR suppression.
- **Lentiviral overexpression**: Construction of ROCR and SOX9 overexpression lentiviruses for MSC infection. Rescue experiment of ROCR deficiency phenotype via SOX9 overexpression.
- **MSC osteoblast and adipocyte differentiation**: Analysis of ROCR and SOX9-AS1 deficiency effects on other differentiation lineages. Alizarin Red staining (osteogenesis), Oil Red O staining (adipogenesis).
- **Bioinformatics analysis**: ROCR expression profiling using public RNA-seq databases (Human Protein Atlas, Illumina BodyMap). Coding potential analysis using ORF Finder, CPAT, CPC, PhyloCSF.

## Results

### LncRNA repertoire of human articular cartilage
- RNA-seq identified 813 lncRNAs among 46,087 transcripts (FPKM>1).
- Expression of SOX9-AS1 and LOC102723505 (named ROCR) confirmed upstream of the SOX9 locus. ROCR is a 4-exon lncRNA located 94 kb upstream of SOX9.
- ROCR is primarily cytoplasmic and shows a more cartilage tissue-specific expression pattern than SOX9-AS1. ROCR variant 2 is restricted to chondrocytes.
- SOX9-AS1 is also expressed in synovium and fat pad, whereas ROCR is cartilage-specific within the joint.

### ROCR expression during MSC differentiation
- SOX9, SOX9-AS1, and ROCR were all upregulated with similar dynamics during MSC chondrogenic differentiation.
- ROCR was not induced during osteoblast or adipocyte differentiation → chondrogenesis-specific.
- SOX9-AS1 was also induced during adipocyte differentiation.

### Essential role of ROCR in chondrogenesis
- ROCR siRNA suppression led to failure of cartilage disc formation, decreased GAG production, and reduced DNA content.
- ROCR deficiency significantly decreased expression of cartilage matrix genes including COL2A1 and ACAN.
- SOX9 mRNA and protein levels were significantly decreased from early differentiation (day 1) following ROCR deficiency.
- SOX5/6 expression was also not induced upon ROCR deficiency.

### Mechanism of ROCR action: SOX9-dependent
- SOX9 overexpression significantly rescued the chondrogenesis defect caused by ROCR deficiency: normalized GAG levels, partial recovery of COL2A1/ACAN, complete recovery of SOX5/6.
- This suggests that the primary function of ROCR is to induce SOX9 expression during chondrogenesis.
- However, ROCR overexpression in adult articular chondrocytes (HACs) did not affect SOX9 expression → ROCR's role is differentiation stage-specific.

---

## Perspective

- This study discovered the first cartilage-specific lncRNA (ROCR) involved in regulating SOX9 expression.
- ROCR mediates SOX9 induction during MSC chondrogenic differentiation, contributing to chondrocyte fate determination, which can be rescued by SOX9 overexpression.
- ROCR is specifically induced only during chondrogenesis and is not required for osteoblast or adipocyte differentiation, playing an important role in MSC commitment to the chondrocyte lineage.
- The ROCR locus, located in the upstream enhancer region of SOX9, coincides with chromosomal rearrangement breakpoints associated with CD and Pierre Robin sequence, suggesting this lncRNA may be involved in the pathogenesis of skeletal developmental disorders.
- Future research directions: (1) elucidation of the molecular mechanism of ROCR action (protein binding partners, etc.), (2) functional analysis of mouse ROCR orthologs, (3) analysis of lncRNA expression changes across articular cartilage zones, growth plate cartilage, weight-bearing, aging, and osteoarthritis, (4) exploration of ROCR-SOX9 axis applications in cartilage tissue engineering.
