# GSK-3 is a master regulator of neural progenitor homeostasis

## Citation (NLM)
Kim WY, Wang X, Wu Y, Doble BW, Patel S, Woodgett JR, Snider WD. GSK-3 is a master regulator of neural progenitor homeostasis. Nat Neurosci. 2009;12(11):1390-1397. doi:10.1038/nn.2408

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nn.2408](https://doi.org/10.1038/nn.2408)

---

## Background

Development of the brain requires exquisite coordination of neural progenitor proliferation and differentiation to build complex circuitry with the correct number of neurons. Multiple signalling pathways — Wnt/β-catenin, fibroblast growth factors (FGFs), Sonic Hedgehog (Shh) and Notch — regulate neural progenitors, but how these signals are integrated in space and time was unknown.

Glycogen synthase kinase 3 (GSK-3), a serine-threonine kinase with two closely related mammalian isoforms (GSK-3α and GSK-3β, 98% identity in the kinase domain), is uniquely positioned to coordinate these pathways because it regulates critical nodes in each: it is inhibited during canonical Wnt signalling (raising β-catenin), acts downstream of FGF/PI3K, regulates Myc-family stability, and modulates Hedgehog and Notch signalling in various systems. The authors set out to define how GSK-3 functions in neural progenitor self-renewal and neurogenesis during mammalian brain development.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Generated a compound conditional knockout: *Gsk3a*-null mice combined with *Gsk3b*^loxP/loxP crossed to a nestin-cre line driving Cre in CNS progenitors from ~E10, eliminating both GSK-3 isoforms specifically in neural progenitors (confirmed by Western blot).
2. Assessed progenitor pools and proliferation by immunostaining (Sox2, phospho-histone H3, BrdU, Ki67) across forebrain and spinal cord at E13.5–E15.5; quantified mitotic, BrdU (S-phase), cell-cycle re-entry and apoptosis (activated caspase-3) indices.
3. Assessed neurogenesis with markers for intermediate neural progenitors (Tbr2/INPs) and postmitotic neurons (Tuj1, Tbr1, NeuN, MAP2, SMI32); progenitor markers Nestin and Pax6; validated by Western blotting.
4. Examined pathway activation: β-catenin and targets (Axin2, c-Jun); Shh effectors (Gli1, Gli2, Gli3, Patched) by Western blot and in situ hybridization; Notch (Hes1, Hes5, cleaved NICD); c-Myc/N-Myc levels and T58 phosphorylation.
5. Functional dissection in dissociated mutant progenitor cultures: siRNA knockdown of Gli1; γ-secretase inhibitor DAPT; dominant-negative TCF (dnTCF), Hes1 (dnHes1), and c-Myc constructs; FGF2 stimulation and PI3K inhibition (LY294002) to test the FGF–PI3K–GSK-3–c-Myc axis.

---

## Results

- GSK-3 deletion caused massive hyperproliferation of neural progenitors along the entire neuraxis. Mutant brains had enlarged, convoluted cortices and expanded ventral regions; Sox2+ progenitor pools were massively expanded, including in the spinal cord (where progenitor hyperproliferation closed the central canal and extended progenitors into the developing dorsal horn).
- Hyperproliferation resulted from a shortened cell cycle and increased cell-cycle re-entry (elevated phospho-histone H3, BrdU, Ki67/BrdU double-positive cells), not from reduced apoptosis.
- Neurogenesis was strongly inhibited: Tbr2+ INPs were reduced ~64%, and postmitotic neurons (Tuj1, Tbr1, NeuN) decreased ~50–70%, while progenitor markers Nestin and Pax6 increased 100–200%. Differentiation was arrested at the radial progenitor stage and this inhibition persisted through E15.5.
- Multiple homeostatic pathways were dysregulated: β-catenin accumulated in nucleus and cytosol with increased Axin2/c-Jun; Shh effectors Gli1/Gli2 and Patched were markedly upregulated (Gli3 unchanged) without changes in Shh protein; Notch signalling was elevated (widespread Hes1, increased Hes5 and cleaved NICD).
- Functional tests showed these pathways contribute largely independently: dnTCF (β-catenin block) or dnHes1 (Notch block) each reduced mutant proliferation by ~50%, but combined inhibition dropped proliferation below 5%. Gli1 siRNA reduced proliferation ~35%; DAPT reduced hyperproliferation and increased MAP2+ neurons.
- GSK-3 mediates FGF–PI3K regulation of c-Myc: c-Myc/N-Myc accumulated in mutants with near-complete loss of stabilizing-related T58 phosphorylation; the PI3K inhibitor LY294002 lowered c-Myc and blunted FGF2-induced proliferation in controls but had no effect in GSK-3-deleted progenitors, placing GSK-3 downstream of FGF–PI3K in controlling Myc stability. GSK-3 was also required for apical-basal polarity of progenitors.

---

## Perspective

This study establishes GSK-3 as a central integrating node that enforces homeostatic control over mammalian neural progenitors, simultaneously restraining proliferation and permitting differentiation by governing β-catenin, Shh, Notch and FGF/PI3K–Myc signalling. A key conceptual advance is that regulation of a single kinase provides a powerful, simple mechanism for setting neuron number, and that several GSK-3-controlled pathways act independently rather than through β-catenin alone.

The relevance to spinal cord and regeneration is direct: progenitor hyperproliferation was observed throughout the neuraxis including the spinal cord, implicating GSK-3 in CNS/spinal progenitor homeostasis and raising the possibility of pharmacologically tuning progenitor self-renewal versus neurogenesis. Limitations include lethality at P0 (limiting analysis to embryonic stages), reliance on some cell-line-derived mechanistic precedents (e.g., direct Notch/Myc regulation), and the fact that requiring near-total GSK-3 loss for the phenotype complicates translation of partial pharmacological inhibition. Future directions include dissecting substrate-specific GSK-3 functions and testing controlled GSK-3 modulation to expand or direct neural progenitor fate.
