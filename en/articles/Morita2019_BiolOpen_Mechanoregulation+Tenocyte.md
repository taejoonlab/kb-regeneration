# The optimal mechanical condition in stem cell-to-tenocyte differentiation determined with the homogeneous strain distributions and the cellular orientation control

## Citation (NLM)
Morita Y, Sato T, Higashiura K, Hirano Y, Matsubara F, Oshima K, Niwa K, Toku Y, Song G, Luo Q, Ju Y. The optimal mechanical condition in stem cell-to-tenocyte differentiation determined with the homogeneous strain distributions and the cellular orientation control. Biol Open. 2019;8(6):bio039164. doi:10.1242/bio.039164

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/bio.039164](https://doi.org/10.1242/bio.039164)

---

## Background
Tendon has limited self-healing capacity due to sparse cellularity and vascularity, often resulting in biomechanically inferior scar tissue. Tendon tissue engineering using mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) differentiated into tenocytes via mechanical stimulation is a promising approach. However, previous studies using cyclic stretching failed to determine the optimal strain amplitude because they did not control cellular reorientation behavior—cells naturally realign perpendicular to stretch direction to avoid mechanical load, confounding results. Additionally, non-uniform strain distribution across culture membranes introduced further variability.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Developed a new PDMS elastic chamber with thicker sidewalls (5 mm) and linear load support to improve strain distribution homogeneity (~50% improvement over commercial chambers)
2. Fabricated aligned microgrooved PDMS membranes (10 µm wide, 5 µm deep grooves) using photolithography to suppress cellular reorientation
3. Cultured human bone marrow-derived MSCs (hBMSCs) on microgrooved membranes and applied cyclic uniaxial stretching at six strain amplitudes (0, 2, 4, 5, 6, 8%) at 1 Hz for 48 h
4. Quantified tenogenic marker mRNA expression (Scx, Mkx, Tnc, Col III, Col I) and osteogenic marker (Runx2) using qRT-PCR
5. Measured strain distribution uniformity using digital image correlation (DIC) method
6. Visualized cellular orientation using fluorescein isothiocyanate cytoskeleton staining

## Results
- The newly developed PDMS chamber achieved 7.97±0.22% strain (mean±s.d.) under 8% applied strain, with ~50% improved homogeneity vs. commercial chambers
- Microgrooved membranes successfully constrained cellular reorientation; cells remained aligned parallel to stretch direction throughout 48 h cyclic stretching
- Maximum expression of all tenogenic markers (Scx, Mkx, Tnc, Col III, Col I) occurred at 5% strain amplitude
- The optimal strain decreased from ~8% (previous studies without reorientation control) to 5% when cellular reorientation was suppressed, indicating cells experience more severe mechanical loading when forced to remain parallel to stretch
- Osteogenic marker Runx2 showed peak expression at 2% strain, consistent with prior findings that lower strain favors osteogenesis
- Microgrooves themselves did not influence tenogenic differentiation in the absence of cyclic stretch
- 5% strain aligns with in vivo tendon/ligament mechanical conditions (4-5% maximum strain during physiological loading)

## Perspective
This study provides the first accurate determination of optimal strain amplitude (5%) for tenogenic differentiation by eliminating two major confounding factors: cellular reorientation and non-uniform strain distribution. The finding that 5% strain is optimal—matching in vivo tendon mechanics—validates the physiological relevance of the approach. The methodology (microgrooved membranes + improved chamber design) represents a powerful platform for optimizing mechanical conditions in tissue engineering. Future work should investigate the interplay between strain amplitude, frequency, and duration, as well as the mechanisms underlying the shift from tenogenic to osteogenic fate at different strain levels.
