# Cellular response to spinal cord injury in regenerative and non-regenerative stages in Xenopus laevis

## Citation (NLM)
Edwards-Faret G, González-Pinto K, Cebrián-Silla A, Peñailillo J, García-Verdugo JM, Larraín J. Cellular response to spinal cord injury in regenerative and non-regenerative stages in Xenopus laevis. Neural Dev. 2021;16:2. doi:10.1186/s13064-021-00152-2

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1186/s13064-021-00152-2](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13064-021-00152-2)

---

## Background

Anatomical and functional regeneration of the spinal cord (SC) varies widely across the animal kingdom. Teleost fish, urodele amphibians (salamanders, triton), lampreys and some reptiles regenerate efficiently, whereas mammals cannot. Xenopus laevis is uniquely valuable because it regenerates the SC efficiently at pre-metamorphic larval stages (regenerative, R-stage; NF stage 50) but loses this capacity after metamorphosis (non-regenerative, NR-stage; NF stage 66), allowing a within-species comparison of regenerative versus non-regenerative responses.

In mammals, the adult SC central canal (CC) contains ependymal cells, astrocytes and CSF-contacting neurons; neural stem/progenitor cells (NSPCs) exist but most activated NSPCs become astrocytes rather than neurons in vivo, and a fibrotic/glial scar (astrocytes, microglia, pericytes, meningeal fibroblasts, CSPGs, collagen) forms and inhibits axon growth. In regenerative species (zebrafish, salamanders) the CC is dominated by ependymo-radial glia (ERG) expressing GFAP and Sox2 that proliferate massively after injury, generate new neurons, and form permissive glial bridges. This study directly compares the cellular response to SC injury between R- and NR-stage Xenopus.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Full spinal cord transection (and, for ablation-gap studies, ~200 μm resection) in R-stage (NF 50) and NR-stage (NF 66) animals, analyzed at 2, 6, 10, 20 (and up to 40) days post transection.
2. Transmission electron microscopy of ultrathin sections and semithin histology (methylene blue) to characterize ultrastructural cellular responses.
3. Immunofluorescence and western blot for glial/ECM markers (Vimentin, Fibronectin, CSPGs) and AFOG staining for collagen.
4. Generation of a zebrafish GFAP regulatory region reporter transgenic line, Xla.Tg(Dre.gfap:EGFP)Larra (zGFAP::EGFP), validated by comparison to CAG::EGFP electroporation.
5. Immunogold anti-EGFP staining and co-staining for Sox2, BLBP, and glutamine synthase to identify EGFP+ cell identity.
6. FACS isolation of EGFP+/EGFP− cells followed by RNAseq and gene ontology / clustering analysis; RT-qPCR of NSPC, neurogenic, astrocyte, and oligodendrocyte markers.
7. EdU pulse labeling to measure proliferation; acetylated tubulin immunofluorescence to distinguish axons from glial processes.
8. Inducible cell ablation using a zGFAP::mCherry-nitroreductase transgenic line, Xla.Tg(Dre.gfap:mCherry-Nitroreductase)Larra, with metronidazole (NTR/MTZ), followed by swimming recovery and Sox2 histology.

## Results

- **R-stage:** Cells lining the CC rapidly sealed the injured stumps by 2 dpt while remaining healthy; a proliferative response followed (mitotic clusters both contacting and not contacting the lumen), with cellular extrusion into the CC lumen between 2–6 dpt.
- Rosette-like structures (clusters of 20–24 neuroepithelial cells) formed in the ablation gap by 6 dpt; by 10 dpt bundles of (mostly unmyelinated) axons with synaptic vesicles filled the caudal CC; near-complete SC reconstruction by 20 dpt.
- **NR-stage:** CC-lining cells were severely damaged/necrotic by 2 dpt, with massive red blood cell and macrophage infiltration and ECM deposition; no proliferation or rosettes; ablation gap ultimately filled with fibroblast-like cells and abundant collagen, with no SC reconstruction even at 40 dpt and complete loss of swimming.
- **Scar markers:** R-stage showed only transient increases in Vimentin, Fibronectin, and Collagen protein (no significant mRNA change); NR-stage showed delayed, sustained accumulation of Vimentin, Collagen, and CSPGs at protein and mRNA levels — a glial-scar-like response absent in R-stage.
- **Transgenic validation:** zGFAP::EGFP labeled radial-glia-morphology cells lining the CC in R-stage that mostly co-expressed Sox2; in NR-stage EGFP marked primarily subependymal astrocytes (co-expressing BLBP and glutamine synthase). RNAseq of R-stage EGFP+ cells showed enrichment for neural precursor/stem cell proliferation gene ontologies and clustered with astrocytes/radial glia, confirming NSPC identity.
- **Injury response of NSPCs:** SCI triggered massive, SC-specific EGFP+ cell proliferation (EdU), not seen in sham or in the intestine control. EGFP+ processes crossing the ablation gap (6–7 dpr) co-localized with acetylated tubulin, indicating they are axons of newly formed neurons rather than purely glial bridges.
- RT-qPCR showed SCI increased NSPC markers (sox2, nestin) and early neurogenic markers (ascl1, neurog2a, neurog3, neurod1, dcx) and astrocyte markers (vim-a, aldh1l1) in EGFP+ cells, but not oligodendrocyte markers (sox10, mbp).
- **Ablation:** MTZ-induced ablation of zGFAP::mCherry-NTR cells strongly reduced Sox2+ cells, impaired swimming recovery, and blocked functional SC regeneration, demonstrating NSPCs are necessary for regeneration at NF stage 50.

---

## Perspective

This study establishes that the profoundly different regenerative outcome between larval and post-metamorphic Xenopus is rooted in distinct cellular responses: rapid stump sealing, NSPC proliferation, neurogenesis and absence of a glial scar in R-stage versus tissue necrosis, sustained inflammation, and glial-scar-like fibrosis in NR-stage. The zGFAP::EGFP and zGFAP::mCherry-NTR lines are validated, broadly useful tools for labeling and manipulating NSPCs in Xenopus.

A central conclusion is that GFAP+ NSPCs are both activated by injury and functionally required for regeneration, and that a key permissive difference is the lack of glial scar formation in regenerative stages. Limitations include RNAseq performed on single biological replicates for EGFP+/EGFP− populations and reliance on EGFP perdurance (rather than a bona fide fate-mapping) to infer cell fate. The within-species R-versus-NR paradigm offers a powerful platform to identify the molecular switches that prevent CC cell death and scar formation, findings potentially translatable to promoting mammalian SC regeneration.
