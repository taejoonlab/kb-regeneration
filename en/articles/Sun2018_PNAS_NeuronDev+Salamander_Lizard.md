# Differences in neural stem cell identity and differentiation capacity drive divergent regenerative outcomes in lizards and salamanders

## Citation (NLM)
Sun AX, Londono R, Hudnall ML, Tuan RS, Lozito TP. Differences in neural stem cell identity and differentiation capacity drive divergent regenerative outcomes in lizards and salamanders. Proc Natl Acad Sci U S A. 2018;115(35):E8256-E8265. doi:10.1073/pnas.1803780115

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1803780115](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1803780115)

---

## Background

Both lizards and salamanders can regenerate amputated tails, but the outcomes differ markedly. Salamanders (e.g., Ambystoma mexicanum) regenerate nearly identical copies of the original tail, complete with dorsoventral patterning of skeletal and spinal cord tissues and roof plate–associated structures such as dorsal root ganglia (DRG). Regenerated lizard tails, in contrast, lack dorsoventral patterning: the skeleton forms a single unsegmented cartilage tube surrounding the spinal cord, and DRG are not reformed. Lizards sit as an "intermediary" amniote species between the highly regenerative axolotl and non-regenerating mammals.

In the regenerating tail, the ependymal tube (radial glia/NSCs lining the central canal) acts as the regenerative analog of the embryonic neural tube, with roof plate (Pax7+, BMP-2+), lateral (Pax6+), and floor plate (Shh+, FoxA2+) domains that pattern surrounding tissues. Salamander ependymal tubes recreate these domains, whereas lizard ependymal tubes express only floor plate (Shh) markers. The authors hypothesized that intrinsic differences in the Sox2+ NSCs residing in the ependyma account for these divergent regenerative outcomes, and set out to characterize NSC positional identity, hedgehog responsiveness, and differentiation capacity.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Comparative anatomy/histology of original and regenerated lizard (Lepidodactylus lugubris, Anolis carolinensis) and salamander (Ambystoma mexicanum) tails via Col2 and βIII-tubulin immunostaining.
2. Immunofluorescent staining of ependymal domain markers (Pax7, BMP-2/4 for roof plate; Pax6 for lateral; FoxA2, Shh for floor plate) in vivo, verified by Western blot.
3. Isolation and in vitro culture of Sox2+ NSCs as neurospheres (FGF-stimulated); domain-marker staining in situ and in neurospheres.
4. EdU incorporation assays to compare NSC proliferation in vivo (14, 28, 56 DPA; proximal/middle/distal) and in vitro.
5. Hedgehog signaling modulation: systemic cyclopamine (inhibitor) and SAG (Smoothened agonist) treatment in vivo, plus in vitro neurosphere treatment; readouts Pax7, Shh, Col2, and RT-PCR.
6. In vivo differentiation assays via allogeneic spinal cord transplantation into regenerating tails; staining for βIII-tubulin, GFAP, Sox2.
7. In vitro differentiation of neurospheres; RT-PCR for GFAP, Sox2, neurofilament heavy, Sox10.
8. Xenogeneic transplantation of DiI-labeled salamander NSCs into regenerating lizard tail spinal cords (with Tacrolimus immunosuppression, ± cyclopamine) to test microenvironment effects.

## Results

- Regenerated lizard tails lack dorsoventral patterning (unsegmented cartilage tube, reduced axonal area, loss of butterfly-shaped cord, no DRG), whereas salamanders faithfully regenerate segmented cartilage rod, cord shape, and DRG.
- In vivo, salamander ependyma exhibits roof, lateral, and floor plate domains; lizard ependyma expresses floor plate (Shh, FoxA2) only. Notably, embryonic lizard neural tubes do contain all three domains—patterning is lost in adult/regenerated lizard.
- Both species harbor Sox2+ NSCs that form neurospheres. In vitro, salamander neurospheres default to roof plate (Pax7+, Shh−), while lizard neurospheres default to floor plate (Pax7−, Shh+).
- Salamander NSCs are more proliferative (42.8% EdU+ vs 7.9% for lizards in vitro); roof plate–localized salamander cells proliferate more than floor plate cells, with no such bias in lizards.
- Hedgehog signaling regulates dorsoventral patterning of salamander—but not lizard—ependymal tubes: cyclopamine dorsalizes and SAG ventralizes salamander tubes/neurospheres, while lizard tubes remain Shh+/Pax7− regardless. Cyclopamine abolishes Col2+ cartilage in both species, showing effective drug delivery, yet lizard ependymal patterning is unresponsive (implying a negative feedback controlling Shh).
- Salamander NSCs differentiate into multiple neural lineages (neurons + astrocytes) in vitro and in vivo; lizard NSCs are lineage-restricted, producing only GFAP+ astrocytic cells (no βIII-tubulin+ neurons). Lizard tail axons arise from extension of proximal axons rather than NSC differentiation.
- Xenogeneically transplanted salamander NSCs are ventralized (become Shh+) by the lizard tail microenvironment, but retain Pax7 positivity when Shh is blocked by cyclopamine—showing native lizard NSCs create a strongly ventralizing environment.
- Critically, salamander NSCs retain neuronal differentiation capacity even within the lizard environment, whereas lizard NSCs remain GFAP-restricted—indicating the lizard differentiation defect is intrinsic to lizard NSCs, not imposed by the microenvironment.

---

## Perspective

The study establishes that the divergent tail regeneration outcomes of lizards versus salamanders are driven by intrinsic differences in their ependymal Sox2+ NSC populations—differences in both positional identity (default roof plate vs floor plate, hedgehog responsiveness) and differentiation capacity (multipotent neural vs GFAP-restricted). Salamander NSCs recapitulate the embryonic neural tube's Shh-dependent dorsoventral patterning; lizard NSCs are constitutively floor plate and cannot dorsalize or make neurons.

The authors propose that lizard NSCs may be "cognate" NSCs lacking a regulator of Shh expression, and suggest the term "Sox2+ ependymal progenitor cells" is more fitting. A possible explanation offered is that the lizard tail spinal cord derives from a secondary (mesodermal) neural tube, versus the primary (ectodermal) neural tube in salamanders. Limitations acknowledged include the inability to selectively knock down Shh in lizard NSCs in vivo (no transgenic tools available for these lizards). Because hedgehog blockade could restore roof plate identity and spatial patterning in the lizard regenerate, modulating NSC behavior is proposed as a route toward improved regeneration—work relevant to understanding why regenerative capacity is lost from salamanders to amniotes/mammals.
