# Transcriptional regulation of ependymal cell maturation within the postnatal brain

## Citation (NLM)
Vidovic D, Ayala Davila R, Gronostajski RM, Harvey TJ, Piper M. Transcriptional regulation of ependymal cell maturation within the postnatal brain. Neural Dev. 2018;13:2. doi:10.1186/s13064-018-0099-4

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1186/s13064-018-0099-4](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13064-018-0099-4)

---

## Background
Radial glial stem cells of the developing nervous system generate neurons, glia, and the multiciliated ependymal cells that line the ventricles of the brain and spinal cord. Ependymal cells separate the brain parenchyma from cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), regulate CSF osmolarity and flow via coordinated ciliary beating, and provide trophic support and signalling to neural tissues; deficits in their development or function are linked to hydrocephalus. While FOXJ1 and SIX3 are known drivers of ependymal maturation, the broader transcriptional mechanisms remain poorly characterised.

Nuclear factor IX (NFIX), one of four NFI-family transcription factors, is expressed by mature ependymal cells, and Nfix-deficient mice develop communicating hydrocephalus with lateral-ventricle expansion. NFIX regulates radial glial differentiation (activating Gfap, repressing Sox9) across neocortex, spinal cord, hippocampus, and cerebellum, but its specific contribution to ependymal cell development had not been defined. This study tests whether NFIX regulates ependymal maturation and identifies Foxj1 as a candidate target.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Co-immunofluorescence and confocal microscopy of NFIX with ependymal markers FOXJ1 and vimentin in wild-type mouse lateral ventricles at postnatal days P0, P5, and P15.
2. Comparison of ependymal maturation between Nfix+/+ and Nfix−/− mice (C57Bl/6J; germline ZP3-cre deletion) using FOXJ1 and s100β markers, with cell counts per unit ventricle length.
3. iDISCO+ tissue clearing plus N-cadherin and acetylated-tubulin staining at P10 for en face imaging of ependymal apical surface morphology and ciliary orientation.
4. In silico genome-wide screen (MEME/FIMO) for NFI binding motifs in promoters of ependymal genes (Foxj1, Six3, Msi1/2, Pdgfc, Myo9a).
5. ChIP-qPCR for NFIX occupancy at two Foxj1 promoter motifs (−935 and −2856) in P15 SVZ tissue.
6. Luciferase reporter assay in Neuro2A cells testing NFIX activation of a Foxj1 promoter fragment containing the −935 motif.

---

## Results
- NFIX is co-expressed with FOXJ1 in maturing ependymal cells lining the lateral ventricles; expression is low at P0 but prominent by P5 and P15, and NFIX+ nuclei are surrounded by vimentin+ filaments. NFIX is also present in the adjacent ventricular-subventricular zone niche.
- Nfix−/− mice show delayed ependymal maturation: significantly fewer FOXJ1- and s100β-expressing cells at P0 and P5.
- By P15, Nfix−/− mice exhibit marked lateral-ventricle expansion and three ependymal-layer deficits: reduced cell numbers where the layer is intact, discontinuous regions (cells sloughing off), and areas of aberrant thickening (>1 cell thick)—the thickening producing a statistically significant increase in FOXJ1+/s100β+ cells per unit length at P15.
- iDISCO en face imaging: wild-type ependyma form a regular cobblestone sheet with coordinated ciliary beating patterns; Nfix−/− cells are irregularly shaped with disorganised cilia projecting directly into the lumen, suggesting adherens-junction and maturation defects.
- The in silico screen identified NFI motifs in Foxj1, Six3, Msi1, Msi2, Pdgfc, and Myo9a promoters. ChIP-qPCR confirmed NFIX enrichment at the −935 and (more modestly) −2856 Foxj1 sites; the luciferase assay showed NFIX significantly activates the Foxj1 promoter fragment containing the −935 motif.

---

## Perspective
The study establishes NFIX as an important regulator of ependymal cell maturation in the postnatal lateral ventricles, acting at least in part through direct transcriptional activation of Foxj1 to control the correct timing of ependymal differentiation. Because delayed differentiation of radial-glial-derived cells ultimately yielding more differentiated cells parallels the previously described hippocampal phenotype, a common NFIX mechanism in promoting progenitor differentiation is implied. The work has clinical relevance: NFIA and NFIX haploinsufficiency in humans is associated with ventriculomegaly/hydrocephalus, and aberrant ependymal biology may contribute.

Limitations include reliance on a germline knockout (confounding radial glial vs. ependymal-specific roles) and the unresolved functional relevance of the −2856 motif. The authors propose using a conditional Nfix allele with an ependymal-specific cre driver (e.g., Six3-CreERT2), together with ChIP-seq and single-cell RNA-seq, and deeper analysis of adherens-junction integrity and ciliary ultrastructure, to fully define NFIX's role in ependymal development and disease.
