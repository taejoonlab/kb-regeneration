# Neurons derived from transplanted neural stem cells restore disrupted neuronal circuitry in a mouse model of spinal cord injury

## Citation (NLM)
Abematsu M, Tsujimura K, Yamano M, Saito M, Kohno K, Kohyama J, Namihira M, Komiya S, Nakashima K. Neurons derived from transplanted neural stem cells restore disrupted neuronal circuitry in a mouse model of spinal cord injury. J Clin Invest. 2010;120(9):3255-3266. doi:10.1172/JCI42957.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI42957](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI42957)

---

## Background

The adult CNS has a severely limited capacity to restore damaged neural networks. Although various treatment regimens can partially alleviate spinal cord injury (SCI), the mechanisms underlying symptomatic improvement remain unclear. Neural stem cells (NSCs) are considered promising for cell-replacement strategies because they can self-renew, differentiate into multiple lineages, and migrate toward damaged sites. However, a major obstacle is that the injured CNS environment strongly biases transplanted NSCs toward astrocytic differentiation at the expense of neuronal differentiation, and a direct contribution of transplant-derived neurons to reconstructing disrupted neuronal circuits had not been demonstrated.

Valproic acid (VPA; 2-propylpentanoic acid) is an established antiepileptic drug that also directly inhibits histone deacetylase (HDAC) activity, causing histone hyperacetylation, and the authors had previously found that VPA induces neuronal differentiation of NSCs while suppressing astrocytic and oligodendrocytic differentiation. Exploiting these properties, this study tests whether combining NSC transplantation with VPA administration improves functional recovery in an SCI model and dissects the mechanism by which the damaged corticospinal tract (CST) is functionally reconstructed.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **Severe SCI mouse model.** 15-week-old male ICR mice underwent laminectomy at the ninth/tenth thoracic vertebrae and contusion injury with an Infinite Horizon impactor (90 kdyn), producing complete disruption of CST axons (BBB score 0 at 1 week; incomplete injuries excluded).
2. **NSC preparation and transplantation.** NSCs were derived from E14.5 embryonic forebrains of transgenic mice expressing GFP (GFP-Tg), GFP and luciferase (GFP.LUC-Tg), or GFP, LUC, and the diphtheria toxin receptor TR6 (TR6.GFP.LUC-Tg), expanded as adherent monolayer cultures with EGF and bFGF. Undifferentiated NSCs were transplanted into the SCI epicenter 7 days after injury.
3. **VPA administration.** Transplanted and control mice received daily intraperitoneal VPA (150 mg/kg) or saline for 7 days; valpromide (VPM), a non-HDAC-inhibiting analog, was used as a control.
4. **Behavioral assessment.** Hind limb motor function was scored with the open-field BBB locomotor scale for up to 14 weeks.
5. **CST anterograde tracing.** Biotinylated dextran amine (BDA) was injected into the motor cortices 12 weeks after SCI to visualize first-order CST axons.
6. **Transsynaptic circuit tracing.** WGA-expressing adenoviruses were injected into the motor cortex to label first-, second-, and third-order neurons across synapses.
7. **Differentiation analysis.** Immunocytochemistry/immunohistochemistry for stem cell markers (Sox2, nestin) and lineage markers (DCX, MAP2 for neurons; GFAP for astrocytes; MBP for oligodendrocytes), and histone acetylation status.
8. **Ultrastructural analysis.** Immunoelectron microscopy to detect synaptic connections between GFP-positive transplant-derived neurons and endogenous neurons.
9. **Cell ablation experiments.** Specific ablation of transplanted cells via the TRECK method (diphtheria toxin on TR6-expressing NSCs), and ablation of local gray-matter neurons via NMDA injection into the injury epicenter.
10. **In vivo imaging.** Bioluminescence (luciferase) imaging to noninvasively track survival of transplanted cells.

---

## Results

- Combined NSC transplantation plus VPA administration produced dramatic recovery of hind limb function (BBB ~9-10) versus marginal recovery in untreated mice (BBB ~4), far exceeding either treatment alone. Recovery plateaued around 6 weeks and was sustained for more than 3 months. VPA alone gave no improvement over untreated mice, indicating VPA acts on the transplanted cells.
- BDA tracing showed CST axons did NOT reextend across the lesion in either untreated or combined-treatment mice, so axon regrowth could not explain the functional recovery.
- Transplant-derived cells migrated rostrally and caudally, extended processes into gray matter and dorsal funiculus; 20-40% survived at 8 weeks and 17% remained viable beyond 1 year. VPA did not significantly affect survival.
- VPA promoted neuronal differentiation of transplanted NSCs (increased DCX- and MAP2-positive cells) while suppressing glial differentiation. HDAC inhibition was essential: VPM (non-HDAC inhibitor) neither promoted neuronal differentiation nor improved motor function, whereas TSA (another HDAC inhibitor) reproduced the effect in vitro.
- Transsynaptic WGA tracing revealed that WGA reached caudal neurons below the lesion in combined-treatment mice despite absent CST reextension; transplant-derived neurons at the lesion contained WGA granules, indicating they act as "relays" reconnecting endogenous neurons across the lesion.
- Immunoelectron microscopy confirmed that transplant-derived neurons both received synaptic projections from endogenous neurons and sent axon terminals forming synapses onto endogenous ventral horn neurons.
- Ablating transplanted cells with diphtheria toxin (TRECK) caused BBB scores to drop rapidly to untreated levels, confirming the transplanted cells contributed directly and majorly to recovery.
- NMDA ablation of local gray-matter neurons completely reversed both spontaneous and treatment-provoked recovery, showing that both endogenous and transplant-derived local neurons are important for hind limb function.
- Among transplant-derived neurons, 16.9% ± 2.3% were VGLUT2-positive (glutamatergic/excitatory) and 70.1% ± 1.8% were GAD65-positive (GABAergic/inhibitory), indicating both excitatory and inhibitory subtypes were generated.

---

## Perspective

This study introduces the "HINT" method (HDAC Inhibitor and NSC Transplantation) — manipulating the epigenetic fate of transplanted NSCs with VPA to overcome the injured CNS's bias toward astrocytic differentiation. Unlike prior work in moderate SCI models (where recovery was attributed largely to remyelination by transplant-derived oligodendrocytes), this severe model with completely disrupted CST demonstrates that transplant-derived NEURONS directly reconstruct broken circuits as relays, transmitting signals between endogenous neurons across the lesion without CST axon reextension.

The significance is that neuron supplementation may be more beneficial than oligodendrocyte supplementation for severe SCI, and that the injured CNS retains greater restorative capacity than previously thought (transplant-derived axons were even remyelinated by endogenous oligodendrocytes). Because VPA is an established clinical drug, this approach may be more translatable than genetic manipulation. Limitations include incomplete recovery and an unverified hypothesis that the excitatory/inhibitory neuronal balance governs functional outcome. The authors propose that more efficient treatment may require a combinatorial "cocktail" adding neurotrophin-mediated neuroprotection, suppression of myelin inhibitors, and remyelination strategies.
