# A Subpopulation of Foxj1-Expressing, Nonmyelinating Schwann Cells of the Peripheral Nervous System Contribute to Schwann Cell Remyelination in the Central Nervous System

## Citation (NLM)
Ma D, Wang B, Zawadzka M, Gonzalez G, Wu Z, Yu B, Rawlins EL, Franklin RJM, Zhao C. A Subpopulation of Foxj1-Expressing, Nonmyelinating Schwann Cells of the Peripheral Nervous System Contribute to Schwann Cell Remyelination in the Central Nervous System. J Neurosci. 2018;38(43):9228-9239. doi:10.1523/JNEUROSCI.0585-18.2018

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.0585-18.2018](https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.0585-18.2018)

---

## Background

Remyelination restores new myelin sheaths to demyelinated axons. In the CNS this is normally achieved by oligodendrocytes newly generated from oligodendrocyte progenitor cells (OPCs). Counterintuitively, demyelinated CNS axons can also be remyelinated by Schwann cells (SCs), the myelinating cells of the PNS, in both experimental models and clinical diseases (autoimmune, traumatic, genetic, toxic). Fate-mapping studies show that most CNS-remyelinating SCs derive from OPCs and few from myelinating PNS SCs, but they do not exclude that PNS cells other than myelinating SCs could be a source.

This study used inducible Cre-Lox genetic fate mapping to test whether Foxj1-expressing cells contribute to remyelination after lysolecithin-induced demyelination of the spinal cord. Foxj1 is a master regulator of motile cilia and a marker of ependymal cells (EPCs); the original rationale was to test whether Foxj1+ EPCs (reported to be plastic after spinal cord injury) contribute to CNS remyelination.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **Inducible genetic fate mapping**: Foxj1-CreERT2, PDGFRa-CreERT2, and Sox10-CreERT2 mice crossed to a Cre-dependent farnesylated GFP reporter (Rosa-CAG-fGFP); tamoxifen (250 mg/kg × 4 d oral gavage) induced recombination. An FGFR3-GFP line (labels EPCs and astrocytes) was also used.
2. **CNS demyelination**: focal lysolecithin (1% LPC) injection into dorsal or ventral white matter funiculi of thoracolumbar spinal cord; analysis at 5, 14, 21 days post-lesion (dpl).
3. **PNS injury models**: sciatic nerve crush and transection; analysis to 28 days post-injury (dpi).
4. **Cell identity/fate**: immunohistochemistry for GFP with markers (Olig2, GFAP, IBA1, PRX/periaxin, MPZ/P0, S100, Sox10, L1cam, P75NTR, NG2, fibronectin, P4HB, SMA, HSP47, CD31, Ki67, Sox2, Foxj1); in situ hybridization (RNAScope); Western blot and RT-PCR for Foxj1.
5. **Ultrastructure**: pre-embedding immunogold labeling electron microscopy against GFP on sciatic nerve and spinal cord.

---

## Results

- Foxj1-driven GFP labeled CNS ependymal cells (lateral ventricles, spinal cord central canal, co-expressing Sox2; ~46% of EPCs labeled) and Bergmann glia, plus scattered elongated cells in the PNS (dorsal/ventral roots, sciatic and trigeminal nerves). PNS GFP+ cells did NOT co-express myelinating SC markers (PRX, MPZ/P0, S100).
- Foxj1+ PNS cells are a subpopulation of **nonmyelinating Schwann cells (NMSCs / Remak cells)** with fibroblast-like features: most expressed L1cam (~78%), Sox10, P75NTR (~73%), GFAP, and associated with small-diameter axons in Remak bundles (immunogold EM). They did NOT express CD140a/PDGFRa, distinguishing them from PDGFRa-labeled endoneurial fibroblast-like cells (a separate, mutually exclusive population).
- After spinal cord demyelination, few GFP+ cells appeared in dorsal lesions, but considerable numbers appeared in **ventral lesions** at 14 and 21 dpl. These GFP+ cells did not express Olig2, GFAP, or IBA1, but ~54% co-expressed PRX (and P0), identifying them as **remyelinating Schwann cells**.
- Using the FGFR3-GFP line, no remyelinating SCs were labeled and no proliferation of central-canal EPCs was seen at lesion levels, arguing that **ependymal cells do NOT contribute to CNS remyelination**. Ki67+ proliferating L1+ cells appeared in ventral roots adjacent to lesions, supporting a peripheral-nerve origin of the CNS-remyelinating SCs.
- Following sciatic nerve crush, Foxj1-GFP+ cells increased and proliferated (Ki67+); by 28 dpi ~80% of GFP+ cells co-expressed PRX and ~30% of PRX+ remyelinated axons were GFP+, showing Foxj1+ NMSCs become repair (myelinating) SCs. PDGFRa-GFP cells increased but never co-expressed PRX (did not make SCs). In transection, Foxj1+ cells populated proximal/distal stumps and the bridge.

---

## Perspective

The study identifies a previously unrecognized population of PNS glia—Foxj1-expressing nonmyelinating Schwann cells—that can generate remyelinating SCs in both the PNS and, importantly, the demyelinated CNS. It refutes the prediction that Foxj1+ ependymal cells are a source of CNS remyelinating cells, at least in the focal lysolecithin model. This has therapeutic relevance for chronic demyelinating diseases such as multiple sclerosis, where enhancing remyelination is a goal, by defining a new cellular target for myelin regeneration.

Limitations: recombination efficiencies differ between Cre lines, complicating quantitative comparison of the relative contributions of myelinating vs nonmyelinating SCs; the authors note definitive cell-specific fate mapping with lines of equal efficiency would be needed. The discrepancy with reports of EPC plasticity may reflect the absence of direct EPC damage in focal demyelination versus traumatic spinal cord injury. Future work should establish the origin and mobilization signals of these Foxj1+ NMSCs and their contribution across different injury paradigms.
