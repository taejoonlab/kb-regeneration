# Foxm1 controls a pro-stemness microRNA network in neural stem cells

## Citation (NLM)
Besharat ZM, Abballe L, Cicconardi F, Bhutkar A, Grassi L, Le Pera L, Moretti M, Chinappi M, D'Andrea D, Mastronuzzi A, Ianari A, Vacca A, De Smaele E, Locatelli F, Po A, Miele E, Ferretti E. Foxm1 controls a pro-stemness microRNA network in neural stem cells. Sci Rep. 2018;8:3523. doi:10.1038/s41598-018-21876-y

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-018-21876-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-018-21876-y)

---

## Background
Neural stem cells (NSCs) are characterized by self-renewal and multipotency, determined by transcription factors and epigenetic regulation. Understanding the mechanisms underlying NSC maintenance is important because they might one day be exploited to repair tissue damage or improve regeneration in neurodegenerative diseases (Parkinson's, Huntington's, multiple sclerosis) or spinal cord injury.

Cerebellar NSCs require Hedgehog-Gli (Hh-Gli) signalling for their maintenance and Nanog expression for self-renewal. The authors had previously shown that Gli1/Gli2 directly regulate Nanog, and that Hh-Gli-driven Nanog controls a set of microRNAs that suppress genes promoting cell-cycle arrest and differentiation. In this study, they used next-generation mRNA- and miRNA-sequencing to further explore this Hh-Gli-driven molecular network in early post-natal cerebellar NSCs, aiming to identify additional molecular players in Hh-regulated self-renewal.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. **mRNA-seq and miRNA-seq** (paired-end polyA+ RNA-seq) of postnatal day 4 (P4) murine cerebellar NSCs grown in stem-cell-selective medium versus after induced differentiation (Diff-NSCs), three replicates each.
2. **Functional/GO analysis** of differentially expressed transcripts using the DAVID platform.
3. **RT-qPCR** validation in murine NSCs and normal human neural progenitors (NHNP), including after Smoothened-inhibitor cyclopamine-KAAD treatment to suppress Hh signalling.
4. **siRNA knockdown of Foxm1** and neurosphere-formation assays to test self-renewal.
5. **qPCR-ChIP assays** for Gli1, Gli2, Nanog and Foxm1 promoter/miRNA-promoter occupancy plus AcH3 (transcriptional activation marker), with site-directed mutagenesis of putative binding sites.
6. **Luciferase reporter assays** of the Foxm1 promoter in NSCs and 293T cells.
7. **LNA-mediated depletion** of candidate miRNAs (miR-130b, miR-301a, miR-19a, miR-15b), individually and combined, with neurosphere assays.

---

## Results
- 988 genes were differentially transcribed between NSCs and Diff-NSCs; GO analysis highlighted cell cycle, DNA replication, and p53 signalling (a known negative regulator of NSC self-renewal).
- Foxm1 was the Hh-Gli-regulated transcription factor most markedly enriched in NSCs prior to differentiation. Suppressing Hh with cyclopamine-KAAD reduced Foxm1 mRNA in both murine NSCs and human NHNP. The 757-amino-acid Foxm1-201 isoform predominated.
- ChIP and luciferase assays showed Gli1 and Gli2 directly occupy and activate the Foxm1 promoter (eight putative Gli-binding sites), with higher occupancy in NSCs than Diff-NSCs.
- Foxm1 knockdown significantly impaired neurosphere formation and upregulated differentiation markers (βIII-tubulin, S100b) without affecting proliferation or apoptosis markers; the effect was not additive with Hh suppression.
- miRNA-seq identified 80 differentially expressed miRNAs; of 40 upregulated in NSCs, 20 had putative Foxm1 binding sites. ChIP confirmed significant Foxm1 recruitment at miR-130b, miR-301a, and members of the miR-15~16 and miR-17~92 clusters.
- Combined LNA depletion of miR-130b, miR-301a and miR-19a (and optionally miR-15b) significantly impaired neurosphere formation and upregulated differentiation markers, whereas single knockdowns did not.
- These miRNAs converge on the p53 pathway; several putatively target Trp53inp1 (a p53 pathway component), linking Foxm1 to p53 suppression that sustains stemness.
- Nanog bound the Foxm1 promoter (four putative sites; s2-s3 functionally required), and Foxm1 is itself known to target Nanog—revealing bidirectional Nanog-Foxm1 crosstalk. Nanog- and Foxm1-regulated miRNA networks were largely non-overlapping except for the miR-17~92 cluster.

---

## Perspective
The study defines a novel Hh-Gli-Nanog-driven Foxm1-microRNA network controlling cerebellar NSC self-renewal, with Foxm1 acting as a second transcriptional activator (alongside Nanog) that upregulates pro-stemness miRNAs converging on suppression of the p53 pathway (via Trp53inp1). This proposes a combinatorial, partially redundant "bypass axis" that maintains NSC self-renewal robustly. Significance: it deepens understanding of the fine-tuning between stemness and differentiation in NSCs, relevant to their potential use as therapeutic/regenerative agents. Limitations: the ability of miR-130b and miR-301a to downregulate Trp53inp1 requires direct verification in future studies, and other miRNA mechanisms cannot be excluded; findings are largely from postnatal murine cerebellar NSCs. The Hh-Gli-Foxm1 axis, given its documented role in cancer, also has implications for cerebellar tumour biology.
