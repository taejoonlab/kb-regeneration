# Restorative effects of human neural stem cell grafts on the primate spinal cord

## Citation (NLM)
Rosenzweig ES, Brock JH, Lu P, Kumamaru H, Salegio EA, Kadoya K, Weber JL, Liang JJ, Moseanko R, Hawbecker S, Huie JR, Havton LA, Nout-Lomas YS, Ferguson AR, Beattie MS, Bresnahan JC, Tuszynski MH. Restorative effects of human neural stem cell grafts on the primate spinal cord. Nat Med. 2018;24(4):484-490. doi:10.1038/nm.4502

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nm.4502](https://doi.org/10.1038/nm.4502)

---

## Background

After spinal cord injury (SCI), robust long-distance regeneration of injured axons remains elusive due to both extrinsic inhibitors (inhibitory extracellular matrix, myelin-associated proteins, lack of growth-promoting factors) and neuron-intrinsic growth failures in the adult CNS. One promising alternative is to graft neural progenitor/stem cells into the injury site to form a neuronal relay circuit that bridges the lesion. The authors had previously optimized this approach in rodents, showing improved electrophysiological and functional outcomes.

Because many therapies effective in rodents fail in primates due to species differences in stem cell biology, CNS size/neuroanatomy, and immune mechanisms, a nonhuman primate model is critical before human translation. This "preclinical trial" tested whether human spinal cord–derived neural progenitor cells could successfully engraft into cervical SCI sites in rhesus monkeys, form relay circuits, and improve function.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Right-side C7 hemisection lesions in nine adult male rhesus monkeys (Macaca mulatta), with grafting 2 weeks post-injury (clinically relevant stabilization window).
2. Grafting of 20 million GFP-expressing human spinal cord–derived multipotent neural progenitor cells (566RSC-UBQT line) suspended in a growth factor cocktail and fibrin–thrombin matrix.
3. Iterative development of primate-specific grafting methods: operative table tilt to drain CSF, increased fibrin–thrombin concentration for rapid gelling, and a robust triple immunosuppression regimen (mycophenolate mofetil, tacrolimus, prednisone).
4. Immunohistochemistry for neuronal/glial markers (NeuN, DCX, GFAP, vimentin, SOX9, OLIG2, Ki-67) and axonal markers (β-III tubulin, NF70, SMI32, NF200) at 2–9 months post-grafting.
5. Anterograde corticospinal tract tracing (biotinylated dextran amine, BDA), serotonergic (5HT) axon labeling, synapse assessment (HOMER1, synaptophysin) and ultrastructural (EM) analysis.
6. Open-field functional scoring (>25 features: object manipulation, climbing, locomotion) and nonlinear PCA composite behavioral analysis; in vivo MRI (3T) for graft visualization.

---

## Results

- Grafting methods effective in rodents initially failed in primates (first 4 monkeys); primate-specific modifications (CSF drainage, higher fibrin–thrombin, stronger immunosuppression) achieved graft survival and lesion fill in the subsequent 5 subjects for at least 9 months.
- Grafts expressed neuronal (NeuN, DCX) and glial markers; cell composition was ~57% neurons (NeuN), ~26% astrocytes (SOX9), ~17% oligodendrocytes (OLIG2). Grafts matured slowly—GFAP appeared only at 5 months; cell density fell 63% (400,000→150,000 cells/mm³) while cell size increased over 9 months.
- Human axons emerged from grafts in extraordinary numbers—up to ~150,000 axons at 2 mm caudal to the lesion—reaching distances up to 50 mm, growing linearly through host white matter (indicating adult myelin did not inhibit these developing human axons).
- Graft-derived axons formed synapses on host neurons (asymmetric, excitatory-type by EM), frequently converging on host motor neurons.
- Host axons regenerated into grafts, including NF200+ intraspinal axons, serotonergic axons, and—for the first time reported in primate—corticospinal axons (penetrating up to ~500 µm into grafts).
- Monkeys with surviving grafts showed a second phase of functional improvement beginning ~10 weeks post-lesion; object manipulation recovered to >25% success in 4/5 grafted monkeys vs 1/4 non-surviving, and composite motor function improved significantly (P = 0.02).
- No teratomas/tumors; grafted cells did not migrate >100 µm from the lesion or form ectopic colonies (except one surface colony at the engraftment site).

---

## Perspective

This preclinical primate trial provides four key advances: (i) human neural stem cells can be engrafted to fill large primate SCI cavities using primate-specific methods; (ii) grafts mature slowly over months, implying potentially late functional effects; (iii) unprecedented numbers of new human axons (up to ~150,000) extend into surrounding cord, representing substantial new circuitry; and (iv) primate corticospinal axons—essential for voluntary movement and the most refractory system—regenerated into grafts for the first time.

Significance: the study strongly supports translation of NPC graft therapy toward human SCI, while emphasizing the indispensable role of large-animal models to avoid clinical trial failures from graft-loss pitfalls. Limitations acknowledged include the absence of a true lesion-only control group, shorter survival in non-surviving-graft animals, only modest corticospinal penetration (~0.5 mm), incomplete axon maturation even at 9 months, and an ongoing low-grade CD8+ T cell immune response. Future work will test whether greater corticospinal regeneration can be achieved by enhancing host neuronal growth state (e.g., transient PTEN inhibition).
