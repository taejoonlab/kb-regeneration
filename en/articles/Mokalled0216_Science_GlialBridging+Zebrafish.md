# Injury-induced ctgfa directs glial bridging and spinal cord regeneration in zebrafish

## Citation (NLM)
Mokalled MH, Patra C, Dickson AL, Endo T, Stainier DYR, Poss KD. Injury-induced ctgfa directs glial bridging and spinal cord regeneration in zebrafish. Science. 2016;354(6312):630-634. doi:10.1126/science.aaf2679

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aaf2679](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aaf2679)

---

## Background

Unlike mammals, adult zebrafish spontaneously and efficiently regenerate functional nervous tissue after major spinal cord injury. In mammals, reactive gliosis produces a glial scar that inhibits regeneration, whereas in zebrafish glial cells instead form a bridge across the severed cord that is thought to provide a scaffold for axonal regrowth. This injury response is distinguished by efficient axon growth, adult neurogenesis, and the absence of glial scarring. Following initial inflammation, ependymal cells proliferate and glia bridge the lesion, the cord reconnects, and new neuronal connections restore function.

The molecular cues that drive glial bridging were unknown. Connective tissue growth factor (CTGF/CCN2) is a secreted matricellular protein that influences cell adhesion, migration, proliferation, and differentiation, and its expression is known to increase after CNS trauma in rodents — but its function after spinal cord injury had not been established. This study sought to identify secreted pro-regenerative factors driving zebrafish spinal cord regeneration.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Genome-wide transcriptome screen of the regenerating zebrafish spinal cord for up-regulated secreted extracellular factors.
2. In situ hybridization on cross and longitudinal spinal cord sections at 1 and 2 weeks post-injury (wpi) to localize candidate transcripts (fn1a, fn1b, ctgfa, mstnb, stc1l).
3. Generation of a ctgfa:EGFP transgenic reporter (5.5-kb upstream sequence) and co-staining with GFAP to identify ctgfa-expressing bridging glial cells.
4. CRISPR/genetic generation of a ctgfa loss-of-function mutant (ctgfa^bns50, a 7-nt frameshift deletion in exon 3); assessment of homozygotes and heterozygotes.
5. Functional swim-capacity assays in a swim tunnel and anterograde axon tracing at 2-6 wpi.
6. GFAP and acetylated α-tubulin immunohistochemistry to quantify glial bridging; EdU incorporation to measure glial proliferation.
7. Heat-inducible transgenic overexpression of full-length ctgfa (hsp70:ctgfa-FL) and of N-terminal or C-terminal fragments (ctgfa-NT, ctgfa-CT).
8. Local delivery of human recombinant CTGF protein (HR-CTGF-FL and HR-CTGF-CT) via gelfoam sponge at the lesion site.

---

## Results

- The screen identified seven secreted-factor genes; fn1a, fn1b, ctgfa, mstnb, and stc1l were induced in ependymal cells at 1-2 wpi. ctgfa was predominantly expressed in ventral ependyma.
- ctgfa expression correlated with glial bridge formation: at 2 wpi ctgfa localized to ventral ependymal cells and marked the cellular bridge; ~97% of GFAP+ bridging glia at the lesion core were ctgfa:EGFP+ at 1 wpi ("pioneer" bridging glia).
- ctgfa is necessary: ctgfa^-/- animals showed no significant functional swim recovery, ~60% (proximal) to ~80% (distal) reduced axon regeneration, ~71% less glial bridging, and ~48% reduced glial proliferation. Heterozygotes showed partial recovery.
- ctgfa is sufficient: whole-animal ctgfa-FL overexpression improved swim recovery, bridging, and axon regeneration. The pro-regenerative activity mapped to the C-terminal domains (ctgfa-CT recapitulated ctgfa-FL effects; ctgfa-CT gave >3x bridging and >2x axon growth).
- Local delivery of human recombinant CTGF (FL or CT, 81% identical to zebrafish Ctgfa) accelerated bridging (>10x at 1 wpi for HR-CTGF-CT) and functional/anatomical regeneration, and rescued the ctgfa mutant phenotype, demonstrating specificity.

---

## Perspective

The study establishes CTGF/ctgfa as both necessary and sufficient to stimulate glial bridging and natural spinal cord regeneration in zebrafish, acting at least partly by promoting proliferation and bridging of pioneer glial cells that themselves express ctgfa. The pro-regenerative activity resides in the C-terminal domains, and exogenous human CTGF protein reproduces the effect, suggesting translational potential.

A key open question the authors raise is whether a mammalian glial subtype exists that can produce CTGF or respond to it — a pro-regenerative counterpart to zebrafish bridging glia. Because mammalian reactive gliosis contains a heterogeneous glial pool releasing both growth-inhibiting and growth-permissive factors, identifying such a subtype could guide strategies to shift mammalian injury responses toward the zebrafish-like bridging outcome. Limitations include the reliance on the zebrafish model and the incomplete definition of the downstream signaling pathways and receptors mediating Ctgfa-CT activity.
