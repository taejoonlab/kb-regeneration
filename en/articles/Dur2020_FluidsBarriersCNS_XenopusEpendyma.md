# In Xenopus ependymal cilia drive embryonic CSF circulation and brain development independently of cardiac pulsatile forces

## Citation (NLM)
Dur AH, Tang T, Viviano S, Sekuri A, Willsey HR, Tagare HD, Kahle KT, Deniz E. In Xenopus ependymal cilia drive embryonic CSF circulation and brain development independently of cardiac pulsatile forces. Fluids Barriers CNS. 2020;17(1):72. doi:10.1186/s12987-020-00234-z

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1186/s12987-020-00234-z](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12987-020-00234-z)

---

## Background

Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) provides hydromechanical protection to the CNS and delivers nutrients while removing catabolites. Pathological expansion of CSF-filled ventricles causes hydrocephalus, a deadly disease. In adults, cardiac (pulsatile arterial) and respiratory forces drive bulk CSF flow, while ependymal cilia generate near-wall flow. However, the mechanics of CSF circulation in the embryonic brain—before choroid plexus formation and active CSF secretion—are poorly understood, largely due to a lack of model systems and imaging technology to access the embryonic CNS in vivo. Emerging data show CSF is critical for early neurogenesis (maintaining neuroprogenitor identity, proliferation, differentiation), and prior zebrafish work gave conflicting views on the relative contribution of the heart versus cilia.

Xenopus tadpole brains share the compartmentalization of the mammalian brain (two lateral, third, fourth ventricles) but are semi-transparent, making them ideal for in vivo imaging. This study uses Xenopus with optical coherence tomography (OCT) to dissect the roles of cardiac versus ciliary forces in embryonic CSF circulation and neurodevelopment.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **Optical coherence tomography (OCT)** imaging (Thorlabs Ganymede 900 nm, ~2.2 μm axial resolution) for serial, non-destructive in vivo visualization of ventricular morphology and CSF flow in Xenopus tropicalis embryos from stage 16 to stage 49.
2. **CSF flow velocity analysis and particle tracking** using endogenous particles (TrackMate in ImageJ) plus a **Gaussian process regression (GPR)** post-processing pipeline for dense velocity estimation per ventricular compartment.
3. **Intraventricular microbead injections** (0.81 / 4.61 μm polystyrene) to track flow at early (stage 30) and late (stage 47-49) stages.
4. **Cardiac ablation** by microdissection of the heart/outflow tract at stage 40 and stage 46, with confirmation via loss of cardinal vein flow; pre/post CSF flow and ventricular size compared.
5. **Chemical cilia paralysis** by intraventricular NiCl2 or sodium orthovanadate injection.
6. **Genetic cilia knockdown** using c21orf59 morpholino (blocks ciliary dynein arm assembly; cilia form but cannot beat).
7. **Immunohistochemistry** (glutamylated tubulin GT335, phalloidin) for cilia; **in situ hybridization** for neuroprogenitor/patterning markers emx1, lhx1, en2.

---

## Results

- OCT enabled continuous tracking of ventriculogenesis from post-neurulation (stage 19). The ventricular space expanded rostrocaudally; CSF circulation initiated ~12 h post-neurulation (stage 32) and became polarized, forming 4 flow fields by stage 39 and 5 by stage 46.
- CSF flow showed a caudo-rostral speed gradient—~tenfold faster caudally (posterior 4th ventricle 33.2 μm/s) than rostrally (lateral ventricle 3.5 μm/s).
- Before stage 32, injected beads remained static, confirming no detectable CSF movement during early ventricular expansion.
- Late tadpoles (stage 49) displayed complex bidirectional intercompartmental CSF mixing across aqueducts. NiCl2 ablation of ciliary beating stopped the fastest 4th-ventricle flow, confirming cilia as the driver; no pulsatile choroid plexus flow was detected.
- **Cardiac ablation** (stages 40/46) produced no change in CSF flow pattern, speed, ventricular size/shape, or interventricular (aqueductal) mixing—CSF circulation is independent of cardiac forces. Only a weak pulsatile flow appeared late (stage 49) near the hypothalamus, not contributing to overall circulation.
- Multiciliated cells were found on the rhombencephalic dorsal surface (fastest flow); monociliated cells dominated lateral/ventral and rostral surfaces.
- **c21orf59 knockdown** (paralyzed cilia) abolished CSF circulation and caused aqueductal stenosis (obstructive hydrocephalus). Rostral ventricles (lateral, 3rd) were disproportionately smaller than caudal ones, showing cilia-driven flow matters more for rostral brain development.
- Loss of cilia-driven flow disrupted neuroprogenitor spatial organization: dorsal telencephalic emx1 was mis-patterned (ectopic expression extending into diencephalon/mesencephalon), and lhx1 (zli/thalamic) was disrupted specifically at the telencephalic region, while en2 (midbrain-hindbrain) was examined as a more caudal marker.

---

## Perspective

This work establishes that in Xenopus the embryonic ventricular system forms an ependymal cilia-driven, polarized CSF flow network that operates independently of cardiac pulsatile forces, and that this cilia-driven flow regulates rostral brain development and the spatial organization of neuroprogenitor cells. It shifts attention in embryonic CSF/hydrocephalus research toward motile cilia rather than cardiac forces relevant only to the adult. The semi-transparent Xenopus embryo combined with OCT and GPR-based particle tracking provides a powerful in vivo platform. Limitations include reliance on morpholino knockdown (with its known caveats), OCT resolution limits, and the architectural simplicity of the amphibian brain relative to mammals, so translation to mammalian congenital hydrocephalus requires validation. The findings are relevant to spinal cord/CNS development given the role of ependymal cilia, CSF dynamics, and neuroprogenitor patterning.
