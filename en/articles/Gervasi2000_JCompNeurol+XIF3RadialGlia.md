# Xenopus laevis Peripherin (XIF3) is Expressed in Radial Glia and Proliferating Neural Epithelial Cells as Well as in Neurons

## Citation (NLM)
Gervasi C, Stewart CB, Szaro BG. Xenopus laevis peripherin (XIF3) is expressed in radial glia and proliferating neural epithelial cells as well as in neurons. J Comp Neurol. 2000;423(3):512-531. doi:10.1002/1096-9861(20000731)423:3<512::AID-CNE13>3.0.CO;2-1

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1002/1096-9861(20000731)423:3<512::AID-CNE13>3.0.CO;2-1](https://doi.org/10.1002/1096-9861(20000731)423:3<512::AID-CNE13>3.0.CO;2-1)

---

## Background

Neuronal intermediate filament (nIF) proteins are the most abundant component of the axonal cytoskeleton, so understanding their expression is key to understanding how axonal structure is built and regulated during development and regeneration. Most vertebrate nIF proteins are type IV (α-internexin, NF-L, NF-M, NF-H), but frogs, cyprinid fishes, and mammals additionally possess type III nIF proteins named XIF3, plasticin, and peripherin, respectively. Type III nIF expression correlates strongly with the earliest phases of axonal outgrowth in fishes, and fish retinal ganglion cells re-express plasticin during optic nerve regeneration, but this correlation is weaker in mammals, whose CNS regenerates poorly.

Because frogs are phylogenetically intermediate between fishes and mammals and their CNS regenerative capacity is likewise intermediate, Xenopus laevis is well suited for comparative study. The authors examined XIF3 expression across development and adulthood in Xenopus and built phylogenetic trees of vertebrate IF proteins to determine whether XIF3, plasticin, and peripherin are orthologs (same locus, diverged with species) or paralogs (arising from gene duplication).

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Cloned full-length XIF3 and Xenopus NF-L by RT-PCR from juvenile Xenopus spinal cord RNA; generated digoxigenin-labeled antisense cRNA probes (including a divergent C-terminal/3'-UTR-only probe to exclude cross-hybridization).
2. Wholemount and section in situ hybridization comparing XIF3, XNIF, xefiltin, and NF-L mRNA across stages (neurula stage 18 through 7-year-old adult), with probes matched for label and length for direct abundance comparisons.
3. Raised and Western-blot-characterized a XIF3 antiserum against a synthetic peptide from the divergent C-terminal tail; compared with NF-L, XNIF, and vimentin antisera.
4. Immunocytochemistry for XIF3 protein and for PCNA (S-phase proliferation marker) on neighboring sections to correlate XIF3 expression with proliferating cells and radial glia.
5. Phylogenetic tree inference (parsimony and minimum-evolution/distance methods, PAUP*, 10,000 bootstrap replicates) using rod-domain alignments of 20 vertebrate IF protein sequences from GenBank, rooted with Xenopus lamin A.

---

## Results

- At neural tube stage 18, XIF3 mRNA was already abundant in spinal cord neurons (Rohon-Beard neurons, motoneurons, interneurons) and trigeminal ganglia, preceding axonal outgrowth; no type IV nIF mRNAs were detectable at this stage.
- Throughout early neural development, XIF3 remained the most widespread and abundant low-molecular-mass nIF, whereas XNIF, xefiltin, and NF-L appeared only after axon initiation. This shows type III nIF is preferentially expressed during early axonal outgrowth in Xenopus, as in fish but not mammals.
- After hatching, neuronal XIF3 expression gradually declined in most CNS neurons while type IV nIFs increased; XIF3 persisted in peripheral neurons (dorsal root ganglia, cranial ganglia, spinal motoneurons, preganglionic sympathetic neurons, enteric neurons) and a few CNS neurons (reticular, tegmental) throughout life, paralleling mammalian peripherin.
- Unexpectedly, XIF3 mRNA was expressed throughout life in CNS regions containing proliferating cells and radial glia: the retinal ciliary margin, olfactory epithelium basal layer, and cells lining the ventricles/central canal of the spinal cord — a feature not reported for plasticin (fish) or peripherin (mammal). A divergent C-terminal probe reproduced the same pattern, excluding vimentin cross-hybridization.
- PCNA immunostaining confirmed proliferating cells in these zones; all PCNA+ cell groups also expressed XIF3, though some XIF3+ cells (ventral central field, ventral motor neurons) lacked PCNA. XIF3 protein was found in radial glia and subsets of neurons.
- Phylogenetic trees strongly supported XIF3, plasticin, and peripherin as orthologs (products of the same locus), implying interspecies expression differences arose from regulatory changes to the ancestral peripherin gene along each lineage.

---

## Perspective

The study establishes that XIF3, plasticin, and peripherin are orthologs and thus should be regarded as Xenopus, fish, and mammalian peripherin, reframing interspecies expression differences as lineage-specific regulatory changes rather than distinct genes. The strong association between type III nIF expression and early axonal outgrowth in Xenopus supports the idea that these proteins confer cytoskeletal properties favorable to axon growth and, potentially, regeneration — relevant to why anamniote CNS retains greater regenerative and plastic capacity than mammalian CNS.

The novel finding of peripherin-ortholog expression in radial glia and proliferative zones raises the question of whether this reflects a Xenopus-specific adaptation or an ancestral feature retained from an IF protein originally expressed in both neurons and glia. Limitations include the largely descriptive, expression-based nature of the evidence and the correlational (not functional) link between XIF3 and proliferation/regeneration. Future work could test XIF3 function directly and probe whether its expression in proliferative/radial glial niches marks cells with neurogenic or regenerative potential in the amphibian CNS and spinal cord.
