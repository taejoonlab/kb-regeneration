# Architecture and evolutionary conservation of Xenopus tropicalis osteoblast-specific regulatory regions shed light on bone diseases and early skeletal evolution

## Citation (NLM)
Castillo H, Godoy F, Gilbert C, Aguilera F, Spicuglia S, Marcellini S. Architecture and evolutionary conservation of Xenopus tropicalis osteoblast-specific regulatory regions shed light on bone diseases and early skeletal evolution. bioRxiv. 2023. doi: 10.1101/2023.08.30.555543

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.08.30.555543](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.08.30.555543)

---

## Background
Understanding the genetic mechanisms underlying osteoblast differentiation has important implications for skeletal diseases and evolution. While the osteoblast regulatory landscape has been extensively studied in mammals, it remains uncharacterized in amphibian models. This study identifies osteoblast-specific regulatory elements in Xenopus tropicalis by comparing ATAC-seq profiles of primary osteoblasts with non-mineralizing tissues (liver, heart, lung), and examines their conservation with human skeletal disease genes and across vertebrate evolution.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. ATAC-seq performed on freshly extracted primary osteoblasts and control tissues (liver, heart, lung) from Xenopus tropicalis larvae, without cell culture
2. Nucleosome-free regions (NFRs) identified to distinguish tissue-specific and ubiquitous open chromatin regions
3. RNA-seq to compare gene expression profiles between osteoblasts and liver
4. BLASTN-based conservation analysis of Xt NFRs across 7 chordate genomes (human, chick, zebrafish, elephant shark, lamprey, amphioxus, Xenopus laevis)
5. Gene Ontology (GO) and transcription factor binding site (TFBS) enrichment analyses
6. Mapping to human skeletal disease databases for phenotype association

## Results
1. 524 promoter (TSS) and 6,750 distal enhancer (non-TSS) regions identified as osteoblast-specific open chromatin in Xt
2. Osteoblast-specific non-TSS regions are enriched for TFBS of osteogenic regulators including RUNX2, FRA1, TEAD, NFIC, TWIST1/2, and NFAT
3. RNA-seq confirmed that genes associated with osteoblast-specific open regions are more highly expressed in osteoblasts than liver, validating functional enhancer activity
4. Human conservation: 370 osteoblast-specific non-TSS regions align to the human genome, with 127 annotated as human enhancers (16-fold enrichment)
5. Among 357 conserved enhancers with synteny, 6 map to loci associated with craniosynostosis (msx2, tcf12), osteopoikilosis (lemd3), osteopenia (gorab), skeletal dysplasia (flnb), and craniofacial abnormalities (gpc4)
6. Elephant shark comparison: 53 Xt osteoblast-specific enhancers are conserved across shark-human-frog, revealing ancestral osteogenic roles for ATOH8, IRX3, NFAT, NFIB, MEF2C transcription factors and FGF, IHH, BMP/TGFb signaling pathways
7. TATA-box is present in ubiquitous Xt promoters, contrasting with mammals where it is associated with tissue-specific promoters

## Perspective
This study provides the first characterization of the osteoblast chromatin regulatory landscape in an amphibian, offering a genomic resource for human skeletal disease research. The discovery of 53 ancient osteogenic enhancers conserved from elephant shark to human suggests that the regulatory network for mineralized tissue originated over 460 million years ago. The authors propose that while sharks lack bone, this network was maintained for its function in odontoblasts, supporting a common origin of dentine and bone. Combined with the genetic tractability of Xenopus, these findings will contribute to skeletal disease modeling and evolutionary developmental biology research.
