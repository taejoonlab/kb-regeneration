# FoxJ1 regulates spinal cord development and is required for the maintenance of spinal cord stem cell potential

## Citation (NLM)
Li X, Floriddia EM, Toskas K, Chalfouh C, Honore A, Aumont A, Vallières N, Lacroix S, Fernandes KJL, Guérout N, Barnabé-Heider F. FoxJ1 regulates spinal cord development and is required for the maintenance of spinal cord stem cell potential. Exp Cell Res. 2018;368(1):84-100. doi:10.1016/j.yexcr.2018.04.017

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yexcr.2018.04.017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yexcr.2018.04.017)

---

## Background

Spinal cord development requires tightly controlled expression of numerous transcription factors that pattern progenitor domains along the dorsal-ventral axis and generate distinct neuronal and glial subtypes. Forkhead Box protein J1 (FoxJ1) is a transcription factor classically involved in ciliogenesis and is considered a specific marker of ependymal cells (ECs) in the adult CNS, where ECs surround the central canal, appear around E15.5, and continuously express FoxJ1 into adulthood. A subpopulation of adult spinal cord ependymal cells retains stem/progenitor properties and responds to spinal cord injury (SCI).

Prior work in the brain suggested FoxJ1 is also expressed by subsets of progenitor cells and is required for proper differentiation into ependymal cells and some astrocytes, but its role during neurogenesis and gliogenesis in the developing spinal cord was unknown. The contribution of ependymal cells to SCI had also recently been challenged using a FoxJ1 knock-in lineage-tracing model, motivating a side-by-side comparison of transgenic models.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Three mouse lines: (1) non-inducible FOXJ1::EGFP (human FoxJ1 promoter, short-term lineage tracing); (2) tamoxifen-inducible FOXJ1-CreERT2 × Rosa26-YFP (intact FoxJ1 alleles, long-term tracing); (3) FoxJ1-KI-CreERT2 × ROSA26-CAG-tdTomato (CreER knocked into FoxJ1 locus → allele knockout with lineage tracing).
2. Immunohistochemistry and in situ hybridization on embryonic (E10.5–E17.5) and postnatal (P0–P10, adult P60–P120) spinal cord; markers for interneurons (PAX2, EVX1, Chx10), MN domain (Olig2), astrocytes (Vimentin, Sox9, GFAP, Reelin, Pax6), ependymal/progenitor (FoxJ1, Vimentin, Sox9), oligodendrocytes (Sox10, NG2, CNPase), proliferation (Ki67).
3. Tamoxifen-induced recombination at defined embryonic/postnatal stages; quantification of reporter+ and endogenous FoxJ1+ cells by region (cervical/thoracic/lumbar; grey matter, central canal, floor plate, white matter).
4. Neurosphere assays from dissociated spinal cord (control and post-SCI): recombination rate, self-renewal across passages, total neurosphere counts, and differentiation potential (Tuj1 neurons, CNPase oligodendrocytes, Sox9 astrocytes).
5. Dorsal funiculus transection SCI at T10 (central canal untouched); assessment of ependymal cell migration to lesion and SCI-induced neurosphere increases in FOXJ1-CreERT2-YFP (FoxJ1+/+) vs FoxJ1-KI-CreERT2-tdTomato (FoxJ1+/−) mice.

## Results

- Endogenous FoxJ1 is expressed by E13 progenitor cells around the ventricular zone (Nkx6.1+), several days before the first ependymal cells appear at E15.5.
- FoxJ1+ progenitors give rise to V1 and a small subset of V2 (Chx10+) interneurons (but not V0/EVX1+ or the Olig2+ pMN domain), and to subpopulations of lateral (Reelin+/Pax6+, from E13.5) and dorsal (from E15.5) astrocytes, but not oligodendrocytes (Olig2−/Sox10−). They also give rise to ependymal and floor plate cells around the central canal.
- FoxJ1 is required for normal progenitor proliferation: FoxJ1 deletion (KI line) markedly reduced endogenous FoxJ1+ cells (no compensation in +/− cells), restricted recombined cells to the central canal/floor plate (>5-fold fewer than the intact-allele line), and significantly reduced Ki67+ proliferation during the neurogenic (E13.5) period.
- FoxJ1 deletion caused premature exit from proliferation and biased fate toward the neurogenic lineage: >3-fold fewer Sox9+/Vimentin+ progenitors around the central canal and upregulated Pax2, though cells did not co-express Chx10 or mature NeuN (incomplete maturation).
- Loss of even a single FoxJ1 allele diminished ependymal stem/progenitor potential: fewer recombined and total neurospheres (maintained across passages), with altered differentiation (more Tuj1+ neurons, fewer CNPase+ oligodendrocytes and Sox9+ astrocytes).
- After SCI: ependymal cell migration to the lesion was reduced in FoxJ1-KI mice (18% vs 43% in intact line); FoxJ1+/− mice showed lower oligodendrocytic/higher neurogenic neurosphere differentiation and failed to mount the SCI-induced increase in neurosphere numbers seen in WT/YFP mice.

---

## Perspective

This study uncovers unexpected developmental functions of FoxJ1 beyond ciliogenesis: it is transiently expressed in progenitors of specific interneurons and astrocytes, regulates progenitor proliferation, and is critical for glial-neuronal fate determination and for maintaining ependymal stem cell potential from development through adulthood and after SCI. FoxJ1 thereby emerges as both a marker and a functional regulator relevant to spinal cord repair.

Importantly, the side-by-side comparison helps reconcile divergent literature on ependymal cell contributions to SCI: the FoxJ1-KI-CreERT2 lineage-tracing model, while useful in other organs, itself compromises stem cell behavior in the spinal cord because both FoxJ1 copies are required for full stem cell properties—a critical caveat for interpreting studies using that model. Limitations include reliance on haploinsufficient (FoxJ1+/−) adults (homozygous knockout is perinatally lethal) and the correlative nature of some fate-mapping. Future work should dissect FoxJ1 target genes and its role in astrocyte development/migration and in ependymal-driven regeneration following SCI.
