# The Landscape of Gene Expression and Molecular Regulation Following Spinal Cord Hemisection in Rats

## Citation (NLM)
Yu B, Yao C, Wang Y, Mao S, Wang Y, Wu R, Feng W, Chen Y, Yang J, Xue C, Liu D, Ding F, Gu X. The Landscape of Gene Expression and Molecular Regulation Following Spinal Cord Hemisection in Rats. Front Mol Neurosci. 2019;12:287. doi:10.3389/fnmol.2019.00287

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.3389/fnmol.2019.00287](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnmol.2019.00287)

---

## Background
Spinal cord injury (SCI) produces devastating neurological deficits, and there are currently no reliable clinical treatments. Permanent deficits arise mainly from the weak regenerative capacity of neurons and the inhibitory environment created by secondary injury, which unfolds over days to weeks and includes ischemia, edema, inflammation, cell death, vascular change, and glial scar formation. Because so many cell types and processes are involved, the spatial and temporal dynamics of gene expression after SCI had remained unclear.

This study uses a rat spinal cord hemisection model to profile the transcriptome of tissue rostral (R) and caudal (C) to the lesion across 11 time points over 28 days, aiming to build a comprehensive picture of gene expression and molecular regulation in the injury microenvironment across glial, immune, and vascular systems.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Left-side spinal cord hemisection at T9-T10 in adult male Sprague-Dawley rats (~200 rats total), with sham controls.
2. Collection of 5-mm rostral and caudal segments at 11 time points (0, 0.5, 3, 6, 12 h and 1, 3, 7, 14, 21, 28 days), pooling 6 rats per point into 3 replicate samples.
3. Whole-transcriptome RNA sequencing (Illumina HiSeq 2500, paired-end); differentially expressed genes (DEGs) identified with DESeq (P<0.05, fold change >2 or <0.5).
4. Ingenuity Pathway Analysis (IPA) to enrich cell-type-specific biological processes, compute Z-score expression trends, and construct gene regulatory networks.
5. qRT-PCR validation of selected key genes and immunofluorescence for cell-type markers (GFAP astrocytes, IBA1 microglia/macrophages, Olig2 oligodendrocytes, CD34 vasculature).

---

## Results
- DEG numbers were negligible 0-3 h, rose to hundreds at 6 h-1 day, and reached thousands from 3-28 days; the caudal region consistently showed fewer DEGs than the rostral region.
- Astrocytes became reactive (hypertrophy, robust GFAP), undergoing survival, proliferation, activation, and astrocytosis sequentially. MMP9 and SERPINE1 peaked at 12 h; IL1b, TLR2, CEBPB were elevated; SNCA (α-synuclein) decreased, suggesting SNCA may inhibit astrocyte activation/glial scar formation.
- Microglia/macrophages accumulated from 3 days and stayed elevated to 28 days. IL-6 and CYBB rose early; anti-inflammatory HMOX1 (heme oxygenase 1) increased substantially, peaking at 3 days, consistent with both deleterious and protective roles.
- Oligodendrocyte numbers rose at 3 days then declined; differentiation-related genes were up before 1 day then down. BMP2 increased (peaking ~6 h), implicated in demyelination/remyelination.
- Immune response showed sequential recruitment of neutrophils, monocytes, then lymphocytes; transcription factors ATF3 and FOS increased, with ATF3 (in IBA1+ cells) sustained to 28 days.
- Vascular changes: blood vessel density peaked at 3 days then decreased; DEGs enriched in VEC migration, proliferation, adhesion, apoptosis, activation, plus hypoxia, sprouting angiogenesis and vessel remodeling. LIF was highly expressed early then declined (positive role in oligodendrocyte survival, negative role in angiogenesis).

---

## Perspective
The study provides a temporally and spatially resolved landscape of gene expression and regulatory networks after SCI, spanning astrocyte, microglia, oligodendrocyte, immune, and vascular responses, and highlights candidate regulators (SNCA, HMOX1, BMP2, ATF3, LIF) as potential therapeutic targets. Compared with prior SCI transcriptome work using few time points, its dense 28-day sampling and network analyses offer a more detailed reference for the injury microenvironment. Limitations noted by the authors include modest transcriptional differences between rostral and caudal regions relative to sham, discrepancies in cytokine dynamics versus earlier reports (attributed to model and section differences), and reliance on RNA-seq trends without qRT-PCR validation for many cytokines. Future work should validate cytokine expression/secretion and develop better analyses to distinguish rostral versus caudal responses.
