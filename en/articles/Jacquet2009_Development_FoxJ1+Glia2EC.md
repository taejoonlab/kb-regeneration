# FoxJ1-dependent gene expression is required for differentiation of radial glia into ependymal cells and a subset of astrocytes in the postnatal brain

## Citation (NLM)

Jacquet BV, Salinas-Mondragon R, Liang H, Therit B, Buie JD, Dykstra M, Campbell K, Ostrowski LE, Brody SL, Ghashghaei HT. FoxJ1-dependent gene expression is required for differentiation of radial glia into ependymal cells and a subset of astrocytes in the postnatal brain. Development. 2009;136(23):4021-4031. doi:10.1242/dev.041129

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.041129](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.041129)

---

## Background

Around birth, radial glial cells lining the ventricular zones of the brain differentiate into ependymal cells and astrocytes, some of which form the postnatal/adult neural stem cell niche (SCN) in the subventricular zone (SVZ). This niche supports lifelong olfactory bulb neurogenesis, yet the molecular mechanisms driving the time- and cell-specific radial-glia-to-ependymal-cell/astrocyte transition were largely unknown. The forkhead transcription factor FoxJ1 had been shown to be required for motile multiciliated cell generation across tissues and species (including Xenopus and zebrafish), but its role in ependymal cell differentiation and SCN organization was undefined.

This study characterizes FoxJ1 expression and function during postnatal establishment of the adult SCN in mouse, using FoxJ1-EGFP reporter mice, FoxJ1-null mice, shRNA knockdown, cross-transplantation, and transcriptome profiling.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. FoxJ1-EGFP reporter mice to map FoxJ1 expression across the SVZ, rostral migratory stream (RMS), and olfactory bulb (OB), with marker immunohistochemistry (S100beta, CD133/Prominin1, Gfap, Blbp, nestin, Pax6, CD31, Egfr).
2. Perinatal/postnatal time-course mapping (E20, P0, P1, P6, P14, P19, P21, 6 weeks, 6 months) and stereological quantification of FoxJ1+ ependymal cells and FoxJ1+ astrocytes.
3. FoxJ1-null (FoxJ1-/-) mice and FoxJ1-/-EGFP reporter crosses to assess loss-of-function; transmission and scanning electron microscopy of the ependymal layer and cilia.
4. Independent hydrocephalus models (Dnaic1-/- genetic; kaolin-induced obstructive) to test whether phenotypes were secondary to hydrocephalus.
5. Acute FoxJ1 knockdown by intraventricular lentiviral shRNA and cross-transplantation of wild-type/knockout EGFP+ cells into wild-type/knockout hosts to test cell-autonomy.
6. FACS-sorted SVZ neurosphere cultures to test self-renewal and multipotency of FoxJ1-EGFP cells.
7. Laser-capture microdissection of FoxJ1-EGFP domains (P1, P7, P14) with Affymetrix microarray transcriptome comparison of FoxJ1-null vs wild-type, validated by qRT-PCR; co-immunoprecipitation/western blot for protein complex analysis.

---

## Results

- FoxJ1 is expressed by ependymal cells and a small subset of Gfap+ astrocytes in the adult SVZ; FoxJ1+ astrocytes often contact CD31+ blood vessels. FoxJ1-EGFP cells in the RMS are Blbp+ nestin+ Pax6+ progenitors, and most FoxJ1-EGFP cells in the OB are astrocytes.
- FoxJ1 is sharply upregulated at birth. Ependymal cells (S100beta+) differentiate in a first postnatal wave (~P1-P6); a second wave produces a small subset of FoxJ1+ SVZ astrocytes between P19-P21, gradually increasing over the first 6 months (~1.6% to 2.1% of SVZ astrocytes).
- FoxJ1-/- mice fail to form mature S100beta+/CD133+ ependymal cells and lack motile cilia, develop hydrocephalus, and show disrupted ependymal-layer organization; residual immature Blbp+ radial glia persist. The OB is most reduced (66% volume reduction).
- The ependymal/astrocyte differentiation defect is independent of hydrocephalus: two other hydrocephalus models (Dnaic1-/-, kaolin) retained S100beta+ ependymal cells.
- shRNA knockdown reproduced the phenotype, and cross-transplantation showed FoxJ1 acts cell-autonomously: wild-type cells matured into multiciliated ependymal cells even in a knockout host, whereas knockout cells stayed Blbp+ immature in a wild-type host.
- A subset of FoxJ1-EGFP SVZ cells formed self-renewing neurospheres (passaged >10 times) that differentiated into neurons, astrocytes, and oligodendrocytes, indicating neurogenic/stem potential.
- Transcriptome comparison found 198 genes consistently altered >1.5-fold (197 downregulated) in FoxJ1-null domains; 55 known-function genes were ependymal-zone-expressed, and 24 encoded cytoskeleton/motor proteins (axonemal dynein heavy/intermediate chains, kinesins Kif6/9/27, Rshl3, Tekt4, Ttll6). Dnahc, Dnaic, and Kif6 co-immunoprecipitated with gamma-tubulin in wild-type but not FoxJ1-null brain, implicating FoxJ1 in basal-body transport/docking during ciliogenesis.

---

## Perspective

The work establishes FoxJ1 as a master regulator of the postnatal radial-glia-to-ependymal-cell transition and, more surprisingly, of a small subset of niche astrocytes, thereby linking FoxJ1 to the assembly of the adult SVZ neural stem cell niche. Mechanistically, it connects FoxJ1 to a transcriptional program of microtubule motor proteins required for basal-body transport and motile ciliogenesis. Because ependymal cells and the SVZ/RMS lineage are directly relevant to CNS regeneration and CSF dynamics, these findings inform how the ventricular/ependymal interface is built and maintained. Limitations include the focus on the forebrain SVZ (rather than spinal cord central canal), reliance on constitutive knockout with secondary hydrocephalus (partly addressed by control models and cell-autonomy experiments), and candidate FoxJ1 target genes that are correlative pending functional validation. Future directions include dissecting how FoxJ1 specifies the astrocyte subset versus ependymal cells, and whether the same program governs ependymal differentiation in the spinal cord and its regenerative potential.
