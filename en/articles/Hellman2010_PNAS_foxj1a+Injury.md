# The zebrafish foxj1a transcription factor regulates cilia function in response to injury and epithelial stretch

## Citation (NLM)
Hellman NE, Liu Y, Merkel E, Austin C, Le Corre S, Beier DR, Sun Z, Sharma N, Yoder BK, Drummond IA. The zebrafish foxj1a transcription factor regulates cilia function in response to injury and epithelial stretch. Proc Natl Acad Sci U S A. 2010;107(43):18499-18504. doi:10.1073/pnas.1005998107

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1005998107](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1005998107)

---

## Background
Cilia are central organelles for organ function and developmental patterning, but their role in injury and regeneration responses was largely unknown. Foxj1 is a winged-helix/forkhead-box transcription factor required for motile ciliogenesis; Foxj1-null mice lack motile respiratory cilia and show left-right axis randomization. This study asked whether foxj1a, a transcriptional regulator of cilia genes, is engaged during tissue damage. The authors examined foxj1a in zebrafish and mouse models of epithelial injury and cystic kidney disease, and importantly extended the analysis to zebrafish spinal cord, where ependymal cells generate motile cilia required for cerebrospinal fluid circulation.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Mechanical obstruction of the zebrafish pronephros (larval) and mesonephros (adult) to induce epithelial distension, followed by whole-mount in situ hybridization and quantitative RT-PCR for foxj1a/foxj1b.
2. Cycloheximide pretreatment to test whether foxj1a induction is a primary (protein-synthesis-independent) response.
3. Proximal vs distal obstruction to distinguish epithelial stretch from propagated crush injury.
4. Spinal cord injury: severing 48-hpf embryo cords with forceps and assaying foxj1a/foxj1b expression 8 h later.
5. Gentamicin nephrotoxin injury and analysis of murine cystic kidney mutants (jck/jck [nek8], Ift88Tg737Rpw-/-) and ischemia-reperfusion injury.
6. High-speed video/confocal line-scan microscopy to quantify cilia beat rate versus tubule diameter, using GFP transgenic lines and sco-eGFP-labeled cilia.
7. Morpholino knockdown of foxj1a and foxj1b (single and double) to assess cilia motility and target-gene induction (efhc1, tektin1, dnahc9).

---

## Results
- foxj1a (but not foxj1b) was rapidly and strongly up-regulated by pronephric obstruction (4.6-fold at 6 h), and induction was insensitive to cycloheximide, identifying foxj1a as a primary response gene to epithelial injury.
- Proximal obstruction induced foxj1a only proximal to the blockage, supporting an epithelial-stretch mechanism; foxj1a was also elevated in cystic kidney mutants without cell death.
- Spinal cord injury markedly up-regulated foxj1a (not foxj1b), propagating in both directions from the lesion without spinal canal dilation, indicating a stretch-independent (possibly propagated calcium) mechanism in the CNS.
- Foxj1 induction was conserved in murine cystic kidney mutants and after kidney ischemia-reperfusion injury.
- Tubule obstruction increased cilia beat rate (49 to 73 beats/s), varying linearly with lumen diameter after a 15-30 min latency.
- foxj1a knockdown paralyzed olfactory and ependymal (spinal canal) cilia and blocked injury-induced up-regulation of the motility genes efhc1, tektin1, and dnahc9.
- Acute increases in multiciliated-cell beat rate did not require foxj1a (redundant foxj1b), but foxj1a was essential to maintain enhanced beat rates over 12-24 h of chronic distension.

---

## Perspective
The study establishes activation of a Foxj1 ciliogenic transcriptional network as an evolutionarily conserved response to multiple forms of tissue damage, highlighting enhanced cilia function as a previously uncharacterized component of organ homeostasis and repair. For CNS regeneration, the finding that spinal cord injury rapidly induces foxj1a in ependymal cells is notable: foxj1a may promote ciliated ependymal cell differentiation and CSF circulation, or influence neuronal regenerative responses. Induction of Foxj1 and downstream ciliogenic genes could represent a therapeutic target for promoting tissue repair. Limitations include the correlative nature of much of the CNS data (the spinal cord mechanism was not dissected as thoroughly as the kidney), reliance on morpholino knockdown, and the fact that the precise stretch-to-foxj1a signaling pathway remained undefined.
