# Reconstitution of the central and peripheral nervous system during salamander tail regeneration

## Citation (NLM)
Mchedlishvili L, Mazurov V, Grassme KS, Goehler K, Robl B, Tazaki A, Roensch K, Duemmler A, Tanaka EM. Reconstitution of the central and peripheral nervous system during salamander tail regeneration. Proc Natl Acad Sci USA. 2012;109(34):E2258-E2266. doi:10.1073/pnas.1116738109

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1116738109](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1116738109)

---

## Background

After tail amputation, salamanders (axolotl, *Ambystoma mexicanum*) regenerate the central body axis, including the spinal cord, segmented vertebrae, myotomes, and peripheral nervous system (PNS) structures such as dorsal root ganglia (DRG) and Schwann cells, as well as neural crest derivatives like melanophores. A key challenge has been defining the stem cells responsible for regenerating the CNS and PNS.

Previous work tracked radial glial cells contributing to spinal cord regeneration but could not resolve the long-term fate of stem cells or the origin of the PNS. The source of neural crest derivatives in the regenerated tail has been an open question since 1885. This study addresses whether newly regenerated DRG, Schwann cells, and melanophores derive from the central spinal cord or from peripheral precursors, and whether a true multipotent spinal cord stem cell exists.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Whole-mount NeuN and βIII-tubulin immunofluorescence to quantify DRG number and inter-DRG spacing during axolotl tail regeneration (35 and 49 dpa).
2. Transplantation of GFP+ transgenic spinal cord segments into white host animals, followed by tail amputation and analysis of GFP contribution to regenerated DRG.
3. Skin transplantation (wild-type into white hosts) to test peripheral contribution to melanophores.
4. Derivation and characterization of axolotl spinal cord neurospheres (FGF-2 based culture); marker analysis (Sox2, Musashi-1, GFAP, βIII-tubulin) and in vitro differentiation.
5. Implantation of primary/passaged GFP+ neurospheres into spinal cord lesions; tracking integration, regeneration, and cell-type contribution.
6. Clonal neurosphere formation assay (single GFP+ cells with carrier feeder cells in AggreWell plates) followed by implantation.
7. Retrograde motor neuron labeling (rhodamine dextran) and Hox (A9/A13) positional identity analysis.

---

## Results

- Correct DRG number is restored by 35 dpa, and correct inter-DRG spacing is restored by 49 dpa.
- Spinal cord transplantation showed most regenerated DRG were GFP+, indicating a population associated with the central spinal cord is a significant source of DRG/PNS cells.
- Skin transplantation showed melanophores in the regenerate derive substantially from preexisting peripheral precursors in the skin.
- Axolotl neurospheres expressed neural stem/progenitor markers (Sox2, Musashi-1, GFAP) and differentiated into βIII-tubulin+ neurons and MBP+ glia (with Shh agonist + PDGF).
- Implanted nonclonal neurospheres integrated into the host spinal cord, self-renewed extensively (>10^6-fold expansion over repeated amputations), and formed radial glia, neurons (including motor neurons), Schwann cells, and DRG.
- Clonally derived neurospheres reconstituted the entire lateral half of the spinal cord including all dorsal/ventral cell types (GFAP+ radial glia, neurons, MBP+ cells, Pax6/Pax7 progenitors, Shh+ floor plate, Msx1+ roof plate), demonstrating multipotency; however, clonal neurospheres did not contribute to DRG.
- Implanted cells acquired appropriate rostral/caudal Hox positional identity.

---

## Perspective

This work demonstrates that a faithful complement and periodicity of PNS structures is regenerated during axolotl tail regeneration, and that a substantial portion of DRG and Schwann cells arise from cells associated with the central regenerating spinal cord, whereas melanophores derive largely from peripheral skin precursors. The clonal analysis provides evidence for a multipotent spinal cord neural stem cell capable of reconstituting all central spinal cord cell types from a single cell.

Limitations: clonal neurospheres did not form DRG, leaving unresolved whether a single CNS/PNS stem cell exists or whether separate CNS- and PNS-specific precursors are associated with the spinal cord region; technical constraints prevented tracking of spinal cord-derived melanophores. The extensive neurogenic behavior of axolotl neurospheres contrasts sharply with mammalian neurosphere transplants (which often yield astrocytes with limited neurogenesis), motivating future study of intrinsic versus extrinsic (injury-site cue) determinants and refined cre/lox lineage tracing.
