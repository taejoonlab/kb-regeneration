# Pericyte-derived fibrotic scarring is conserved across diverse central nervous system lesions

## Citation (NLM)
Dias DO, Kalkitsas J, Kelahmetoglu Y, Estrada CP, Tatarishvili J, Holl D, et al. Pericyte-derived fibrotic scarring is conserved across diverse central nervous system lesions. Nat Commun. 2021;12(1):5501. doi:10.1038/s41467-021-25585-5

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-021-25585-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-021-25585-5)

---

## Background
Fibrotic scar tissue that forms after CNS lesions seals off injured tissue but also acts as a permanent barrier to regenerating axons, contributing to lasting functional deficits. Most research has focused on the glial component (reactive astrocytes, NG2 glia); the fibrotic/stromal component is less understood. The authors had previously identified a discrete subset of perivascular cells — type A pericytes (PDGFRβ+, CD13+, Tbx18+, GLAST/Slc1a3+, but negative for desmin and αSMA) — as the origin of stromal fibroblasts in fibrotic scar after penetrating spinal cord injury.

It remained unknown whether the extent and distribution of fibrotic tissue is similar across different lesion types and species, and whether scar-forming stromal cells share a common origin throughout the CNS. This study systematically compared fibrotic scarring across multiple mouse lesion models and corresponding human pathological tissue.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. In vivo genetic fate mapping of type A pericytes using GLAST-CreERT2 mice crossed to Rosa26-EYFP or Rosa26-tdTomato reporters (tamoxifen-induced recombination, 7-day clearing before injury).
2. Multiple mouse lesion models: penetrating spinal cord injury (dorsal funiculus incision), non-penetrating complete spinal cord crush, traumatic brain injury, ischemic stroke, experimental multiple sclerosis (EAE), and glioblastoma (GBM).
3. Time-course analysis at subacute (5 dpi), glial-stromal border formation (14 dpi), and chronic (6-7 wpi) stages.
4. Immunohistochemistry for stromal (PDGFRβ, CD13, Tbx18), (myo)fibroblast (αSMA, vimentin, fibronectin, collagen I), proliferation (Ki67), and glial (GFAP) markers; quantification of cell numbers, vessel association ("ON" vs "OFF" vessel), and lesion core area.
5. Analysis of human pathological tissue from spinal cord injury, multiple sclerosis, ischemic stroke, and grade IV GBM, plus healthy human brain and spinal cord for type A pericyte marker profiles.

---

## Results
- Type A pericytes represent ~10% of PDGFRβ+ perivascular cells in the uninjured adult mouse spinal cord, cortex, and striatum, sharing the same marker profile and distribution.
- After both penetrating and non-penetrating spinal cord injury, EYFP+ type A pericyte-derived cells markedly expanded in the lesion, proliferated (Ki67+), detached from the vessel wall, expressed (myo)fibroblast markers (αSMA, vimentin) and were embedded in collagen I/fibronectin ECM. Roughly 56% (dorsal funiculus incision) and 74% (complete crush) of EYFP+PDGFRβ+ cells were no longer vessel-associated at chronic stages.
- EYFP+PDGFRβ+ cell numbers peaked at 14 dpi (coincident with GFAP+ glial-fibrotic border formation) then dropped as the scar matured and condensed, increasing cell density in the core.
- Type A pericyte-derived cells were largely distinct from NG2-expressing cells in the scar.
- Across the mouse models, type A pericytes were the main source of PDGFRβ+ stromal cells after spinal cord injuries, traumatic brain injury, ischemic stroke, and MS, but contributed less to tumor stroma in GBM. The extent and distribution of stromal cells was lesion-type-specific.
- Perivascular cells with a type A pericyte marker profile exist in the healthy human brain and spinal cord. Human lesions showed lesion-specific stromal patterns: SCI developed abundant non-vessel-associated stromal fibroblasts; active MS accumulated PDGFRβ+ cells in the perivascular space; stroke and GBM showed increased but mostly vessel-associated stromal cells. Human and mouse patterns were similar in most cases.

---

## Perspective
The study establishes type A pericyte-derived fibrosis as a conserved mechanism of fibrotic scarring across diverse CNS lesions and, importantly, that analogous perivascular cells and lesion-appropriate stromal responses occur in humans. This positions type A pericytes as a potential common therapeutic target to modulate fibrotic scarring and improve regeneration/recovery after CNS injury. Limitations include the use of transgenic fate-mapping restricted to mouse, GLAST-CreERT2 recombination in some astrocytes/ependymal cells (though these are PDGFRβ-negative and non-scar-forming), and largely descriptive/correlative human data. Future work should test whether manipulating type A pericyte-derived fibrosis functionally improves axon regeneration and recovery across lesion types.
