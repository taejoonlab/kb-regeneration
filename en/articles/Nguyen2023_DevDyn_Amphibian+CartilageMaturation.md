# Common features of cartilage maturation are not conserved in an amphibian model

## Citation (NLM)

Nguyen JKB, Gomez-Picos P, Liu Y, Ovens K, Eames BF. Common features of cartilage maturation are not conserved in an amphibian model. _Dev Dyn._ 2023;252(12):1478-1493. doi: [10.1002/dvdy.594](https://doi.org/10.1002/dvdy.594).

---

## Background

In endochondral ossification, cartilage maturation follows a standard program of chondrocyte hypertrophy, downregulation of cartilage markers (Sox9, Col2a1), and upregulation of Col10a1. This process is considered highly conserved across vertebrates, including chickens, mice, and zebrafish. However, studies on cartilage maturation in amphibians (Western clawed frog, _Xenopus tropicalis_) have been limited, with a particularly interesting difference reported: col10a1 is not expressed in mature chondrocytes of the _X. tropicalis_ hindlimb. Additionally, thyroid hormone is known to promote cartilage maturation in various tetrapods, but its role in amphibian cranial cartilage remains unknown. This study systematically investigated how well cartilage maturation processes are conserved in amphibians using histological and molecular biological methods.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

**1. Animal model and sample preparation**
- _Xenopus tropicalis_ breeding and rearing (27–28°C)
- Specimen collection at Nieuwkoop-Faber (NF) developmental stages (NF39–NF66)
- Analysis of forelimb humerus and head cartilage (ceratohyal, Meckel's cartilage)

**2. Tissue staining**
- Safranin O staining for sulfated proteoglycan detection
- Col2 immunohistochemical staining to confirm collagen type II protein expression
- Chondrocyte hypertrophy quantification: lacunae cross-sectional area measurement

**3. RNA in situ hybridization**
- Spatial expression pattern analysis of sox9, col2a1, col10a1
- NF59 tooth (odontoblast) used as col10a1 positive control

**4. Laser-capture microdissection RNA-seq (LCM-RNAseq)**
- Laser capture of immature (IMM) and mature (MAT) chondrocytes from NF57 humerus (3 replicates each)
- Normalized gene expression counts comparison from isolated RNA sequencing
- Analysis of 11 immature cartilage markers and 7 hypertrophic cartilage markers

**5. Thyroid hormone antagonist treatment experiments**
- Treatment of NF34/NF35 tadpoles with tetrabromobisphenol A (TBBPA, 1 μM) or NH-3
- Measurement of ceratohyal hypertrophy at 7, 26, and 30 hours post-treatment
- Assessment of global developmental delay: quantitative comparison of external morphology (NF stage)

## Results

**Humerus: largely follows the standard cartilage maturation process** Comparing NF55 (cartilage stage) and NF57 (perichondral ossification stage) humeri, chondrocytes progressively hypertrophied over several days (5–7 days; 43 μm² → 154 μm²). RNA in situ hybridization showed sox9 and col2a1 expressed in NF55 immature cartilage and downregulated in NF57 mature cartilage, but col10a1 was not detected at either time point (confirmed positive in tooth control). LCM-RNAseq data showed that most immature cartilage markers (sox9, col2a1, acan, sox5, col9a2) had higher expression in MAT, though only col9a2 reached statistical significance. Hypertrophic cartilage markers ihh, runx2, and mef2c were upregulated in MAT, but col10a1 and mmp13 were barely detected. GO analysis showed that among 7,589 shared genes between IMM and MAT, at least 109 were cartilage-formation-related genes.

**Ceratohyal (cranial cartilage): marked divergence from the standard process** Hypertrophy progressed rapidly from immediately after chondrocyte differentiation at NF42 to NF43 (within hours), far faster than in the humerus (days) or zebrafish ceratohyal (1–2 days). Unlike the standard pattern where hypertrophy begins in the center of the endochondral element and progresses to the ends, hypertrophy in the ceratohyal initiated simultaneously throughout the entire element. Col2 protein was expressed throughout the entire ceratohyal before, during, and after hypertrophy. sox9 and col2a1 were maintained rather than downregulated during hypertrophy, and col10a1 was not expressed at all. Even at NF57 late hypertrophy, col2a1 remained strong, and sox9 may have partially decreased but was distinctly different from the standard process. After metamorphosis, the ceratohyal persisted as hyaline cartilage without ossifying (confirmed by NF66 Alcian blue/Alizarin red staining).

**Thyroid hormone antagonist effects: no specific inhibition of cartilage hypertrophy** Ceratohyal chondrocyte size significantly decreased after 30 hours of TBBPA treatment (89 μm² vs 119 μm²), but at the 7-hour time point, mesenchymal condensation itself was already delayed. Both TBBPA and NH-3 treated tadpoles showed significant global developmental delay beginning 10 hours after treatment, suggesting that thyroid hormone antagonists slow overall developmental rate rather than exerting cartilage-maturation-specific effects.

---

## Perspective

This study systematically demonstrated that histological and molecular features of cartilage maturation in amphibians (Western clawed frog) are **not universally conserved** across vertebrates. While limb cartilage (humerus) followed a relatively standard program except for the absence of col10a1 expression, cranial cartilage (ceratohyal) showed striking differences: ultra-rapid hypertrophy, maintained sox9/col2a1 expression, absence of col10a1, and lack of ossification.

These differences are likely related to the amphibian metamorphosis process. In indirect-developing frogs, cranial cartilage degenerates during metamorphosis, suggesting that a molecular mechanism distinct from the conventional ossification-linked cartilage maturation program may be operating. Thyroid hormone signaling did not appear to act as a specific regulator, and the potential involvement of other signaling pathways such as Wnt, BMP, FGF, and Hh was suggested.

This study indicates that amphibian models may provide useful insights into the mechanisms of ectopic cartilage maturation (hypertrophy) observed in human osteoarthritis (OA). Future challenges include identifying amphibian-specific regulators of cartilage maturation, elucidating the molecular mechanisms of cartilage degeneration during metamorphosis, and exploring the possibility of functional co-orthologs of col10a1 in _X. tropicalis_.
