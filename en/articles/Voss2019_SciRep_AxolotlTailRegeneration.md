# HDAC Regulates Transcription at the Outset of Axolotl Tail Regeneration

## Citation (NLM)
Voss SR, Ponomareva LV, Dwaraka VB, Pardue KE, Al Haj Baddar NW, Rodgers AK, Woodcock MR, Qiu Q, Crowner A, Blichmann D, Khatri S, Thorson JS. HDAC Regulates Transcription at the Outset of Axolotl Tail Regeneration. Sci Rep. 2019;9:6751. doi:10.1038/s41598-019-43230-6

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-019-43230-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-019-43230-6)

---

## Background
Highly regenerative salamanders such as the axolotl (Ambystoma mexicanum) repair damaged tissues without scarring and can regenerate appendages, including the tail and its enclosed spinal cord. Regeneration is associated with complex changes in gene expression and post-translational modifications of transcription factors and histones. Injury cues are thought to act partly through epigenetic mechanisms (DNA methylation, histone acetylation/deacetylation) that alter chromatin structure. Prior work with valproic acid and trichostatin A suggested that histone deacetylase (HDAC) activity is required within the first hours of injury for amphibian tail regeneration, but transcriptional evidence was lacking.

This study used a chemical genetic screen of epigenetic-targeting compounds in an axolotl embryo tail amputation assay to identify epigenetic mechanisms required for regeneration and to define, transcriptionally, when HDAC activity is needed.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Tail amputation assay on developmental stage 42 axolotl embryos (removal of ~2 mm / ~20% distal tail), scored at 7 days post amputation (dpa).
2. Chemical genetic screen of 172 epigenetic-targeting compounds (Structural Genomics Consortium panel + Selleckchem library) at 10 μM (and 20 μM re-tests), scoring survival and tail patterning.
3. Focused temporal dosing experiments with the class I HDAC inhibitors romidepsin and belinostat (FDA-approved anticancer drugs), varying treatment windows from 1-minute post amputation (mpa) to continuous 7 dpa.
4. Microarray analysis (Ambystoma Affymetrix array) of romidepsin- and belinostat-treated versus control embryos at 0, 12, 24, 48, 72 hpa; second and third experiments at 3 and 6 hpa (0–3 hpa treatment) and at 3 hpa (0–1 mpa treatment). Data in GEO (GSE118515).
5. Gene ontology enrichment analysis (PANTHER); differential expression via limma with FDR <0.05 and 1.5-fold change criteria.
6. EdU cell proliferation assay to count dividing cells near the amputation plane at 3 hpa.

---

## Results
- 55 of 172 compounds reproducibly inhibited tail regeneration; HDAC inhibitors were the most frequent hit class (18 HDACi), followed by bromodomain inhibitors (8). ~47% of hits related to writing/reading of lysine acetylation.
- Romidepsin (class I HDACi) potently blocked regeneration; a treatment as brief as 1 minute post amputation (≥1.0 μM) was sufficient to prevent regeneration at 7 dpa, defining a narrow post-injury window. Pre-treatment 24 h before amputation did not inhibit.
- Romidepsin-treated embryos eventually resumed regeneration after 7 dpa but with abnormal, variable tailfin patterning; longer treatment gave more severe defects. Regenerated spinal cord length at 21 dpa did not differ from controls, suggesting the drug specifically affects tailfin mesenchyme progenitors while tailfin outgrowth is needed for spinal cord regeneration.
- Microarray: romidepsin altered early transcription at 3 and 6 hpa, affecting many transcriptional regulators, signaling factors, and patterning genes (Notch, BMP, Wnt, TGFβ, FGF, retinoic acid pathways; Hox genes). 110 upregulated genes encode transcription regulators. Effects were highly reproducible (r = 0.94 between 0–3 hpa and 0–1 mpa treatments).
- Romidepsin and belinostat induced similar, directional expression changes; romidepsin was more potent (dose-dependent, titratable).
- EdU counts showed no significant reduction in dividing cells at 3 hpa, so acute inhibition of regeneration was not due to global cell-cycle arrest.

---

## Perspective
The study provides direct transcriptional evidence that class I HDAC activity is required at the very moment of injury to shape the initial transcriptional response that enables successful tail (and spinal cord) regeneration. It establishes the axolotl embryo as a tractable chemical-genetic model linking epigenetic regulation to regenerative competence and draws parallels between mechanisms shared by cancer (HDACi/bromodomain targets) and tissue regeneration. Limitations include the correlative nature of microarray data, the inability to resolve which specific cell populations are affected against a background of abundant embryonic proliferation, and lack of direct histone-acetylation measurements. Future directions include cell-type-resolved analyses, bromodomain-inhibitor comparisons, and testing whether regeneration-specific signals de-repress HDAC corepressor complexes to control transcription temporally and spatially.
