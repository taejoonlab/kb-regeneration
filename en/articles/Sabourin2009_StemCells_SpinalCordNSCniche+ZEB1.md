# A mesenchymal-like ZEB1+ niche harbors dorsal radial glial fibrillary acidic protein-positive stem cells in the spinal cord

## Citation (NLM)
Sabourin JC, Ackema KB, Ohayon D, Guichet PO, Perrin FE, Garcès A, Ripoll C, Charité J, Simonneau L, Kettenmann H, Zine A, Privat A, Valmier J, Pattyn A, Hugnot JP. A mesenchymal-like ZEB1+ niche harbors dorsal radial glial fibrillary acidic protein-positive stem cells in the spinal cord. Stem Cells. 2009;27(11):2722-2733. doi:10.1002/stem.226

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1002/stem.226](https://doi.org/10.1002/stem.226)

---

## Background
Compared with the well-characterized brain neural stem cell (NSC) niches — the subventricular zone (SVZ) and the hippocampus — very little is known about the NSC niche of the adult spinal cord. Bona fide, long-term passageable neurosphere-forming stem cells appear to reside only around the central canal, and after trauma or in degenerative disease these cells migrate to the lesion and differentiate mainly into astrocytes and oligodendrocytes, potentially contributing to either glial scar exacerbation or axonal regeneration. However, the precise identity, location, and molecular signaling of these cells remained ill-defined and controversial (e.g., disagreement over whether the stem cells are GFAP-negative ependymal cells). This study set out to provide a detailed cellular and molecular characterization of this third CNS stem cell niche in the mouse.

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Microdissection of the central-canal region from adult (12-15 week) OF1 wild-type and hGFAP-GFP transgenic mouse spinal cord
2. Clonal neurosphere assay (1 cell/µl) from dissociated cells and directly from microdissected spinal cord slices to spatially localize the stem cells (dorsal vs ventral)
3. Flow cytometry (FACS) of GFAP-GFP+ cells to isolate and quantify the candidate stem-cell population
4. Immunohistochemistry for niche markers: GFAP, Sox2, Nestin, doublecortin (Dcx), Nkx6.1, CD15/LeX, Jagged1, Hes1, and DAN
5. Analysis of rostro-caudal (cervical vs lumbar) Hox gene expression to test positional identity of the niche cells
6. Detection of mesenchymal/EMT markers (ZEB1, smooth muscle actin) and functional in vitro knockdown of ZEB1 to test its role in NSC survival/expansion

## Results
1. Adult spinal cord NSCs are predominantly dorsally located GFAP+ cells lying ependymally and subependymally, extending radial processes toward the pial surface (dorsal radial-glia-like morphology), rather than GFAP-negative ependymal cells.
2. The niche additionally harbors Dcx+ Nkx6.1+ neurons that send processes into the central-canal lumen.
3. Cervical and lumbar NSCs maintain distinct rostro-caudal Hox gene combinations, indicating that positional identity is preserved in the niche.
4. The niche shows high levels of stem-cell signaling proteins CD15, Jagged1 (Notch ligand), Hes1, and DAN.
5. Unexpectedly, the niche displays mesenchymal traits, expressing the epithelial-mesenchymal-transition transcription factor ZEB1 and smooth muscle actin.
6. ZEB1 is essential for spinal cord NSC survival/expansion in vitro.
7. Niche proliferation progressively ceases around 13 weeks, when the spinal cord reaches its final size, suggesting an active role in postnatal growth.

## Perspective
The study establishes the adult spinal cord central canal as a third CNS stem cell niche, alongside the SVZ and hippocampus, but with its own distinct cellular composition, positional (Hox) identity, signaling milieu, and — surprisingly — mesenchymal-like features. Identifying ZEB1 as a survival factor and cataloging the niche's signaling components provide molecular handles that could ultimately be manipulated to promote endogenous regeneration after spinal cord trauma and degenerative disease.
