# Molecular Pathways Needed for Regeneration of Spinal Cord and Muscle in a Vertebrate

## Citation (NLM)
Beck CW, Christen B, Slack JMW. Molecular pathways needed for regeneration of spinal cord and muscle in a vertebrate. Dev Cell. 2003;5(3):429-439.

*(Note: the file stem is dated 2008, but the extracted article header and text indicate publication in Developmental Cell, Vol. 5, 429-439, September 2003.)*

---

## Background

Epimorphic regeneration — the regrowth of amputated structures from an anatomically complex stump — occurs rarely in adult vertebrates and has been studied most extensively in urodele amphibians (newts, salamanders). Anuran tadpoles (Xenopus) can also regenerate their tails, including spinal cord, notochord, and myotomes, up to metamorphosis. Unlike urodeles, however, Xenopus offers powerful molecular tools: transgenesis (Amaya and Kroll) and the inducible, heat-shock HSP70 promoter allow gain- and loss-of-function manipulation of candidate genes at postembryonic stages.

The authors exploited the observation that tail regenerative ability is not continuous through development. Using stage-specific amputation, they characterized a "refractory period" and asked which developmental signaling pathways (BMP, Notch) are necessary and sufficient for axial (spinal cord, muscle, notochord) regeneration.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Performed 50% tail amputations on Xenopus laevis tadpoles at defined stages (42, 46/47, 48, 49) and scored regeneration after 7 days; used Nomarski/DIC optics to follow wound epithelium and blastema/bud formation.
2. Analyzed expression of tail-development genes (Xbra, X-delta-1, lunatic fringe/Lfng, X-Notch-1, BMP4, Msx1, Msx2) by whole-mount in situ hybridization in regenerating vs. non-regenerating stumps.
3. Generated inducible transgenic F0 tadpoles (HGEM vector; HSP70 promoter driving the transgene, with γ-crystallin-GFP lens marker) expressing activated pathway components: constitutively active BMP receptor Alk3, constitutively active Notch intracellular domain (NICD), hyperactive eveMsx1, and a non-functional ΔNMsx1 control.
4. Generated inhibitory transgenics: dominant-negative BMP receptor tBR and BMP antagonist Noggin; validated Noggin transgene by heat-shocking at stage 14.
5. Blocked Notch signaling pharmacologically with the γ-secretase inhibitor MG132.
6. Performed epistasis experiments: (a) Alk3 transgenics ± MG132 during the refractory period; (b) double transgenics co-expressing HSP70-tBr (BMP block) and HSP70-NICD (Notch activation) in older tadpoles.
7. Assessed tissue composition of regenerates by immunohistochemistry: 12/101 (muscle), 2G9 (spinal cord), anti-collagen II (notochord).

---

## Results

- Regeneration was stage-dependent: ~68% of stage 42 and ~67% of stage 48 tails regenerated, but only ~7% at stage 46/47 (a "refractory period"), rising to near 100% at stage 49+. Refractory-stage stumps healed slowly under a thick skin-like epidermis without forming a blastema.
- Tail-development genes (Notch pathway: Notch-1, X-delta-1, Lfng; BMP pathway: BMP4, Msx1, Msx2; and Xbra) were reexpressed specifically in the regeneration bud within 24 h in regenerating tails but not in non-regenerating (refractory) tails.
- Activating BMP (Alk3) or Notch (NICD) during the refractory period was sufficient to induce regeneration: Alk3 gave faithful complete regenerates (90%) with muscle, spinal cord, notochord, and fin; NICD gave imperfect regenerates (73%) with spinal cord and notochord but little or no muscle.
- Hyperactive eveMsx1 could substitute for BMP, driving well-patterned tail regeneration (83%), including muscle; the repression-domain-deleted ΔNMsx1 control produced no regeneration.
- Inhibiting BMP (Noggin, tBR) or Notch (MG132) at later regenerating stages blocked regeneration; F1 Noggin transgenics reproducibly failed to regenerate (22/22) with no Msx1 expression, indicating that F0 variability was largely due to transgene insertion site.
- Epistasis: MG132 (Notch block) suppressed Alk3/BMP-induced regeneration; double tBr+NICD transgenics regenerated only spinal cord and notochord (no muscle). This places BMP upstream of Notch for spinal cord regeneration, with muscle regeneration requiring BMP but not Notch — mirroring embryonic tail development.

---

## Perspective

This work demonstrates, for the first time in an anuran, that axial regenerative ability is not continuous through development and that reactivation of the same developmental pathways (BMP and Notch) used in tail bud development is both necessary and sufficient for tail (spinal cord, notochord, muscle) regeneration. Msx1 is identified as a key BMP target driving regeneration. Critically, regenerative capability can be enabled during a non-regenerative (refractory) stage by genetic reactivation of specific developmental programs.

The BMP→Notch epistatic relationship, with a BMP-dependent/Notch-independent arm for muscle, provides a mechanistic framework. A central open question raised by the authors is whether activating these same pathways could achieve regeneration of differentiated axial tissues (including damaged spinal cord) in higher vertebrates, where such regeneration normally does not occur.
