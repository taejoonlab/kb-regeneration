# FoxJ1-dependent gene expression is required for differentiation of radial glia into ependymal cells and a subset of astrocytes in the postnatal brain

## Citation (NLM)
Jacquet BV, Salinas-Mondragon R, Liang H, Therit B, Buie JD, Dykstra M, Campbell K, Ostrowski LE, Brody SL, Ghashghaei HT. FoxJ1-dependent gene expression is required for differentiation of radial glia into ependymal cells and a subset of astrocytes in the postnatal brain. Development. 2009;136(23):4021-4031. doi:10.1242/dev.041129

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.041129](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.041129)

---

## Background

In the developing CNS, multipotent stem cells with radial glial features generate neurons and glia, and around birth radial glia are thought to differentiate into astrocytes and ependymal cells that line the cerebral ventricles. The astrocytes and ependymal cells separating the subventricular zone (SVZ) from the ventricular fluid form an adult stem cell niche (SCN) that supports lifelong olfactory bulb (OB) neurogenesis. The SCN is organized into three layers: an epithelial layer of ependymal cells and specialized astrocytes (arranged in rosette/pinwheel structures), a layer of transit-amplifying progenitors (TAPs), and migrating neuroblasts traveling via the rostral migratory stream (RMS) to the OB. The molecular mechanisms driving the time- and cell-specific differentiation of radial glia into ependymal cells and astrocytes were largely unknown.

Ependymal cell differentiation requires ciliogenesis (generation of motile multiciliated cells). The forkhead transcription factor FoxJ1 is necessary and sufficient for motile cilia generation in Xenopus and zebrafish, but its role in ependymal cell differentiation and SCN organization in the mammalian brain had not been defined. This study reports the expression and function of FoxJ1 during postnatal and adult SCN formation in mice.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. FoxJ1EGFP reporter mice, FoxJ1-null (FoxJ1–/–), and FoxJ1–/–EGFP mice used to map FoxJ1 expression and phenotypes across embryonic and postnatal stages.
2. Immunohistochemistry and in situ hybridization with cell-type markers (S100β, CD133/Prominin1, Gfap, Egfr, Blbp, nestin, Pax6, PSA-NCAM, CD31) to characterize FoxJ1+ cells in SVZ, RMS, and OB.
3. Transmission and scanning electron microscopy (TEM/SEM) of the ventricular surface to assess cilia, ependymal organization, junctional complexes, basal bodies, and deuterosomes.
4. Intraventricular lentiviral shFoxJ1 knockdown and cross-transplantation of FoxJ1+/+EGFP and FoxJ1–/–EGFP cells into wild-type and knockout hosts to test cell-autonomy.
5. Independent hydrocephalus models (genetic Dnaic1–/– and obstructive kaolin injection) to dissociate FoxJ1 phenotypes from hydrocephalus.
6. Neurosphere assays from FACS-sorted FoxJ1EGFP SVZ cells to test self-renewal and multipotency.
7. Laser-capture microdissection of FoxJ1EGFP domains (P1, P7, P14) with Affymetrix microarray transcriptome profiling; qRT-PCR validation; co-immunoprecipitation/Western blotting for γ-tubulin, dynein (Dnahc, Dnaic), and kinesin (Kif6).

## Results

- FoxJ1 is expressed in the adult SCN: most S100β+ CD133+ ependymal cells and an intriguing small subset of Gfap+ astrocytes (many contacting CD31+ blood vessels) express FoxJ1, whereas Egfr+ TAPs do not. RMS FoxJ1EGFP cells are Blbp+ nestin+ Pax6(low) progenitors; OB FoxJ1EGFP cells are largely astrocytes.
- FoxJ1+ cells emerge in two postnatal waves: FoxJ1 is markedly upregulated at birth (P0); S100β+ ependymal cells differentiate during the first postnatal week (by P6), and a small subset of FoxJ1+ SVZ astrocytes appears later, between P19–P21, increasing in density over the first 6 months.
- FoxJ1 is required for postnatal differentiation: FoxJ1–/– mice fail to form S100β+ ependymal cells, lack motile cilia, show disrupted ependymal organization and hydrocephalus, retain residual immature Blbp+ radial glia, and have severely reduced OB volume (66±12% reduction).
- FoxJ1 acts cell-autonomously: shRNA knockdown reproduced the null phenotype; wild-type cells transplanted into FoxJ1–/– ventricles differentiated into multiciliated ependymal cells, whereas FoxJ1–/– cells in wild-type ventricles remained immature (Blbp+, no CD133) and accumulated cytoplasmic γ-tubulin aggregates.
- Phenotypes are independent of hydrocephalus: Dnaic1–/– and kaolin hydrocephalus models retained S100β+ ependymal cells, unlike FoxJ1–/– mice.
- A subset of FoxJ1EGFP SCN cells self-renew as neurospheres (passaged ≥10 times) and differentiate into astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, and neurons, indicating neurogenic potential; EGFP– neurospheres began expressing FoxJ1EGFP in culture.
- Transcriptome analysis identified 198 consistently altered genes (197 downregulated in FoxJ1–/–); 55 had known function and ependymal expression, of which 24 (43%) encoded cytoskeleton-associated molecular motors — including six axonemal dynein heavy-chain isoforms, dynein intermediate chains, kinesins (Kif6/9/27), Rshl3, Tekt4, Ttll6. Co-IP showed Dnahc, Dnaic, and Kif6 form a complex with γ-tubulin in wild-type but not FoxJ1–/– brains, implicating FoxJ1 in basal body transport to the ventricular surface during ciliogenesis.

---

## Perspective

This work establishes FoxJ1 as a master regulator of the time- and cell-specific differentiation of radial glia into ependymal cells and a subset of astrocytes that constitute the postnatal/adult neural stem cell niche. By coupling loss-of-function, knockdown, and cross-transplantation, it demonstrates a cell-autonomous requirement, and the transcriptome/co-IP data provide a mechanistic model in which FoxJ1 drives expression of dynein/kinesin motors that transport basal bodies to the apical surface for motile ciliogenesis. Importantly, the ependymal and astrocytic defects are shown to be independent of hydrocephalus.

The discovery of a FoxJ1+ astrocyte subset with self-renewal and neurogenic potential links FoxJ1 to the identity and maintenance of adult neural stem cells, relevant to CNS regeneration and cell-based therapy. Limitations include reliance on whole-body knockout (partially addressed by shRNA and transplantation) and the focus on the brain SVZ rather than the spinal cord central canal, though the ependymal/radial glia biology is directly relevant to spinal cord ependymal stem cell niches. Future directions include defining direct FoxJ1 target promoters, dissecting the astrocyte-lineage program, and testing whether FoxJ1+ niche cells contribute to regeneration.
