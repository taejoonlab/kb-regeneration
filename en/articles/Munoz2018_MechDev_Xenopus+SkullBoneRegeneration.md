# Bone regeneration after traumatic skull injury in Xenopus tropicalis

## Citation (NLM)

Muñoz D, Castillo H, Henríquez JP, Marcellini S. Bone regeneration after traumatic skull injury in Xenopus tropicalis. _Mech Dev._ 2018;154:274-283. doi:[10.1016/j.mod.2018.06.007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mod.2018.06.007)

---

## Background

Mammalian skull defects exceeding a critical size cannot spontaneously regenerate, making the development of clinical therapies an important challenge. Non-mammalian vertebrates (fish, amphibians) possess superior tissue regeneration capabilities, and understanding their regenerative mechanisms offers key insights for regenerative medicine. The genus Xenopus has been widely used to study skin, spinal cord, tail, and limb regeneration, but a bone regeneration model for the skull via intramembranous ossification has not been established. This study established the first traumatic skull injury model in Xenopus tropicalis tadpoles during metamorphosis and comprehensively analyzed the bone regeneration process at morphological, histological, and molecular levels.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

**1. Traumatic skull injury induction**

- Xenopus tropicalis tadpoles at NF60 (pre-metamorphosis) to NF66 (completed metamorphosis)
- 500 μm circular defect drilled in the parietal skull using a 0.5 mm micro-drill
- Analysis at 5, 7, 15, and 30 days post-injury (dpi)

**2. Whole mount and tissue staining**

- Alizarin red (mineralized matrix) + Alcian blue (cartilage) double staining
- Alexa Fluor 488-phalloidin (actin cytoskeleton) and Hoechst (nuclei) staining for cellular structure visualization
- Fluorescence and confocal microscopy with 3D reconstruction

**3. In vivo double fluorochrome bone labeling**

- Alizarin red (0.1%) pulse administered 1 hour before injury → Calcein green (0.1%) pulse at 1, 7, 15 dpi
- Alizarin red single-positive = pre-existing bone before injury; Calcein green single-positive = newly formed regenerating bone
- Labeling validation in developing bone during NF56+ to NF57 progression

**4. Collagen second harmonic generation (SHG) imaging**

- Collagen I fiber organization analysis in coronal sections of skull at 15 and 30 dpi using two-photon microscopy
- Distinction between lamellar bone and woven bone

**5. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM)**

- Observation of collagen fibers and osteocytic lacunae after surface cell removal at 30 dpi

**6. In situ hybridization**

- Gene expression analysis of Col1a1, SPARC, and Dlx5 in osteoblasts at the regeneration site

## Results

**Establishment of a new X. tropicalis skull injury regeneration model** Immediately after drilling (0 dpi), a circular defect was clearly visible in the skull. At 5 dpi, the wound was covered by regenerated skin, but no cartilage was detected by Alcian blue staining, indicating intramembranous bone regeneration. Confocal microscopy revealed that the defect site at 0 dpi was largely hollow with few cells, whereas at 5 dpi, dense cell clusters and abundant angiogenesis were observed (Supplementary File S1, S2). Damaged bone fragments remained at the wound margins.

**In vivo double fluorochrome labeling demonstrates new bone formation** Alizarin red/Calcein green double labeling showed that newly mineralized matrix, Alizarin red-negative and Calcein green-positive, began to appear in the defect area at 7 dpi, indicating ongoing regenerating bone formation. By 15 dpi, the newly formed bone matrix had completely closed the defect. The double fluorochrome labeling distinguished three types of matrix: (1) Alizarin red single-positive pre-existing bone, (2) Alizarin red and Calcein green double-positive residual bone fragments at the drilling margin, and (3) Calcein green single-positive regenerating new bone.

**Bone matrix structural analysis** Collagen SHG imaging showed that normally developing bone and 30 dpi regenerated bone consisted of parallel collagen fiber layers (lamellar bone), whereas the 15 dpi regenerated matrix exhibited thin, irregular woven bone characteristics. This suggests rapid matrix deposition during early regeneration followed by remodeling into lamellar structure. SEM analysis revealed that regenerated bone contained circular cavities not observed in developing bone, and osteocytic lacunae and canaliculi were present, suggesting active osteocyte participation in the regenerative process.

**Histological and molecular characterization** H&E staining showed that existing bone at the wound margin contained empty lacunae, whereas newly deposited matrix was rich in lacunae containing osteocytes. The entire regeneration site was covered by a thick mesenchymal plug with abundant vascularization and a complete absence of chondrocytes or cartilage matrix. In situ hybridization revealed strong expression of Col1a1, SPARC, and Dlx5 in osteoblasts at the regeneration site at 15 and 30 dpi, confirming activation of the bone formation gene program.

---

## Perspective

This study reports the first intramembranous bone regeneration after traumatic skull injury in the amphibian Xenopus tropicalis. Key findings include:

First, 500 μm defects closed completely within 15 days with very high survival rates (often 100%), offering advantages as a reproducible regeneration model. Second, regeneration follows an intramembranous ossification pathway without a cartilage intermediate, with initial woven bone deposition maturing into normal lamellar bone within 30 days. Third, abundant angiogenesis accompanied regeneration from early stages (5 dpi), suggesting a conserved role for endothelial-osteoblast interactions in bone regeneration.

The origin of regenerative osteoblasts remains a key open question. Consistent with the dedifferentiation hypothesis reported in zebrafish studies, where peri-wound osteoblasts dedifferentiate, this study observed regenerating bone growing from the defect periphery toward the center. The possible involvement of Sox2+ progenitor cells cannot be excluded, but the "regeneration-competent/incompetent stage" reported in spinal cord regeneration does not appear to apply to bone tissue.

Given that Xenopus is amenable to drug and genetic screening (CRISPR/Cas9, transgenesis), this model provides a powerful platform for discovering and functionally analyzing key molecular regulators of intramembranous bone regeneration. Future in-depth studies are needed on the morphogenetic rules of bone regeneration, the influence of mechanical environment, and the role of bone remodeling (osteoclasts).
