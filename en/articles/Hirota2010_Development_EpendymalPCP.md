# Planar polarity of multiciliated ependymal cells involves the anterior migration of basal bodies regulated by non-muscle myosin II

## Citation (NLM)
Hirota Y, Meunier A, Huang S, Shimozawa T, Yamada O, Kida YS, Inoue M, Ito T, Kato H, Sakaguchi M, Sunabori T, Nakaya M, Nonaka S, Ogura T, Higuchi H, Okano H, Spassky N, Sawamoto K. Planar polarity of multiciliated ependymal cells involves the anterior migration of basal bodies regulated by non-muscle myosin II. Development. 2010;137(18):3037-3046. doi:10.1242/dev.050120

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.050120](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.050120)

---

## Background
Motile cilia generate directional fluid flow over epithelial tissue, a function that depends on the planar polarity of cilia and orientation of their basal bodies. Multiciliated ependymal cells line the brain ventricles and drive cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow. Two types of planar polarity have been described in ependymal cells: "rotational" polarity (direction of the basal foot), whose cellular/molecular mechanisms via the planar cell polarity (PCP) pathway and fluid flow are established, and "translational" polarity (anterior positioning of basal bodies within the apical membrane), whose formation during postnatal ependymal development had not been elucidated.

Non-muscle myosin II (NMII), a hexameric motor protein whose activity is regulated by phosphorylation of its regulatory light chains via ROCK and MLCK, had been implicated in PCP formation in other tissues, and its disruption causes hydrocephalus. This study examined how translational polarity is established and the role of NMII in ependymal cell differentiation.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Whole-mount immunostaining of lateral ventricular walls (markers: pericentrin/Pcnt for basal bodies, ZO1 for tight junctions) from mice of different postnatal ages (P5, P7, P9, adult); quantification of ciliated-area centroid position by quadrant.
2. Time-lapse imaging of GFP-labeled basal bodies in living ventricular walls of P5-8 Centrin2::GFP (Cent2GFP) transgenic mice embedded in Matrigel.
3. High-speed live imaging (up to 917 fps) of ciliary beating in brain slices from P4-6 and P14-19 Cent2GFP mice, with circular statistics (CSD) and ciliary beat frequency (CBF) measurement.
4. Primary ependymal cell culture with/without artificial fluid flow; transfection of dominant-negative Dvl2 constructs (Dvl2ΔPDZ-GFP, Dvl2-C-GFP) and lentiviral delivery in vivo to test PCP signaling.
5. NMII perturbation: pharmacological inhibitors (blebbistatin/myosin II, ML-9/MLCK, Y27632/ROCK, SP600125/JNK) injected into P5 lateral ventricles, and vector-based RNAi knockdown of Myh9/Myh10/Myh14 by electroporation; immunostaining for NMHC isoforms and pMLC.

---

## Results
- Ependymal cells acquire translational polarity between P5 and P9: basal bodies, initially broadly dispersed in the apical membrane, become densely accumulated in the anterior region (peaking at P9), with the ciliated-area centroid significantly enriched in the anterior quadrant from P7 onward.
- Time-lapse imaging showed all basal bodies within a single cell migrate simultaneously toward the anterior apical membrane over ~10 hours, providing direct evidence of basal body movement after docking.
- Translational and rotational polarity are established at around the same developmental stage; cells with anteriorly localized ("localized") basal bodies showed coordinated anterior-directed beating, longer cilia, and higher ciliary beat frequency than dispersed cells.
- PCP signaling (Dvl2) regulates rotational but not translational polarity: dominant-negative Dvl2 disrupted coordinated ciliary beating and basal foot orientation (rotational), but did not affect anterior localization of basal bodies; fluid flow alone was insufficient to induce translational polarity in culture.
- NMII regulates translational but not rotational polarity: NMHC isoforms Myh9 (broad), Myh10 and Myh14 (near basal bodies), and pMLC localized in differentiating ependymal cells. Blebbistatin and ML-9 (but not Y27632) dispersed basal bodies and dose-dependently reduced anterior localization; Myh9/Myh10/Myh14 knockdown likewise dispersed basal bodies cell-autonomously without impairing ciliogenesis or ciliary beating.

---

## Perspective
The study demonstrates that ependymal translational polarity is established by simultaneous anterior migration of docked basal bodies within the apical membrane, and identifies NMII (activated via MLCK) as a key regulator of this process, distinct from the PCP-pathway control of rotational polarity. Thus the two planar polarities are governed by independent cellular and molecular mechanisms. The authors propose that rotational polarity is triggered by CSF flow and PCP signaling, while translational polarity is initiated by anterior basal-body localization in radial glial primary cilia and completed by basal body migration during the radial-glia-to-ependymal transition. The signal activating NMII-driven migration remains unclear, with calcium/calmodulin-MLCK signaling suggested as a candidate. This work is directly relevant to ependymal cell biology (a CNS cell type central to spinal cord neural stem cell niches) and CSF dynamics.
