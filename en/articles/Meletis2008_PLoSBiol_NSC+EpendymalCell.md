# Spinal Cord Injury Reveals Multilineage Differentiation of Ependymal Cells

## Citation (NLM)
Meletis K, Barnabé-Heider F, Carlén M, Evergren E, Tomilin N, Shupliakov O, Frisén J. Spinal cord injury reveals multilineage differentiation of ependymal cells. PLoS Biol. 2008;6(7):e182. doi:10.1371/journal.pbio.0060182

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.0060182](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.0060182)

---

## Background
Spinal cord injury frequently results in permanent functional impairment. Transplantation of stem cells can improve functional recovery after spinal cord injury in rodents and primates, with benefits attributed to replacement of lost cells, secretion of neurotrophic factors, and—probably most importantly—the generation of oligodendrocytes that remyelinate spared axons near the lesion. Neural stem cells present in the adult spinal cord can be propagated in vitro and promote recovery when transplanted to the injured cord, demonstrating that cells capable of promoting regeneration exist but normally fail to do so efficiently. Manipulating endogenous neural stem cells would offer a noninvasive alternative to transplantation, avoiding the need for immune suppression.

However, this approach has been held back by difficulties in identifying adult spinal cord neural stem cells. Indirect studies had variously proposed that the neural stem cell potential resides in the white matter parenchyma or close to the central canal, either in the ependymal layer or subependymally. This study uses genetic fate mapping to characterize a candidate neural stem cell population and to determine its identity and its response to injury.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. **Generation of transgenic mouse lines**: Two lines expressing tamoxifen-dependent Cre recombinase (CreER) were made—Nestin-CreER (rat nestin second-intron enhancer with minimal hsp68 promoter) and FoxJ1-CreER (human FOXJ1 promoter fused to a CreERT2-ires-EGFP construct). FoxJ1 is specific to cells with motile cilia.
2. **Inducible genetic fate mapping**: Mice were crossed to R26R (β-gal) or Z/EG (GFP) Cre reporter backgrounds. Recombination was induced by five daily intraperitoneal tamoxifen injections, permanently and heritably labeling CreER-expressing cells lining the central canal.
3. **Phenotypic and ultrastructural characterization**: Immunohistochemistry for neural stem/progenitor markers (nestin, vimentin, CD133/prominin-1, Musashi1, PDGFR-α, Sox2, Sox3, Sox9, GFAP, Olig2) plus immunoelectron microscopy and serial-section ultrastructural analysis to classify ependymal cell morphology.
4. **In vitro neural stem cell assays**: Neurosphere formation at clonal density, serial passaging (self-renewal), and multipotency differentiation assays; recombination frequency in neurospheres was normalized to in vivo recombination frequency.
5. **Prospective isolation by flow cytometry**: FACS sorting of GFP+ (FoxJ1 ires-GFP) versus GFP− cells to test neurosphere-forming potential independently of Cre recombination.
6. **In vivo self-renewal assessment**: One month of BrdU in drinking water to label dividing cells; tracking recombination rate from 2 days up to 10 months after tamoxifen.
7. **Spinal cord injury models**: Dorsal funiculus incision (and lateral funiculus incision) at mid-thoracic level, with a 6-day washout between last tamoxifen dose and injury to ensure recombination preceded injury. Sham lesions and no-tamoxifen controls were included.
8. **Fate analysis of injury-induced progeny**: Marker analysis (Sox9, vimentin, GFAP, nestin, Olig2, NeuN), analysis of scar composition, CSPG/laminin/fibronectin distribution, neurofilament axon relationships, and correlative light/electron microscopy of remyelinating cells.

## Results
- **CreER expression is restricted to the ependymal layer.** In both Nestin-CreER and FoxJ1-CreER lines, CreER was limited to cells lining the central canal. Tamoxifen induced recombination in 82 ± 4% (Nestin-CreER) and 88 ± 4% (FoxJ1-CreER) of transgene-expressing cells; recombination without tamoxifen was exceptionally rare.
- **Ependymal cells express immature neural markers but not GFAP.** They are immunoreactive for Crocc (ciliated-cell marker), nestin, vimentin, CD133, Musashi1, PDGFR-α, Sox2, Sox3, and Sox9, but negative for GFAP and the oligodendroglial marker Olig2. Ultrastructure revealed three related morphologies—cuboidal ependymal cells, tanycytes, and radial ependymal cells—with frequent intermediate phenotypes and a homogeneous molecular profile.
- **Neural stem cell potential resides largely in ependymal cells.** 76 ± 5.7% (Nestin-CreER) and 85 ± 2.2% (FoxJ1-CreER) of neurospheres were recombined; normalization to in vivo recombination rate indicated that close to all neurosphere-initiating potential resides in ependymal cells. Recombined neurospheres were 100% multipotent (neurons, astrocytes, oligodendrocytes) and could be serially passaged at least eight times. FACS-sorted GFP+ ependymal cells formed neurospheres (0.18 ± 0.06%), whereas GFP− non-ependymal cells formed none.
- **Ependymal cells self-renew in vivo.** One-month BrdU labeled 19.9 ± 4.2% of ependymal cells, mostly found in pairs (self-duplication). Recombination rate remained unchanged from 2 days to 10 months, indicating maintenance by self-renewal rather than replenishment from another population, with no evidence of progeny leaving the ependymal layer under normal conditions.
- **Injury activates ependymal cells.** A dorsal funiculus incision (not compromising the ependymal layer) dramatically increased ependymal proliferation. By 4 days, recombined cells migrated out of the ependymal layer, lost ependymal markers (Sox2, Sox3), and most expressed Sox9 with some expressing GFAP. The response was restricted to the injured segment; sham lesions produced no emigrating recombined cells.
- **Ependymal progeny contribute substantially to the glial scar.** Recombined cells accumulated in the forming scar and occupied 18.3 ± 6.9% of scar area at 2 weeks, persisting at least 10 months. The scar comprised two astrocyte-like populations: a majority Sox9+/vimentin+ population derived from ependymal cells (core of scar) and a mainly GFAP+/nestin+ population of reactive resident astrocytes (near cord surface). Lateral incisions produced fewer migrating progeny, suggesting midline process severance augments but is not required for activation. CXCR4 was largely negative, arguing against SDF1/CXCR4-mediated attraction.
- **Ependymal progeny do not produce the main axon-inhibitory factor.** Ependyma-derived cells formed a complementary, non-overlapping pattern with CSPG-immunoreactive areas, and were often in direct proximity to neurofilament+ axonal sprouts, arguing they are not a major impediment to axonal growth and may support local sprouting.
- **Ependymal cells generate myelinating oligodendrocytes.** A subpopulation of recombined cells expressed Olig2 and, by 10 months, displayed mature oligodendrocyte morphology with processes enwrapping MBP-immunoreactive myelin around axons. Serial-section electron microscopy confirmed mature oligodendrocyte ultrastructure and myelin-sheath association. No recombined cells showed neuronal morphology or NeuN expression.

---

## Perspective
This work establishes that ependymal cells lining the central canal constitute the vast majority of in vitro neural stem cell potential in the adult spinal cord. They self-renew in vivo but are largely quiescent under homeostasis; upon injury they proliferate, migrate, and generate astrocytes (the major fate, forming much of the glial scar) and, to a lesser degree, myelinating oligodendrocytes.

The significance lies in identifying an endogenous, injury-responsive stem cell population that could be pharmacologically manipulated as a noninvasive alternative to cell transplantation. Because the differentiation pattern of injury-activated ependymal cells resembles that of transplanted in vitro-expanded neural stem cells—whose recovery benefit is enhanced when biased toward oligodendrocyte production—promoting oligodendrocyte generation from ependymal cells in vivo could potentially improve recovery. Notably, ependyma-derived scar cells avoid the growth-inhibitory CSPG domains, suggesting they are not a major barrier to axonal regrowth.

Limitations acknowledged by the authors: stem cell behavior cannot be conclusively demonstrated at the single-cell level in vivo, so ependymal cells cannot be firmly declared stem cells in vivo despite population-level self-renewal and multilineage output. Parenchymal progenitors (NG2/Olig2/Nkx2.2) may still contribute under other conditions, and some early Olig2+ ependymal progeny might represent regenerated parenchymal progenitors. Future directions center on developing pharmacological strategies to modulate the fate of endogenous ependymal progeny after spinal cord injury.
