# Amputation-Induced Reactive Oxygen Species Signaling is Required for Axolotl Tail Regeneration

## Citation (NLM)
Al Haj Baddar NW, Chithrala A, Voss SR. Amputation-Induced Reactive Oxygen Species Signaling is Required for Axolotl Tail Regeneration. Dev Dyn. 2019;248(3):189-196. doi:10.1002/dvdy.5

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1002/dvdy.5](https://doi.org/10.1002/dvdy.5)

---

## Background

Salamanders (urodele amphibians) are unparalleled among vertebrates in their ability to regenerate appendages throughout life, yet the earliest signals that initiate regeneration remain poorly understood. Most work on salamander appendage regeneration has focused on progenitor cells recruited relatively late in the process, while the injury-associated cues that precede progenitor proliferation have received less attention.

In other regenerative models (fish, frog tadpoles, planaria), injury cues such as ion gradients, hypoxia, and reactive oxygen species (ROS) help initiate regeneration. ROS—including superoxide anion (O2-), hydrogen peroxide, and hydroxyl radicals—are components of innate immune responses but at moderate concentrations also act in wound healing, cellular signaling, and gene expression. This study tests whether amputation-induced ROS are produced and required for tail (and spinal cord) regeneration in the primary salamander model, the axolotl (*Ambystoma mexicanum*).

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **Tail amputation regeneration assay** on stage 42 axolotl embryos: removal of the distal 2 mm of tail; regeneration length measured relative to the non-regenerating notochord as a fixed landmark.
2. **In vivo ROS imaging** with dihydroethidium (DHE), a dye that reports superoxide anion production as a red fluorescent (ethidium) signal; fluorescence quantified in the distal tail by ImageJ over a 0–7 dpa time course.
3. **Pharmacological NOX (NADPH oxidase) inhibition** using diphenyleneiodonium chloride (DPI, 4 µM) and VAS2870 (5 µM) to test the requirement of ROS-generating enzyme activity; treatment windows extended in 24-h intervals.
4. **Histology** with hematoxylin & eosin (H&E) staining of longitudinal sections to detail tissue changes (wound epithelium, spinal cord/ependymal tube, cartilaginous rod).
5. **Cell proliferation assays**: phospho-histone H3 (PH3) immunostaining for mitosis (mitotic index by tissue compartment) and EdU incorporation for S-phase/cell-cycle re-entry.

---

## Results

- The tail amputation removes the tail tip; a thin epithelium covers the wound by ~40 min post-amputation (mpa). By 2 dpa the wound is sealed and the regenerating spinal cord (terminal vesicle/ependymal tube) forms dorsal to the notochord, extending ~100 µm beyond the amputation plane; a cartilaginous rod forms ventral to the regenerating spinal cord by 7–12 dpa.
- DHE staining showed ROS production rose within minutes of amputation in midline axial tissues (somites, spinal cord, notochord), remained elevated through 24 hpa, then dropped to basal levels—linking ROS to early wound-healing events preceding proliferation.
- NOX inhibition reduced ROS: VAS2870 (5 µM) significantly lowered ROS at 6 hpa but was lethal past 24 hpa; DPI (4 µM) lowered ROS by 24 hpa. Only sustained DPI treatment (0–3 dpa) significantly inhibited tail and spinal cord regeneration, implicating NOX activity after 24 hpa.
- In DPI-treated embryos, mesenchyme failed to form distal to the amputation plane, spinal cord regeneration was reduced, and the cartilaginous rod did not form.
- DPI significantly reduced PH3+ mitotic cells (notably in the spinal cord and epidermis, but not mesenchyme) and reduced EdU incorporation, indicating NOX/ROS are required for cell-cycle re-entry and the proliferative response driving regenerate outgrowth—especially in the spinal cord.

---

## Perspective

This is the first study to demonstrate a requirement for NOX activity and ROS signaling in urodele appendage (tail and spinal cord) regeneration, extending a phylogenetically conserved role for ROS previously shown in planaria, zebrafish fin, Xenopus tail, and adult newt brain. The findings position ROS as an early, injury-induced, instructive (if not initiating) cue upstream of progenitor proliferation.

Limitations: the direct molecular targets of ROS during axolotl regeneration were not identified; the authors speculate—based on zebrafish and Xenopus work—that ROS may act on mitogens such as SHH, BMP, FGF, and Wnt/β-catenin signaling. Higher VAS2870 doses were lethal, constraining pharmacological analysis. Future work should define which signaling pathways are redox-regulated in the axolotl tail regeneration model and whether ROS instruct blastema/spinal cord patterning directly.
