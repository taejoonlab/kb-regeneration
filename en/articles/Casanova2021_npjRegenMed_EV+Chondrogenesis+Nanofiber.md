# Chondrogenic differentiation induced by extracellular vesicles bound to a nanofibrous substrate

## Citation (NLM)

Casanova MR, Osório H, Reis RL, Martins A, Neves NM. Chondrogenic differentiation induced by extracellular vesicles bound to a nanofibrous substrate. npj Regen Med. 2021;6(1):79. doi:10.1038/s41536-021-00190-8

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41536-021-00190-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41536-021-00190-8)

---

## Background

Human articular cartilage has limited or no self-repair capacity, and damaged cartilage typically requires surgical interventions such as autologous chondrocyte implantation (ACI) or matrix-induced ACI (MACI). Human bone marrow mesenchymal stem cells (hBM-MSCs) are attractive candidates for cartilage regeneration, but obtaining a well-defined population that maintains a stable cartilaginous phenotype—without progressing to osteogenesis or hypertrophy—remains difficult with standard chondrogenic media. Extracellular vesicles (EVs) are lipid particles that carry proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids to target cells and are recognized for regenerative potential, yet their capacity to promote cartilage repair, particularly EVs derived from human articular chondrocytes (hACs), had not been thoroughly investigated. This study aimed to develop an EV immobilization system able to selectively bind EVs from conditioned medium of hACs or hBM-MSCs and to test whether bound EVs can guide the chondrogenic commitment of naïve hBM-MSCs without any further medium supplementation.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

**1. Fabrication of the biofunctional nanofibrous substrate**
- Electrospun polycaprolactone (PCL) nanofibrous meshes (NFMs) activated by UV-ozone and grafted with amine groups using 1,6-hexanediamine
- Anti-CD63 antibody covalently immobilized via EDC/NHS coupling; maximum immobilization at ~4 μg mL⁻¹, verified by fluorescence microscopy

**2. EV sourcing and immobilization**
- Conditioned media collected from hACs (expansion medium ACEM, differentiation medium ACDM) and hBM-MSCs (basal medium MSCBM, chondrogenic medium MSCCM)
- EVs quantified by CD63 exosome ELISA (6.8–11.2 × 10⁸ particles mL⁻¹) and bound to the anti-CD63 NFM at 5.6–6.9 × 10⁸ particles mL⁻¹
- Bound EVs (~100 nm) characterized by SEM, EDS (phospholipid P element), and CD63/CD81/CD9 immunofluorescence

**3. Proteomic and bioinformatic analysis of bound EVs**
- Label-free nanoLC-MS/MS proteomics of the four EV systems
- Venn, Gene Ontology (biological process / cellular component / molecular function), and heat-map clustering

**4. Chondrogenic assessment of hBM-MSCs**
- hBM-MSCs seeded on the four EV systems and cultured 28 days in basal medium only (no supplementation); controls were non-biofunctionalized NFMs in basal (NFM_Ctrl−) or standard chondrogenic (NFM_Ctrl+) medium
- Biochemistry: MTS metabolism, DNA proliferation, Micro-BCA protein, GAG/DNA
- qPCR: chondrogenic Sox9, COMP, Aggrecan, Collagen type II; hypertrophic Collagen type I, Collagen type X
- Histology: Alcian blue (sulfated proteoglycans), immunodetection of collagen type II and type Iα

---

## Results

**EVs were selectively captured and phenotypically confirmed**
The anti-CD63 biofunctional NFM bound EVs from all four conditioned media at 5.6–6.9 × 10⁸ particles mL⁻¹ regardless of culture condition (culture condition did not affect EV yield). Bound vesicles of ~100 nm carried the tetraspanins CD63, CD81, and CD9, and proteomic profiling identified 10, 15, 20, and 5 proteins for ACEM, ACDM, MSCBM, and MSCCM systems respectively, with five proteins common across systems and enrichment for extracellular-space and vesicle components.

**Bound EVs drove chondrogenic commitment of hBM-MSCs without medium supplementation**
hBM-MSCs on the EV systems showed significantly higher metabolic activity than controls at day 14 (p < 0.0001), with proliferation and protein synthesis comparable to controls. GAG synthesis was significantly higher on NFM/EV-ACEM, NFM/EV-ACDM, and NFM/EV-MSCCM than basal control, and the NFM/EV-ACDM system produced significantly more GAG than NFM/EV-ACEM at day 21 (p < 0.01).

**hAC-derived EVs from differentiation medium gave the strongest chondrogenic signal**
Sox9, COMP, Aggrecan, and Collagen type II were overexpressed in all systems except NFM/EV-MSCBM. At day 28, NFM/EV-ACEM and NFM/EV-ACDM induced significantly higher Sox9 and COMP than the standard chondrogenic control (NFM_Ctrl+, p < 0.0001), and NFM/EV-ACDM produced significantly higher Aggrecan and Collagen type II than NFM_Ctrl+ and NFM/EV-MSCCM (p < 0.01). Alcian blue and collagen type II immunostaining confirmed a cartilaginous ECM—most evident in NFM/EV-ACDM—while the MSCBM system showed no proteoglycan staining.

**Low hypertrophic drift**
Hypertrophy markers Collagen type X and type Iα were expressed at consistently low levels across all EV systems, and significantly lower than the standard chondrogenic control over time, together with absent collagen type Iα immunostaining—indicating the EV systems favor a stable hyaline-like phenotype rather than progression toward hypertrophy/osteogenesis.

---

## Perspective

This work demonstrates that naturally secreted EVs, immobilized on a biofunctional nanofibrous substrate through an anti-CD63 antibody, can direct the chondrogenic commitment of homotypic hBM-MSCs more effectively than conventional chondroinductive media—without any soluble chemical or genetic inducers.

First, the anti-CD63 immobilization strategy provides sustained, local presentation of bioactive EV cargo at the surface, avoiding the rapid degradation that limits soluble-protein or free-RNA delivery, and offering a cell-free / secretome-based alternative to direct cell injection.

Second, EVs from hACs cultured under differentiation medium (NFM/EV-ACDM) consistently outperformed the other sources, matching the bioinformatic finding that their cargo differs from expansion-medium and MSC-derived EVs—supporting the idea that donor-cell chondrogenic status shapes the regenerative EV cargo.

Third, the low and stable expression of hypertrophy markers (Collagen type X, type Iα) is clinically relevant for cartilage regeneration, as it suggests reduced risk of fibrocartilage or endochondral/hypertrophic failure—an important distinction given that dedifferentiation toward a fibroblast-like phenotype is a central obstacle in cartilage tissue engineering.

The authors envision translation via microfracture (implanting the EV system so that migrating homotypic cells differentiate in situ) or an autologous MACI-like ex vivo approach. Key limitations are the absence of in vivo validation and the use of a single donor per cell type; confirming cartilage regeneration in animal models remains the next step.
