# Microglia-organized scar-free spinal cord repair in neonatal mice

## Citation (NLM)
Li Y, He X, Kawaguchi R, Zhang Y, Wang Q, Monavarfeshani A, Yang Z, Chen B, Shi Z, Meng H, Zhou S, Zhu J, Jacobi A, Swarup V, Popovich PG, Geschwind DH, He Z. Microglia-organized scar-free spinal cord repair in neonatal mice. Nature. 2020;587(7835):613-618. doi:10.1038/s41586-020-2795-6

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-020-2795-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-020-2795-6)

---

## Background

Spinal cord injury (SCI) in adult mammals triggers formation of a scar composed of reactive astrocytes, fibroblasts, microglia, and macrophages, with little spontaneous axon regeneration. By contrast, fish and amphibians form permissive glial/meningeal bridges that allow axons to regenerate across the lesion. How the mammalian spinal cord organizes scar-based wound healing—and why regeneration fails—remained poorly understood, further complicated by the diminished intrinsic growth capacity of mature neurons.

The authors asked whether spinal cord regeneration occurs in neonatal mice, a developmental stage when CNS neurons still have strong growth capacity, and set out to define the cellular and molecular basis of any observed repair.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **Age-dependent crush injury**: T10 spinal cord crush at P2, P7, P20, and adult stages; axon regrowth assessed via serotonergic (5-HT) axon staining and AAV-ChR2-mCherry-labeled corticospinal tract (CST) axons.
2. **Lesion characterization**: immunostaining for CD68, GFAP, fibronectin, collagen I, CSPG, laminin, P2Y12 (homeostatic microglia), CD31 (vasculature) across time points (1-14 dpi).
3. **Microglia depletion**: CSF1R inhibitor PLX3397 and Cx3cr1cre;Csf1rfl/fl conditional knockout in neonates.
4. **Lineage/reporter mice**: Cx3cr1GFP, Ccr2RFP (monocyte-derived macrophages).
5. **scRNA-seq (10X)**: CD11b+CD45low-sorted cells from lesion at 0, 3, 5 dpi; cell-cycle regression and re-clustering into microglia states MG0-MG4; GO and gene regulatory network analyses.
6. **Cell-type-specific fibronectin deletion**: Fn1fl/fl crossed with Cx3cr1cre (microglia), Albcre (liver), Tie2cre (endothelium).
7. **Transplantation**: neonatal (P1) microglia, or adult microglia treated with proteinase inhibitors (E64 + serpinA3N), transplanted into adult SCI lesions.

---

## Results

- **Neonatal (P2) crush injury heals scar-free**: serotonergic and CST axons grow across the lesion, whereas P7, P20, and adult injuries show little/no regrowth. P2 lesions have minimal CD68+ cells and ECM deposition, continuous CD31+ vasculature, and evenly distributed P2Y12+ microglia.
- **Transient microglial activation**: after P2 injury, microglia accumulate at stumps (2 dpi), lose P2Y12 and become amoeboid/SPP1+/CD68+, form fibronectin+ bridges (3 dpi), then revert to a homeostatic (P2Y12+, SPP1−/CD68−) state by ~7 dpi. Monocyte-derived (CCR2-RFP+) macrophages appear transiently, unlike their persistence in adults.
- **Microglia are required**: PLX3397 depletion or Csf1r conditional KO disrupts bridge formation, leads to a GFAP+ astroglial scar, and stalls axon regrowth.
- **Repair-promoting microglial signature (scRNA-seq)**: injury-induced states MG1 and MG3. MG3 (Ms4a7+Fn1+, immediately in the lesion) is enriched for ECM/wound-healing genes (Fn1, Thbs1, Anxa1), anti-inflammatory and peptidase-inhibitor genes (Cstb, Stfa1, Serpinb6a). Adult microglia instead show persistent Fn1 and no proteinase-inhibitor induction.
- **Microglia-derived fibronectin is essential**: only Cx3cr1cre;Fn1fl/fl mice (not liver- or endothelium-specific deletions) show defective bridge formation and reduced axon growth.
- **Therapeutic transfer to adults**: transplanting neonatal microglia, or proteinase-inhibitor-treated adult microglia, into adult SCI lesions reduces collagen I/CSPG deposition and immune infiltration, restores homeostatic microglia, and increases serotonergic axon crossing (neonatal microglia most effective).

---

## Perspective

This work reveals that neonatal mice, like fish and amphibians, achieve scar-free spinal cord healing and spontaneous axon regrowth, with microglia acting as the primary orchestrator through two mechanisms: (1) transiently secreting fibronectin/ECM to bridge severed stumps, and (2) expressing peptidase inhibitors and anti-inflammatory molecules that resolve inflammation and prevent scarring. Crucially, the temporal control of microglial activation—transient rather than persistent—is key to scar-free repair.

The transplantation experiments demonstrate translational potential: recapitulating neonatal microglial properties (via cell transfer or pharmacological proteinase inhibition) improves healing and axon regrowth in adult SCI, suggesting concrete strategies for adult CNS repair. Limitations noted include that regenerating vs. late-arriving axons cannot be fully distinguished, the contribution of descending axons vs. intraspinal reorganization to functional recovery is unknown, adult transplants remained less effective than neonatal microglia (implying additional unidentified factors), and no sample-size predetermination or blinding was performed. Future work should identify further molecular components restoring homeostatic microglia and test proteinase inhibitors in neurodegenerative contexts.
