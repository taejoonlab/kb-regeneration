# Musashi and Plasticity of Xenopus and Axolotl Spinal Cord Ependymal Cells

## Citation (NLM)
Chernoff EAG, Sato K, Salfity HVN, Sarria DA, Belecky-Adams T. Musashi and Plasticity of Xenopus and Axolotl Spinal Cord Ependymal Cells. Front Cell Neurosci. 2018;12:45. doi:10.3389/fncel.2018.00045

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.3389/fncel.2018.00045](https://doi.org/10.3389/fncel.2018.00045)

---

## Background
Ependymal cells (ependymoglia) line the central canal of the spinal cord in all vertebrates and act as an injury-reactive stem/progenitor population that reconstructs the central canal during regeneration. The ependymal response after spinal cord injury (SCI) is far more complete and beneficial in regeneration-competent amphibians than in mammals. However, this response is not uniform: it differs between anurans (frogs/toads, which regenerate only as young tadpoles) and urodeles (salamanders/newts, strong regenerators into adulthood), and it changes with life stage. This study compares the differentiated state of ependymal cells between a constitutively active developing organism (Xenopus laevis tadpole) and a quiescent-but-activatable adult salamander (Ambystoma mexicanum, the Axolotl).

The RNA-binding protein Musashi (Msi), a member of the Notch signaling pathway, maintains neural stem/progenitor status by binding Numb mRNA to suppress Numb translation, thereby sustaining Notch signaling and preventing premature neuronal differentiation. Msi-1/Msi-2 (originally identified as Nrp-1/Xrp-1 in Xenopus ependymal cells) are therefore candidate markers of the plasticity required for ependymal participation in spinal cord regeneration. The authors examine Msi-1 and Msi-2 expression across life stage, regeneration competence, and anuran vs. urodele cord, relating it to the dorsoventral patterning gene Shh, the intermediate filament GFAP, and the proliferation marker PCNA.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Spinal cord transection (lesioning) surgery in staged Xenopus laevis tadpoles (NF 50-64) and in embryonic, juvenile, and adult Axolotls.
2. Molecular cloning of Axolotl Msi-1 and Msi-2 and previously unknown Xenopus Msi-2 isoforms; sequencing and multispecies amino-acid alignment (ClustalX) of RNA-recognition motifs (RRM1/RRM2).
3. Semi-quantitative RT-PCR for Msi-1, Msi-2, and Shh (GAPDH as housekeeping control) across life stages and regeneration time points, using primers outside the mRNA-binding domains to avoid Msi-1/Msi-2 cross-reaction.
4. Section and dish in situ hybridization with digoxigenin-labeled riboprobes to localize Msi-1 and Shh mRNA in developing and regenerating cord.
5. Immunohistochemistry for Msi-1, GFAP, and PCNA (with DAPI nuclear counterstain) on paraffin sections.
6. Primary ependymal cell culture from Xenopus tadpole cord and injury-responsive adult Axolotl cord on poly-D-lysine/fibronectin dishes in EGF-containing defined medium; F-actin (phalloidin) imaging to assess mesenchymal outgrowth.

---

## Results
- **Sequence conservation**: Vertebrate Msi-1 is highly conserved; RRM1/RRM2 showed >90% amino-acid identity among Xenopus, Axolotl, mouse, and human. Both Xenopus and Axolotl produce long and short Msi-2 isoforms; the Axolotl short form has a truncated RRM2 (potentially affecting mRNA binding), and a previously unreported short Xenopus Msi-2 isoform was cloned.
- **Xenopus expression**: Msi-1 was strongly expressed in regeneration-competent NF 50-54 cord and in 5-day regenerating cord, but was greatly reduced at non-regenerating NF 62+ and in adult frog. The smaller Msi-2 isoform appeared only in non-regenerating (NF 62+) tadpoles and adult frogs.
- **Axolotl expression**: Msi-1 was strong in late-tailbud embryos and juvenile brain/cord but absent in intact adult cord; it was strongly up-regulated after transection (3 weeks regenerating adult cord). Msi-2 persisted in intact adult cord and did not increase during regeneration.
- **In situ / patterning**: In regenerating Xenopus cord, Msi-1 formed an arched pattern in the ependymal outgrowth complementary to a ventral Shh domain; by 10 days the central canal was reconstructed with restored floorplate Shh/Msi-1 coexpression. In Axolotl, Shh was low in intact adult cord and strongly up-regulated during regeneration, paralleling Msi-1.
- **GFAP / proliferation**: GFAP-labeled radial ependymal fibers were reduced during mesenchymal outgrowth. PCNA-positive proliferation overlapped Msi-1 distribution in regeneration-competent stages; notably, intact adult Axolotl ependymal cells were PCNA-positive but Msi-1-negative.
- **Cell culture**: In EGF-containing medium, regeneration-competent NF 50 Xenopus and adult Axolotl ependymal cells both formed proliferative mesenchymal outgrowth and maintained Msi-1 expression, whereas non-regeneration-competent NF 62-64 Xenopus cells attached poorly and did not expand.

---

## Perspective
The study establishes that active Msi-1 expression - especially its up-regulation after injury in the ependymal outgrowth and stump region - is a strong, cross-species indicator of regeneration competence in the amphibian spinal cord, both in vivo and in vitro. Despite differences in whether Msi-1 is present at the moment of injury (constitutive in developing Xenopus tadpole vs. re-activated in adult Axolotl), regeneration-competent ependymal cells of both species share a common EGF-driven mesenchymal growth response, linking Notch/Msi-mediated plasticity to successful regeneration. Placing Msi-1 in the context of Shh (DV patterning), GFAP (tissue reorganization), and PCNA (proliferation) shows that regeneration competence is not simply about proliferation - adult Axolotl ependymal cells proliferate without Msi-1 in the intact cord - but about the injury-induced re-acquisition of a Msi-1-positive, Notch-active plastic state. Limitations include reliance on semi-quantitative RT-PCR and antibody/in situ localization (adult urodele in situ gave high background, requiring antibody methods) and the descriptive/correlative nature of the marker analysis; functional perturbation of Msi is not performed. The work motivates testing whether manipulating Msi/Notch signaling can confer or restore ependymal regenerative competence, with relevance to mammalian SCI where ependymal cells are a clinical target.
