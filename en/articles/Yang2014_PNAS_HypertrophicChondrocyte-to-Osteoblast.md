# Hypertrophic chondrocytes can become osteoblasts and osteocytes in endochondral bone formation

## Citation (NLM)

Yang L, Tsang KY, Tang HC, Chan D, Cheah KSE. Hypertrophic chondrocytes can become osteoblasts and osteocytes in endochondral bone formation. _Proc Natl Acad Sci USA._ 2014;111(33):12097-12102. doi:[10.1073/pnas.1302703111](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1302703111)

---

## Background

During endochondral ossification, chondrocytes and osteoblasts originate from common osteochondroprogenitors but have long been considered independent cell lineages. The traditional doctrine held that proliferative chondrocytes differentiate into hypertrophic chondrocytes (HCs) and then undergo apoptosis at the growth plate terminus, while osteoblast precursors entering via vascular invasion replace the cartilage. However, the debate over whether HCs can transdifferentiate into osteoblasts and contribute to bone formation has persisted for over a century. Previous studies using non-specific lineage tracing tools, short half-life fluorescent proteins, or strong apoptosis-inducing signals in the HC microenvironment failed to clearly demonstrate HC survival and transdifferentiation potential. This study employed a chondrocyte-specific Cre/loxP recombination system driven by the Col10a1 promoter to trace HC fate and demonstrated that HCs survive and transdifferentiate into osteoblast and osteocyte lineages.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

**1. Generation of Col10a1-Cre (C10cre) and Col10a1-CreERt mice**

- Targeted insertion of Cre or CreERt (tamoxifen-inducible Cre) into the endogenous Col10a1 locus to generate HC-specific Cre-expressing mice
- Crossed with Rosa26R-locus reporter mice (RLacZ, RYFP, Z/EG) for permanent labeling of HCs and their progeny

**2. Tamoxifen-induced pulse-chase lineage tracing**

- Single tamoxifen injection in C10creERt mice at defined developmental stages to induce Cre activation only within a restricted time window
- HC progeny traced following tamoxifen injection at E12.5, E13.5, E14.5, P5, P9, and other time points

**3. X-Gal staining, immunohistochemistry, in situ hybridization**

- Visualization of HC-derived cells via LacZ reporter activity (X-Gal staining)
- Confirmation of osteoblast/osteocyte marker expression using Col1a1, OSX (osterix), SOST (sclerostin) probes
- Confocal microscopy analysis using fluorescent reporters (YFP, GFP)

**4. Bone injury model for HC contribution assessment**

- Isolation of hypertrophic cartilage from distal tibia of P10 C10cre::RLacZ/YFP donors
- 0.4 mm drilling of 3-month-old adult female tibiae followed by HC transplantation
- Analysis of reporter+ cells at the bone healing site at 2, 5, and 8 days post-injury

**5. Quantitative contribution analysis**

- Calculation of HC labeling rate (%) and proportion of HC-derived cells among Col1a1+ cells at E16.0, E18.5, and P5 following tamoxifen injection at E13.5

## Results

**Validation of HC-specific Cre reporter system** Cre transcript and LacZ activity in Col10a1-Cre mice were restricted to HCs and not detected in the periosteum (bone collar). Stage-specific analysis revealed that only HCs were LacZ+ before primary ossification center (POC) formation (E15.0), but after POC formation (E15.5 onward), LacZ+ cells appeared within the POC in addition to HCs, demonstrating that HC-derived cells survive in the ossification center. In P10 neonatal mice, Col10a1-GFP was restricted to HCs, whereas in C10cre::RYFP mice, YFP signals extended beyond the HC zone into trabecular bone, endosteum, and cortical bone, indicating the presence of progeny cells with osteoblast and osteocyte morphology. No YFP+ cells were observed in the calvaria (intramembranous ossification), confirming HC-specific labeling.

**Demonstration of HC→osteoblast conversion via tamoxifen-induced lineage tracing** Analysis of C10creERt mice at 24 and 36 hours after tamoxifen injection at E14.5 revealed more LacZ+ cells within the POC in proximal skeletal elements (humerus) than distal elements (tibia). At 36 hours, LacZ+ cells expressed Col1a1 and OSX, confirming differentiation into immature and mature osteoblasts. After tamoxifen injection at E13.5, LacZ+ cells were continuously detected in trabecular bone, endosteum, and cortical bone from E18.5 to P1m, expressing OSX, Col1a1, and SOST, demonstrating differentiation across the entire osteoblast-osteocyte lineage.

**Quantitative contribution of HC-derived osteoblasts** In the E13.5 tamoxifen group, approximately 49.3% of HCs were labeled at E16.0. Subsequently, at E18.5, approximately 16.4% of Col1a1+ osteoblasts were HC-derived (LacZ+), and at P5, approximately 8.5%. Considering dilution by continuously added periosteum-derived osteoblasts, this is likely an underestimate of actual contribution. Approximately 80% of HC-derived cells expressed Col1a1, suggesting that most HC progeny differentiate into osteoblasts.

**Postnatal HC contribution to bone formation and bone regeneration** Following tamoxifen injection at P5 or P9, LacZ+ cells appeared in the HC zone and cartilage-bone interface within 16-24 hours, some expressing Col1a1. LacZ+ osteocytes persisted in 16-month-old mice, confirming long-term survival of HC-derived cells. In the bone injury model, HC transplants began expressing collagen I and SOST at 5 days, and by 8 days, the cartilage graft had resorbed and LacZ+ osteoblasts and osteocytes were clearly observed at the injury site, demonstrating direct HC contribution to bone regeneration.

## Perspective

This study provides decisive in vivo evidence for the century-old debate on whether HCs can become osteoblasts, using the Col10a1-Cre/CreERt lineage tracing system. The authors demonstrated that HCs survive the cartilage-bone transition without apoptosis, differentiate into osteoblasts and osteocytes during embryonic and postnatal endochondral ossification, and directly contribute to bone regeneration.

This finding demands a conceptual shift regarding the origin of osteoblasts in endochondral ossification. A new model proposes three origins for osteoblasts: (1) direct differentiation from periosteal mesenchymal cells forming cortical bone, (2) derivation from periosteum-derived progenitors entering with vascular invasion, and (3) HC transdifferentiation contributing to trabecular and endosteal osteoblasts (Fig. 5K).

Future studies should investigate the respective roles and relative contributions of osteoblasts from different origins (periosteum-derived vs. HC-derived) in bone homeostasis, aging, and fracture healing. This study also prompts reinterpretation of how mutations in cartilage-expressed genes cause bone phenotypes, providing important insights for bone disease and regenerative medicine research.
