# Separation of wound healing from regeneration in the cockroach leg

## Citation (NLM)
Truby PR. Separation of wound healing from regeneration in the cockroach leg. J Embryol Exp Morphol. 1985;85:177-190.

---

## Background

Earlier work (Truby, 1983) showed that cockroach limb regeneration from the trochanter-femur breakage plane involves considerable respecification (dedifferentiation) of epidermal cells proximal to the wound, forming a blastema that grows into a new leg. However, because both wound healing and regeneration require epidermal activation and cell division, the observed patterns of division could not be cleanly attributed to one process versus the other.

Cockroaches (and many arthropods) have a "critical point" roughly halfway through the moult cycle, after which regeneration will not occur until the following moult cycle, although wound healing still proceeds. This study exploits that fact: by amputating a leg after the critical point, wound healing and regeneration can be temporally separated, allowing their cell-division patterns to be studied independently.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Amputation of the metathoracic leg at the trochanter-femur breakage plane in second-instar *Periplaneta americana*, before or after the critical point.
2. Determination of the timing of the critical point by amputating on different days after moulting and scoring whether regeneration occurred within the same intermoult period.
3. Colchicine injection (12 h before fixation) to reveal cell-division patterns in light-microscope sections; mitotic figures counted per 100 µm of epidermis, averaged over sections.
4. Semithin (1.5 µm) longitudinal resin sections stained with toluidine blue; cell densities measured as nuclei per 100 µm epidermis.
5. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) of trochanter cuticle after the post-amputation ecdysis to assess tissue respecification via bristle patterns.

---

## Results

- The critical point in the second intermoult period falls between days 7 and 12 of the ~16-day moult cycle (5% failed to regenerate on day 7, rising to 100% by day 12); legs were removed on day 10 (90% past critical point).
- During wound healing (after the critical point), activation and cell division spread only ~150 µm — about the distal half of the trochanter — then cells return to the resting state, a new cuticle is secreted, and the animal moults with no blastema formed.
- After the following ecdysis, cells in the distal trochanter reactivate (~2 days post-ecdysis) and activation/division spread ~750 µm (~80 cell diameters) to include the whole trochanter and distal coxa, forming a blastema that segments and grows.
- Because the epidermis returns to rest before ecdysis and only later reactivates, blastema formation is not driven by the low cell density of wound healing but by some other stimulus — most likely discontinuities in the normal epidermal pattern created by leg removal.
- Cell-division patterns during regeneration are the same whether it occurs in the same intermoult period as leg removal or the following one; distal structures (femur-tibia, tibia-tarsus joints) form before proximal (coxa-trochanter), supporting a distoproximal order of redifferentiation.
- SEM confirmed that after wound healing alone (before regeneration), the trochanter/distal coxa bristles resemble a normal (not regenerated) leg, indicating dedifferentiation had not yet occurred.
- The critical point coincides with an ecdysone peak; wound-healing activation/division is ecdysone-independent, but blastema formation occurs only at low ecdysone levels (though, once started, the blastema grows even at high ecdysone).

---

## Perspective

By using the critical point to temporally uncouple wound healing from regeneration, this study establishes that blastema formation in the cockroach leg is not a direct consequence of wounding or wound-healing-induced low cell density, but rather a response to positional discontinuities in the epidermal pattern, dependent on appropriate (low) ecdysone levels. It supports models (Lewis, Mittenthal) predicting a spread of activation over a region around a discontinuity rather than purely local intercalation at the wound surface, and reinforces the distoproximal redifferentiation order. The technique of separating the two processes is proposed as generally useful, e.g., for clarifying cell-division patterns after grafting. Within the knowledgebase's comparative regeneration scope, it is a foundational invertebrate appendage-regeneration study dissecting the distinct cellular logic of repair (wound healing) versus true regeneration (blastema). Limitations are its descriptive histological approach, use of colchicine-arrested mitoses as the division readout, and absence of molecular/mechanistic detail on the proposed discontinuity signal.
