# Sex-Specific Features of Microglia from Adult Mice

## Citation (NLM)
Villa A, Gelosa P, Castiglioni L, Cimino M, Rizzi N, Pepe G, Lolli F, Marcello E, Sironi L, Vegeto E, Maggi A. Sex-Specific Features of Microglia from Adult Mice. Cell Rep. 2018;23(12):3501-3511. doi:10.1016/j.celrep.2018.05.048

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2018.05.048](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2018.05.048)

---

## Background

Microglia are the CNS-resident myeloid cells that shape neurodevelopment (axon guidance, neurite growth, synaptic pruning) and maintain lifelong brain homeostasis, but prolonged activation can drive neuronal damage and neuroinflammation implicated in neurological and neuropsychiatric disease. Sex influences the incidence and outcome of many neurological illnesses, and estrogens are known to have anti-inflammatory activity, suggesting that sex steroids may contribute to sex-dependent manifestation of brain pathology. Because microglia participate in perinatal brain masculinization and express estrogen receptor alpha, the authors hypothesized that perinatal estrogen exposure could exert permanent (organizational) effects on microglia.

This study set out to define transcriptomic differences between male and female microglia isolated from healthy adult mice, to test whether these differences are hormone-dependent, and to determine whether microglial sex has functional consequences for a CNS pathology (focal cerebral ischemia).

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **RNA-seq of purified adult microglia** from male and female C57BL/6 mice (pools of 6 brains; 12 mice/sex), with AltAnalyze mapping to confirm purity and CuffDiff differential-expression analysis (FDR < 0.01); qPCR validation on additional cohorts.
2. **Gene Ontology / transcription-factor enrichment** (Enrichr, ChEA) of sex-biased differentially expressed genes (DEGs).
3. **NF-kB reporter mouse (NFkB-luc2)** for in vivo whole-body bioluminescence imaging and luciferase assays in purified microglia to assess inflammatory (NF-kB) activation state.
4. **Ovariectomy (OVX) ± 17b-estradiol replacement (3 or 24 hr)** with RNA-seq to test the contribution of circulating estrogens to NF-kB-regulated gene expression.
5. **Primary adult microglia cultures** co-cultured with rat embryonic neurons; morphological (Iba1) and qPCR analysis to test retention of sex-specific phenotype in vitro.
6. **Microglia transplantation**: endogenous microglia depleted with the CSF1R inhibitor PLX3397 (transnasal route), then bioluminescent/fluorescent female or male microglia transnasally transferred into male brains; sex-of-origin gene expression tracked via Xist/C1qa qPCR and FACS.
7. **Brain masculinization**: neonatal E2-benzoate treatment (P2/P5/P8) of pups, then qPCR of male-biased DEGs.
8. **Permanent focal cerebral ischemia (pMCAO)** in transplanted mice with diffusion-weighted MRI (2/24/48 hr) to quantify infarct progression, plus immunostaining for pro- (CD16/32) and anti-inflammatory (Ym1) microglial markers.

---

## Results

- RNA-seq identified 546 DEGs between adult male and female microglia (204 higher in males, 342 higher in females). Male-biased genes were enriched for inflammatory processes (cell migration, cytokine production) under NF-kB, RUNX1, FOXM1, GATA2 control; female-biased genes were enriched for morphogenesis/development/cytoskeleton terms under NANOG, TCF3, and ESR1.
- Male microglia showed a more inflammation-prone state: microglial luciferase activity in NFkB-luc2 mice was 2.4-fold higher in males, and 79% of 95 NF-kB target inflammatory genes were more expressed in males.
- Sex differences were largely estrogen-independent: ovariectomy did not significantly change male-female correlation of NF-kB genes, and 17 of 34 DEGs remained lower in OVX females than males despite the absence of circulating estrogens; estrogen replacement produced only transient, non-predictable shifts.
- Sex-specific gene expression was cell-intrinsic and stable: microglia retained their sex-of-origin expression pattern and morphology (male microglia ~75% globular/pro-inflammatory vs. lower in females) in culture, and female microglia transplanted into male brains (F/M) maintained a female expression profile.
- Neonatal E2-benzoate treatment ("masculinization") of female pups raised male-biased genes (Akt1s1, Trem1, Cxcl2) toward male levels, supporting a perinatal, organizational origin of microglial sex.
- Female microglia are neuroprotective: after pMCAO, male recipients transplanted with male microglia (M/M) had significantly greater infarct progression (+26% at 48 hr) than those receiving female microglia (F/M). Ym1 (anti-inflammatory) immunoreactivity in peri-lesional Iba1+ cells was +92% higher in F/M than M/M.

---

## Perspective

The study establishes that murine microglia are sexually differentiated in a way that is cell-autonomous, retained in culture and after cross-sex transplantation, and largely independent of the adult circulating hormonal environment—pointing to a perinatal, organizational programming (analogous to neuronal brain masculinization) rather than ongoing activational hormone effects. Functionally, female microglia limit ischemic damage even inside a male brain, providing a mechanistic basis for the well-documented sex bias in stroke susceptibility and outcome and, more broadly, for sex-dependent vulnerability to neuroinflammatory disease. This argues that microglial sex should be considered when designing sex-tailored therapies targeting neuroinflammation. Limitations include reliance on a mouse model, small pooled-sample RNA-seq designs, incomplete resolution of the precise (genomic/epigenetic) mechanism of sexual differentiation, and the inability to fully exclude local aromatase-derived estrogen effects; the male-into-female transplant results were also less clear-cut, possibly buffered by resident female microglia. Future work on the epigenetic basis of microglial sex and its relevance to human disease is warranted.
