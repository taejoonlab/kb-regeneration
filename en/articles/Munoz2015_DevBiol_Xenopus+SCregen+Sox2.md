# Regeneration of Xenopus laevis spinal cord requires Sox2/3 expressing cells

## Citation (NLM)
Muñoz R, Edwards-Faret G, Moreno M, Zuñiga N, Cline H, Larraín J. Regeneration of Xenopus laevis spinal cord requires Sox2/3 expressing cells. Dev Biol. 2015;408(2):229-243. doi:10.1016/j.ydbio.2015.03.009

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ydbio.2015.03.009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ydbio.2015.03.009)

---

## Background

Spinal cord injury (SCI) in mammals, including humans, causes permanent loss of motor and sensory function because the mammalian CNS shows almost no tissue regeneration. In contrast, teleost fish, urodele amphibians, and anuran larvae such as *Xenopus laevis* can functionally regenerate the spinal cord after transection. *X. laevis* is especially useful because it exhibits stage-dependent regeneration: pre-metamorphic larvae (stages 50–54) regenerate robustly, whereas this capacity is progressively lost during metamorphosis and is absent in post-metamorphic froglets (stage 66). This natural transition between regenerative and non-regenerative states within one organism allows direct comparison of the underlying cellular and molecular mechanisms.

Sox2 and Sox3 are SoxB1-family transcription factors expressed in neural stem and progenitor cells and required for maintenance of the neural progenitor pool. Prior work showed Sox2+ cells are needed for tail regeneration in *X. laevis* and axolotl. This study characterizes the response of Sox2/3-expressing ventricular-zone cells to spinal cord transection at regenerative versus non-regenerative stages and tests their functional requirement for regeneration.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Complete spinal cord transection at mid-thoracic level in pre-metamorphic (stages 50, 54), pro-metamorphic (56), climax (58), and post-metamorphic (66) animals.
2. Hematoxylin and eosin histology of longitudinal sections to track anatomical repair (2, 6, 10, 20 dpt).
3. Quantitative locomotor recovery assay by video-tracking free swimming distance over 5 min (ANY-maze).
4. Immunofluorescence and in situ hybridization for Sox2/3, Doublecortin (Dcx), neuroD, neurogenin2/3; Western blot and RT-qPCR for Sox2/3 protein and mRNA.
5. BrdU/CldU proliferation assays with Sox2/3 double labeling.
6. In vivo 2-photon time-lapse imaging of cells electroporated with a *X. tropicalis* Sox3 promoter driving GFP (pSox3:GFP).
7. Loss-of-function: Sox2 morpholino knockdown by spinal cord electroporation, plus a heat-shock-inducible dominant-negative Sox2 (dnSox2) transgenic line; Sox3-specific and combined morpholinos tested for specificity.

---

## Results

- Pre-metamorphic (stage 50/54) animals restored anatomical continuity of the ependymal canal and axon tracts and significantly recovered swimming by 20 dpt; pro-/post-metamorphic (stage 56/66) animals filled the gap with fibrillary material, failed to reconnect, and showed no swimming recovery — defining R- and NR-stages.
- Sox2/3 protein levels and the number of Sox2/3+ cells (with elongated progenitor-like nuclei) were high in the ventricular zone at R-stages and declined during metamorphosis, while *sox2*/*sox3* mRNA remained unchanged, indicating post-transcriptional regulation.
- After injury, R-stage animals showed rapid, transient, massive proliferation (~50% of Sox2/3+ cells BrdU+ at 2 dpt), and Sox2/3+ cells populated the ablation gap forming neural-tube-like structures. NR-stage froglets showed only a delayed, limited response (~15% at 6 dpt) and no Sox2/3+ cells in the gap.
- In vivo imaging confirmed injury-induced increase of pSox3:GFP+ cells (>50% of animals), their migration into the gap, and their differentiation into cells with neuronal morphology (axon and dendritic arbor).
- Sox2 knockdown or dnSox2 overexpression abolished/delayed both swimming recovery and axon regrowth across the injury; Sox3 morpholinos alone had no significant effect.
- Neurogenesis markers (Dcx, neuroD, neurogenin2, neurogenin3) increased after injury specifically in R-stages, implicating injury-induced neurogenesis in regeneration.

---

## Perspective

The study establishes *X. laevis* as a tractable, quantifiable model of stage-dependent spinal cord regeneration and provides functional evidence that Sox2 (and the proliferative activation of Sox2/3+ neural progenitors and their neurogenic differentiation) is necessary for regeneration. The correlation between loss of Sox2/3+ cell activation and loss of regenerative capacity during metamorphosis points to progenitor competence, rather than mere presence, as the decisive factor — a concept relevant to why the mammalian CNS fails to regenerate.

Limitations include the Sox2 antibody's cross-reactivity (detecting both Sox2 and Sox3), the inability to resolve a distinct role for Sox3, and imperfect functional recovery (abnormal swimming behavior) suggesting incomplete circuit reconstruction. Future directions include dissecting the post-transcriptional control of Sox2/3 protein and the upstream signals that trigger progenitor activation, with the goal of reactivating equivalent programs in non-regenerative contexts.
