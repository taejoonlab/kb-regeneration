# A Single Cell Atlas of Spared Tissue Below a Spinal Cord Injury Reveals Cellular Mechanisms of Repair

## Citation (NLM)
Matson KJE, Russ DE, Kathe C, Maric D, Hua I, Krynitsky J, Pursley R, Sathyamurthy A, Squair JW, Courtine G, Levine AJ. A Single Cell Atlas of Spared Tissue Below a Spinal Cord Injury Reveals Cellular Mechanisms of Repair. bioRxiv. 2021 Apr 29. doi:10.1101/2021.04.28.441862

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1101/2021.04.28.441862](https://doi.org/10.1101/2021.04.28.441862)

---

## Background
After spinal cord injury (SCI), the "spared" tissue below the lesion contains undamaged cells that could support or augment recovery, but targeting these cells requires understanding their injury responses and repair capacity. Following acute trauma, a complex interplay of responses among neurons, astrocytes, microglia, oligodendrocytes, vascular cells, and other cell types shapes the outcome.

Beyond the lesion epicenter, there is emerging focus on the spared tissue below a SCI and its potential for anatomical reorganization and recovery, underscored by therapies such as epidural electrical stimulation combined with rehabilitation. This study profiled how each cell type in the lumbar spinal cord changes over time after a thoracic injury, seeking molecular and cellular mechanisms that promote or restrict recovery.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Severe thoracic (T9) contusion injury (90 kdyn impactor) in mice, with locomotor tracking across acute-to-chronic timepoints (1 dpi, 1 wpi, 3 wpi, 6 wpi); complete transection injuries used for comparison.
2. Single nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-Seq) of entire lumbar spinal cords (triplicate per condition), yielding 67,903 nuclei; hierarchical clustering into 8 major classes and 39 cell types.
3. Highly multiplexed immunohistochemistry and multiplexed RNA in situ hybridization (Agt, Gja1, Aqp4) for in-tissue validation of cell-type proportions.
4. Augur analysis to rank cell-type perturbation responsiveness (cluster-size unbiased); differential expression, gene ontology, and KEGG pathway analysis across timepoints.
5. Label transfer against a mouse spinal cord meta-analysis to annotate neuronal families; RNAscope in situ (Sprr1a, Atf3) with spatial and triple-labeling (Slc17a6/vGluT2, Vsx2/Chx10, Shox2) analyses of regeneration-associated gene (RAG)-expressing neurons.
6. AAV-mediated over-expression of Igf1 and Spp1 (OPN) in lumbar cord (uninjured and post-transection); analysis of microglia expansion, phospho-IGF1R, Ki67 proliferation, and myelin/degraded-myelin clearance.

---

## Results
- The eight major cell classes (neurons, astrocytes, microglia/hematopoietic, oligodendrocyte lineage, Schwann, endothelial, pericytes, ependymal, leptomeninges) and 39 subtypes were resolved; overall cellular composition remained relatively stable after injury.
- Two exceptions within the first week: microglia/hematopoietic proportion tripled and the sequencing-based astrocyte proportion dropped; in-tissue validation showed the microglia increase was genuine but the astrocyte decrease largely reflected relative proportional shifts rather than true cell loss.
- Augur ranked microglia as the most perturbation-responsive cell type at 1 dpi; the greatest gene expression changes occurred at 1 dpi (glial metabolism burst, reactive astrocyte markers Lcn2/Serpina3n, near-universal Mt1/Fth1 metal-binding induction suggesting extravascular blood cues), with neuronal stress/plasticity changes emerging at 1-3 wpi and a return toward baseline by 6 wpi.
- A rare, heterogeneous population of ascending projection neurons induced a regeneration-associated gene (RAG) signature after injury (Atf3, Sprr1a, Tnfrsf12a/Fn14, Sox11, Bdnf, Adcyap1, Gap43); RAG-expressing cells were largely excitatory, showed a rostral-caudal gradient, and ventral RAG+ neurons were nearly all excitatory and often Shox2+ (putative spinocerebellar), with high specificity (only Shox2+, not Vsx2+, neurons induced RAGs). Both contusion and complete transection elicited RAG expression.
- A "trauma associated microglia" (TAM) population, resembling disease-associated microglia (Alzheimer's, ALS) and postnatal myelin-phagocytosing microglia, localized along white matter tracts of degenerating axons (rubrospinal, corticospinal) and expressed phagocytic (Cd68, Gpnmb, Itgax), lipid-processing (Apoe, Lgals3, Fabp5), and trophic (Igf1, Spp1/OPN) genes.
- TAM were the only cells co-expressing Igf1 and Spp1 (OPN); active (phospho) IGF1R and Ki67 proliferation localized to white-matter microglia clusters, supporting an autocrine self-amplification loop. AAV over-expression of Igf1/Spp1 expanded TAM (phagocytic microglial nodules) in both injured and uninjured cords and drove clearance/degradation of myelin debris.

---

## Perspective
This study provides a reference single-cell/nucleus atlas of cell-type-specific responses in the spared lumbar cord after thoracic SCI and uncovers two endogenous, if limited, repair mechanisms: a rare RAG-expressing pro-regenerative neuronal population and TAM cells capable of clearing growth-inhibitory myelin debris. Exogenous Igf1/Spp1 delivery can amplify TAM-mediated myelin clearance, suggesting a candidate therapeutic route, potentially most effective with temporally regulated delivery. The similarity of TAM to microglia in Alzheimer's disease and ALS suggests shared CNS trauma/disease pathways. Limitations noted include the use of nuclei (lower depth, no post-transcriptional readout) and the demonstrated need for independent in-tissue validation of single-cell proportion changes. As a preprint, findings await peer review.
