# Spinal cord regeneration in Xenopus tadpoles proceeds through activation of Sox2-positive cells

## Citation (NLM)
Gaete M, Muñoz R, Sánchez N, Tampe R, Moreno M, Contreras EG, Lee-Liu D, Larraín J. Spinal cord regeneration in Xenopus tadpoles proceeds through activation of Sox2-positive cells. Neural Development. 2012;7:13.

---

## Background
Unlike mammals, where spinal cord injury (SCI) results in massive local neuron loss and no neural regeneration, amphibians such as anuran larvae (Xenopus) and adult urodeles can regenerate their spinal cord after injury. In Xenopus this ability is restricted to larval stages and is lost at the end of metamorphosis, making tadpoles an attractive comparative model to understand why mammals fail to regenerate. The SoxB1 family of transcription factors (Sox1, Sox2, Sox3) is expressed in neural stem/progenitor cells and ependymal cells and blocks cell-cycle exit to maintain NSC/NPC identity. This study examines the expression and functional role of Sox2 in the two main Xenopus regeneration paradigms — tail amputation and spinal cord transection.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Immunofluorescence for Sox2 and acetylated α-tubulin on spinal cord cryosections/whole-mounts of stage 49 tadpoles to characterize ependymal progenitors.
2. RT-PCR and whole-mount in situ hybridization for sox2 (and Xbra) mRNA at different days post amputation (dpa) at the regenerating tail tip and in spinal cord rostral to the amputation.
3. BrdU/Sox2 double-labeling and confocal microscopy to quantify proliferation of Sox2+ cells after tail amputation and after transection.
4. Transgenesis (meganuclease method) expressing a heat-shock-inducible dominant-negative Sox2 (Sox2BD(−)-GR, dexamethasone-controlled) versus HS::EGFP controls; scoring of tail regeneration and BrdU incorporation.
5. Complete spinal cord transection at mid-thoracic level in larvae at different metamorphic stages (50, 54, 58, 66) with 8-week behavioral scoring of functional recovery; sham operations as controls.
6. Western blot of Sox2, GFAP, Vimentin, Nestin in isolated spinal cord across metamorphic stages.

---

## Results
- Ependymal-tube cells in larval spinal cord are ciliated with nuclear Sox2, hallmarks of neural progenitors; Sox2 shows a dorsal-to-ventral gradient (higher ventrally).
- Tail amputation upregulated sox2 mRNA and protein and increased the number of Sox2+ cells, not only near the amputation but systemically (up to 5–8 mm rostral, plus lateral line and olfactory epithelium).
- BrdU labeling showed tail amputation activates proliferation of Sox2+ cells (≈20–60% of Sox2+ cells proliferating at 4 dpa); proliferation begins in spinal cord cells before notochord and mesenchyme.
- Overexpression of dominant-negative Sox2 impaired tail regeneration in a dose-dependent manner (46% vs 95% regeneration at stage 42) and reduced BrdU incorporation in spinal cord (and notochord), and shortened regenerated spinal cord.
- Functional recovery after spinal cord transection declines sharply during metamorphosis: stage 50/54 recover coordinated swimming, but recovery is abruptly reduced at stage 58 and completely lost in stage-66 froglets.
- Sox2 protein levels fall during metamorphosis (strong drop at stage 58), correlating with loss of regenerative capacity; GFAP/Nestin/Vimentin drop later (stage 66).
- After transection, Sox2+ cells lining the ependymal canal decrease and change from elongated to round nuclei; by 11 dpt Sox2+ cell aggregates colonize the ablation gap, suggesting migration rather than massive local proliferation.

---

## Perspective
The work provides functional (not merely correlative) evidence that Sox2+ neural progenitor activation is required for spinal cord and tail regeneration in Xenopus, and links loss of Sox2 to the metamorphic loss of regenerative capacity. It proposes two complementary mechanisms — proliferative amplification of Sox2+ cells after amputation and migration of Sox2+ cells to bridge the ablation gap after transection (reminiscent of the Schwann-cell "nerve bridge"). Limitations acknowledged by the authors: the dominant-negative construct may also interfere with other SoxB1 members (Sox1/Sox3); transgene mosaicism at later stages weakens the phenotype; and the reduced BrdU+ signal after transection leaves open whether rapid early proliferation or migration dominates. Comparing regenerative and non-regenerative metamorphic stages is proposed as a fertile paradigm to uncover why mammals cannot regenerate the spinal cord.
