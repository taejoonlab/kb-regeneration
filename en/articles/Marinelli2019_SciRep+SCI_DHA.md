# Innovative mouse model mimicking human-like features of spinal cord injury: efficacy of Docosahexaenoic acid on acute and chronic phases

## Citation (NLM)
Marinelli S, Vacca V, De Angelis F, Pieroni L, Orsini T, Parisi C, Soligo M, Protto V, Manni L, Guerrieri R, Pavone F. Innovative mouse model mimicking human-like features of spinal cord injury: efficacy of Docosahexaenoic acid on acute and chronic phases. Sci Rep. 2019;9(1):8883. doi:10.1038/s41598-019-45037-x

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-019-45037-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-019-45037-x)

---

## Background

Traumatic spinal cord injury (SCI) affects 250,000–500,000 people worldwide each year and imposes devastating, lifelong disability. Most experimental knowledge derives from animal models, but existing surgical models (transection, compression, contusion) each imperfectly reproduce the human condition. Notably, the standard contusion model requires laminectomy before impact, which removes bone compression and thereby reduces the damage relative to real human injuries. SCI is a two-step process: a primary mechanical injury followed by a secondary phase (acute from ~2 h, subacute 7–30 days, chronic beyond 30 days) driven by inflammation, Wallerian degeneration, demyelination, oxidative stress, excitotoxicity, oligodendrocyte death, and glial scar formation around a cystic cavity.

This study introduces a new mouse contusion model performed **without laminectomy**, so that damage from bone destruction and persistent compression is preserved, more faithfully mimicking human SCI. The authors then test Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), an omega-3 fatty acid reported to reduce neuroinflammation and promote neuroplasticity, on both the acute and chronic phases—addressing the gap that most prior DHA studies used models with spontaneous recovery and focused only on the acute phase.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **Non-laminectomy contusion model:** Adult female CD1 mice mounted on a stereotaxic frame; a computer-controlled PinPoint precision impactor delivered contusion at T9–T11 (T10) directly onto the vertebral bone (severe parameters: tip #4, velocity 3 m/s, depth 5 mm, dwell time 800 ms). Injury severity graded as mild/moderate/severe.
2. **Functional assessment:** Basso Mouse Scale (BMS) open-field locomotor scoring and tail-flick (TF) thermal reflex, tracked to 30 days; male mice excluded due to spontaneous recovery.
3. **Imaging/histology:** microCT of vertebral fractures; GFAP, DAPI, MBP, NeuN, OLIG1, caspase-3 immunostaining at 7, 30, and 60 days; morphometric analysis of astrocyte/microglia reactivity.
4. **DHA treatment:** Systemic i.p. DHA (vs saline) daily from day 1 to day 5 after injury.
5. **Molecular analyses:** Comparative proteomics (SAL vs DHA at day 7) interpreted with Ingenuity Pathway Analysis (IPA); growth factor antibody array (10 trophic factors) at 24 h and 7 days; Western blot of MBP time course, p75NTR and phospho-JNK.

---

## Results

- **Model validity:** Only severe impact produced permanent hindlimb paraplegia persisting 30 days; mild/moderate impacts recovered spontaneously. microCT showed vertebral fractures/fragments, as in human trauma. CD1 mice developed progressive cystic cavitation (microcysts at 7 d, cavity at 30 d, extended cavity + gliosis at 60 d).
- **Functional recovery with DHA:** DHA-treated mice improved BMS scores within one week and fully recovered motor performance by 4 weeks, while saline mice remained totally paralyzed. DHA also restored the tail-flick reflex by day 20.
- **Tissue protection:** DHA reduced microcysts, cavity formation, and increased the regenerated area (60 d). GFAP-marked gliosis was reduced not only at the epicenter but also at caudal/cranial levels; astrocytes were less hypertrophic and did not form a scar.
- **Microglia:** DHA increased resting (R) microglia and reduced hyperactive/bushy (H/B) and unramified/amoeboid (U/A) phenotypes in distal and peri-lesioned areas.
- **Proteomics:** 109 proteins differentially modulated (56 up, 53 down in DHA). Myelin inhibitors MOG and PLP1 were up-regulated in saline but not DHA. DHA up-regulated glycolysis/gluconeogenesis, semaphorin signaling, and mitochondrial/antioxidant proteins (SOD2, ATP synthase subunits, VDAC), predicted to activate IGF1R/PPARG/INSR regulators.
- **Myelin, growth factors, apoptosis:** DHA normalized the SCI-induced rise in MBP. Trophic factors (bFGF, IGF-I, EGF, VEGF) were strongly up-regulated after injury but attenuated by DHA. DHA reduced caspase-3 co-localization in NeuN+ neurons and OLIG1+ oligodendrocytes, counteracted ventral-horn neuronal loss at 30 d, and lowered p75NTR and p-JNK.

---

## Perspective

The study delivers two contributions: a clinically more realistic, reproducible severe-SCI mouse model that retains the bone-derived compression component absent from laminectomy-based contusion models and shows no spontaneous recovery, and evidence that repeated systemic DHA given only in the first 5 days produces long-lasting neuroprotection, reduces demyelination/apoptosis/neuroinflammation, limits glial scarring, and restores motor and sensory function across both acute and chronic phases. Together these support DHA as a candidate translational therapy and provide a stringent platform for testing neuroprotective/anti-inflammatory strategies.

Limitations include use of a single sex (female CD1) after males were excluded for spontaneous recovery, small histology group sizes (often n = 3–4), and correlative (IPA-based) mechanistic inference from proteomics rather than direct pathway manipulation. The contusion model's known difficulty distinguishing spared from regenerated axons also applies. Future work should define the mechanism of DHA action, extend dosing/timing studies, and validate across strains, sexes, and species toward clinical translation.
