# RNA Profiling of the Human and Mouse Spinal Cord Stem Cell Niches Reveals an Embryonic-like Regionalization with MSX1+ Roof-Plate-Derived Cells

## Citation (NLM)
Ghazale H, Ripoll C, Leventoux N, Jacob L, Azar S, Mamaeva D, et al. RNA Profiling of the Human and Mouse Spinal Cord Stem Cell Niches Reveals an Embryonic-like Regionalization with MSX1+ Roof-Plate-Derived Cells. Stem Cell Reports. 2019;12(5):1159-1177. doi:10.1016/j.stemcr.2019.04.001

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.stemcr.2019.04.001](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.stemcr.2019.04.001)

---

## Background

The adult CNS maintains neural stem cells in specialized niches. Beyond the well-studied subventricular zone (SVZ) and hippocampus, a third niche exists around the central canal of the spinal cord in anamniotes, rodents, and humans—the ependymal zone (EZ), which originates from the embryonic neuroepithelium and is organized as a pseudoepithelium. The EZ can activate upon injury and, depending on lesion type, contribute to glial scar formation, making it a possible endogenous source for repair in mammalian spinal cord lesions.

Compared with brain niches, the spinal cord EZ is poorly characterized: cell diversity and region-specific genes are ill-defined, and a detailed transcriptomic profiling of the human and mouse EZ was lacking. Understanding the EZ's molecular identity could clarify why, in contrast to anamniotes, mammalian ependymal cells cannot regenerate neurons after spinal cord injury. This study provides a cellular and molecular resource for the mouse and human EZ using RNA profiling, immunostaining, and fluorescent transgenic mice.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Microdissection of the EZ and adjacent peri-EZ tissue from two human samples (aged 17 and 46 years, thoracolumbar, with a central lumen) and four mice, followed by microarray RNA profiling.
2. Volcano-plot and Venn-diagram analysis to identify genes enriched in mouse and human EZ (fold change ≥2), with GO and KEGG pathway analyses.
3. Microdissection of dorsal, lateral, and ventral subregions of the mouse EZ to identify regionally expressed genes.
4. Comparison with published spinal cord single-cell transcriptome datasets (Rosenberg et al. 2018; Zeisel et al. 2018) and the Allen brain atlas / GENSAT.
5. Immunofluorescence (IF) validation of transcription factor (TF) proteins (ARX, FOXA2, FOXJ1, ID4, MEIS2, MSX1, NFIA, PAX6, PBX1, SOX4, SOX9, SOX11) in human and mouse EZ.
6. Vegfr3-YFP transgenic mice to detect roof/floor EZ subpopulations; Msx1-CreERT2/Rosa-Tomato genetic lineage tracing (tamoxifen at E11.5 or in adults) to trace developmental origin of MSX1+ cells; Vegfr3-YFP × Msx1-Tomato and Msx1-Tomato × hGFAP-GFP crosses.
7. EdU incorporation assays and 3D spinal cord clearing/reconstruction to assess proliferation and morphology of MSX1+ cells.

---

## Results

- 8,733 and 2,122 genes were enriched in the mouse and human EZ respectively, with 1,223 genes commonly enriched (including ~120 conserved transcription factors). Enriched pathways included cilia formation, smoothened/SHH and Hippo signaling, cell division, transcription, and solute carrier (SLC) transport; CFTR was specifically expressed in the EZ of both species.
- Only a few genes identified in prior single-cell studies overlapped; Tmem212 was the only ependymal-enriched gene shared across three studies, and Espn and Pkd2l1 marked CSF-contacting neurons (CSF-N) across studies.
- The EZ unexpectedly maintains an embryonic-like dorsal-ventral pattern of spinal cord developmental TFs: PAX6 (dorsal/lateral), MSX1 (dorsal roof), ARX and FOXA2 (ventral floor). This regionalization was conserved between mouse and human at the protein level.
- Ventral EZ expressed SHH-pathway genes (Arx, Foxa1, Sulf1) and Decorin (Dcn); dorsal EZ expressed BMP morphogens Bmp6 and Gdf10/Bmp3b plus Prokr2 and Thbs2. DCN and TAL1 marked mouse CSF-N but were not detected in human EZ, indicating divergent ventral EZ organization between species; no convincing CSF-N were identified in human.
- Vegfr3-YFP labeled distinct roof and floor EZ subpopulations: ~67% of roof YFP+ cells expressed MSX1 (and c-RET), ~90% of floor YFP+ cells expressed ARX.
- Genetic tracing (Msx1-Tomato, tamoxifen at E11.5) showed MSX1+ and ARX+ EZ cells arise early (present at E13 in embryonic roof/floor plates), migrate centrally, decline in number postnatally, and persist as few roof/floor cells in the adult EZ. Thus ependymal cells are not entirely ventral-derived but also incorporate dorsal (roof plate) cells.
- Roof MSX1+ cells are long radial cells (processes toward the pial surface); only ~15% were hGFAP-GFP+, and no MSX1+/EdU+ cells were observed, indicating they are quiescent (or very slowly proliferating) radial cells.

---

## Perspective

This study delivers a conserved human/mouse molecular atlas of the adult spinal cord ependymal stem cell niche and reveals the unexpected persistence of embryonic-like dorsal-ventral patterning (ARX, FOXA2, MSX1, PAX6) into adulthood, with dorsal roof-plate-derived MSX1+ quiescent radial cells and ventral floor-plate-derived ARX+ cells. The identification of NFIA/SOX9 (gliogenic TFs) may explain the glial fate restriction of EZ cells after injury, relevant to understanding limited mammalian spinal cord regeneration. The MSX1 link is notable given its reported role in promoting regeneration of the amputated Xenopus tail (including spinal cord).

Limitations include the small human sample size (n=2, one of reduced histological quality), reliance on microarray bulk profiling of microdissected tissue rather than single-cell resolution, and species divergence (absence of clear human CSF-N and of DCN/TAL1 markers). The functional significance of the roof/floor-derived subpopulations and of Vegfr3 in the adult spinal cord EZ, and whether these quiescent cells can be mobilized for repair, remain to be tested.
