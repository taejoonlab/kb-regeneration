# Ependymal cilia beating induces an actin network to protect centrioles against shear stress

## Citation (NLM)
Mahuzier A, Shihavuddin A, Fournier C, Lansade P, Faucourt M, Menezes N, Meunier A, Garfa-Traoré M, Carlier MF, Voituriez R, Genovesio A, Spassky N, Delgehyr N. Ependymal cilia beating induces an actin network to protect centrioles against shear stress. Nat Commun. 2018;9:2279. doi:10.1038/s41467-018-04676-w

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-04676-w](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-04676-w)

---

## Background

Multiciliated ependymal cells line all brain ventricles, form a protective barrier, and contribute to the neural stem cell niche. The beating of their motile cilia drives cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow required for brain homeostasis, toxin washout, delivery of signalling molecules, and orientation of newborn neuron migration; defective cilia motility is linked to hydrocephalus. Ependymal cells are generated from radial glial cells postnatally, regenerate poorly, and must persist throughout life. Their motile cilia, nucleated from centrioles, therefore have to withstand the mechanical forces produced by external fluid flow and by their own beating.

How centrioles resist these lifelong mechanical constraints was unknown. Prior work in multiciliated Xenopus epidermis showed apical and subapical actin networks connecting centrioles, but how the actin cytoskeleton is assembled in ependymal cells and its direct link to centriole stability had not been addressed. This study investigates the coordination between ciliary beating and actin organisation in protecting ependymal cilia and centrioles against shear stress.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Whole-mount immunostaining of lateral ventricular walls at postnatal stages (P4, P6, P15) for F-actin (phalloidin), centrioles (FOP, Centrin, Cep164, Akap450, Rootletin), and cilia (GT335).
2. High-resolution STED microscopy and 3D modelling to map apical vs subapical actin networks relative to centriole distal/proximal markers.
3. Genetic loss of cilia/motility: hGFAP-Cre;Kif3A-cKO and Nestin-Cre;Ift88-cKO (no motile cilia); hydin mutants (stiff, slow-beating cilia).
4. Postnatal electroporation of shRNA against Lrrc6 (axonemal dynein) to reduce cilia beating, and against Cobl (actin nucleator); Cobl-DsRed for localisation.
5. Pharmacology on P15 lateral wall explants: NiCl2 (blocks beating), cytochalasin-D (depolymerises actin), CK666 (Arp2/3 inhibitor), SMIFH2 (formin inhibitor).
6. High-speed video microscopy/kymography of cilia beating frequency; centriole counting with Centrin2-GFP; supernatant collection to detect expelled centrioles/cilia; physical force estimation modelling cilia as cylinders.

---

## Results

- During ependymal cell maturation actin assembles progressively around centrioles into two networks: an apical network (from distal appendages to distal centriole lumen) and a subapical network (proximal end to rootlets), forming a 3D actin structure; enrichment correlates with motile cilia elongation.
- Motile cilia are required for apical actin enrichment: Kif3A-cKO and Ift88-cKO cells lacking motile cilia lack the apical centriolar actin network, while the subapical network is unaffected.
- Cilia motility specifically drives apical enrichment: hydin mutants, Lrrc6 depletion, and NiCl2 treatment all reduce beating and decrease apical (not subapical) actin at the centriolar patch, showing beating both initiates and actively maintains it.
- Actin maintains centrioles: cytochalasin-D depolymerisation caused a time- and dose-dependent loss of centrioles and reduced centriolar patch size and inter-centriolar spacing, without affecting cilia length or beating frequency.
- The apical network specifically maintains centriole number: formins (Fhod1, Fmn1, Daam1, mDia1) localise to the centriolar patch; SMIFH2 (formin inhibition) selectively depleted apical actin and reduced centriole number, while the actin nucleator Cobl localised to and was required for the apical network and its depletion also reduced centriole number.
- Apical actin protects against beating-generated force: centriole loss occurred only when cilia were actively beating; blocking beating (NiCl2) or removing cilia (Kif3A-cKO) rescued centriole loss caused by actin disruption. Detached centrioles/cilia were recovered in the supernatant of cytochalasin-D-treated cells, indicating beating-generated shear stress expels centrioles when apical actin is impaired.
- Force estimates: beating counters an F_flow of ~33 pN and generates a local torque F_torque of ~728 pN at the centriole base, comparable to forces some flagella use for propulsion.

---

## Perspective

The work establishes a mechanical feedback loop: cilia beating induces a dense apical actin network around centrioles, and this network in turn stabilises centrioles against the shear stress/torque that beating produces, preserving the correct number of motile cilia and efficient CSF flow. This is important because impaired CSF flow is linked to hydrocephalus and age-related dementia, and ependymal cells regenerate poorly. Formins (some mechanosensitive, e.g. mDia1) and Cobl are implicated as nucleators of the protective apical network.

Limitations: the direct molecular links between centrioles and actin were not identified, and whether formins are directly mechanically activated by beating remains to be demonstrated; the expulsion mechanism was partly inferred from drug-based actin disruption in vitro. Future directions include defining the structural organisation of both networks, identifying subapical nucleators, and testing centriolar maintenance factors (Poc1, FOP, Cep135) in this context.
