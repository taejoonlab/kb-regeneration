# Cockroach Leg Regeneration: Effects of Ecdysterone in vitro

## Citation (NLM)

Marks EP, Leopold RA. Cockroach Leg Regeneration: Effects of Ecdysterone in vitro. Science. 1970;167(3914):61-62. doi:10.1126/science.167.3914.61

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1126/science.167.3914.61](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.167.3914.61)

---

## Background

Regenerating insect legs provide a classic model for studying appendage regeneration and the hormonal control of epidermal secretion. Prior reports of cuticle-like deposits appearing on cultured insect epidermal tissues had generated considerable confusion over the chemical nature of the deposited materials, their relationship with epithelial cells, the conditions required for deposition, and the hormonal requirements of the explants.

To clarify these issues, the authors performed parallel in vivo and in vitro studies using regenerating leg tissue from the cockroach *Leucophaea maderae*, characterizing the successive coverings formed by the regenerating epithelium and testing whether the molting hormone ecdysterone could induce cuticle deposition in culture.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Mesothoracic legs of late-instar nymphs were amputated at the trochantero-femoral articulation 24 hours after ecdysis; regenerates were recovered 8-42 days later.
2. Some regenerates were fixed, sectioned, and examined histochemically; others were cultured in Rose multipurpose tissue chambers under dialysis strips in M-18 nutrient medium.
3. Histochemistry: triple-stain technique for protein and carbohydrate (with acetylation, deacetylation, and pepsin-digestion controls to verify specificity); thiazine red dichroic method for birefringence; fluorescent chitinase test for chitin; PAS (periodic acid-Schiff) reaction.
4. Hormonal induction: Rose chambers were set up in pairs, each containing a leg from opposite sides of the same insect. One chamber was treated with ecdysterone (2.5-12 µg/ml), the other with water or with the reportedly inactive analog 22-isoecdysone. After 14 days, control chambers were washed and re-treated with ecdysterone.

---

## Results

- Regenerates dissected at ~25 days were covered by a thin gelatinous sheath that was PAS-positive; acetylation abolished and deacetylation restored the reaction, and pepsin treatment prevented it, indicating a carbohydrate complex conjugated with protein (a glyco-/mucoprotein). The sheath lacked dichroic birefringence and chitinase affinity, so it contained no chitin.
- At 40-42 days, a second, wrinkled membrane bearing spines and setae formed between the original sheath and the epithelial cells. This layer was PAS-negative, showed dichroic birefringence, and bound the fluorescent chitinase conjugate — the early chitinous cuticle.
- In untreated cultures, epithelial cells migrated over the injury and deposited a new sheath (with hairlike processes), but no recognizable cuticle formed even after 30 days.
- Adding ecdysterone (2.5-12 µg/ml) to cultures induced cuticle deposition: a refractile layer appeared between epithelial cells and sheath by day 7, thickened and wrinkled, setae sockets formed, and by day 14 a chitinase-positive rugose, pigmented cuticle developed.
- All 14 ecdysterone-treated regenerates formed a recognizable cuticle within 14 days (8 also formed setae); doses as low as 2.5 µg/ml were 100% effective. None of the 14 controls (water or 22-isoecdysone) produced cuticle or setae.
- When controls were subsequently re-treated with ecdysterone, all water controls formed cuticle within 8 days; 22-isoecdysone-pretreated explants also formed cuticle but required 2-5 additional days.
- The cuticle formed in vitro remained thin, lacking the heavy endocuticle layer deposited in vivo at ecdysis.

---

## Perspective

The study demonstrates that ecdysterone directly switches the secretory activity of regenerating leg epidermal cells from gelatinous-sheath deposition to chitinous-cuticle deposition in vitro, and that this switch is not triggered by much larger doses of an inactive ecdysone analog — establishing a specific hormonal induction. Because the epidermal cells remain accessible for microscopic observation throughout both secretory phases, the system offers a clean means of studying hormone action on cuticle deposition during appendage regeneration.

Limitations include the incomplete maturation of the in vitro cuticle (absent endocuticle) compared with in vivo development, and the partial characterization of the gelatinous sheath's composition and function. The work is an early foundational example of dissecting the endocrine control of regenerative epidermal differentiation.
