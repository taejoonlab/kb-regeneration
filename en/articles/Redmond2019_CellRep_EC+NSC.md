# Development of Ependymal and Postnatal Neural Stem Cells and Their Origin from a Common Embryonic Progenitor

## Citation (NLM)
Redmond SA, Figueres-Oñate M, Obernier K, Nascimento MA, Parraguez JI, López-Mascaraque L, Fuentealba LC, Alvarez-Buylla A. Development of Ependymal and Postnatal Neural Stem Cells and Their Origin from a Common Embryonic Progenitor. Cell Rep. 2019;27(2):429-441. doi:10.1016/j.celrep.2019.01.088

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2019.01.088](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2019.01.088)

---

## Background

The adult mouse brain harbors an extensive neurogenic niche in the lateral walls of the lateral ventricles (the ventricular–subventricular zone, V-SVZ). This epithelium has a characteristic "pinwheel" organization consisting of multiciliated ependymal (E1) cells with large apical domains surrounding the small apical endings of neural stem cells (B1 cells, a subpopulation of GFAP+ astrocytes). E1 cells propel cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) via coordinated ciliary beating, whereas B1 cells act as primary progenitors of neurons and oligodendrocytes.

This postnatal germinal epithelium develops from the embryonic ventricular zone (VZ), and radial glia (RG) are known to generate both B1 and E1 cells. However, it was unknown whether single RG cells can give rise to both cell types or whether distinct RG populations produce exclusively E1 or B1 cells. Since E1 and B1 cells are born at similar embryonic times (around E14.5–E15.5), the authors investigated the developmental transformation of the VZ into the adult V-SVZ and tested whether E1 and B1 cells share a common embryonic progenitor.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Whole-mount immunostaining (β-catenin to delineate apical profiles) of the lateral ventricle wall at embryonic and postnatal stages (E17.5 to P40) to measure surface area, apical domain size, and cell number.
2. Systematic random sampling-based stereology to estimate total ventricular epithelial cell populations over time.
3. Marker analysis of apical domains using FoxJ1 (E1 cells), VCAM1 (B1 cells), γ-tubulin (deuterosomes/basal bodies) across perinatal/postnatal stages, subdividing the wall into four quadrants (A-D, A-V, P-D, P-V).
4. Retroviral barcode lineage tracing (QmGFP-OL library) combined with laser microdissection of ependymal cells and olfactory bulb (OB) neurons.
5. UbC-StarTrack clonal analysis (12 transposon constructs, six fluorophores, hyPBase + CreERT2) via in utero electroporation at E14.5, analyzed at P60.
6. Nestin::CreER;Ai14 low-dose tamoxifen clonal labeling with dual BrdU pulses, and Nestin::CreER;Confetti high-dose labeling, analyzed at P10/P21.

---

## Results

- The lateral wall surface area grew ~2-fold from E17.5 (3.19 mm²) to P15 (5.97 mm²), then plateaued to P40, while total epithelial cell number declined ~44% from E17.5 (117,148 cells) to P15 (65,879 cells).
- This growth despite net cell loss is explained by dramatic expansion of E1 apical domains (up to ~11-fold; ~16.5 µm² in E17.5 RG cells to ~185.6 µm² in P10 FoxJ1+ cells).
- VCAM1 is transiently expressed in nearly all RG cells around birth; it is downregulated in maturing E1 cells (which upregulate FoxJ1) but maintained in B1 cells. A brief transitional VCAM1+/FoxJ1+ stage (some with deuterosomes) was observed until ~P5.
- Pinwheels emerge from ~P5 in a posterior-ventral to anterior-dorsal, ventral-to-dorsal gradient, matching the gradient of E1 differentiation.
- Retroviral barcoding found E1 cells sharing barcodes with OB neurons, suggesting E1 and B1 lineage relatedness.
- Four independent lineage-tracing methods directly revealed clonally related E1-B1 cell pairs (plus E1-E1 pairs) in the adult V-SVZ, with bipotent E1-B1 clones peaking around E14.5.
- These findings demonstrate that at least a subpopulation of RG cells is bipotent, giving rise to both ependymal (E1) and neural stem (B1) cells.

---

## Perspective

This study charts the cellular transformation of the embryonic VZ into the adult V-SVZ niche and provides direct clonal evidence that ependymal cells and adult neural stem cells—two cell types with strikingly different structure and function—share a common embryonic origin. The work clarifies how the ventricle grows via apical expansion of E1 cells rather than cell addition, and how pinwheel architecture forms in a defined spatiotemporal gradient.

Significance: understanding the shared origin and maturation of E1 and B1 cells is fundamental for CNS regeneration strategies that aim to mobilize or engineer endogenous ependymal/stem cell populations. Limitations noted by the authors include the possibility that some clonally related siblings were undetected due to cell death, migration, or consuming neurogenic divisions, and that the intrinsic/extrinsic signals determining RG fate remain elusive. Future directions include identifying molecular determinants of the epithelial remodeling and of E1 versus B1 fate divergence, and how the relative numbers of the two cell types are controlled.
