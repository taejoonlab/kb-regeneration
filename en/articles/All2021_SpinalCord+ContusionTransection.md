# Comparative analysis of functional assessment for contusion and transection models of spinal cord injury

## Citation (NLM)
All AH, Al-Nashash H. Comparative analysis of functional assessment for contusion and transection models of spinal cord injury. Spinal Cord. 2021;59(11):1206-1209. doi:10.1038/s41393-021-00698-2

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41393-021-00698-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41393-021-00698-2)

---

## Background

Standardized, reproducible animal models are essential to characterize the onset and progression of spinal cord injury (SCI) and to compare results across studies. The two most widely used rodent SCI models are contusion (impact-based, produced with the NYU-Impactor) and transection (a clean surgical incision producing a well-defined interruption of neuropathways). In real-world human SCI, both types of damage frequently occur simultaneously around the injury epicenter: blunt force can cause vertebral fracture/dislocation leading to laceration or partial/full transection alongside the contusion. However, a true translational model combining both elements is still lacking, and the two injuries have distinct pathoanatomy and pathophysiology.

This study performs a descriptive secondary analysis comparing somatosensory evoked potential (SSEP) and Basso-Beattie-Bresnahan (BBB) motor behavioral assessments across graded severities of contusion and transection SCI over the 3-week acute phase, to elucidate their functional similarities and differences (without attempting to directly correlate the two mechanistically distinct injury types).

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Adult male and female rats (225-250 g) randomly grouped (n = 5 per group), with identical animal weight, body temperature, anesthesia, surgical procedures, recording instruments, and statistical analysis across all groups.
2. Contusion injuries at four severities via NYU-Impactor weight-drop heights: mild (6.25 mm), moderate (12.5 mm), severe (25 mm), very severe (50 mm).
3. Transection injuries: right T10 hemi-transection (RxI); left T8 + right T10 double hemi-transection (DxI); T8 complete transection (CxI). Control group received laminectomy only (no injury).
4. SSEP monitoring: four skull screw electrodes over forelimb/hindlimb somatosensory cortices plus a reference near Lambda; needle electrodes near Median (forelimb) and Tibial (hindlimb) nerves for stimulation. N1 and P2 peaks detected; N1-P2 peak-to-peak amplitude normalized to baseline; ensemble averaging of 100-700 sweeps; processed in MATLAB R2019a.
5. Motor assessment: BBB 21-point open-field locomotor rating scale by two independent observers.
6. Statistical analysis: repeated-measures ANOVA over 5 time points (Baseline, Day 4, Week 1, Week 2, Week 3), with pairwise multivariate t-tests (Fisher Least Significant Difference) and correction for multiple comparisons.

---

## Results

- Laminectomy-only control showed no SSEP amplitude attenuation and no BBB deficit, as expected.
- Relative SSEP amplitudes and BBB scores decreased with increasing injury severity in both contusion and transection groups.
- Statistical analysis showed significant differences (P < 0.05) for both models, with a differential progression pattern emerging after week 1.
- Week-3 relative SSEP amplitudes revealed model correspondences:
  - Mild contusion (66%) ≈ right T10 hemi-transection (55%) — attributed to signal propagation via anatomically continuous spared ascending fibers.
  - Moderate contusion (53%) ≈ double hemi-transection (48%) — attributed to continuity of axons and healthy neuropathways on the opposite side.
  - Severe (11%) and very severe (2%) contusion ≈ complete T8 transection (19%) — substantial/near-total axonal interruption; residual continuity between transected edges (no insulating material used) allows some non-discriminatory signal spread.
- BBB motor scores followed the same trend of correspondence.
- Overall conclusion: although contusion and transection have completely different pathophysiologies, their injury progression during the acute phase follows a similar trend.

---

## Perspective

The study provides a useful cross-model functional map, allowing researchers to interpret contusion severity in terms of equivalent transection injuries (and vice versa) using objective SSEP and behavioral BBB endpoints during the acute phase. This may aid model selection and comparison across the fragmented SCI literature. Notably, the authors deliberately avoid claiming a mechanistic correlation between the two injury types, given their distinct pathoanatomy (irregular parenchymal spread from gray matter into white matter in contusion vs. clean incision with Wallerian degeneration in transection).

Limitations include the small group sizes (n = 5), focus on only the 3-week acute phase (avoiding the unstable sub-acute phase), and the absence of histological data in this report (referenced to the authors' prior publications). A true translational model combining both contusion and transection elements concurrently remains an unmet need. Future work extending the comparison into sub-acute and chronic phases, and correlating functional readouts with histology, would strengthen translational value.
