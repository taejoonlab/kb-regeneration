# Changes in type of collagen synthesized as clones of chick chondrocytes grow and eventually lose division capacity

## Citation (NLM)
Mayne R, Vail MS, Mayne PM, Miller EJ. Changes in type of collagen synthesized as clones of chick chondrocytes grow and eventually lose division capacity. Proc Natl Acad Sci USA. 1976;73(5):1674-1678. doi:10.1073/pnas.73.5.1674

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.73.5.1674](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.73.5.1674)

---

## Background

Fibroblasts from various tissues have a limited lifespan in culture and eventually lose their capacity to divide. In contrast, relatively few studies have examined the in vitro senescence of differentiated cells such as chondrocytes. Chacko et al. (1969) observed that upon serial passage of chick embryo chondrocyte clones, the polygonal chondrocyte morphology shifted to flattened, amoeboid cells that no longer accumulated metachromatic matrix, a phenomenon termed dedifferentiation. Similar morphological changes were observed after short-term treatment with 5-bromo-2'-deoxyuridine (BrdUrd). The aim of this study was to track changes in collagen biosynthesis during serial passage of chondrocyte clones until they lost division capacity in vitro.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. **Chondrocyte clone isolation**: Chondrocytes were isolated from the sterna of 13-day chick embryos. Single cells were separated using a glass micropipette and cultured as individual clones in 60 mm dishes.
2. **Serial passage**: Cultures were passaged 1–4 times upon reaching high density. Total culture duration was 7–9 weeks until cellular senescence.
3. **Collagen labeling and extraction**: At the end of each passage, cells were labeled with [2-³H]glycine for 24 h. Cell layers and media were pooled, collagen was precipitated with 25% ammonium sulfate, and extracted with 0.5 M acetic acid.
4. **CM-cellulose chromatography**: Denatured collagen α-chains were separated to determine the α1:α2 chain ratio. Elution was performed with a linear NaCl gradient (0–0.14 M).
5. **Differential salt precipitation**: Type I collagen was precipitated at 2.4 M NaCl, and Type I trimer was precipitated at 0.01 M Na₂HPO₄.
6. **CNBr peptide analysis**: Purified α-chains were cleaved with CNBr. The resulting peptides were analyzed by CM-cellulose chromatography to identify collagen types.
7. **Molecular sieve chromatography**: An agarose (Agarose A-5m) column was used to confirm the molecular weight of collagen chains.

---

## Results

- Early chondrocyte populations ("floaters") showed only α1(II) chains (α1:α2 ratio > 50), indicative of Type II collagen [α1(II)]₃.
- With successive passaging, the α1:α2 ratio progressively declined: first passage 26.4 → second 8.4 → third 5.5 → fourth (final) 2.9.
- Fibroblast clones maintained an α1:α2 ratio of approximately 2.0 from the outset, consistently synthesizing only Type I collagen.
- At the final passage, a mixture of Type I collagen [α1(I)]₂α2 and Type I trimer [α1(I)]₃ was synthesized. Salt precipitation fractionation yielded: (1) a 2.4 M NaCl precipitate (α1:α2 ≈ 2.0, Type I collagen) and (2) a 0.01 M Na₂HPO₄ precipitate (α1 chains only, Type I trimer).
- CNBr peptide analysis confirmed that the Type I trimer consisted of α1(I) chains (CNBr pattern distinct from α1(II)).
- BrdUrd treatment experiments: 5-day BrdUrd exposure at the first passage yielded an α1:α2 ratio of 3.4, indicating that BrdUrd accelerated the collagen switch.
- Additional experiments: Culture in dialyzed embryo extract also induced collagen switching without cell flattening, suggesting that collagen switching is not solely dependent on cellular senescence.

---

## Perspective

- This study demonstrates the instability of the chick chondrocyte phenotype in vitro through collagen switching, ruling out fibroblast overgrowth and confirming chondrocyte-intrinsic dedifferentiation.
- The transition from Type II to Type I + Type I trimer collagen synthesis in culture was also observed with BrdUrd treatment, suggesting that BrdUrd accelerates the senescence process.
- Type I trimer synthesis was not clearly observed in fibroblasts, suggesting it may be relatively specific to the chondrocyte population.
- These findings predict that hyaline cartilage may synthesize Type I collagen during in vivo aging. Reports of Type I collagen in osteoarthritic cartilage are consistent with this prediction.
- Collagen switching can be induced by factors other than cellular senescence (also observed in embryo extract culture), suggesting a mechanism involving random error accumulation from BrdUrd incorporation.
- The limited lifespan of chondrocytes (~30 population doublings) is consistent with the Hayflick limit, providing an important model for cellular senescence research.
