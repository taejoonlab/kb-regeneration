# Calcification as an indicator of osteoinductive capacity of biomaterials in osteoblastic cell cultures

## Citation (NLM)
Declercq HA, Verbeeck RMH, De Ridder LIFJM, Schacht EH, Cornelissen MJ. Calcification as an indicator of osteoinductive capacity of biomaterials in osteoblastic cell cultures. Biomaterials. 2005;26(24):4964-4974. doi:10.1016/j.biomaterials.2005.01.025

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biomaterials.2005.01.025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biomaterials.2005.01.025)

---

## Background

In vitro screening of the osteoinductive capacity of biomaterials is essential before clinical application. Various osteoblast types (primary osteoblasts from bone marrow, calvaria, periosteum, long bone; UMR-106 osteosarcoma cell line) are used for biomaterial evaluation. Indicators of the osteoblast phenotype include alkaline phosphatase (ALP) activity, type I collagen synthesis (early markers), osteocalcin secretion (late marker), and formation of calcified extracellular matrix. However, it is important to distinguish whether in vitro calcification represents true cell-mediated bone formation or merely dystrophic calcification. This study aimed to compare mineralization patterns in various rat-derived osteoblast culture models using von Kossa staining, FTIR, X-ray diffraction (XRD), and transmission electron microscopy (TEM), and to correlate these with ALP, collagen I, and osteocalcin expression to evaluate the suitability of each cell model for biomaterial testing.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

- **Cell culture**: Six types of rat-derived osteoblast cultures — bone marrow (6-week-old), calvaria (fetal), periosteum (adult, subcultured P1-P8), long bone (fetal/adult), UMR-106 osteosarcoma cell line.
- **ALP activity measurement**: Biochemical quantification (p-nitrophenylphosphate substrate) and histochemical staining (BCIP/NBT).
- **Osteocalcin secretion analysis**: Osteocalcin measurement in medium by EIA (enzyme immunoassay) after 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D3 stimulation.
- **Mineralization evaluation**:
  - **Von Kossa staining**: Visualization of calcium phosphate deposition.
  - **Tetracycline labeling**: Identification of new mineralization sites by fluorescence microscopy.
  - **Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)**: Observation of collagen fibers, matrix vesicles, and needle-like crystal structures within the ECM.
  - **X-ray diffraction (XRD)**: Crystal structure analysis of mineral phases. Measurement of relative intensity and full width at half maximum (FWHM) of [002] diffraction peak.
  - **Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR)**: Chemical composition analysis of mineral phases (confirmation of B-type carbonated apatite).
- **Experimental design**: 3-4 independent experiments performed for each cell type at 4, 14, and 21 days of culture (duplicate).

---

## Results

### Differences in mineralization patterns by cell type

#### Nodular mineralization — bone marrow, calvaria, early passage periosteum
- Mineral deposition restricted to limited three-dimensional nodular areas. Well-defined nodules observed by Von Kossa staining.
- Electron microscopy: abundant cross-banded collagen I fibers within nodules, matrix vesicles containing needle-like crystals observed between fibers. Some cells showed in vivo osteoblast-like morphology.
- Osteocalcin secretion: detectable only in bone marrow-derived cells (increased from day 4 to day 21).
- XRD: poorly crystalline B-type carbonate apatite (similar to in vivo bone mineral). Recrystallization upon alkali treatment.
- FTIR: characteristic peaks of collagen and carbonate apatite identified. [002] peak FWHM 0.82, the broadest (lowest crystallinity).

#### Diffuse mineralization — long bone (fetal/adult), late passage periosteum (P6-8), UMR-106
- Diffuse mineral deposition throughout the culture monolayer without nodule formation.
- Electron microscopy: no collagen I fibers or matrix vesicles observed. Only needle-like crystals present between cells.
- Osteocalcin: barely detectable (UMR-106, long bone).
- ALP activity remained high even in long bone-derived cells.
- XRD: UMR-106 [002] peak FWHM 0.59, narrower than bone marrow (0.82) → higher crystallinity. Less recrystallization upon alkali treatment.
- FTIR: B-type carbonated apatite confirmed, but protein peaks were more prominent.

### Summary of osteoinductive capacity evaluation indicators
- Bone marrow-derived cells: ALP(++++), collagen I(+), nodules(+), osteocalcin(+), bone apatite(+) → meeting all indicators.
- Calvaria/early periosteum: collagen and nodules present but osteocalcin(−).
- Long bone/late periosteum/UMR-106: nodules(−), collagen(−), osteocalcin(−) → risk of false positives.
- Von Kossa staining alone cannot distinguish true cell-mediated calcification from dystrophic calcification.

---

## Perspective

- This study systematically compared the mineralization characteristics of various osteoblast culture models and presents suitable cell models for evaluating biomaterial osteoinductive capacity.
- Cells capable of nodule formation (bone marrow, calvaria, early passage periosteum) are the most reliable models reflecting true osteoblast differentiation.
- Long bone-derived cells, late passage periosteum cells, and UMR-106 cells, despite high ALP activity, lack nodule formation, collagen I fibers, matrix vesicles, and osteocalcin secretion, posing a risk of dystrophic calcification.
- Von Kossa and Alizarin Red S staining alone cannot distinguish cell-mediated mineralization from dystrophic calcification, making complementary analyses such as TEM, XRD, and FTIR essential.
- When evaluating biomaterial osteoinductive capacity, assessment should not rely solely on ALP activity but should comprehensively consider nodule formation, collagen I fibers, matrix vesicles, and osteocalcin secretion.
