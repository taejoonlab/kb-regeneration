# Increased Expression of Foxj1 after Traumatic Brain Injury

## Citation (NLM)
Cui G, Yu Z, Li Z, Wang W, Lu T, Qian C, Li J, Ding Y. Increased Expression of Foxj1 after Traumatic Brain Injury. J Mol Neurosci. 2011;45(2):145-153. doi:10.1007/s12031-011-9504-8

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1007/s12031-011-9504-8](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12031-011-9504-8)

---

## Background
Foxj1 is a member of the Forkhead/winged-helix (Fox) family of transcription factors with a conserved DNA-binding domain, best known for its role in cilia formation and left–right axis determination. Importantly, Foxj1 is required for the postnatal differentiation of ependymal cells and a subset of subventricular zone (SVZ) astrocytes; this astrocyte subpopulation can self-renew and has neurogenic potential, giving rise to astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, and neurons—functionally resembling adult neural stem cells. Foxj1-promoter-active cells in the spinal canal and SVZ have been reported to participate in neurogenesis and gliogenesis after spinal cord injury and stroke.

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) is a major cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide and triggers both primary and secondary injury involving neuronal apoptosis, neuroinflammation, and reactive astrogliosis. Because the cellular and molecular mechanisms after TBI remain incompletely understood, the authors hypothesized that Foxj1 may be involved in the pathophysiology and injury-induced neurogenesis of the brain after TBI, and investigated its expression and distribution in the adult rat brain cortex following injury for the first time.

---

## Key Experiment Methods
1. Traumatic brain injury model in adult male Sprague–Dawley rats using a controlled stereotactic microknife lesion to the right cortex; sham controls underwent identical procedures without knife insertion.
2. Animals sacrificed at multiple time points (12 h, 1, 3, 5, 7, 14, 28 days after injury); normal and sham controls sacrificed at day 3.
3. Western blot analysis of Foxj1 protein levels in the peri-wound cortex (with GAPDH as loading control), quantified semiquantitatively by relative optical density.
4. Immunohistochemistry to map the changed spatial distribution of Foxj1 (ipsilateral vs contralateral vs sham hemispheres) at day 3.
5. Double immunofluorescent staining to identify Foxj1-expressing cell types using MAP-2 (neurons) and GFAP (astrocytes); quantification of the proportion of marker-positive cells co-expressing Foxj1.
6. Double immunofluorescence with proliferation marker Ki67 combined with NeuN, GFAP, and Foxj1 to assess cellular proliferation and its relationship to Foxj1.
7. Statistical analysis by one-way ANOVA with Tukey post hoc tests (P < 0.05 significant).

---

## Results
- Foxj1 protein was low in normal and sham cortex, increased from 12 h after TBI, peaked at day 3, then gradually declined; at day 28 it remained higher than sham controls, indicating a temporal upregulation after injury.
- Immunohistochemistry showed Foxj1 immunostaining deposited strongly adjacent to the lesion site in the ipsilateral cortex at day 3, significantly higher than the contralateral hemisphere and sham-operated brain.
- Double immunofluorescence revealed Foxj1 co-localized with both MAP-2 (neurons) and GFAP (astrocytes); co-expression in both cell types was significantly increased at day 3 compared with sham.
- Ki67 co-localized with NeuN, GFAP, and Foxj1, indicating that neurons and astrocytes proliferated after injury and that Foxj1 was associated with cell proliferation.
- The authors observed the altered Foxj1 expression localized in the cytoplasm of cells in the injured brain.

---

## Perspective
This study provides the first demonstration that Foxj1 expression is increased in the adult rat brain after TBI, peaking at day 3 and localizing to the peri-lesional cortex in neurons and astrocytes that also show proliferation. The findings support the concept that Foxj1 is implicated in CNS pathophysiology after trauma and may be required for the differentiation of injury-responsive cells that act as adult neural stem cells (giving rise to neurons, astrocytes, and oligodendrocytes), potentially migrating to the lesion to compensate for neuronal loss. The work suggests a possible novel strategy for treating CNS trauma through injury-induced neurogenesis. Limitations include its descriptive/correlative nature: it demonstrates increased expression and co-localization but does not establish the functional role of Foxj1 (no loss- or gain-of-function manipulation), nor does it directly trace lineage or migration. The authors note that further studies are needed to confirm the inherent mechanisms of Foxj1's role after brain injury.
