# Regenerative capacity in the lamprey spinal cord is not altered after a repeated transection

## Citation (NLM)
Hanslik KL, Allen SR, Harkenrider TL, Fogerson SM, Guadarrama E, Morgan JR. Regenerative capacity in the lamprey spinal cord is not altered after a repeated transection. PLoS ONE. 2019;14(1):e0204193. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0204193

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0204193](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0204193)

---

## Background

Unlike mammals, many non-mammalian vertebrates (lampreys, bony fishes, amphibians, reptiles) can regenerate the spinal cord and functionally recover after complete transection. Lampreys are a well-established model: after a single spinal transection they recover undulatory swimming within ~10–12 weeks, accompanied by lesion tissue repair, axon and synapse regeneration, and stereotyped survival/regeneration behavior of 32 identified giant reticulospinal (RS) neurons.

While repeated-injury regeneration has been studied in a few models (planaria, zebrafish fin can regrow after many amputations; salamander/newt limbs regenerate imperfectly after a second amputation), almost nothing was known about how nervous-system regeneration is affected by repeated injury. This study tests the resilience of lamprey spinal cord regeneration after a second transection (re-transection) at the same lesion site.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Complete spinal cord transection in larval sea lampreys (*Petromyzon marinus*) at the 5th gill; after 11 weeks post-injury (wpi) recovery, re-transection through the original lesion scar, then another 11 wpi recovery (cohort n = 18).
2. Behavioral movement scoring (0–4 scale) twice weekly during both recovery periods; sigmoidal (Boltzmann) curve fitting.
3. Bright-field imaging of fixed spinal cords to measure stump gap and lesion-to-end ratio (ImageJ/FIJI).
4. Immunofluorescence on horizontal cryosections through the central canal: NF-180 (axons), SV2 (synapses), α-tubulin (microtubules), phalloidin (F-actin); quantified in proximal/lesion/distal regions.
5. Anterograde labeling (Alexa Fluor 488 dextran) to count regenerated axons at 1-mm intervals.
6. Retrograde labeling (tetramethylrhodamine dextran) of giant RS neurons to score axon regeneration cell-by-cell.
7. Nissl (Toluidine blue O) staining to assess giant RS neuron survival; ANCOVA correlation of survival vs. regeneration.

---

## Results

- **Functional recovery:** Recovery after re-transection was statistically indistinguishable from the first transection (~90% vs ~85% of normal, half-max at ~2.6 vs ~2.4 wpi; two-way ANOVA p = 0.37).
- **Tissue repair:** Complete but mildly altered after re-transection — smaller stump gap at 1 wpi (0.29 vs 0.91 mm, p = 0.04) and reduced lesion-to-end ratio at 3 and 11 wpi (narrower lesion site).
- **Axon/synapse/cytoskeleton:** NF-180 axon counts, SV2 synapse density, tubulin and phalloidin intensity showed the same reductions at the lesion but no differences between transection and re-transection.
- **Axon regeneration:** Anterograde labeling showed similar numbers of regenerated axons across distances; distal/proximal ratio (~40–60%) was equal or slightly higher after re-transection.
- **Giant RS neuron regeneration:** Similar per-brain regeneration (52.6% vs 51.9%, p = 0.92); "good" and "poor" regenerators behaved as before, with some poor regenerators (B1, B3, Mauthner) trending slightly better.
- **Neuronal survival:** Nissl(+) fractions were similar (61.3% vs 65.6%, p = 0.24); positive survival–regeneration correlation preserved (R² = 0.91 vs 0.84, ANCOVA p = 0.11).

---

## Perspective

The lamprey spinal cord retains robust, essentially undiminished regenerative capacity after a second transection at the same site — more persistent than the imperfect repeated-limb regeneration of salamanders/newts. This indicates the pro-regenerative machinery (giant RS neuron intrinsic survival/regeneration programs, ependymal-driven tissue bridging) is not exhausted by repeated CNS injury. Limitations include modest sample sizes for some anatomical measures and that only minor lesion-morphology differences were characterized without mechanistic dissection. The model offers a platform to identify pro-regenerative targets deployable in non-regenerative (mammalian) conditions.
